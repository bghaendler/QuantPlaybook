Antithetic Variables & Control Variates

Two classical variance-reduction techniques: exploit symmetry of the driving noise, or exploit a correlated quantity whose answer you already know.

1. Why Variance Reduction?

The Monte Carlo error of an estimator with per-path variance $s^2$ over $N$ paths is

$$\text{SE} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}.$$

Halving the error costs 4× the paths. Variance reduction attacks $s$ instead of $N$: a technique that cuts variance by factor $k$ is worth exactly $k\times$ more paths, for (usually) a few lines of code.

2. Antithetic Variables

If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ drives a path, then $-Z$ is equally probable. Pair each draw with its mirror and average:

$$\hat\theta_{AV} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(Z_i) + f(-Z_i)}{2}.$$

The estimator stays unbiased; its variance per pair is

$$\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{f(Z) + f(-Z)}{2}\right) = \frac{\sigma_f^2}{2}\left(1 + \rho\right), \qquad \rho = \operatorname{corr}\big(f(Z), f(-Z)\big).$$

Gain iff $\rho < 0$, which holds whenever $f$ is monotone in $Z$ (e.g. vanilla calls/puts on terminal $S_T$). For non-monotone payoffs (straddles, barriers near the barrier) antithetics can increase variance. Bonus: paired sampling also enforces a zero sample mean of the driving normals — a free first-moment match.

3. Control Variates

Suppose alongside the target payoff $Y$ you can compute on the same path a quantity $X$ with known expectation $\mathbb{E}[X]$. Form

$$\hat\theta_{CV} = \bar{Y} - \beta\left(\bar{X} - \mathbb{E}[X]\right), \qquad \beta^* = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Y, X)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)},$$

estimating $\beta^*$ by regression on the simulated sample (a pilot run or the same run — the bias is $O(1/N)$). The variance shrinks by the squared correlation:

$$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta_{CV}) = (1 - \rho_{XY}^2)\operatorname{Var}(\bar{Y}).$$

$\rho = 0.95$ → 10× variance reduction; $\rho = 0.99$ → 50×. The technique is a one-line regression with a guarantee: with estimated $\beta$, it can never do worse than plain MC (asymptotically).

4. The Classic Example: Arithmetic Asian via Geometric Control

The arithmetic-average Asian call has no closed form, but the geometric-average Asian call does (the geometric mean of lognormals is lognormal). Simulate both on identical paths:

$$\hat{C}_{arith} = \bar{Y}_{arith} - \beta\left(\bar{Y}_{geo} - C_{geo}^{closed}\right).$$

Arithmetic and geometric averages correlate at ρ ≈ 0.99+, so variance drops by a factor of 50–150 — the single most cost-effective trick in derivatives Monte Carlo. Other standard controls: the underlying itself ($\mathbb{E}[S_T] = S_0e^{bT}$), a European option under BS when pricing under a fancier model, or the delta-hedge portfolio (a "martingale control").

5. Practical Notes

  • Combine freely: antithetics + controls are complementary; add stratification or quasi-random (Sobol) sequences on top.
  • Estimate SE honestly: for antithetics, treat each pair as one observation; for controls, use the regression-residual variance.
  • Multiple controls: $\beta$ becomes a multivariate regression — diminishing returns and collinearity caution.
  • Greeks too: controls work on pathwise/likelihood-ratio Greek estimators just as well as on prices.
  • Effort check: a control with $\rho^2 = 0.9$ but 2× path cost still wins 5× overall — always compare variance×cost.

Key Takeaways

  • MC error ∝ $s/\sqrt{N}$: cut $s$, not your compute budget.
  • Antithetics: average $f(Z)$ with $f(-Z)$; works iff payoff is monotone in the noise.
  • Control variates: regress out a correlated quantity with known mean; gain $= 1/(1-\rho^2)$.
  • Geometric-Asian control for arithmetic Asians: the canonical 100× win.
  • Techniques stack — and always validate with the measured standard error.
