1.3.1 Explicit vs. Implicit Forms

Mathematical expressions can represent relationships in two distinct ways:

  • Explicit Form ($y = f(x)$): The dependent variable $y$ is isolated on one side of the equation. E.g. $y = 3x^2 + 2x - 1$.
  • Implicit Form ($f(x,y) = 0$): Variables are bundled together in a joint relation. E.g. $y^2 + x^2 - 16 = 0$. Implicit functions require specialized rules (like implicit differentiation) to evaluate derivatives without separating the variables.

Quadratic Systems: Solving quadratic equations of form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ is done by completing the square to derive the quadratic formula: $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$ The behavior of the roots depends strictly on the **Discriminant** ($\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$): * $\Delta > 0$: 2 distinct real roots (parabola crosses the X-axis twice). * $\Delta = 0$: 1 repeated real root (parabola touches the X-axis at its vertex). * $\Delta < 0$: 2 complex conjugate roots (parabola never touches the X-axis).

Worked Solutions: Completing the Square Proof

Derive the standard quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ from $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ by completing the square.

Step-by-Step Proof:
  1. Divide by $a$ (assuming $a \ne 0$): $$x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{c}{a} = 0 \implies x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{c}{a}$$
  2. Complete the Square: Add $\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ to both sides: $$x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 - \frac{c}{a}$$ $$\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \frac{b^2}{4a^2} - \frac{4ac}{4a^2} = \frac{b^2 - 4ac}{4a^2}$$
  3. Take the square root and solve for $x$: $$x + \frac{b}{2a} = \frac{\pm\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \implies x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \quad \blacksquare$$
