5. Hull-White Model (Time-Inhomogeneous)

Vasicek and CIR have only 4 parameters ($\kappa, \theta, \sigma, r(0)$), meaning they generate a rigid forward curve that usually fails to fit observed market data. We fix this by adding a time-dependent deterministic shift function $\phi(t)$:

$$ r(t) = \tilde{r}(t) + \phi(t) $$

where $\tilde{r}(t)$ is an auxiliary Vasicek model. We set $\phi(t)$ exactly equal to the difference between the market's initial forward curve $f^M(0,t)$ and the auxiliary model's implied forward curve $f^V(0,t)$. This gives the Hull-White model:

$$ dr(t) = (\kappa(\theta(t) - r(t))) dt + \sigma dW^*(t) $$

It matches any initial term structure perfectly while retaining closed-form affine pricing.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The ultimate custom-tailored suit for bond pricing.

  • The Problem: A 4-parameter model cannot match the complex, squiggly shape of today's actual yield curve. Off-the-shelf models don't fit the real world.
  • The Solution: We introduce a "fudge factor" ($\phi(t)$) that changes over time to bend the model exactly to the market data.
  • Example: We run the basic Vasicek model, compare its predicted forward curve to the real market data, and use the exact difference to perfectly align our model with reality—all without losing the fast formulas!

Practice Exercise: Calculating the Shift Factor

You are calibrating a Hull-White model. Your underlying Vasicek model implies a forward rate at $T=5$ of $f^V(0, 5) = 2.5\%$. However, the actual market data shows a forward rate of $f^M(0, 5) = 4.2\%$. What is the deterministic shift $\phi(5)$ required at year 5? If the market drops by 50 basis points across the board tomorrow, how does $\phi(5)$ change?

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution
  1. The Core Equation: The shift function bridges the gap between the model and the market: $\phi(t) = f^M(0, t) - f^V(0, t)$.
  2. Calculate Today's Shift: $\phi(5) = 4.2\% - 2.5\% = 1.7\%$. The model requires a +1.7% "fudge factor" at year 5.
  3. New Market Scenario: Tomorrow, the market drops 50 bps (0.5%). The new market forward rate is $f^M(0, 5) = 4.2\% - 0.5\% = 3.7\%$.
  4. New Shift: Since the underlying Vasicek parameters haven't changed, $f^V(0, 5)$ is still $2.5\%$. The new shift is $\phi(5) = 3.7\% - 2.5\% = 1.2\%$.

Conclusion: The Hull-White model is extremely flexible. $\phi(t)$ automatically acts as a shock-absorber that adapts to daily market movements, ensuring your initial yield curve is always fit perfectly without needing to constantly recalibrate $\kappa$, $\theta$, or $\sigma$.
