Asymptotics of Stochastic Volatility

Share Measures & Small-Time Asymptotics

The share (stock) measure as an asymptotic instrument: symmetrizing the smile, taming wings, and sharpening small-time expansions — measure changes as analysis tools.

1. The Share Measure

Take $S_t/S_0$ (discounted) as numéraire: the share measure $\mathbb{\hat Q}$ with $\frac{d\hat{\mathbb{Q}}}{d\mathbb{Q}}\big|_T = \frac{S_T}{\mathbb{E}[S_T]}$. Immediate dividends for analysis:

  • Call decomposition without integrals: $C = S_0\hat{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K) - Ke^{-rT}\mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$ — in Black-Scholes, $N(d_1)$ is the share-measure exercise probability ($N(d_2)$ the risk-neutral one): the mysterious $d_1$ explained in one line.
  • Put-call symmetry: under $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$ the role of $S$ inverts ($1/S$ is a $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$-martingale, up to carry) — calls at strike $K$ map to puts at strike $F^2/K$: the right wing under $\mathbb{Q}$ is the left wing under $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$.
  • Moment shift: $\hat{\mathbb{E}}[S_T^u] = \mathbb{E}[S_T^{u+1}]/\mathbb{E}[S_T]$ — analyticity strips translate by 1; Lee's right-wing slope under $\mathbb{Q}$ becomes a left-wing statement under $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$ (the two wings of Lee's formula are one result seen in two measures).

2. Sharpening Small-Time Expansions

Out-of-the-money expansions converge best for the wing they're centered on. The share-measure trick: expand calls under $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$ (equivalently, puts under $\mathbb{Q}$) for high strikes, and vice versa:

  • In SV models, the $\hat{\mathbb{Q}}$-dynamics shift the vol drift by the covariation term: $d\alpha$ picks up $+\rho\nu\alpha\,(\text{vol of }S)\,dt$ — SABR under the share measure is SABR with a tilted vol drift; the geometry (Part 01) is unchanged but the starting point of geodesics effectively shifts, which is exactly the $d_1$-vs-$d_2$ asymmetry at smile level.
  • Practical payoff: expansions done symmetrically in both measures and stitched at ATM produce smiles accurate over a wider strike range than one-measure expansions — the trick behind several "improved SABR" wing treatments (and behind Antonov-style mappings).
  • For Lévy models (Part 05): the share measure is an Esscher tilt $\nu(dx) \to e^x\nu(dx)$ — wings literally exchange tail parameters (Kou's η₁ ↔ η₂ − 1 pattern), making the put-call symmetry of jump smiles explicit.

3. Delta, Skew and the Share Measure

  • BS delta $= N(d_1) = \hat{\mathbb{Q}}(\text{exercise})$: delta is an exercise probability under the share measure — why "delta ≈ probability ITM" is wrong by exactly the measure change (a ~$\sigma\sqrt{T}$-sized gap).
  • ATM skew identity: the gap between the two measures' densities at the strike controls $\partial\sigma_{BS}/\partial k$ at the money — small-time skew limits (the $\rho\nu/2$-type constants of Part 03) drop out cleanly from the two-measure comparison.
  • Quanto/composite adjustments are the same algebra with a different numéraire — learn the mechanism once, reuse for every numéraire change in the hybrids world.

4. Working Recipe

  1. Identify which wing/quantity you need (high-strike calls? deltas? left-tail risk?).
  2. Move to the measure where that quantity is a central (not tail) object — share measure for right wing and deltas, risk-neutral for left wing and digitals.
  3. Apply the small-time machinery (Parts 01–03) in that measure — drift-adjusted dynamics, same geometry.
  4. Map back via the symmetry ($K \leftrightarrow F^2/K$, $u \leftrightarrow u+1$) — and use agreement of the two routes as a correctness test (the asymptotics analogue of AD's dot-product test).

Key Takeaways

  • The share measure makes $d_1$, delta and put-call symmetry transparent: $C = S_0\hat{\mathbb{Q}} - Ke^{-rT}\mathbb{Q}$.
  • Moments shift by one ($u \to u+1$): Lee's two wing formulas are one statement in two measures.
  • Expand each wing in its natural measure and stitch — wider-validity smile asymptotics for SV and Lévy alike.
  • Numéraire changes are analytic instruments, not just pricing conveniences — the same algebra runs quantos and hybrids.
