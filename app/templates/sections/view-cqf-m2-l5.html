Five Ways to Define Volatility

Volatility is the variability of asset prices, defined generally as the standard deviation of returns. Depending on application, it is defined in five distinct ways:

  • 1. Constant Parameter ($\sigma$): Appears in classic continuous-time models like Geometric Brownian Motion: $$dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t$$
  • 2. Realized Volatility: Computed directly from historical return data over a specific window, such as: $$\hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{t=1}^N (r_t - \bar{r})^2}$$ Typically estimated using 40-50 daily returns (~2 months).
  • 3. Conditional Volatility ($\sqrt{h_t}$): A forward-looking, time-varying quantity dependent on a specified mathematical model and the asset return history $I_{t-1}$.
  • 4. Stochastic Volatility: A continuous-time variable with its own stochastic differential equation: $$dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)dt + \xi \sqrt{v_t} dW_t^v$$
  • 5. Implied Volatility: The forward-looking market expectation of volatility solved backwards from options prices using Black-Scholes. Differs from real-world volatility due to risk premia.

Why Does Volatility Change?

While no complete unified theory exists, researchers identify several primary factors:

  • Macroeconomic & Geopolitical Crises: Volatility surges during extreme episodes, including the Great Depression (1929), Watergate (1973-74), Black Monday 1987 Stock Crash (-20% in one day), the Global Financial Crisis (2008), and COVID-19 (2020).
  • Leverage Effect: When stock prices drop, debt-to-equity ratios increase. This makes equity riskier and amplifies volatility (although empirical index-level asymmetry is too strong to be explained by leverage alone).
  • Information Flow: The arrival rate of relevant economic, corporate, and political news directly impacts trading volume and price shifts.
  • Trading Volume Correlation: Volatility and volume are highly correlated, though it is inappropriate to assign causality; both are driven by the rate of information flow.

The GARCH(1, 1) Framework

Generalized AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (Bollerslev, 1986) is the most popular credible model for volatility clustering. Let $r_t$ be daily continuously compounded returns:

GARCH(1,1) Stochastic Equations
$$r_t \mid I_{t-1} \sim N(\mu, h_t)$$ $$h_t = \omega + \alpha(r_{t-1} - \mu)^2 + \beta h_{t-1}$$

Variance Positivity

To guarantee that the conditional variance $h_t$ is always positive, we require: $$\omega \ge 0, \quad \alpha \ge 0, \quad \beta \ge 0$$

Stationarity

For the covariance stationarity of returns, the sum of lag parameters must satisfy: $$\alpha + \beta < 1$$ If $\alpha+\beta=1$, volatility is integrated (IGARCH).

Empirical Estimates

For daily asset returns: $$\alpha < 0.10 \quad \text{(weak daily shock response)}$$ $$\alpha + \beta > 0.95 \quad \text{(highly persistent cluster decays)}$$

Key Properties & Stylised Facts

  • Infinite Squared Lag Representation: Solving the recursive equation backward expresses the current conditional variance as an exponentially weighted moving average of all historical squared excess returns: $$h_t = \frac{\omega}{1-\beta} + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \beta^{j-1}(r_{t-j} - \mu)^2$$
  • Leptokurtic Tail Distribution: The unconditional returns in GARCH(1,1) have excess kurtosis, reproducing the fat-tailed return properties observed in real markets.
  • Uncorrelated Returns: Returns are linearly uncorrelated because the conditional mean is constant ($\mu$), conforming to the market efficiency stylized fact.
  • Squared Return Autocorrelations: The autocorrelations of squared returns $s_t = (r_t - \mu)^2$ exist (when kurtosis is finite) and match the decaying shape of an ARMA(1,1) process: $$\rho_{\tau, s^2} = C(\alpha + \beta)^\tau$$

Asymmetric Volatility & GJR-GARCH(1, 1)

Standard GARCH has the limitation that positive and negative return residuals ($e_{t-1} = r_{t-1} - \mu$) of equal size have the identical impact on future volatility. In equity markets, volatility routinely increases much more after negative shocks than positive shocks.

The GJR-GARCH(1, 1) model (Glosten, Jagannathan & Runkle, 1993) resolves this by incorporating a binary asymmetry dummy variable $S_{t-1}$:

$$h_t = \omega + (\alpha + \alpha^{-} S_{t-1})(r_{t-1} - \mu)^2 + \beta h_{t-1}$$ $$S_{t-1} = 1 \quad \text{if } r_{t-1} - \mu < 0 \quad \text{(Negative Shock)}$$ $$S_{t-1} = 0 \quad \text{if } r_{t-1} - \mu \ge 0 \quad \text{(Positive Shock)}$$
  • Asymmetric Multipliers:
    • Positive Shock multiplier: $\alpha$
    • Negative Shock multiplier: $\alpha + \alpha^{-}$
    For equity index returns, $\alpha^{-}$ is strongly positive.
  • GJR Persistence ($\phi$): Represents the rate of volatility decay back to the mean: $$\phi = \alpha + \frac{1}{2}\alpha^{-} + \beta$$ Covariance stationarity requires $\phi < 1$. Unconditional variance is given by: $$V = \frac{\omega}{1 - \phi}$$

Index vs. Firm Level Asymmetry

Stephen Taylor's lecture highlights a crucial empirical stylized fact regarding volatility asymmetry:

"Asymmetry is structurally stronger at the stock index level than for individual firms."
  • Mechanism: Individual firms show a higher baseline $\alpha$ and lower $\alpha^{-}$. Stock indices show $\alpha \approx 0$ and highly elevated $\alpha^{-}$ (e.g. FTSE 100 has $\alpha = 0.000$, $\alpha^{-} = 0.202$).
  • The Driver: Index volatility is heavily influenced by covariances between stock constituents. When the overall stock market declines, the correlations between firm returns increase dramatically, driving index volatility up, whereas positive days do not trigger such correlation changes.

The ARCH-in-Mean (ARCH-M) Model

Modern portfolio theory states that investors should demand a premium (higher expected returns) for holding riskier assets. The ARCH-M framework models this risk compensation directly by placing a function of the conditional variance $h_t$ or conditional standard deviation $\sqrt{h_t}$ directly into the conditional mean equation.

A popular specification includes an MA(1) term to capture short-term microstructure dependencies, and the risk premium parameter $\lambda$:

ARCH-M Mathematical Formulation
$$r_t = \mu_t + e_t, \quad e_t = \sqrt{h_t} z_t \quad (z_t \sim i.i.d.)$$ $$\mu_t = \mu + \lambda \sqrt{h_t} + \Theta e_{t-1}$$ $$h_t = \omega + (\alpha + \alpha^{-} S_{t-1}) e_{t-1}^2 + \beta h_{t-1}$$

Empirical Textbook Findings (S&P 100 Index, 1991-2000):

  • Risk-Premium Parameter ($\lambda$): Positive estimated value (around 0.1-0.2), indicating a weak but positive relation where higher market volatility demands higher average returns.
  • MA Coefficient ($\Theta$): Extremely near zero, meaning asset returns show negligible linear autocorrelation at daily levels.
  • Shock Coefficients: $\alpha = 0.011, \alpha^{-} = 0.087$. This represents a very powerful asymmetry: squared negative return shocks are multiplied by $\alpha + \alpha^{-} = 0.098$, which is 9 times the multiplier for positive shocks ($\alpha = 0.011$).

Parameter Estimation via Maximum Likelihood

ARCH and GARCH parameters are calculated by selecting the values of $\theta$ that maximize the joint probability density function (likelihood) evaluated at the observed data sample. For large datasets, maximum likelihood estimators (MLE) are asymptotically efficient and normally distributed.

Log-Likelihood Math Formulation

Under the standard assumption that the conditional density is normal, the likelihood function $L(\theta)$ is:

$$L(\theta) = f(r_1 \mid \theta) \prod_{t=2}^{n} f(r_t \mid I_{t-1}, \theta)$$ $$f(r_t \mid I_{t-1}, \theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi h_t(\theta)}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\frac{(r_t - \mu_t(\theta))^2}{h_t(\theta)}\right)$$

Taking natural logs yields the Log-Likelihood Function:

$$\log L(\theta) = \sum_{t=1}^{n} l_t(\theta)$$ $$l_t(\theta) = -\frac{1}{2} \left[ \log(2\pi) + \log(h_t(\theta)) + z_t^2(\theta) \right]$$ $$z_t(\theta) = \frac{r_t - \mu_t(\theta)}{\sqrt{h_t(\theta)}} \quad \text{(i.i.d. Standardised Residuals)}$$

Step-by-Step Optimization Process (e.g. inside Excel or Python)

  1. Initialization: Pick starting seeds for the parameter vector $\theta = (\mu, \omega, \alpha, \beta)$ and set initial values:
    • $\mu_1$: Sample mean of returns.
    • $h_1$: Sample variance of returns.
  2. Iterative Filtering: For each row (date $t=1 \dots n$):
    • Compute standardized residual: $z_t = (r_t - \mu_t)/\sqrt{h_t}$.
    • Compute row likelihood log density: $l_t = -0.5 [\log(2\pi) + \log(h_t) + z_t^2]$.
    • Calculate next step conditional variance recursive step: $h_{t+1} = \omega + \alpha(r_t - \mu)^2 + \beta h_t$.
  3. Aggregate & Solver: Sum all rows to obtain $\log L(\theta) = \sum l_t$, and run a numerical optimizer (such as GRG Non-Linear in Excel Solver, or BFGS in Python's `scipy.optimize`) to maximize $\log L(\theta)$ subject to constraints ($\omega \ge 0, \alpha \ge 0, \beta \ge 0, \alpha+\beta < 1$).

Statistical Hypothesis Testing

  • Is Volatility Asymmetric? Test $H_0: \alpha^{-} = 0$ against $H_1: \alpha^{-} > 0$ using a Likelihood Ratio test: $$LR = 2(L_{\text{GJR}} - L_{\text{GARCH}}) \sim \chi^2_1$$
  • Is the Distribution Normal? If standardized residuals $z_t$ are fat-tailed, we estimate using Student's t-distribution with degrees-of-freedom parameter $\nu$. Let $\zeta = 1/\nu$. Testing $H_0: \zeta = 0$ (normal) against $H_1: \zeta > 0$ (t-dist) routinely rejects normality at extreme statistical significance.
