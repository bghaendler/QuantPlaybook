Trivariate Normal Distribution

Three correlated Gaussian events: the integral behind two-dividend Americans, three-asset rainbows and double-decision exotics — and where analytic pricing finally hands over to simulation.

1. Definition

$$N_3(a_1, a_2, a_3; \rho_{12}, \rho_{13}, \rho_{23}) = \mathbb{P}(X_1 \le a_1, X_2 \le a_2, X_3 \le a_3),$$

for standard normals with correlation matrix $R$ (which must be positive semi-definite — with three pairwise correlations this is the constraint $1 - \rho_{12}^2 - \rho_{13}^2 - \rho_{23}^2 + 2\rho_{12}\rho_{13}\rho_{23} \ge 0$; violating it is the classic three-asset input error).

2. Where Pricing Needs It

  • American calls with two discrete dividends (Welch-Chen / Haug extensions of Roll-Geske-Whaley): three decision events — exercise before either ex-date or hold to expiry — with correlations $\sqrt{t_i/t_j}$.
  • Three-asset rainbows (Johnson 1987): calls on the max/min of three assets — each "asset $i$ wins and beats $K$" event is a trivariate probability in suitably rotated coordinates.
  • Twice-compound options (option on option on option; multi-stage real options).
  • Window/partial barriers with two monitoring sub-periods, two-date Bermudans on top of a barrier, etc.

The pattern: each additional Gaussian decision/extremum event raises the CDF dimension by one. Dimension 3 is the last one commonly shipped in closed-form pricing libraries.

3. Computation

  • Reduction to 1-D integrals of bivariate CDFs: condition on $X_3 = z$ — given $z$, $(X_1, X_2)$ is bivariate normal with shifted limits and adjusted correlation: $$N_3 = \int_{-\infty}^{a_3} \phi(z)\, M\!\left(\frac{a_1 - \rho_{13}z}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{13}^2}},\ \frac{a_2 - \rho_{23}z}{\sqrt{1-\rho_{23}^2}};\ \rho_{12|3}\right) dz, \qquad \rho_{12|3} = \frac{\rho_{12} - \rho_{13}\rho_{23}}{\sqrt{(1-\rho_{13}^2)(1-\rho_{23}^2)}},$$ evaluated by Gauss-Legendre — simple and accurate with a good $M$ underneath.
  • Genz-Bretz TVPACK: specialized transformations achieving ~10⁻¹⁴; the reference implementation (used by R's mvtnorm, QuantLib).
  • General MVN (n > 3): Genz's separation-of-variables transform + quasi-Monte Carlo — accuracy becomes statistical; this is the practical bridge to high dimensions.

4. Identities & Checks

  • Margins: $N_3(a_1, a_2, \infty) = M(a_1, a_2; \rho_{12})$ — every implementation test starts here.
  • Independence: $N_3 = N(a_1)N(a_2)N(a_3)$ at zero correlations.
  • Singular $R$ (e.g. $\rho_{13} = \rho_{12}\rho_{23}$ boundaries) needs care — conditional correlations hit ±1 and the inner $M$ must be Genz-robust.
  • Complementation in 3-D has $2^3$ orthant terms — derive once, encode carefully (sign errors here are a classic bug in rainbow-option code).

5. The Practical Frontier

Beyond trivariate, closed-form pricing stops being worth it: a four-asset rainbow needs $N_4$ evaluated thousands of times for calibration, while a quasi-MC simulation prices the whole payoff with smiles and discrete dividends included. The modern division of labor: $N$, $M$, $N_3$ in the analytics library for the formula catalogue; Genz-QMC or full simulation beyond. Knowing both sides — and that the trivariate identities validate your Monte Carlo in the Gaussian limit — is the actual skill.

Key Takeaways

  • $N_3$ prices two-dividend Americans, three-asset rainbows, and double-decision exotics.
  • Check the correlation matrix is PSD: $1 - \sum\rho^2 + 2\rho_{12}\rho_{13}\rho_{23} \ge 0$.
  • Compute by conditioning: a 1-D Gaussian integral over bivariate CDFs with conditional correlation $\rho_{12|3}$.
  • Genz-Bretz routines are the precision reference; margins-to-$M$ identities are your unit tests.
  • Dimension 3 is the practical end of the closed-form catalogue — QMC takes over beyond.
