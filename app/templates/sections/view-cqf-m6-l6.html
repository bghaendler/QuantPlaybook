L6: LIBOR Market Model (LMM)

Discrete Yield Curve Modeling, LMM Drift Derivations, and Bermudan Swaption Optimizations

Modeling Observable Market Quotes

The core philosophy of a market model (like the LIBOR Market Model) is to directly model the dynamics of observable, tradeable market quotes, rather than unobservable hidden factors (such as the instantaneous short rate). The yield curve is discretized into a set of discrete, spanning forward rates $f_i(t)$ over adjacent intervals $[t_i, t_{i+1}]$ with accruals $\tau_i \approx t_{i+1} - t_i$.

Discount Factors & FRA Mechanics: For a borrower, the effective funding discount factor over the forward interval $[t_i, t_{i+1}]$ is: $$\frac{P(t, t_{i+1})}{P(t, t_i)} = \frac{1}{1 + f_i(t) \tau_i}$$

Market Yield Curve

Financial markets are driven by tradable instruments rather than an abstract yield curve. The instruments include:

  • Interbank cash deposits: Unsecured lending rates.
  • Government bond repos: Used to set benchmark rates.
  • Swap rates: Swapping fixed and floating cash flows.
  • Futures & FRAs: Standardized forward contracts.

LMM Governing Equations

The standard LIBOR Market Model assumes that each of the $n$ discretely compounded spanning forward rates $f_i(t)$ evolves according to a log-normal SDE: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}f_i(t)}{f_i(t)} = \mu_i(\mathbf{f}, t)\mathrm{d}t + \sigma_i(t) \cdot \mathrm{d}\widetilde{W}_i(t)$$ where $\sigma_i(t)$ is the instantaneous volatility of the forward rate, $\rho_{ij}\mathrm{d}t = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{d}\widetilde{W}_i\mathrm{d}\widetilde{W}_j]$ is the correlation, and the drift $\mu_i$ is determined entirely by no-arbitrage requirements.

Relative Valuation & Numéraire Selection

To evaluate contingent claims, we select a tradeable positive security $N(t)$ to act as the numéraire. Under the associated martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}_N$, the price of any tradeable asset $X(t)$ rescaled by the numéraire (i.e., $X(t)/N(t)$) must be a martingale. Common numéraire choices include:

  • Continuous money market account $A(t)$: Discounting along the stochastic path of the short rate.
  • Zero-coupon bond $P_N(t)$ (Terminal Measure): Discounting using today's ZCB price, causing stochastic discount factor components inside expectations to vanish.
  • Swap Annuity $B_i(t)$ (Annuity Measure): Used to simplify swaption pricing.

Deriving the No-Arbitrage Drift Formula

Let the numéraire be the zero-coupon bond $P_N(t)$ maturing at $t_N$. The ratio of any traded asset to the numéraire must be a martingale under the terminal measure $\mathbb{T}_N$. Consider the asset $f_i P_{i+1}$ representing the payoff of a forward contract. Its ratio to the numéraire $f_i X_i(t) \equiv f_i \frac{P_{i+1}(t)}{P_N(t)}$ must be a martingale. Applying Itô's product rule and taking expectations: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{T}_N}\left[ \mathrm{d}(f_i X_i) \right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{T}_N}\left[ X_i \mu_i f_i \mathrm{d}t + \mathrm{d}f_i \mathrm{d}X_i \right] = 0 \implies \mu_i \mathrm{d}t = -\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{T}_N}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}f_i}{f_i} \frac{\mathrm{d}X_i}{X_i} \right]$$

The Vaillant Relative Covariance Bracket

We define the instantaneous relative covariance bracket $[a, b] \equiv \mathbb{E}[\frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a}\frac{\mathrm{d}b}{b}]/\mathrm{d}t$. Using the bracket properties and expanding the bond ratio $P_{i+1}/P_N = \prod_{k=i+1}^{N-1}(1 + f_k \tau_k)^{-1}$: $$\mu_i = -\left[ f_i, \frac{P_{i+1}}{P_N} \right] = -\sum_{k=i+1}^{N-1} \left[ f_i, (1 + f_k \tau_k)^{-1} \right] = -\sum_{k=i+1}^{N-1} \frac{f_k \tau_k}{1 + f_k \tau_k} \sigma_i \sigma_k \rho_{ik}$$

The LMM Drift Formula

This yields the analytical LMM Drift Formula under the $t_N$-terminal measure: $$\mu_i(\mathbf{f}, t) = \begin{cases} -\sigma_i(t) \sum_{k=i+1}^{N-1} \frac{f_k(t) \tau_k}{1 + f_k(t) \tau_k} \sigma_k(t) \rho_{ik} & \text{for } i < N-1 \\ 0 & \text{for } i = N-1 \\ \sigma_i(t) \sum_{k=N}^i \frac{f_k(t) \tau_k}{1 + f_k(t) \tau_k} \sigma_k(t) \rho_{ik} & \text{for } i \geq N \end{cases}$$ Since the drift of each forward rate depends on the other stochastic forward rates $f_k(t)$, the drift is itself stochastic, requiring advanced numerical stepping.

Volatility & Correlation Parameterizations

To calibrate the model to market quotes, LMM uses parametric forms to restrict the thousands of degrees of freedom. Instantaneous Volatility (ABCD Skeleton): $$\sigma_i(t) = k_i \left[ \left( a + b \cdot (t_i - t) \right) \cdot e^{-c \cdot (t_i - t)} + d \right] \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{t < t_i\}}$$ where $a, b, c, d$ define the smooth exponential humped skeleton, and $k_i$ scaling factors perfectly fit caplet prices. Instantaneous Correlation Decay: $$\rho_{ij} = e^{-\beta \cdot |t_i - t_j|}$$ where $\beta \approx 0.1$ controls the rate at which correlation decays between distant tenors.

European Swaption Jacobian Transition Matrix

Using Itô's lemma, the relative covariance of swap rates $C^s$ can be mapped directly from the forward covariance matrix $C^f$ using a Jacobian transition matrix $Z^{f \to s}$: $$C^s = Z^{f \to s} \cdot C^f \cdot \left( Z^{f \to s} \right)^T, \quad Z^{f \to s}_{ik} = \left[ \frac{P_{k+1} N_k f_k \tau_k}{A_i} + \frac{(A_i B_k - A_k B_i) f_k \tau_k}{A_i B_i (1 + f_k \tau_k)} \right] \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{k \geq i\}}$$ For a perfectly flat yield curve, the shape correction term is zero, reducing $Z^{f \to s}$ to the constant-weights approximation. Inverting this mapping yields the non-iterative calibration matrix $M$: $C^f_{\text{calibrated}} = M \cdot C^f_{\text{parametric}} \cdot M^T$.

Predictor-Corrector Numerical Scheme

Simple log-Euler discretization is highly inaccurate for large time steps $\Delta t$ due to stochastic drift variation. The Predictor-Corrector Scheme stabilizes LMM paths:

  • Prediction: Evolve rates forward using current drift $\mu_i(\mathbf{f}(t), t)$.
  • Drift Recalculation: Evaluate the drift vector at the predicted state $\hat{\mathbf{f}}(t+\Delta t)$.
  • Correction: Evolve rates again using the averaged drift $\tilde{\mu}_i = \frac{1}{2}[\mu_i(t) + \mu_i(t+\Delta t)]$ and the same random draws.

Exercise 1. Vaillant Bracket Product Proof

Question: Prove that the relative covariance bracket satisfies the product expansion property: $[a, bc] = [a, b] + [a, c]$.

Solution:
By definition, the relative covariance bracket is: $$[a, b] \equiv \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a} \frac{\mathrm{d}b}{b} \right] \Big/ \mathrm{d}t$$ Applying Itô's product rule to the process $y = bc$: $$\mathrm{d}y = \mathrm{d}(bc) = b\mathrm{d}c + c\mathrm{d}b + \mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c$$ Dividing both sides by $y = bc$: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}(bc)}{bc} = \frac{b\mathrm{d}c + c\mathrm{d}b + \mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c}{bc} = \frac{\mathrm{d}c}{c} + \frac{\mathrm{d}b}{b} + \frac{\mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c}{bc}$$ Now, evaluating the bracket $[a, bc]$: $$[a, bc] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a} \frac{\mathrm{d}(bc)}{bc} \right] \Big/ \mathrm{d}t = \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a} \left( \frac{\mathrm{d}c}{c} + \frac{\mathrm{d}b}{b} + \frac{\mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c}{bc} \right) \right] \Big/ \mathrm{d}t$$ Expanding the terms: $$[a, bc] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a}\frac{\mathrm{d}c}{c} \right]\Big/\mathrm{d}t + \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a}\frac{\mathrm{d}b}{b} \right]\Big/\mathrm{d}t + \mathbb{E}\left[ \frac{\mathrm{d}a}{a}\frac{\mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c}{bc} \right]\Big/\mathrm{d}t$$ Since $\mathrm{d}a\mathrm{d}b\mathrm{d}c$ is a higher-order differential $(\mathrm{d}t^{1.5} \text{ or } \mathrm{d}t^2)$, its expectation vanishes in the limit $\mathrm{d}t \to 0$: $$[a, bc] = [a, c] + [a, b] + 0 \implies [a, bc] = [a, b] + [a, c]$$ This proves the relative covariance bracket property.

Exercise 2. Flat Yield Curve Transition Matrix Proof

Question: Prove that the Jacobian transition matrix $Z^{f \to s}$ reduces exactly to the constant-weights approximation when the initial yield curve is perfectly flat.

Solution:
The complete formula for the Jacobian transition matrix element is: $$Z^{f \to s}_{ik} = \left[ \frac{P_{k+1} N_k f_k \tau_k}{A_i} + \frac{(A_i B_k - A_k B_i) f_k \tau_k}{A_i B_i (1 + f_k \tau_k)} \right] \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{k \geq i\}}$$ where the second term is the shape correction factor, which can be expanded as: $$\frac{(A_i B_k - A_k B_i) f_k \tau_k}{A_i B_i (1 + f_k \tau_k)} = \frac{f_k \tau_k}{A_i B_i (1 + f_k \tau_k)} \sum_{l=i}^{k-1} \sum_{m=k}^{n-1} P_{l+1} P_{m+1} N_l N_m \tau_l \tau_m (f_l - f_m)$$ If the initial yield curve is perfectly flat, all discrete forward rates are equal: $f_l = f_m = f_{\text{flat}}$ for all indices $l, m$.
Substituting this equality: $$f_l - f_m = f_{\text{flat}} - f_{\text{flat}} = 0 \implies \sum_{l=i}^{k-1} \sum_{m=k}^{n-1} P_{l+1} P_{m+1} N_l N_m \tau_l \tau_m (0) = 0$$ Therefore, the entire shape correction term vanishes: $$Z^{f \to s}_{ik} = \left[ \frac{P_{k+1} N_k f_k \tau_k}{A_i} + 0 \right] \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{k \geq i\}} = \frac{P_{k+1} N_k f_k \tau_k}{A_i} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{k \geq i\}}$$ This is exactly the constant-weights approximation, proving the result.

Exercise 3. Early Exercise Boundaries & Bermudans

Question: Detail the backward Bellman optimality loop used to price Bermudan swaptions and explain how exercise decision boundaries are parameterised.

Solution:
A Bermudan swaption allows the holder to enter into a swap at strike $K$ on any of the exercise dates $\{t_1, \dots, t_{n-1}\}$. The intrinsic value at exercise is $I(t_j) = \sum_{k=j}^{n-1} P_{k+1}(t_j)(f_k(t_j) - K)\tau_k$.
The continuation value is the expected profit from keeping the option alive. The Bellman optimality equation is: $$V(t_j) = \max \left\{ I(t_j), \mathbb{E}_{t_j}[V(t_{j+1})] \right\}$$ To parameterise the early-exercise decision boundary in Monte Carlo simulations without computing expensive nested expectations, we define a decision rule $\mathcal{E}_i$: $$\mathcal{E}_i(\mathbf{f}(t_i)) = \phi_\pm \cdot \left( f_i(t_i) - \left[ p_{i1} \cdot \frac{s_{i+1}(0)}{s_{i+1}(t_i) + p_{i2}} + p_{i3} \right] \right)$$ where the holder exercises if $\mathcal{E}_i > 0$. The parameters $\mathbf{p}_i = (p_{i1}, p_{i2}, p_{i3})$ are optimized backward in time on training paths using the BFGS multi-dimensional variable metric method.
