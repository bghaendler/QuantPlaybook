High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Volume Distribution Modeling & Heavy-Tail Spikes

Trade sizes and volumes are power-law objects: how to model them, estimate tail indices honestly, and survive the spikes that Gaussian thinking calls impossible.

1. The Empirical Facts

  • Trade sizes: survival $\mathbb{P}(v > x) \sim x^{-\zeta_v}$ with $\zeta_v \approx 1.5$–$2.5$ — finite mean, marginal variance. Round-lot clustering (100s, 1000s) and odd-lot proliferation overlay the tail.
  • Per-interval volume: heavy-tailed and strongly autocorrelated (volume clustering mirrors volatility clustering — the two are linked through the mixture-of-distributions hypothesis: both driven by information arrival).
  • Spikes: opens/closes, index events, news halts ⇒ volumes 10–50× the median — these are structural, not outliers to delete.
  • Number of trades is better behaved than volume — counts are nearer log-normal; size-of-trade carries most of the tail.

3. Estimating Tail Indices Without Fooling Yourself

  • Hill estimator: $\hat\zeta^{-1} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}\ln\frac{x_{(n-i+1)}}{x_{(n-k)}}$ on the top $k$ order statistics — plot $\hat\zeta$ vs. $k$ (the "Hill plot") and look for a stable plateau; never report a single-$k$ value.
  • Log-log rank plots are diagnostics, not estimators — OLS on them is biased; use MLE/GPD fits over a threshold chosen by mean-excess-plot linearity.
  • Traps: mixing regimes (open/close with midday) fakes heavier tails; lognormals masquerade as power laws over 1–2 decades; finite samples make $\zeta < 2$ vs. $\zeta > 2$ (infinite vs. finite variance!) genuinely hard to distinguish — and that distinction changes which statistics even converge.

4. Consequences for Trading Systems

  • Participation algos: a % -of-volume strategy inherits volume's tails — cap child-order sizes, or one spike prints an outsized slice at the worst moment.
  • Volume forecasts (for VWAP/IS schedules, Part 05–07): model log-volume with seasonal mean + AR dynamics + GPD shocks; evaluate with quantile loss, not RMSE — the tail is the business case.
  • Risk of liquidity metrics: ADV-based position limits computed on means are fragile; use medians/quantiles of the spliced fit.
  • Impact interaction: heavy-tailed sizes + concave impact (Part 02) ⇒ aggregate cost dominated by the few largest prints — exactly where estimation (Part 03) has least data. Stress-test there.

Key Takeaways

  • Sizes and volumes are power-law tailed ($\zeta \approx 1.5$–$2.5$); spikes are structural features, not outliers.
  • Production fit: lognormal body + GPD tail; counts × sizes for simulation.
  • Hill plateaus and mean-excess plots, never single-point tail estimates; beware regime mixing and lognormal impostors.
  • Cap participation exposure and evaluate volume models on tail quantiles — the largest prints carry the P&L.
