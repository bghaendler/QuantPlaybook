Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Polynomials, Implied Volatility & Complex Numbers

Roots, where they live, and how root-finding becomes a daily production task the moment markets quote prices instead of parameters.

1. Polynomials & Their Roots

A degree-$n$ polynomial $p(x) = a_n x^n + \dots + a_0$ has exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$ (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra) — the reason $\mathbb{C}$ exists in this course at all. Quadratics solve by formula; the discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ decides real vs. complex-conjugate pairs. Finance hits polynomials constantly: characteristic equations of difference/differential equations (Cauchy-Euler, Part 08), the "fundamental quadratic" of perpetual options $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\gamma(\gamma-1) + b\gamma - r = 0$, and bond-yield equations $\sum c_i x^{t_i} = P$ with $x = e^{-y}$ — degree = number of cash flows, no closed form beyond degree 4 (Abel-Ruffini), hence numerics.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Financial equations (like bond yields or option values) are polynomials where we must solve for the roots (interest rate or yield).

  • Degree Constraints: Equations of degree higher than 4 cannot be solved algebraically and require numerical solvers.
  • Bond Yields: Finding the Yield to Maturity (YTM) of a bond requires finding the roots of a cash-flow polynomial.

2. Implied Volatility as Inverse-Problem Prototype

Markets quote prices; models want parameters. Implied vol solves $C_{BS}(\sigma) = C_{mkt}$ — no algebraic solution exists (the normal CDF is not elementary-invertible), so we use the structure instead:

  • $C_{BS}$ is continuous, strictly increasing in σ (vega > 0), with known limits — a unique root is guaranteed inside the no-arbitrage bracket (Bolzano/IVT).
  • Bisection: guaranteed, linear. Newton-Raphson: $\sigma_{n+1} = \sigma_n - (C(\sigma_n) - C_{mkt})/\text{vega}(\sigma_n)$, quadratic near the root, needs vega ≥ ε.
  • Seed with the ATM expansion $C \approx 0.4\, S\sigma\sqrt{T}$ ⇒ $\sigma_0 \approx C/(0.4 S\sqrt{T})$.

This pattern — monotone pricing function, bracketed root, derivative known analytically — recurs for yields, OAS, CDS hazard bootstraps, and SABR calibration. Learn it once here.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Implied volatility is an inverse problem: we know the option price and want to find the input volatility that matches it.

  • Newton-Raphson: Uses the slope of the pricing curve (Vega) to quickly converge to the implied volatility.
  • Bisection: A slower but guaranteed solver that brackets the volatility root until it finds the exact value.

3. Complex Numbers: The Minimum Working Set

$z = x + iy = re^{i\theta}$ with $r = |z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, $\theta = \arg z$. Euler's formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ converts multiplication into angle addition: $z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$.

  • Complex roots of real polynomials come in conjugate pairs $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$ — in ODEs they produce damped oscillations $e^{\alpha t}(\cos\beta t, \sin\beta t)$ (mean-reverting models with cyclical components).
  • Characteristic functions $\varphi_X(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iuX}]$ encode whole distributions; Heston and Lévy models price options by integrating $\varphi$ (Carr-Madan FFT) — the single most practical reason a quant needs $\mathbb{C}$.
  • de Moivre: $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$ — the generator of trig identities and $n$-th roots of unity (FFT grids).

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Complex numbers are the foundation of characteristic functions, enabling fast Fourier options pricing.

  • Euler's Formula: Converts complex numbers into angles and rotations, simplifying trigonometric integrals.
  • Fourier Transform: Solves option pricing under stochastic volatility (like Heston) by integrating waves in the complex plane.

Key Takeaways

  • Degree-$n$ ⇒ $n$ complex roots; beyond quartics, root-finding is numerical by theorem, not laziness.
  • Implied vol = the canonical inverse problem: monotonicity + IVT guarantee, Newton + bisection deliver.
  • Polar form + Euler's formula make complex arithmetic geometric; conjugate-pair roots = oscillatory solutions.
  • Characteristic functions are the working quant's bridge from $\mathbb{C}$ to option prices (FFT pricing).
