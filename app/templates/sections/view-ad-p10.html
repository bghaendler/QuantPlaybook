Algorithmic Adjoint Differentiation

Second-Order Adjoints & Hessian Extraction

Gammas and cross-gammas at AD precision: forward-over-reverse composition, Hessian-vector products, and the structure tricks that avoid ever forming the full matrix.

1. Composing Modes

Second derivatives = differentiate a derivative. Four compositions exist; two matter:

  • Forward-over-reverse (the workhorse): the reverse sweep computes $\nabla f(x)$; run that whole computation in tangent mode with seed $\dot{x} = v$ ⇒ output is $\nabla^2 f(x)\,v$ — one Hessian-vector product (HVP) at ~10–25× a primal valuation, independent of $n$.
  • Reverse-over-forward: equivalent cost by symmetry; tool ergonomics decide (JAX: jvp(grad(f)) vs grad(jvp) compositions).
  • Full Hessian: $n$ HVPs with $v = e_1..e_n$ — $O(n)$× gradient cost; symmetric, so only $n(n+1)/2$ entries needed. For large $n$, don't form it (see §3).

2. What Desks Actually Need

  • Gamma ladder (diagonal): per-bucket $\partial^2 PV/\partial x_i^2$ — $n$ HVPs, or cheaper: colored/compressed HVPs when the Hessian is banded (curve gammas couple only neighboring buckets — Part 05's coloring applies to Hessian columns too).
  • Cross-gammas of a block (e.g. FX×rates for quanto books): HVPs seeded with the block's coordinates only — cost scales with the block, not the book.
  • Delta-gamma VaR inputs (ARM): the quadratic approximation needs Σ-weighted Hessian contractions — i.e. HVPs against factor principal components: ~5–10 HVPs, never the full matrix.
  • Optimizer curvature: Newton/trust-region calibration consumes HVPs directly (conjugate-gradient inner loops) — again matrix-free.

3. Matrix-Free Discipline

The recurring theme: the Hessian is an operator, not an array.

  • CG/Lanczos on HVPs solves Newton systems and extracts extreme eigenvalues (the book's largest convexity directions — useful risk information in itself) in ~10–50 HVPs.
  • Low-rank + diagonal structure: portfolio Hessians are typically a few stiff directions (level/slope/vol-level) plus a near-diagonal remainder — randomized sketching (HVPs against random $v$) certifies the rank cheaply.
  • Sparsity: trade-level Hessians touch few factors; aggregate by summing sparse contributions rather than densifying.
  • Smoothness warning: second derivatives amplify payoff kinks — smoothed payoffs (Part 09's policy) are mandatory for stable gammas in MC; bump-validate on smooth test books only.

4. Worked Sketch: JAX-Style Gamma Ladder

grad_f = jax.grad(pv)                      # reverse mode: full delta vector
def hvp(x, v):                             # forward-over-reverse
    return jax.jvp(grad_f, (x,), (v,))[1]  # = H @ v, ~one extra valuation multiple

# banded curve Hessian: 3-coloring gives all gammas in 3 HVPs
colors = [seed_vec(c) for c in (0, 1, 2)]   # e.g. buckets 0,3,6.. | 1,4,7.. | 2,5,8..
columns = [hvp(x0, v) for v in colors]      # scatter back to diagonal + off-diagonals

Validation: HVP against central second differences of the gradient (not of the value — one less cancellation level), and symmetry checks $v^\top H w = w^\top H v$ on random pairs — the second-order analogue of the dot-product test.

Key Takeaways

  • Forward-over-reverse gives Hessian-vector products at constant multiple of one valuation — the second-order primitive.
  • Desk needs (gamma ladders, block cross-gammas, ΔΓ-VaR contractions, Newton steps) are all HVP-shaped — stay matrix-free.
  • Coloring, low-rank sketching and sparsity make even full ladders a handful of HVPs.
  • Smooth the payoffs first; validate with gradient-differences and symmetry tests.
