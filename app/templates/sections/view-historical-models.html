Historical Models: Bachelier, Sprenkle, Boness

Seventy years of option pricing before Black-Scholes: each precursor had the right structure but the wrong discounting — until the hedging argument removed preferences entirely.

1. Bachelier (1900): Arithmetic Brownian Motion

Louis Bachelier's Théorie de la Spéculation modeled prices as arithmetic Brownian motion, $dS = \sigma\, dW$, five years before Einstein's Brownian-motion paper. The (undiscounted) call price:

$$c = (S - K)\, N(d) + \sigma\sqrt{T}\, \phi(d), \qquad d = \frac{S - K}{\sigma\sqrt{T}},$$

with normal volatility $\sigma$ in price units (e.g. \$/year1/2). Defects: prices can go negative, no discounting, zero drift assumed. Yet the model never died — it is the market standard for spread options (which can be negative) and was revived wholesale for negative interest rates (normal vols for swaptions post-2014) and the negative WTI futures episode of April 2020.

2. Sprenkle (1961): Lognormal Prices, Unknown Preferences

Sprenkle switched to lognormal dynamics with drift $\rho$ and added a risk-aversion discount $k$ on the strike leg:

$$c = S e^{\rho T} N(d_1) - (1-k)\, K\, N(d_2), \qquad d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (\rho + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}.$$

Structurally this is Black-Scholes — but $\rho$ (the stock's expected growth) and $k$ (the market's risk premium) are free preference parameters nobody could estimate. Sprenkle himself could not pin them down empirically.

3. Boness (1964): Discounting at the Stock's Expected Return

Boness introduced time-value-of-money consistently, discounting the terminal payoff at the stock's expected return $\rho$:

$$c = S\, N(d_1) - K e^{-\rho T} N(d_2), \qquad d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (\rho + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}.$$

This is literally the Black-Scholes formula with $\rho$ in place of $r$. One substitution away from the answer — but justifying that substitution requires the no-arbitrage hedging argument, not assumptions about investor expectations. Samuelson (1965) generalized further, letting the option and stock earn different expected returns ($\alpha \ne \rho$), adding a second unknown.

4. The Conceptual Leap (1973)

Black, Scholes and Merton showed that a continuously rebalanced portfolio $\Pi = V - \Delta S$ with $\Delta = \partial V/\partial S$ is instantaneously riskless, so it must earn $r$. The drift $\mu$ (alias $\rho$) cancels out of the PDE:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + r S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0.$$

Preference-free pricing: every earlier model failed because it needed expected returns and risk premia; the hedge eliminates both. Boness's formula becomes correct once $\rho \to r$ — not because investors are risk-neutral, but because the hedged position is riskless.

Key Takeaways

  • Bachelier solved the mathematics (normal model, heat equation) 73 years early; his model survives for spreads and negative rates.
  • Sprenkle and Boness had the lognormal formula's shape but with preference parameters ($\rho$, $k$) in place of $r$.
  • The 1973 breakthrough is economic, not mathematical: delta hedging makes drift and risk premia irrelevant.
  • Knowing the precursors clarifies what Black-Scholes actually proves — and when its logic (continuous, frictionless hedging) fails.
