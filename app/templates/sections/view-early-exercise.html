Why Analytical Solutions Fail for Early Exercise

American options as free-boundary problems: the exercise boundary must be found as part of the solution — which destroys the separable structure that closed forms rely on.

1. The American Feature

An American option may be exercised at any stopping time $\tau \le T$:

$$V(S, t) = \sup_{\tau \in [t, T]} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[e^{-r(\tau - t)}\, \Pi(S_\tau)\right],$$

an optimal stopping problem. The holder continuously compares continuation value against intrinsic value $\Pi(S) = (K-S)^+$ or $(S-K)^+$. The state space splits into a continuation region and an exercise region, separated by the unknown early exercise boundary $S^*(t)$.

2. The Free-Boundary (Obstacle) Problem

Instead of a single PDE on a fixed domain, the American price satisfies a linear complementarity problem:

$$\mathcal{L}V \le 0, \qquad V \ge \Pi, \qquad (\mathcal{L}V)\,(V - \Pi) = 0,$$

where $\mathcal{L}V = V_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 V_{SS} + bSV_S - rV$. In the continuation region the BS equation holds with equality and $V > \Pi$; in the exercise region $V = \Pi$ and the BS operator is strictly negative. On the boundary $S^*(t)$, two conditions hold:

$$V(S^*(t), t) = \Pi(S^*(t)) \quad \text{(value matching)}, \qquad \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}(S^*(t), t) = \Pi'(S^*(t)) \quad \text{(smooth pasting)}.$$

The boundary's location is part of the unknown. The domain on which you must solve the PDE depends on the solution itself — this circularity is what closed-form methods cannot digest.

3. Why the BS Machinery Breaks

  • No fixed domain: the heat-equation transformation that solves European BS needs a rectangle $[0,T] \times \mathbb{R}$; the American domain is bounded by an unknown curve $S^*(t)$, which is not even known in closed form itself (near expiry it has a famously non-analytic expansion, $S^*(t) \sim K(1 - \sigma\sqrt{(T-t)\ln(\dots)})$).
  • No single expectation: the price is a supremum over infinitely many stopping rules, not one integral against a lognormal density.
  • Early exercise premium representation (Kim 1990; Carr-Jarrow-Myneni): $$P_{Am} = P_{Eur} + \int_t^T \left[rKe^{-r(u-t)}N(-d_2(S, S^*_u)) - qSe^{-q(u-t)}N(-d_1(S, S^*_u))\right] du,$$ exact — but it is an integral equation, since $S^*_u$ appears inside; solvable only numerically (or by iteration).

4. When Early Exercise Is (Never) Optimal

ContractEarly exercise?Driver
Call, no dividendsNever (Merton): $C_{Am} = C_{Eur}$Time value of strike + insurance always positive
Call, discrete dividendsPossibly, just before ex-div datesCapture dividend vs. lose time value
Put, $r > 0$Yes, when deep ITMInterest on strike received early
Perpetual put/callClosed form exists!Time-independence ⇒ ODE, boundary is a constant

The perpetual case (McKean/Merton) is the exception that proves the rule: removing time dependence turns the PDE into an ODE, the boundary into a single number $S^* = \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - 1}K$, and a closed form pops out. It is the time-dependence of the boundary that kills finite-maturity closed forms.

5. The Practical Response

Since exact closed forms are impossible (for finite-maturity American puts under BS dynamics), the industry uses:

  • Lattices & FDM: trees and PDE grids handle the obstacle condition trivially ($V = \max(\text{cont}, \Pi)$ at each node) — the benchmark.
  • Analytical approximations: Roll-Geske-Whaley (discrete dividends), Barone-Adesi & Whaley (quadratic), Bjerksund-Stensland (boundary parametrization), Ju-Zhong — covered in the following sections.
  • Monte Carlo: Longstaff-Schwartz least-squares regression for high-dimensional/multi-asset American problems.

Key Takeaways

  • American pricing = optimal stopping = free-boundary PDE with value-matching and smooth-pasting conditions.
  • The unknown, time-dependent boundary destroys the fixed-domain transforms behind closed forms.
  • The early-exercise premium has an exact representation — as an integral equation, not a formula.
  • No-dividend American calls are European; American puts (r > 0) always carry a premium.
  • Perpetual options admit closed forms because the boundary collapses to a constant.
