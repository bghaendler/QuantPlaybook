Beating Markowitz with Sentiment & Downside Control

Augmenting mean-variance with alternative-data sentiment signals and asymmetric (downside) risk measures — and the honest evidence on whether it works out of sample.

1. Two Upgrades to Markowitz

  • Better expected returns (sentiment): Markowitz is hostage to its μ estimates (the equities/RSI Part 04 curse). Sentiment from news/social/options flow offers a forward-looking μ input distinct from noisy historical means.
  • Better risk measure (downside): variance penalizes upside and downside equally — investors don't. Replace it with semivariance, CVaR, or drawdown, optimizing asymmetric risk (the ARM Part 07 coherent-measure theme applied to allocation).

2. Sentiment as a Return Signal

  • Sources: news tone (NLP, the NLP-SDG/LLM talks), social-media volume/polarity, analyst revisions, options-implied skew (fear gauge), search trends.
  • Construction: cross-sectional sentiment scores → ranks → tilts; or feed as Black-Litterman views (RSI Part 04) so they blend with equilibrium rather than dominate.
  • Decay & capacity: sentiment alpha is fast-decaying and capacity-limited — it interacts with the execution cost stack (HFT Part 07); a signal that needs daily turnover may not survive costs.
  • The honesty test: most published "sentiment beats market" results are in-sample or pre-cost. Out-of-sample, point-in-time, post-cost (portfolio-workshop standards) is where claims live or die.

3. Downside-Risk Optimization

  • Mean-CVaR: minimize Conditional VaR at confidence α subject to a return target — a convex (linear-programming) problem (Rockafellar-Uryasev), tractable and coherent.
  • Mean-semivariance: penalize only below-target deviations — matches loss-averse preferences (the behavioral link).
  • Why it can beat MV out of sample: downside measures are less sensitive to the symmetric-tail mis-estimation that wrecks variance-based weights, and they avoid loading on negatively-skewed "picking up nickels" exposures that variance rewards (ARM Part 06).
  • Caveat: downside measures need more data (they use fewer observations — only the tail), so estimation error can offset the benefit; pair with shrinkage and robust covariance (the HRP/max-diversification talks).

4. The Verdict

  • "Beating Markowitz" is usually about robustness, not a magic signal: better μ inputs (sentiment, BL priors) and risk measures aligned with real preferences improve realized outcomes more reliably than chasing in-sample Sharpe.
  • Combine cautiously: sentiment views + CVaR optimization + cost-aware implementation, validated out of sample — each piece earns its place or is cut.
  • Benchmark against simple baselines (equal-weight, risk-parity): if a complex sentiment-CVaR model barely beats 1/N after costs, prefer the simpler one (the recurring portfolio-workshop lesson).

Key Takeaways

  • Two upgrades to mean-variance: forward-looking sentiment for μ, and asymmetric downside measures (CVaR/semivariance) for risk.
  • Sentiment alpha is fast-decaying and cost-sensitive — only point-in-time, post-cost validation counts.
  • Mean-CVaR is convex and coherent; downside measures resist symmetric-tail mis-estimation but need more data.
  • The real win is robustness; always benchmark against 1/N and risk-parity before accepting complexity.
