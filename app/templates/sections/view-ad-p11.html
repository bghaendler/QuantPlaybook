Algorithmic Adjoint Differentiation — Part 11

Iterative Code Optimization & Performance Profiles

The series capstone: profiling adjoint codes, the optimization ladder from naive to production, and the correctness harness that keeps speed from breaking math.

1. Profile First: Where Adjoint Time Goes

  • The adjoint ratio $R = \frac{\text{cost(value + gradient)}}{\text{cost(value)}}$ is the headline KPI: theory says 3–5; naive implementations ship at 20–100. Track it per release.
  • Typical profile of a slow OO adjoint: 40–60% tape memory traffic (allocation + cache misses), 15–30% interpreter dispatch, only ~20% actual FLOPs — it's a memory problem wearing a math costume (Part 06's point, measured).
  • Tools: perf/VTune for cache/bandwidth counters; per-phase timers (record vs. reverse vs. primal); peak-RSS tracking for the tape. Measure before touching anything.

2. The Optimization Ladder (In Payoff Order)

  1. Structural wins: per-path tape reset; checkpointing schedule; custom super-node adjoints for solvers/regressions/interpolators (Parts 06–08) — order-of-magnitude items.
  2. Tape engineering: preallocated flat buffers (no per-node allocation), struct-of-arrays layout, preaccumulation of straight-line blocks, passive-type fast path.
  3. Vectorization: batch paths (AoS→SoA), SIMD the reverse sweep (it's mostly FMAs over contiguous adjoint arrays), vector-forward for the few-input pieces (Part 05).
  4. Parallelism: paths across threads with thread-local tapes + deterministic reduction of $\bar\theta$ (Part 07's reproducibility note); GPU offload last — counter-based RNGs and the per-path structure port directly (the GPU workshop's subject).
  5. Micro: recompute-vs-store per operation (an exp is often cheaper than its cache miss), fused payoff kernels — only after 1–4 plateau.

3. The Correctness Harness (Speed's Seatbelt)

  • Dot-product test $\langle\dot y, \bar y\rangle = \langle\dot x, \bar x\rangle$ on random seeds/inputs — catches reversal bugs to machine precision; run per commit.
  • Tangent vs. central bump with common random numbers (Part 04) at tolerance ~1e-6; adjoint vs. tangent exactly.
  • Golden gradients: archived full-precision gradients on a trade zoo (vanilla, barrier-smoothed, XVA netting set); any drift > 1 ulp-scale flags tool/compiler changes.
  • Determinism gate: bit-identical gradients across runs and thread counts where governance requires (fixed reduction trees); otherwise documented tolerance.
  • Performance regression CI alongside: $R$-ratio and peak-tape budgets as build-failing assertions — speed and correctness regress together or not at all.

The shape is universal: structure buys the first 10×, memory engineering the next 2–3×, vectorization the last. At ~3–5× you've hit the theoretical floor — stop and ship.

Key Takeaways

  • Track the adjoint ratio R; profile shows adjoints are memory-bound — optimize traffic before flops.
  • Ladder: structure (paths, checkpoints, super-nodes) → tape engineering → SIMD/threads → micro.
  • Dot-product, tangent-vs-bump, golden gradients and determinism gates make optimization safe.
  • R ≈ 3–5× is the physics floor — reaching it means done, not "keep going".
