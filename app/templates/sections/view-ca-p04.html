Complex Analysis for Quants

Mapping the z-Plane to the w-Plane: f(z)

Complex functions as geometric transformations: how grids, circles and strips travel under w = f(z), and why conformality is the property worth tracking.

1. Functions as Transformations

A complex function $w = f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$ cannot be graphed (4 real dimensions); instead we watch how it transports sets from the $z$-plane to the $w$-plane. The basic alphabet:

  • $w = z + b$: translation. $w = az$: rotation by $\arg a$, scaling by $|a|$. $w = az + b$: every similarity.
  • $w = \bar{z}$: reflection in the real axis (orientation-reversing — not holomorphic, by Part 06).
  • $w = 1/z$: inversion — moduli reciprocate ($|w| = 1/|z|$), arguments negate; maps the family of lines-and-circles to itself (a line through the origin ↔ itself; a line missing the origin ↔ a circle through the origin).

2. The Two Mappings Behind Pricing Coordinates

  • $w = z^2$: doubles arguments, squares moduli — the right half-plane opens to the whole plane (minus a slit); Cartesian grid lines map to orthogonal parabolas. Where angles at the origin get doubled is exactly where conformality fails ($f'(0) = 0$).
  • $w = e^z$: horizontal lines $y = c$ → rays $\arg w = c$; vertical lines $x = c$ → circles $|w| = e^c$; the strip $0 < y < \pi$ → the upper half-plane. Its inverse $\ln z$ is multi-valued (add $2\pi i k$) — the reason log-price coordinates need a branch choice, and the prototype for branch cuts.

The substitution $S = Ke^x$ from the Black-Scholes-to-heat-equation reduction (MM Part 13) is precisely the $e^z$ mapping restricted to the real line: pricing already works in mapped coordinates.

3. Conformality

Where $f$ is holomorphic and $f'(z_0) \ne 0$, the map preserves angles (magnitude and orientation) between intersecting curves: locally it is rotation by $\arg f'(z_0)$ plus scaling by $|f'(z_0)|$. Consequences:

  • Orthogonal grids stay orthogonal — the level curves of $u$ and $v$ always cross at right angles (the geometric face of Cauchy-Riemann, Part 06).
  • Harmonic functions stay harmonic under conformal pullback — solve Laplace problems on a simple domain (disk/half-plane) and transport the solution (Part 07's method).
  • Möbius maps $w = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}$ ($ad - bc \ne 0$): the conformal automorphisms taking lines/circles to lines/circles, with exactly three degrees of freedom — pick any three points to send to any three points.

4. Worked Example: Strip → Disk

Map the analyticity strip $-1 < \mathrm{Im}\,z < 1$ to the unit disk: first $\zeta = e^{\pi z/2}$ sends it to the right half-plane $\mathrm{Re}\,\zeta > 0$; then the Cayley-type Möbius $w = \frac{\zeta - 1}{\zeta + 1}$ sends the half-plane to $|w| < 1$. Composition:

$$w = \frac{e^{\pi z/2} - 1}{e^{\pi z/2} + 1} = \tanh\!\left(\frac{\pi z}{4}\right).$$

This is the standard trick for numerics on strips (where characteristic functions live): transplant the problem to the disk or half-plane where quadrature nodes, kernels and convergence theory are off-the-shelf.

Key Takeaways

  • Read $f(z)$ as transport: translations/rotations ($az+b$), inversion ($1/z$), doubling ($z^2$), strip-to-rays ($e^z$).
  • $e^z$/$\ln z$ are the coordinates pricing already uses; multi-valuedness of log forces branch cuts.
  • Holomorphic + $f' \ne 0$ ⇒ conformal: angles preserved, grids stay orthogonal, harmonicity transports.
  • Möbius maps move lines/circles around with three free points — the canonical tool for normalizing domains.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Mapping transforms one complex coordinate space to another. Quants use it to wrap complex, boundary-restricted options into simple circles or planes that are easy to price.

  • Conformal Mapping: Transforms the grid while preserving angles. This keeps the underlying physics (and Black-Scholes PDE structure) intact.
  • Simplifying Boundaries: A complex boundary (like a double barrier option) can be mapped into a simple half-plane, making the math solvable.
  • Visualizing Risk: Allows quants to map how changes in model parameters warp the volatility smile.
