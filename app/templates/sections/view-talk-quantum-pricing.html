A Quantum Finance Approach to Option Pricing

How quantum algorithms could accelerate Monte Carlo pricing — the quadratic speedup of quantum amplitude estimation, what it would take to be useful, and an honest assessment of the timeline.

1. The Target: Monte Carlo's √N Wall

Classical Monte Carlo (MM Part 09, ARM Part 08) converges as $O(N^{-1/2})$ — halving the error needs 4× the paths. For high-dimensional pricing (baskets, XVA), this is the dominant cost. The quantum promise is to break that wall.

2. Quantum Amplitude Estimation

  • The core result: Quantum Amplitude Estimation (QAE, built on Grover/amplitude amplification) estimates an expectation to error ε with $O(1/\varepsilon)$ quantum operations — a quadratic speedup over the classical $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$.
  • The pricing recipe (Rebentrost, Stamatopoulos et al.): (1) load the risk-neutral distribution into a quantum state (amplitude encoding), (2) apply a quantum circuit computing the discounted payoff, (3) run QAE to read out $\mathbb{E}[\text{payoff}]$ with quadratically fewer samples.
  • For a price needing 10⁶ classical paths, QAE would need ~10³ quantum oracle calls — if each were cheap, a transformative speedup.

3. The Hard Parts (Why It's Not Here Yet)

  • State preparation: loading an arbitrary distribution into amplitudes can itself cost exponentially many gates — efficient loaders (quantum GANs, special structure) are an active research problem and can erode the speedup.
  • Circuit depth: QAE needs deep, coherent circuits — far beyond today's noisy intermediate-scale (NISQ) hardware; it requires fault-tolerant quantum computers with error correction.
  • Payoff arithmetic on qubits (comparisons, max, exp) is expensive in quantum gates — the per-oracle cost can dwarf the sample-count savings.
  • Only quadratic: unlike Shor's exponential factoring speedup, this is a 2× exponent improvement — meaningful only when N is enormous and the constants/overheads are controlled.

4. Honest Assessment

  • The theory is sound and the speedup real in principle; the bottleneck is hardware (fault-tolerance) and state-loading efficiency — years to decades out for practical advantage over GPU Monte Carlo (the GPU workshop).
  • Near-term value is more likely in adjacent problems (optimization, sampling) than vanilla pricing; classical methods (AAD, GPU, QMC/Sobol) keep improving the baseline that quantum must beat.
  • The right quant stance: understand QAE's structure (it's elegant and may matter), track hardware progress, but build today's systems on classical acceleration — and be skeptical of "quantum advantage" claims that omit state-preparation and error-correction costs.

Key Takeaways

  • Quantum Amplitude Estimation offers a quadratic ($1/\varepsilon$ vs $1/\varepsilon^2$) speedup over classical Monte Carlo.
  • Pricing recipe: amplitude-encode the distribution, compute the payoff on qubits, read the expectation via QAE.
  • Blockers: efficient state preparation, deep fault-tolerant circuits, costly on-qubit arithmetic — only quadratic, so constants matter.
  • Sound theory, hardware-limited practice — years out; build on classical AAD/GPU/QMC today and scrutinize quantum-advantage claims.
