L6: Martingales

Tower Property, Lévy's Characterization, and Itô Isometry

1. The Martingale Property

A martingale is a mathematical formalization of a **fair game**. Given an information filtration $\mathcal{F}_s$ up to time $s$, the expectation of the future value $X_t$ ($t > s$) is simply the current value $X_s$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = X_s$$

For any continuous diffusion process satisfying $dX_t = A(t, X_t)dt + B(t, X_t)dW_t$, integrating and taking expectations reveals that the martingale property holds **if and only if the drift component is zero**:

$$A(t, X_t) = 0 \implies dX_t = B(t, X_t)dW_t$$

This means standard Brownian motion $W_t$ and all Itô integrals (which are drift-free) are automatically martingales.

General Rule: A stochastic process is a martingale if and only if its SDE has zero drift.

2. Lévy's Characterization & Itô Isometry

**Lévy's Martingale Characterization** states that a continuous process $X_t$ is standard Brownian motion if and only if:

1. $X_t$ is a martingale with respect to its filtration.
2. $X_t^2 - t$ is also a martingale (representing the linear accumulation of quadratic variation).

For any stochastic integrand $\theta_\tau$, the **Itô Isometry** relates the variance of the stochastic integral to the time-integral of the squared integrand:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \int_{0}^{t} \theta_\tau dW_\tau \right)^2 \right] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_{0}^{t} \theta_\tau^2 d\tau \right]$$

This is a fundamental property used to calculate SDE path variances, such as in interest rate option pricing under Vasicek.

Markov vs Martingale: Martingales describe a constant mean property, while Markov states that future distribution depends solely on current state.

1. Equivalent Measures & Radon-Nikodym

Two probability measures $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ sharing the same sample space $\Omega$ are **equivalent** ($\mathbb{P} \sim \mathbb{Q}$) if they agree on which events are possible or impossible ($\mathbb{P}(A) = 0 \iff \mathbb{Q}(A) = 0$).

The transition between equivalent measures is managed by a strictly positive random variable called the **Radon-Nikodym Derivative** ($\Lambda_t$):

$$\Lambda_t = \frac{d\mathbb{Q}}{d\mathbb{P}}$$

Using this ratio, expectations are transformed: $\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q}[X_t] = \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{P}[\Lambda_t X_t]$. For no-arbitrage financial modeling, we shift probability weights to find equivalent risk-neutral measures.

Equivalent measures: Drift parameters can change across measures, but volatility paths cannot!

2. Girsanov's Theorem & Shifted BM

If a process $\theta_t$ satisfies the **Novikov condition** $\mathbb{E}[\exp(\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T \theta_s^2 ds)] < \infty$, the Radon-Nikodym derivative $\Lambda_t$ is an exponential martingale:

$$\Lambda_t = \exp\left( -\int_{0}^{t} \theta_s dX_s - \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{t} \theta_s^2 ds \right)$$

Under the new measure $\mathbb{Q}$, Girsanov's theorem states that the shifted process $X_t^\mathbb{Q}$ is a standard Brownian motion:

$$X_t^\mathbb{Q} = X_t + \int_{0}^{t} \theta_s ds$$

This allows us to completely eliminate deterministic drift from asset SDEs by choosing the market price of risk $\theta_t = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma}$.

Girsanov shift: Shifting the coordinate system is equivalent to changing the measure!

Problem A: Stochastic Integration by Parts

In standard calculus, integration by parts is derived directly from the product rule. In stochastic calculus, we must account for quadratic covariation via Itô's product rule.

Step-by-Step Derivation:
  1. State the Stochastic Product Rule: For two SDEs $dX_t$ and $dY_t$, the differential of their product is: $$d(X_t Y_t) = X_t dY_t + Y_t dX_t + dX_t dY_t$$ where the cross term $dX_t dY_t$ arises from the non-zero quadratic covariation of paths.
  2. Isolate the Integrand Term: Rearranging to isolate $X_t dY_t$: $$X_t dY_t = d(X_t Y_t) - Y_t dX_t - dX_t dY_t$$
  3. Integrate over the Horizon $[0, t]$: Applying standard continuous integration: $$\int_{0}^{t} X_s dY_s = \int_{0}^{t} d(X_s Y_s) - \int_{0}^{t} Y_s dX_s - \int_{0}^{t} dX_s dY_s$$
  4. Evaluate the Exact Differential: Evaluating the first integral on the RHS yields: $$\int_{0}^{t} X_s dY_s = X_t Y_t - X_0 Y_0 - \int_{0}^{t} Y_s dX_s - \int_{0}^{t} dX_s dY_s$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why state the product rule? In ordinary math, if you multiply two changing quantities (like speed and time), the change in their combined value depends on both. In the random world of financial assets, there is a third "friction" or "jiggle" term ($dX_t dY_t$) because asset prices are extremely jagged. We start here to capture this hidden randomness friction.

2. Why isolate the term? We want to find the formula for the accumulated product (represented by the integral $\int X dY$). Moving terms around (simple algebra) places the target term on one side, and everything else on the other.

3. Why integrate? Integration is just summing up tiny changes over time. Summing both sides allows us to find the total accumulated value over the whole time horizon.

4. Why evaluate the exact differential? The sum of tiny changes in a total product is simply the final product minus the starting product. This simplifies a complex sum into a basic subtraction.

Problem B: Stochastic Quotient Rule SDE

We find the differential of the quotient $f(X, Y) = \frac{X}{Y}$ by applying the multi-variable Itô's lemma.

Step-by-Step Derivation:
  1. Compute Bivariate Partial Derivatives: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial X} = \frac{1}{Y} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial Y} = -\frac{X}{Y^2}$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2} = 0 \quad , \quad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial Y^2} = \frac{2X}{Y^3} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X \partial Y} = -\frac{1}{Y^2}$$
  2. State the Bivariate Itô Expansion: $$df(X, Y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial X} dX + \frac{\partial f}{\partial Y} dY + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2} dX^2 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial Y^2} dY^2 + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X \partial Y} dX dY$$
  3. Substitute the Derivatives: $$d\left(\frac{X}{Y}\right) = \frac{1}{Y} dX - \frac{X}{Y^2} dY + \frac{1}{2}(0) dX^2 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2X}{Y^3}\right) dY^2 - \frac{1}{Y^2} dX dY$$ $$d\left(\frac{X}{Y}\right) = \frac{1}{Y} dX - \frac{X}{Y^2} dY - \frac{1}{Y^2} dX dY + \frac{X}{Y^3} dY^2$$
  4. Formulate Common Denominators: Grouping terms reveals the final quotient differential: $$d\left(\frac{X}{Y}\right) = \frac{Y dX - X dY - dX dY}{Y^2} + \frac{X}{Y^3} dY^2$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why compute partial derivatives? A quotient ($X/Y$) is a fraction. If $X$ (the numerator) or $Y$ (the denominator) changes, the fraction changes. Derivatives tell us exactly how sensitive the fraction is to changes in $X$ and $Y$ individually.

2. Why state the Itô expansion? This is the master blueprint (Itô's Lemma) for random changes. It tells us: "To find the total change of a fraction, add the sensitivity to $X$, the sensitivity to $Y$, and the extra random jiggle terms."

3. Why substitute? We plug our specific fraction sensitivity formulas into the master blueprint to customize it for our fraction problem.

4. Why find common denominators? We do basic algebra to merge all these fractional pieces into a single, neat equation that is easy to read and use.

Problem C: Martingale Drift Elimination

We solve for the parameter $k$ that guarantees the process $Y_t = W_t^4 - 6tW_t^2 + kt^2$ is a martingale.

Step-by-Step Derivation:
  1. Compute Partial Derivatives: $$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial t} = -6W_t^2 + 2kt$$ $$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial W} = 4W_t^3 - 12tW_t \quad , \quad \frac{\partial^2 Y}{\partial W^2} = 12W_t^2 - 12t$$
  2. Apply Itô's Lemma: $$dY_t = \left( \frac{\partial Y}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 Y}{\partial W^2} \right) dt + \frac{\partial Y}{\partial W} dW_t$$
  3. Substitute and Simplify the Drift: $$dY_t = \left( -6W_t^2 + 2kt + \frac{1}{2}(12W_t^2 - 12t) \right) dt + (4W_t^3 - 12tW_t) dW_t$$ $$dY_t = \left( -6W_t^2 + 2kt + 6W_t^2 - 6t \right) dt + (4W_t^3 - 12tW_t) dW_t$$ $$dY_t = (2k - 6)t \, dt + (4W_t^3 - 12tW_t) dW_t$$
  4. Enforce Martingale Condition: For $Y_t$ to be a martingale, its drift must equal zero: $$(2k - 6)t = 0 \implies 2k - 6 = 0 \implies k = 3$$ Thus, $W_t^4 - 6tW_t^2 + 3t^2$ is a driftless martingale!
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why compute partial derivatives? We want to see how our combined target formula ($Y_t$) changes as time ($t$) ticks forward and as the random walker ($W_t$) moves up or down.

2. Why apply Itô's Lemma? This is the tool that splits any random movement into two piles: "Drift" (a smooth, predictable, one-way conveyor belt) and "Diffusion" (pure, fair, unpredictable random shaking).

3. Why substitute? We plug in our derivatives to see what the conveyor belt (drift) and the shaker (diffusion) look like for our specific formula.

4. Why set drift to zero? A **Martingale** is a perfectly fair game. For a game to be fair, there must be NO predictable conveyor belt pushing you up or down over time (drift = 0). Forcing the conveyor belt to zero allows us to solve for the exact value of $k$ ($k=3$) that makes the game perfectly fair.

Problem D: Asymptotic Vasicek Moments

We evaluate the long-term limits ($t \to \infty$) of the Vasicek interest rate SDE: $dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t) dt + \sigma dW_t$.

Step-by-Step Derivation:
  1. State the SDE Exact Solution: $$r_t = r_s e^{-\kappa(t-s)} + \theta\left(1 - e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\right) + \sigma \int_s^t e^{-\kappa(t-u)} dW_u$$
  2. Compute Conditional Expectation: Taking expectations conditional on $r_s$: $$\mathbb{E}[r_t \mid r_s] = r_s e^{-\kappa(t-s)} + \theta\left(1 - e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\right) + \sigma \mathbb{E}\left[\int_s^t e^{-\kappa(t-u)} dW_u\right]$$ Since Itô integrals are driftless martingales with zero expectation: $$\mathbb{E}[r_t \mid r_s] = r_s e^{-\kappa(t-s)} + \theta\left(1 - e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\right)$$ As $t \to \infty$, the exponential terms decay ($e^{-\kappa(t-s)} \to 0$): $$\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathbb{E}[r_t \mid r_s] = \theta$$
  3. Apply Itô Isometry for Variance: $$\text{Var}(r_t \mid r_s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \sigma \int_s^t e^{-\kappa(t-u)} dW_u \right)^2 \right]$$ Applying Itô Isometry converts the stochastic variance into a deterministic integral: $$\text{Var}(r_t \mid r_s) = \sigma^2 \int_s^t \left( e^{-\kappa(t-u)} \right)^2 du = \sigma^2 \int_s^t e^{-2\kappa(t-u)} du$$
  4. Evaluate the Integral & Limit: $$\text{Var}(r_t \mid r_s) = \sigma^2 \left[ \frac{1}{2\kappa} e^{-2\kappa(t-u)} \right]_s^t = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}\left( 1 - e^{-2\kappa(t-s)} \right)$$ Taking the limit as $t \to \infty$ reveals the steady-state variance bounds: $$\lim_{t \to \infty} \text{Var}(r_t \mid r_s) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why state the exact solution? This is the master recipe showing exactly where the interest rate will be in the future. It shows the rate is made of a predictable pull towards a long-term target, and an accumulated sum of random shocks.

2. Why compute the conditional expectation? Expectation is the "average guess." Since random shocks are just as likely to be positive as negative, our average guess for the shocks is exactly zero, making the random part vanish.

3. Why take the limit $t \to \infty$? We want to see what happens in the long run (steady state). Over a very long time, the influence of where we started decays to zero, and the average rate settles exactly on the long-term target ($\theta$).

4. Why apply Itô Isometry? Variance measures the "uncertainty range." The random shocks are a sum of many individual random inputs over time. Itô Isometry is a mathematical magic trick that lets us calculate this complex random variance sum by turning it into a simple, standard area calculation (a deterministic integral).

5. Why evaluate the limit? In the long run, the accumulated uncertainty doesn't grow infinitely because the interest rate is constantly pulled back to its target (mean-reversion). The variance settles on a stable steady-state limit of $\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}$.
