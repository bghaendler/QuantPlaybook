Question 1 (MMA Doubling Time) Numeric Input

Suppose the short rate is constant over time and equals $r = 2\%$. If you invest in the money market account, how many years does it take to double your investment? Round your answer to the next full year.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

The money market account value at time $t$ under a constant short rate $r$ is given by:

$$ B(t) = B(0)e^{\int_0^t r ds} = B(0)e^{r t} $$

Without loss of generality, we set $B(0) = 1$. To find the time $t$ at which the investment doubles ($B(t) = 2$):

$$ e^{0.02 t} = 2 $$

Taking the natural logarithm on both sides yields:

$$ 0.02 t = \ln(2) \implies t = \frac{\ln(2)}{0.02} \approx \frac{0.693147}{0.02} = 34.657 \text{ years} $$

The question requires us to "round your answer to the next full year", which is the ceiling of $34.66$. Therefore:

$$ t_{\text{rounded}} = \lceil 34.657 \rceil = \mathbf{35} \text{ years} $$

Correct Answer: 35

Question 2 (Simple Rate to Yield) Numeric Input

If the 5-year simple rate is 10%, what is the corresponding 5-year yield? Express your answer in percentage points and round to two decimal places.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

A quoted 5-year simple compounding rate of $L = 10\%$ corresponds to a Zero-Coupon Bond (ZCB) discount factor of:

$$ P(0, 5) = \frac{1}{1 + \tau L} = \frac{1}{1 + 5 \times 0.10} = \frac{1}{1.5} \approx 0.666667 $$

The continuously compounded yield $y$ over the same period $[0, 5]$ satisfies the ZCB pricing relation:

$$ P(0, 5) = e^{-\tau y} \implies e^{-5 y} = \frac{1}{1.5} \implies e^{5 y} = 1.5 $$

Taking the natural logarithm on both sides gives:

$$ 5 y = \ln(1.5) \implies y = \frac{\ln(1.5)}{5} \approx \frac{0.405465}{5} = 0.081093 \text{ (or } \mathbf{8.11\%}\text{)} $$

So the yield expressed in percentage points is exactly 8.11.

Correct Answer: 8.11

Question 3 (Default Risk Effect) Multiple Choice

What would happen to the prices of bonds if one takes into account default risk?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Analysis & Explanation:

Default risk represents the probability that the bond issuer will fail to meet coupon or principal payments. When default risk is taken into account:

  • Investors demand a higher yield to compensate for taking on the credit risk of default (the credit risk premium).
  • Mathematically, the discount rate (yield) $y_{\text{risky}} = y_{\text{riskfree}} + s$ increases, where $s > 0$ represents the credit spread.
  • Since the present value of cash flows is inversely related to the discount rate, a higher yield spreads the discount factor downwards: $$ P_{\text{risky}}(t, T) < P_{\text{riskfree}}(t, T) $$

Thus, bond prices will **Decrease**.

Correct Answer: Decrease

Question 4 (Comparing Loan Quotes) Multiple Choice

Suppose you want to take out a 10-year loan and you shop around at different banks to get the best deal. Bank A quotes an interest rate of 9% while Bank B quotes an interest rate of 7%. Obviously you are tempted to take the offer of Bank B, however in the small print of the contracts you read that Bank A uses simple rates and Bank B uses continuously compounded rates. Is Bank B truly the best deal?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Analysis:

To determine which bank is the best deal for taking out a 10-year loan, we calculate the total amount of payback required at maturity $T = 10$ for a principal loan of 1 unit:

  • Bank A (Simple Interest Rate $L = 9\%$): Using simple linear interest payback formulation: $$ \text{Payback}_A = 1 + T \times L = 1 + 10 \times 0.09 = 1.90 \text{ units} $$
  • Bank B (Continuously Compounded Yield $y = 7\%$): Using continuous compounding exponential payback formulation: $$ \text{Payback}_B = e^{T \times y} = e^{10 \times 0.07} = e^{0.7} \approx 2.01375 \text{ units} $$

Since this is a loan, you want to pay back the **least** amount of money possible. Since $\text{Payback}_A (1.90) < \text{Payback}_B (2.01)$, Bank A is actually the cheaper and better deal, and Bank B is **NOT** the best deal. Hence, the answer is **No**.

Correct Answer: No

Question 5 (MMA Monotonicity) True / False

Is the following statement true or false?
The money market account is always increasing over time if the short rate is a positive process.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

The money market account value at time $t$ is mathematically defined as:

$$ B(t) = e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds} $$

Taking the time derivative of the money market account via the fundamental theorem of calculus:

$$ \frac{d B(t)}{d t} = r(t) e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds} = r(t) B(t) $$

Since the exponential function is always positive ($B(t) > 0$), and the short rate is a positive process ($r(t) > 0$ for all $t$):

$$ \frac{d B(t)}{d t} = r(t) B(t) > 0 $$

Since the derivative is strictly positive, the money market account $B(t)$ is strictly increasing over time. Therefore, the statement is **True**.

Correct Answer: True

Question 6 (Yield Curve Shape) True / False

Is the following statement true or false?
The yield curve is always upward sloping.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Analysis & Explanation:

The yield curve (or term structure of interest rates) represents the relation between the contract maturity $T$ and the annualized continuously compounded yield $y(t, T)$. Market pricing expectations, liquidity preferences, and risk premiums dynamically shape this curve:

  • Upward Sloping (Normal): Longer-term yields are higher than short-term yields, which is the most common state to compensate for maturity and inflation risks.
  • Flat Yield Curve: Short-term and long-term rates are identical.
  • Downward Sloping (Inverted): Short-term yields are higher than long-term yields. This typically occurs when the central bank maintains tight monetary policy and the market anticipates future rate cuts and economic recession.
  • Humped Yield Curve: Mid-term yields are higher than both short-term and long-term yields.

Since all of these shapes are economically plausible and observed in real-world interest rate markets, the statement is **False**.

Correct Answer: False

Question 7 (n-Fold Rate Properties) Multiple Choice (Select all that apply)

Which of the following statements about the $n$-fold compounded interest rate $y_n(t, T)$ are true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Analysis of the Statements:

  1. Statement 1: $\lim_{T \to t} y_n(t, T) = r(t)$ (TRUE)

    At the short end, as maturity $T$ approaches spot time $t$, all compounding conventions (simple compounding, continuous yield, and $n$-fold compounding) converge to the same instantaneous short rate $r(t)$.

  2. Statement 2: $y_1(t, T) = L(t, T)$ (FALSE)

    Under annual compounding ($n=1$), the bond price is $P(t, T) = (1 + y_1)^{-(T-t)}$. Under simple compounding, the bond price is $P(t, T) = (1 + (T-t)L)^{-1}$. In general, $(1 + y_1)^{T-t} \neq 1 + (T-t)L$, so they do not coincide unless $T-t = 1$ year exactly.

  3. Statement 3: $\lim_{n \to \infty} y_n(t, T) = y(t, T)$ (TRUE)

    Continuous compounding is defined as the mathematical limit of $n$-fold compounding as the frequency $n$ goes to infinity. Applying the standard calculus limit: $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{y_n}{n}\right)^{-n(T-t)} = e^{-y(T-t)} \implies \lim_{n \to \infty} y_n(t, T) = y(t, T) $$

  4. Statement 4: $y_n(t, T) \geq y_{n+1}(t, T)$ (TRUE)

    As the compounding frequency $n$ increases, interest compounds more frequently, earning interest-on-interest throughout the year. Therefore, a smaller quoted annualized rate is required to achieve the exact same total repayment (discount factor) over the period. Thus, $y_n$ is strictly decreasing in $n$, satisfying $y_1 > y_2 > y_4 > y_{12} > y_{365} > y$.

Correct Answers: Statement 1, Statement 3, and Statement 4 are all TRUE.

Question 8 (ZCB Discount Application) Multiple Choice (Select all that apply)

Let $C$ denote a cash flow at some future date $T$. In which of the following cases is the time-$t$ value given by $P(t, T) \times C$?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Analysis & Explanation:

The time-$t$ ZCB price $P(t, T)$ represents the discount factor for a guaranteed, risk-free unit payment at maturity $T$ under no-arbitrage conditions:

  • Fixed Payment of Five Dollars ($C = 5$): (TRUE) A fixed dollar payment has no default risk, no indexation risk, and no timing uncertainty. Its value is completely deterministic under risk-free conditions. Thus, by no-arbitrage, its present value is exactly given by multiplying by the risk-free discount factor: $$ V(t) = P(t, T) \times C $$
  • Stock Dividend Payment: (FALSE) A stock dividend is a risky cash flow. It depends on the company's financial performance, equity value, and board decisions (dividend cuts, suspensions). Since it contains both default/equity risks and timing/amount uncertainty, it cannot be discounted using the risk-free ZCB price alone; it requires an equity risk premium or risk-adjusted expectation.

Correct Answer: Fixed payment of five dollars ($C$ is the second option).
