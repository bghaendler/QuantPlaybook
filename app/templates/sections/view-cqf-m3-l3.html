One-Period Foundations: Delta Hedging vs. Risk Neutrality

Let $S_0$ be the stock price at time $0$. Over a time period $\delta t$, the stock can move to either $S_u = u S_0$ with physical probability $p$ or down to $S_d = d S_0$ with probability $1-p$, such that $d < 1 < u$. Let the discount factor be $D = (1 + r\delta t)^{-1}$ and the bank factor be $B = 1/D = 1 + r\delta t$.

Method 1: Delta Hedging

We construct a portfolio $\Pi_0 = V_0 - \Delta S_0$. To make it perfectly risk-free, we choose $\Delta$ so that the portfolio value in the upper and lower state is equal: $$V_u - \Delta u S_0 = V_d - \Delta d S_0 \implies \Delta = \frac{V_u - V_d}{(u - d)S_0}$$ To prevent arbitrage, this risk-free portfolio must return the risk-free rate: $$V_u - \Delta S_u = \frac{1}{B}(V_0 - \Delta S_0) \implies V_0 = \frac{(1 - Dd)V_u - (Du - 1)V_d}{u - d}$$

Method 2: Risk-Neutral Valuation

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{P}^*$ defined by probability $p^*$, the expected stock growth must equal the risk-free rate: $$S_0 = D \left[ p^* S_u + (1-p^*)S_d \right] \implies p^* = \frac{1/D - d}{u - d}$$ Option pricing matches exactly: $V_0 = D \left[ p^* V_u + (1 - p^*)V_d \right]$.

Martingale Properties

The parameters $p \in (0, 1)$ define a probability measure $\mathbb{P}$ over the sample space $\Omega = \{\text{up move, down move}\}$.

Equivalent Martingale Measure (EMM)

The martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}$ is the equivalent probability measure such that the discounted stock price follows a martingale: $$S_0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[D S_T] \implies S_0 = D \left[ q S_u + (1-q)S_d \right]$$ Solving for $q$ yields the unique equivalent martingale probability: $$q = \frac{1/D - d}{u - d} = p^*$$ Thus, there is one and only one equivalent martingale measure in the one-period binomial model, and it matches the risk-neutral probability $p^*$.

Bayes Formula for Expectations: Clarifies that the price computed under $\mathbb{Q}$ holds in the real world. We also define the Q-bar measure ($\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$) where the stock itself acts as the discounting numeraire (groceries paid in stocks analogy). Black-Scholes risk-neutral measure is unique among all equivalent measures.

Connecting Binomial Delta-Hedging to Continuous PDEs

In the Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR) model, the parameters are chosen to be consistent with continuous Geometric Brownian Motion as $\delta t \to 0$: $$u = e^{\sigma \sqrt{\delta t}}, \quad d = e^{-\sigma \sqrt{\delta t}} = \frac{1}{u}, \quad D = e^{-r \delta t}$$

Taylor Expansions of Parameters & Differences

We expand the CRR parameters up to order $\delta t$: $$u \approx 1 + \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \delta t, \quad d \approx 1 - \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \delta t, \quad 1/D = e^{r \delta t} \approx 1 + r \delta t$$ This yields the parameter differences: $$u - d \approx 2\sigma \sqrt{\delta t}, \quad 1/D - d \approx r\delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\delta t, \quad u - 1/D \approx \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\delta t - r\delta t$$

Expansion of $V_u$: $$V_u \approx V + \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} \delta t + \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\delta S_u + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} (\delta S_u)^2$$ $$\text{where } \delta S_u = S(u - 1) \approx S \left( \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \delta t \right)$$ $$(\delta S_u)^2 \approx S^2 \sigma^2 \delta t$$
Expansion of $V_d$: $$V_d \approx V + \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} \delta t + \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\delta S_d + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} (\delta S_d)^2$$ $$\text{where } \delta S_d = S(d - 1) \approx S \left( -\sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \delta t \right)$$ $$(\delta S_d)^2 \approx S^2 \sigma^2 \delta t$$

Substituting into Equation (5):

Substituting all expanded terms into $(1/D)(u-d)V = (1/D)(V_u - V_d) + (uV_d - dV_u)$, we obtain: $$(2\sigma \sqrt{\delta t})(1 + r\delta t)V \approx \left( V_u \right)\left( r\delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\delta t \right) + \left( V_d \right)\left( \sigma \sqrt{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\delta t - r\delta t \right)$$ Developing and dividing through by $2\sigma \sqrt{\delta t}\delta t$ yields: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + rS \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - rV \approx 0$$

Taking the limit as $\delta t \to 0$, we retrieve the continuous Black-Scholes PDE exactly!

The $N$-Period Recombining Binomial Model

We split the lifetime $T$ into $N$ steps of length $\delta t = T/N$. Let's trace how the recursive formula generalizes to time $0$ via mathematical induction.

Mathematical Induction Proof

1. Conjectured Step Pricing: $V(N - k, j) = D^k \sum_{i=0}^k \binom{k}{i} (p^*)^i (1 - p^*)^{k - i} V(N, j + i)$.
2. One-Step Relationship: $V(N-k, j) = D \left[ p^* V(N - (k-1), j+1) + (1-p^*) V(N - (k-1), j) \right]$.
3. Substituting Inductive Hypotheses: $$V(N-k, j) = D^k \left[ \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \binom{k-1}{i} (p^*)^{i+1} (1-p^*)^{k-1-i} V_{j+1+i} + \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \binom{k-1}{i} (p^*)^i (1-p^*)^{k-i} V_{j+i} \right]$$ 4. Realignment of Index (Shift $i+1 \to i$): $$= D^k \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{k} \binom{k-1}{i-1} (p^*)^i (1-p^*)^{k-i} V_{j+i} + \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \binom{k-1}{i} (p^*)^i (1-p^*)^{k-i} V_{j+i} \right]$$ 5. Isolating Endpoints & Applying Combinatorial Identity $\binom{k-1}{i-1} + \binom{k-1}{i} = \binom{k}{i}$: $$V(N - k, j) = D^k \sum_{i=0}^{k} \binom{k}{i} (p^*)^i (1 - p^*)^{k - i} \max[S(N, j + i) - E, 0]$$

Term 2: Cash strike payment

$$E D^N \sum_{i=0}^N \binom{N}{i} (p^*)^i (1 - p^*)^{N - i} \mathbf{1}_{S(N, i) > E}$$ Simplifies to $E e^{-rT} \mathbb{P}^*[S_T > E]$, which is the binomial analogue of $E e^{-rT} N(d_2)$.

Term 1: Stock delivery & $\bar{p}$

$$S_0 \sum_{i=0}^N \binom{N}{i} (\bar{p})^i (1 - \bar{p})^{N - i} \mathbf{1}_{S(N, i) > E}$$ where $\bar{p} = D u p^*$. This is the binomial analogue of $S_0 N(d_1)$, representing the Q-bar stock measure probability of expiring in-the-money.

The BIG Idea: Equivalence of Methods

In the Black-Scholes setting, the no-arbitrage replication approach and the equivalent martingale measure approach are strictly equivalent!

- No Arbitrage implies EMM: To prevent arbitrage, we can rewrite the delta hedging equation to define a risk-neutral EMM probability $p^*$.
- EMM implies No Arbitrage: For an equivalent martingale measure to exist, we require $0 < p^* < 1$, which translates to $d < 1/D < u$.

Arbitrage Boundaries:

  • If $p^* \ge 1 \iff u \le 1/D$: Arbitrage opportunity exists by short-selling the stock and investing in cash (guaranteed minimum return).
  • If $p^* \le 0 \iff 1/D \le d$: Arbitrage opportunity exists by borrowing cash to buy the stock.

Complete vs. Incomplete Markets

Complete Markets: A market where there are enough tradeable instruments to perfectly replicate and hedge all risk sources.

Redundancy & Hedging

In the Black-Scholes world, options are redundant securities: they do not introduce new risk factors and can be perfectly replicated by trading the underlying stock. This is why no-arbitrage replication pricing works flawlessly.

Incomplete Markets: Markets where we cannot find enough tradeable assets to hedge the risk sources (e.g. interest rate bond markets, where rates are not directly tradeable, requiring us to hedge with other bonds). We will explore this extensively in Module 4!

Exercise 1: Feynman-Kac Bridge Verification
Problem:
Prove that the solution to the custom PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \alpha S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - r V = 0$$ with terminal payoff $V(S, T) = S^k$ is given by $V(S, t) = S^k \exp\left( \left[ k\alpha + \frac{1}{2}k(k-1)\sigma^2 - r \right](T-t) \right)$ using both: 1. Direct SDE integration via Geometric Brownian Motion expectation. 2. Substitution of the ansatz into the PDE.
Detailed Solution:

Part 1: Expectation Method
Under the stochastic process, the stock evolves under drift $\alpha$: $$S_T = S_t \exp\left( \left( \alpha - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)(T-t) + \sigma (W_T - W_t) \right)$$ Taking the expectation of the discounted payoff $V(S, t) = e^{-r(T-t)} \mathbb{E}[ S_T^k \mid S_t ]$: $$V(S, t) = e^{-r(T-t)} S_t^k \exp\left( k\left( \alpha - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)(T-t) \right) \mathbb{E}\left[ \exp(k \sigma (W_T - W_t)) \right]$$ Using the normal moment generating function $\mathbb{E}[e^{\theta Z}] = e^{\frac{1}{2}\theta^2 \text{Var}(Z)}$: $$\mathbb{E}\left[ \exp(k \sigma (W_T - W_t)) \right] = \exp\left( \frac{1}{2} k^2 \sigma^2 (T-t) \right)$$ Multiplying terms: $$V(S, t) = S_t^k \exp\left( \left[ k\alpha - \frac{1}{2}k\sigma^2 + \frac{1}{2}k^2\sigma^2 - r \right](T-t) \right)$$ Simplifying the exponent gives exactly the stated solution.

Part 2: PDE Substitution Method
Let $V(S, t) = S^k e^{\gamma(T-t)}$. Calculate partial derivatives: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = -\gamma S^k e^{\gamma(T-t)} = -\gamma V \quad , \quad \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} = k S^{k-1} e^{\gamma(T-t)} = \frac{k}{S} V$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = k(k-1) S^{k-2} e^{\gamma(T-t)} = \frac{k(k-1)}{S^2} V$$ Substitute these into the custom PDE: $$-\gamma V + \alpha S \left( \frac{k}{S} V \right) + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \left( \frac{k(k-1)}{S^2} V \right) - r V = 0$$ Dividing by non-zero $V$: $$-\gamma + k\alpha + \frac{1}{2}k(k-1)\sigma^2 - r = 0 \implies \gamma = k\alpha + \frac{1}{2}k(k-1)\sigma^2 - r$$ The PDE is perfectly satisfied.

Exercise 2: Discrete Replicating Portfolio Construction
Problem:
In a one-period model, let $S_0 = 100$, strike $E = 100$, up factor $u = 1.15$, down factor $d = 0.85$, and risk-free rate $r = 5.0\%$ per period. Calculate: 1. The exact replicating stock holdings ($\Delta$) and cash bond holdings ($B$) for a European Call. 2. Verify that replicating wealth exactly covers Call payoffs in both states.
Detailed Solution:

Part 1: Portfolio Variables
First, evaluate stock prices and Call payoffs: * Up State: $S_u = 115 \implies V_u = \max(115 - 100, 0) = 15$. * Down State: $S_d = 85 \implies V_d = \max(85 - 100, 0) = 0$. Calculate delta: $$\Delta = \frac{V_u - V_d}{(u-d)S_0} = \frac{15 - 0}{(1.15 - 0.85) \times 100} = \frac{15}{30} = 0.50 \text{ shares}$$ Replicating cash bond holdings ($B$) must satisfy: $$B = e^{-r \Delta t} \frac{u V_d - d V_u}{u - d} = e^{-0.05} \frac{1.15 \times 0 - 0.85 \times 15}{1.15 - 0.85} = e^{-0.05} \frac{-12.75}{0.30} \approx 0.95123 \times (-42.50) \approx -40.427$$

Part 2: Value Verification
Initial replicating portfolio cost: $$V_0 = \Delta S_0 + B = 0.50 \times 100 - 40.427 = \$9.573$$ Let's verify terminal state values: * Up State terminal wealth: $$\Delta S_u + B e^{r \Delta t} = 0.50 \times 115 - 40.427 \times e^{0.05} = 57.50 - 42.50 = \$15.00 \quad (\text{Perfect matches } V_u)$$ * Down State terminal wealth: $$\Delta S_d + B e^{r \Delta t} = 0.50 \times 85 - 40.427 \times e^{0.05} = 42.50 - 42.50 = \$0.00 \quad (\text{Perfect matches } V_d)$$ This matches option payoffs exactly, proving replication correctness.
