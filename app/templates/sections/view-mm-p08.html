Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Cauchy-Euler Equations (Perpetual Option Solutions)

The equidimensional ODE whose power-law solutions price perpetual options in closed form — the one American option you can solve on paper.

1. The Cauchy-Euler Equation

$$a x^2 y'' + b x y' + c y = 0.$$

Coefficients scale with derivatives' orders ("equidimensional": invariant under $x \to \lambda x$). Try $y = x^m$:

$$a\,m(m-1) + b\,m + c = 0$$

— the auxiliary quadratic. Real distinct roots $m_1, m_2$ ⇒ $y = Ax^{m_1} + Bx^{m_2}$; repeated root ⇒ $x^m(A + B\ln x)$; complex $\alpha \pm i\beta$ ⇒ $x^\alpha(A\cos(\beta\ln x) + B\sin(\beta\ln x))$. Equivalently, substitute $x = e^z$ to get constant coefficients — Cauchy-Euler is the constant-coefficient equation in log coordinates, which is exactly why it appears under geometric Brownian motion.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Cauchy-Euler equations are special ODEs where the coefficients scale with the power of the variable (e.g. S^2 V'').

  • Power Solutions: The solution takes the form of S^γ. We solve the characteristic quadratic equation to find γ.
  • Scale Invariance: These equations model systems where risks scale proportionally with the asset price.

2. Perpetual Options: Time Drops Out

With no expiry, $V_t = 0$ and the Black-Scholes PDE becomes a Cauchy-Euler ODE in $S$:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 V'' + (r - q) S V' - rV = 0,$$

auxiliary quadratic $\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 m(m-1) + (r-q)m - r = 0$ with roots $m_1 > 1 > 0 > m_2$ (for $r > 0$). General solution $V = AS^{m_1} + BS^{m_2}$: the two power solutions are the building blocks of every perpetual and first-passage formula (they are the same powers in Bjerksund-Stensland's $\beta$ and the one-touch exponents $\mu \pm \lambda$).

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Perpetual options have no expiration date. Because time doesn't affect their value, the Black-Scholes PDE simplifies into a Cauchy-Euler ODE.

  • No Theta: Since the option never expires, the derivative with respect to time is zero.
  • One-Dimensional: Reduces the options problem from a complex 2D PDE grid to a simple 1D ODE.

3. The Perpetual American Put (McKean/Merton)

Hold region $S > S^*$: $V = BS^{m_2}$ (discard $S^{m_1}$ — value must vanish as $S \to \infty$). Two conditions at the unknown boundary:

$$V(S^*) = K - S^* \;\;\text{(value matching)}, \qquad V'(S^*) = -1 \;\;\text{(smooth pasting)}.$$

Solving:

$$S^* = \frac{m_2}{m_2 - 1}K, \qquad V(S) = \frac{K - S^*}{(S^*)^{m_2}}\, S^{m_2} = \frac{K}{1 - m_2}\left(\frac{m_2 - 1}{m_2}\cdot\frac{S}{K}\right)^{m_2}.$$

With $q = 0$: $m_2 = -2r/\sigma^2$. Numbers: $r = 5\%$, $\sigma = 20\%$, $K = 100$ ⇒ $m_2 = -2.5$, $S^* = \frac{-2.5}{-3.5}\times100 = 71.4$ — exercise the perpetual put when spot falls below ~71. The perpetual call (with $q > 0$) mirrors with $m_1$: $S^* = \frac{m_1}{m_1 - 1}K$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The perpetual American put is the classic model for analyzing early exercise. We solve it analytically using the Cauchy-Euler roots.

  • Exercise Boundary: Traders use this formula to find the exact stock price threshold where they should exercise the option.
  • Value Function: The option value is a power function of the stock price, decaying to zero as the stock price rises.

4. Why Smooth Pasting?

Value matching alone leaves a one-parameter family — one for each candidate boundary. Smooth pasting (equal first derivatives) is the optimality condition: exercising at any other boundary gives a value function with a kink, and a kinked value can be improved (the holder could do better by waiting or acting earlier). Economically it equates the marginal value of waiting to the marginal intrinsic gain. This pair of conditions — match value, match slope, solve for the free boundary — recurs throughout real options (investment timing à la McDonald-Siegel, abandonment, debt default in Leland's model), all of which are Cauchy-Euler problems with different payoffs.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Smooth pasting is the condition that determines the optimal exercise boundary by matching both the option price and its Delta.

  • Price Matching: The option value must equal the payoff value at the boundary (continuity).
  • Slope Matching: The slope (Delta) of the option price must match the slope of the payoff at the boundary (smoothness), preventing arbitrage.

Key Takeaways

  • $ax^2y'' + bxy' + cy = 0$: try $x^m$, solve the auxiliary quadratic; log-substitution explains why GBM produces it.
  • Perpetual BS = Cauchy-Euler; the two power roots bracket 0 and 1 and build all first-passage prices.
  • Perpetual put: $S^* = \frac{m_2}{m_2-1}K$ via value matching + smooth pasting — the only American option with a clean closed form.
  • Smooth pasting is optimality, not aesthetics; the same recipe runs all of real-options theory.
