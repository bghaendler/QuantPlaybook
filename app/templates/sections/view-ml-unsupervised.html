Unsupervised Learning: K-Means & GMM Regime Detection

Letting the data label itself: clustering market observations into regimes — calm, stressed, trending — without ever telling the algorithm what a regime is.

1. The Regime-Detection Problem

Market behavior is non-stationary but not arbitrary: it visits recurring states (low-vol grind, risk-off stress, recovery). Features per period $x_t$ — realized vol, returns, correlation level, credit spreads, term-structure slope — form a point cloud; clustering asks whether that cloud has natural groups. Found regimes feed: vol scaling, dynamic risk limits, strategy switching, and regime-conditional parameter estimation.

2. K-Means: Hard Assignments

Choose $K$ centroids $\mu_k$ to minimize within-cluster variance:

$$\min_{\{c_t\},\{\mu_k\}} \sum_t \left\lVert x_t - \mu_{c_t}\right\rVert^2.$$

Lloyd's algorithm alternates: assign each point to its nearest centroid; recompute centroids as cluster means. Converges fast (to a local optimum — run multiple seeds, or k-means++).

  • Choosing K: elbow on inertia, silhouette score, or stability across subsamples; for markets, $K = 2$–4 (calm/stress, plus trend states) is typical.
  • Must standardize features — Euclidean distance is meaningless across mixed units.
  • Limitations: spherical equal-size clusters, hard boundaries — a day is 100% in one regime even when evidence is ambiguous.

3. Gaussian Mixture Models: Soft, Probabilistic Regimes

Model the density as a mixture of $K$ Gaussians:

$$p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k\, \mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k).$$

The EM algorithm alternates: E-step computes responsibilities (posterior regime probabilities)

$$\gamma_{tk} = \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_t \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\sum_j \pi_j \mathcal{N}(x_t \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)},$$

M-step re-estimates $(\pi_k, \mu_k, \Sigma_k)$ from responsibility-weighted moments. Advantages over k-means (which is the $\Sigma_k = \epsilon I$ limit):

  • Soft membership: "today is 70% stress" — directly usable as a position-scaling weight.
  • Full covariances: regimes can be elongated/correlated clusters (e.g. stress = high vol and high correlation).
  • Model selection by likelihood: BIC/AIC choose $K$ on statistical grounds.
  • A GMM on returns is literally a fat-tailed model: a 2-state mixture of normals reproduces excess kurtosis and skew with 5 parameters.

Adding Markov dynamics on the hidden state gives the Hidden Markov Model (Hamilton's regime-switching) — same E/M logic with transition probabilities, yielding regime persistence and filtered/smoothed state paths.

4. A Concrete Pipeline

  1. Features: 21-day realized vol, 21-day return, average pairwise correlation of sector ETFs, slope 2s10s — standardized (z-scores on a rolling window to avoid lookahead).
  2. Fit GMM with $K = 3$ on history up to $t$; classify day $t+1$ out-of-sample.
  3. Typical output: regime A (low vol, positive drift, ~70% of days), B (elevated vol, flat), C (vol spike, negative drift, high correlation, ~5%).
  4. Use: scale exposure by $1 - \gamma_{t,C}$, or fit per-regime covariance matrices for VaR.

Pitfalls: label switching across refits (order regimes by vol), regime-count instability, lookahead bias in standardization, and the fact that clusters describe the past — transition timing is where the money is, and unsupervised models are weakest exactly there.

Key Takeaways

  • K-means: fast hard clustering by within-cluster variance; standardize features, multi-seed, elbow/silhouette for K.
  • GMM via EM generalizes k-means: soft probabilistic membership, full covariances, BIC for K.
  • Mixtures double as fat-tail return models; HMM adds regime persistence.
  • Out-of-sample discipline (rolling fits, no lookahead) decides whether "regimes" are real or artifacts.
