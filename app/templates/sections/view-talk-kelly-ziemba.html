The Kelly Capital Growth Criterion & Its Applications

Ziemba's perspective on the growth-optimal criterion: the theory, the great investors who used it, the real-world record, and the practical case for betting fractionally.

1. The Criterion and Its Guarantees

Maximize expected log wealth (RSI Part 03): $f^* = (\mu - r)/\sigma^2$ continuously, or $f^* = $ edge/odds for discrete bets. The asymptotic theorems are strong:

  • Maximal growth: Kelly wealth grows faster than any "essentially different" strategy's, almost surely, over a long horizon.
  • Minimal time to reach large wealth goals.
  • Never ruin with a properly sized fractional bet (you bet a fraction, never the whole stake).

These are long-run and asymptotic — the source of both Kelly's appeal and its practical danger.

2. The Practitioners' Record

  • Thorp (blackjack, then Princeton-Newport): Kelly/fractional-Kelly sizing, decades of high risk-adjusted returns.
  • Buffett (Ziemba's analysis): concentrated bets consistent with Kelly-like conviction sizing — though with long horizons that tolerate the volatility.
  • Simons / Renaissance, and many sports-betting syndicates: growth-optimal sizing on many small uncorrelated edges — the breadth that smooths Kelly's ride (the trend-following lesson too).
  • The pattern: Kelly works spectacularly with many independent edges and a long horizon; it is punishing on a single lumpy bet.

3. Why Ziemba Argues for Fractional Kelly

  • The drawdowns are real: full Kelly's interim losses (50%+ drawdowns with high probability) are unbearable for almost all real investors and clients — the long run arrives only if you survive the journey.
  • Estimation error overbets: $f^*$ uses μ, the least-known quantity; the growth curve is asymmetric, so overestimating the edge destroys growth faster than underestimating (RSI Part 03). Fractional Kelly is the rational response.
  • Half-Kelly keeps ~3/4 of the growth at half the volatility — the trade most investors should take; Ziemba's empirical work documents this across racetrack and market data.
  • Wrong-input danger: Ziemba's "great investment" examples are matched by cautionary ones where a too-aggressive Kelly bet on a mis-estimated edge wiped out capital — the criterion amplifies input error.

4. Practical Synthesis

  • Estimate edges conservatively; size at a fraction (quarter to half Kelly); diversify across many uncorrelated bets to smooth the path.
  • Add drawdown constraints (RSI Part 03) — de-risk as you approach a floor; never bet so the long run can't be reached.
  • Use Kelly as a sizing discipline and an upper bound on aggression, not a literal prescription — its value is preventing both over- and under-betting relative to edge.

Key Takeaways

  • Kelly maximizes long-run growth and avoids ruin — but only asymptotically, and only if you survive the drawdowns.
  • The great Kelly practitioners had many uncorrelated edges and long horizons; single lumpy bets are where it hurts.
  • Ziemba's case for fractional Kelly: brutal drawdowns + estimation error make half-Kelly (3/4 growth, half vol) the rational choice.
  • Treat Kelly as a conservative sizing discipline and aggression ceiling, with diversification and drawdown floors.
