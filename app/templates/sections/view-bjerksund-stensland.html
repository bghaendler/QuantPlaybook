Bjerksund-Stensland (1993 & 2002)

American options priced by assuming a simple exercise boundary and valuing the resulting stopping strategy exactly — a guaranteed lower bound that is remarkably tight.

1. The Strategy-Based Idea

Any feasible exercise strategy gives a lower bound on the American price (the optimal strategy is the supremum). Bjerksund & Stensland choose a deliberately simple strategy: exercise the first time $S$ hits a flat boundary $X$:

$$\tau = \inf\{t : S_t \ge X\}.$$

The value of this strategy under GBM has a closed form (first-passage mathematics), and maximizing over the single number $X$ — or using their analytical near-optimal choice of $X$ — yields a price that is both fast and a true lower bound. Contrast with BAW: BAW approximates the PDE (error of unknown sign); BS approximates the strategy (error of known sign, always ≤ true value).

2. The 1993 Formula

For a call with carry $b < r$ (otherwise European), define $\beta$ as the positive root of the fundamental quadratic:

$$\beta = \left(\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{b}{\sigma^2}\right) + \sqrt{\left(\tfrac{b}{\sigma^2} - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 + \tfrac{2r}{\sigma^2}}.$$

The trigger price is parametrized as

$$X = B_0 + (B_\infty - B_0)\left(1 - e^{h(T)}\right), \qquad B_\infty = \frac{\beta}{\beta - 1}K, \quad B_0 = \max\!\left(K, \frac{r}{r - b}K\right),$$ $$h(T) = -\left(bT + 2\sigma\sqrt{T}\right)\frac{B_0}{B_\infty - B_0}\cdot\frac{K^2}{B_0(B_\infty-B_0)}\;\;\text{(1993 variant: } h(T) = -(bT + 2\sigma\sqrt{T})\tfrac{K^2}{B_0(B_\infty - B_0)}\text{)},$$

interpolating between the perpetual boundary $B_\infty$ and the at-expiry boundary $B_0$. The price assembles from the auxiliary function $\varphi(S, T, \gamma, H, X)$ — the value of receiving $S^\gamma$ at $T$ if the boundary $X$ was never hit, known in closed form from the reflection principle:

$$C = \alpha S^\beta - \alpha\,\varphi(S,T,\beta,X,X) + \varphi(S,T,1,X,X) - \varphi(S,T,1,K,X) - K\varphi(S,T,0,X,X) + K\varphi(S,T,0,K,X),$$

with $\alpha = (X - K)X^{-\beta}$. Puts price via the Bjerksund-Stensland transformation $P(S, K, T, r, b, \sigma) = C(K, S, T, r - b, -b, \sigma)$ — no separate derivation needed.

3. The 2002 Refinement: Two-Step Boundary

The 2002 version splits $[0, T]$ at $t_1 = \frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{5} - 1)T$ and uses a two-level flat boundary (higher trigger early, lower trigger late), valuing the strategy with bivariate normal terms ($\Psi$ functions alongside $\varphi$). Cost: bivariate CDF evaluations. Benefit: the lower bound tightens dramatically — typical errors drop from ~0.1–0.5% (1993) to ~0.01–0.1% (2002), competitive with fine trees at a tiny fraction of the cost.

Key Takeaways

  • Value a suboptimal but explicit stopping strategy exactly → guaranteed lower bound.
  • 1993: one flat boundary interpolated between $B_0$ and the perpetual $B_\infty = \frac{\beta}{\beta-1}K$.
  • 2002: two-step boundary + bivariate normals → near-benchmark accuracy.
  • Put = transformed call: swap $S \leftrightarrow K$, $r \to r-b$, $b \to -b$.
  • No iteration ⇒ fast, stable Greeks; the production standard for analytic Americans.
