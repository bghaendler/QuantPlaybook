L9: Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

Credit Events, Recovery Rates, Survival Probabilities, Hazard Rate Bootstrapping, and Proxy CDS Curve Calibration

What is a Credit Default Swap?

Dr. Jon Gregory defines a Credit Default Swap (CDS) as a bilateral derivative contract enabling market participants to transfer or trade default credit risk. - Protection Buyer: Pays a periodic premium (the spread, quoted in basis points per annum) to the seller. - Protection Seller: Receives premium and commits to pay a lump sum of $1 - \\text{RR}$ (Loss Given Default, or $\\text{LGD}$) if a Credit Event occurs.

Credit Events: Governed by ISDA rules and decided by a regional Determination Committee (10 dealers, 5 buy-side), events include: Bankruptcy, Failure to Pay, Repudiation/Moratorium, Restructuring, and Government Intervention.

Settlement: Physical vs. Cash

- Physical Settlement: Buyer delivers the defaulted bond to the seller, who pays full par notional. Historically suffered from cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) options (buyers searching for distressed, low-value bonds) and delivery squeezes (when gross CDS notional exceeds the total outstanding deliverable debt, such as during the Delphi 2005 default). - Cash Settlement: Seller pays $\\text{Notional} \\times (1 - \\text{RR})$. Most defaults are now cash-settled via ISDA Credit Event Auctions to establish the final recovery price.

Risky Discount Factors & Survival Probability

Default is modeled as a Poisson process driven by an instantaneous conditional default intensity or hazard rate $h$: - Survival Probability: The probability that the reference entity survives up to time $t$: $$S(t) = \\exp(-ht)$$ - Risky Discount Factor: Combining risk-free discounting $B(t) = \\exp(-rt)$ and survival probability $S(t)$ yields the credit-risk adjusted discount factor: $$DF_{\\text{risky}}(t) = \\exp[-(r + h)t]$$

CDS Present Value & Fair Spread Calibration

A CDS comprises two legs: - Premium Leg: Paid by the buyer while the reference entity survives: $$PV_{\\text{premium}} = s \\cdot \\int_0^T \\exp[-(r+h)t] \\,\\mathrm{d}t = s \\cdot \\frac{1 - \\exp[-(r+h)T]}{r + h}$$ - Default Leg: Paid by the seller at default: $$PV_{\\text{default}} = (1 - \\text{RR}) \\cdot h \\cdot \\frac{1 - \\exp[-(r+h)T]}{r + h}$$ Equating the two legs yields the fair CDS spread formula: $$s_{\\text{CDS}} = (1 - \\text{RR}) \\cdot h \\implies h = \\frac{s_{\\text{CDS}}}{1 - \\text{RR}}$$

Proxy CDS Curve Modeling for Illiquid Names

Under Basel's Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB-CVA) and Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) capital frameworks, banks must hold capital against counterparty spread risks. For illiquid counterparties lacking traded CDS curves, banks are required to approximate spreads using Proxy CDS Curves.

The Cross-Sectional Log-Linear Regression Model

To estimate credit default swaps, banks perform a regression on observable liquid names in log-space (to ensure spreads are strictly positive): $$\\log\\left(s_i^{\\text{proxy}}\\right) = \\beta_{\\text{glob}} + \\beta_{\\text{rating}(i)} + \\beta_{\\text{region}(i)} + \\beta_{\\text{sector}(i)}$$ Where: - Rating: AAA, AA, A, BBB, BB, B, CCC - Region: Europe, North America, Japan, LatAm - Sector: Financials, Energy, Healthcare, Tech Regulators explicitly require these broad category attributes rather than granular metrics to avoid overfitting sparse interbank data.

Counterparty Wrong-Way Risk (WWR)

Purchasing a CDS creates counterparty credit risk to the protection seller. Wrong-Way Risk (WWR) occurs when there is a strong positive correlation between the default probability of the reference entity and the default probability of the protection seller itself (e.g. buying corporate bond protection from a financial bank).

If the default correlation $\\rho_{12}$ approaches $1.0$, the present value of the protection leg collapses because the counterparty is highly likely to default at the same moment they owe the payout.

The Bivariate Gaussian Copula

To model joint default probabilities $p_{12}$ given marginal default probabilities $p_1$ (seller) and $p_2$ (reference): $$p_{12} = \\Phi_2\\left( \\Phi^{-1}(p_1), \\Phi^{-1}(p_2); \\lambda_{12} \\right)$$ Where $\\lambda_{12}$ is the asset correlation calibrated via equity markets, and $\\Phi_2$ represents the bivariate normal cumulative distribution function.

Sovereign Defaults: The Ukraine Moratorium

Case Background: During the 2022 wartime crisis, Ukraine suspended debt interest payments. While a sovereign suspension may not be a formal bankruptcy, the ISDA regional Determination Committee ruled it a Repudiation/Moratorium Credit Event, triggering full CDS payouts.

Pricing Impact: Sovereign spreads often spike to extreme ranges (exceeding 10,000 basis points). At this stage, quoting standard annual spreads becomes meaningless; instead, contracts trade on an upfront premium basis due to extreme annuity default risk (the risk that premium streams stop instantly).

Credit Suisse vs. UBS Government Intervention

Case Background: In 2023, the Swiss government forced the acquisition of Credit Suisse by UBS, writing down Additional Tier 1 (AT1) bonds to zero. However, existing CDS contracts referenced Tier 2 subordinated debt, meaning AT1 bondholders lost everything without being able to trigger a CDS protection payout.

Regulatory Reform: This structural mismatch prompted the addition of Government Intervention to credit event definitions to bridge the gap between bank restructuring write-downs and derivative protection triggers.
