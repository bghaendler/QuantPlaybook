Complex Analysis for Quants

Cauchy's Integral Formula with Singularity Splitting

Boundary values determine interior values — the formula, its derivative ladder, and the split-the-singularity technique that proves it and powers multi-pole integrals.

1. The Formula

For $f$ holomorphic on and inside a simple closed contour $\gamma$, and $z_0$ inside:

$$f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}\,dz.$$

Proof by deformation + splitting: shrink $\gamma$ to a tiny circle around $z_0$ (Part 10), then split the integrand:

$$\oint \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}dz = \oint\frac{f(z_0)}{z - z_0}dz + \oint\frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0}dz = f(z_0)\cdot 2\pi i + 0,$$

the second piece vanishing by ML (the ratio is bounded by continuity, the circle's length → 0). Splitting off the singular part and bounding the remainder is the move to internalize — it reappears in every residue computation.

2. The Derivative Ladder

Differentiating under the integral sign:

$$f^{(n)}(z_0) = \frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{(z - z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz.$$

Holomorphic once ⇒ all derivatives exist (the Part 05 promise, now proved) — and they're computable from boundary data. With ML on a circle of radius $r$: Cauchy's estimates $|f^{(n)}(z_0)| \le n!\,M_r/r^n$, whence Liouville's theorem (bounded entire ⇒ constant) and the fundamental theorem of algebra in three lines.

3. Multiple Singularities: Split the Contour

For an integrand with several enclosed singular points (e.g. $\frac{g(z)}{(z-a)(z-b)}$), partial fractions split the singularity:

$$\frac{1}{(z-a)(z-b)} = \frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{z-a} - \frac{1}{z-b}\right),$$

and each term gets its own shrunken circle — the integral is the sum of contributions, each evaluated by the formula. Worked: $\oint_{|z|=3}\frac{e^z}{z(z-2)}dz$. Split: $\frac{1}{z(z-2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{z-2} - \frac{1}{z}\right)$, so the integral is $\frac{2\pi i}{2}(e^2 - e^0) = \pi i(e^2 - 1)$. This additivity over singularities is the residue theorem (Part 15) in all but name.

4. Quant Readings of the Formula

  • Coefficient extraction: $a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}dz$ pulls Taylor coefficients off a contour — for probability generating functions this reads off $\mathbb{P}(N = n)$ numerically (evaluate $f$ on a circle, FFT the samples): the standard method for compound-Poisson loss distributions.
  • Mean value: on a centered circle the formula says $f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}f(z_0 + re^{i\theta})d\theta$ — Part 07's harmonic mean-value property, inherited from holomorphy.
  • Numerical differentiation without subtraction error: $f'(z_0)$ via the contour formula (or its tiny-imaginary-step cousin $\mathrm{Im}\,f(x + ih)/h$) computes Greeks of analytic pricers to machine precision — no finite-difference cancellation. The "complex-step derivative" trick used in AAD validation is this formula in disguise.

Key Takeaways

  • $f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f}{z - z_0}dz$: interior values are boundary averages; proof = deform + split + ML.
  • The derivative ladder gives all $f^{(n)}$ from the boundary, Cauchy's estimates, and Liouville.
  • Partial fractions + one-circle-per-singularity = additivity that becomes the residue theorem.
  • Practical spin-offs: PGF coefficient extraction by FFT, and complex-step derivatives for noise-free Greeks.
