L13: Cointegration for Trading

Stationary Spreads, the Engle-Granger Two-Step Procedure, Vector Error Correction Models (VECM), and the Johansen Rank Test

Integrated Time Series & Unit Roots

Dr. Richard Diamond introduces the vital distinction between price changes and price levels: - Price Changes ($\Delta P_t$): Are typically stationary processes, meaning their mean and variance are constant over time. They are integrated of order zero, written $I(0)$. - Price Levels ($P_t$): Are non-stationary random walks, accumulating random shocks over time. They require differencing once to become stationary and are integrated of order one, written $I(1)$.

Regressing one non-stationary series directly on another violates standard regression assumptions, causing the Spurious Regression Hazard where $R^2$ values are artificially close to $1.0$ even with zero causal relationship.

The Cointegration Breakthrough

If there exists a linear combination of two $I(1)$ price series $P^A_t$ and $P^B_t$ that is stationary $I(0)$ : $$\text{Spread}_t = P^A_t - \beta P^B_t - \text{const} \sim I(0)$$ then the series are cointegrated. This means they share a single underlying Brownian motion driver. Any random divergence is temporary, providing a robust foundation for Statistical Arbitrage and Pairs Trading.

The Engle-Granger Two-Step Estimating Procedure

Developed by Robert Engle and Clive Granger (who won the Nobel Prize in Economics), this simple procedure is the classical test for cointegration:

Step 1: Naive Cointegrating Regression

Regress Asset A levels on Asset B levels to extract the fitted residual $\hat{e}_t$: $$P^A_t = \text{const} + \beta P^B_t + e_t \implies \hat{e}_t = P^A_t - \hat{\beta} P^B_t - \text{const}$$ We test this residual $\hat{e}_t$ for stationarity using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) unit root test. If we reject the null hypothesis of a unit root, the residual is stationary ($I(0)$), confirming cointegration.

Step 2: Dynamic Error Correction Model (ECM)

Plug the lagged residual $\hat{e}_{t-1}$ (the cointegrating spread) into the dynamic error correction equation: $$\Delta P^A_t = \phi \Delta P^B_t - (1-\alpha)\hat{e}_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$$ The adjustment coefficient $-(1-\alpha)$ represents the speed of mean reversion. If the spread is wide today, a corrective change pulls the prices back toward equilibrium on the next step.

Vector Error Correction Models (VECM) & Johansen's Method

While Engle-Granger is intuitive, it suffers from several limitations: it forces a choice of dependent variable (A vs B) and cannot handle multiple cointegrating vectors. The Johansen Procedure resolves this using a unified Vector Autoregressive (VAR) framework estimated on differences: $$\Delta \mathbf{Y}_t = \boldsymbol{\Pi} \mathbf{Y}_{t-1} + \sum_{k=1}^{p-1} \boldsymbol{\Gamma}_k \Delta \mathbf{Y}_{t-k} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t$$

The Reduced Rank Decomposition: $\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \boldsymbol{\alpha}\boldsymbol{\beta}'$

The matrix $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ captures the long-run cointegrating relationships. If the variables are cointegrated, $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ has a reduced rank $r < n$, allowing us to decompose it: $$\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \boldsymbol{\alpha}\boldsymbol{\beta}'$$ Where: - $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the $n \times r$ matrix containing the cointegrating vectors (determining the stationary portfolio spreads). - $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ is the $n \times r$ matrix of adjustment speeds (determining how quickly each variable reverts to equilibrium).

Case Study A: Marriott vs. IAG Stock Proxy

During acquisition talks prior to 2017, stock swap agreements made International Airlines Group (IAG) a trading proxy for Marriott. Because of this structural connection, the two stocks co-moved tightly: $$\text{Spread}_t = \text{Price}^{\text{Marriott}}_t - \beta \text{Price}^{\text{IAG}}_t - \text{const}$$ When normalized, the cointegrating residual is highly stationary and mean-reverting, generating frequent statistical arbitrage entry and exit signals.

Case Study B: 10-Year vs. 25-Year Gilt Spot Rates

Dr. Richard Diamond analyzes the cointegration of GBP spot interest rates. The cointegrating regression yields: $$\\hat{r}_{10Y} = 0.159 + 0.77 r_{25Y} + \\hat{e}_t$$ The two-way Vector Error Correction estimation gives asymmetric adjustment coefficients: $$\\Delta r_{10Y} = -0.058 e_{t-1} + \\dots \\qquad \\Delta r_{25Y} = -0.027 e_{t-1} + \\dots$$ *Interpretation:* The long end of the curve ($25Y$) is relatively unanchored (behaving like a "tail free from the dock"), whereas the shorter tenor ($10Y$) adjusts significantly faster to correct yield spread imbalances.

Exercise 1: Dynamic ECM Equivalence Proof

Question: Show through algebraic substitution that a two-lag autoregressive model $y_t = \\alpha y_{t-1} + \\beta_0 + \\beta_1 x_t + \\beta_2 x_{t-1} + \\epsilon_t$ is mathematically equivalent to the error correction formulation $\\Delta y_t = \\beta_1 \\Delta x_t - (1-\\alpha)(y_{t-1} - a - b x_{t-1}) + \\epsilon_t$.

Derivation:
Subtract $y_{t-1}$ from both sides of the AR equation: $$y_t - y_{t-1} = (\\alpha - 1)y_{t-1} + \\beta_0 + \\beta_1 x_t + \\beta_2 x_{t-1} + \\epsilon_t$$ $$\\Delta y_t = -(1-\\alpha)y_{t-1} + \\beta_0 + \\beta_1 x_t - \\beta_1 x_{t-1} + \\beta_1 x_{t-1} + \\beta_2 x_{t-1} + \\epsilon_t$$ $$\\Delta y_t = \\beta_1 \\Delta x_t - (1-\\alpha)y_{t-1} + \\beta_0 + (\\beta_1 + \\beta_2)x_{t-1} + \\epsilon_t$$ Factor out the adjustment speed $-(1-\\alpha)$: $$\\Delta y_t = \\beta_1 \\Delta x_t - (1-\\alpha)\\left[ y_{t-1} - \\frac{\\beta_0}{1-\\alpha} - \\frac{\\beta_1 + \\beta_2}{1-\\alpha} x_{t-1} \\right] + \\epsilon_t$$ This yields: $$\\Delta y_t = \\beta_1 \\Delta x_t - (1-\\alpha)(y_{t-1} - a_e - b_e x_{t-1}) + \\epsilon_t \\qquad \\text{where } a_e = \\frac{\\beta_0}{1-\\alpha}, \\; b_e = \\frac{\\beta_1 + \\beta_2}{1-\\alpha}$$

Exercise 2: Half-Life of Reversion

Question: Given a fitted Ornstein-Uhlenbeck spread model $\\mathrm{d}X_t = -\\theta(X_t - \\mu)\\mathrm{d}t + \\sigma \\mathrm{d}W_t$, derive the half-life of mean reversion $\\tau$ (the average time for a shock to decrease by half).

Solution:
The expectation of the OU process starting from $X_0$ is: $$\\mathrm{E}[X_t] - \\mu = (X_0 - \\mu) e^{-\\theta t}$$ To find the half-life $\\tau$, we set the deviation at $\\tau$ to be half of the initial deviation: $$\\mathrm{E}[X_{\\tau}] - \\mu = \\frac{1}{2}(X_0 - \\mu) \\implies (X_0 - \\mu) e^{-\\theta \\tau} = \\frac{1}{2}(X_0 - \\mu)$$ $$e^{-\\theta \\tau} = \\frac{1}{2} \\implies -\\theta \\tau = \\ln\\left(\\frac{1}{2}\\right) = -\\ln(2)$$ $$\\tau = \\frac{\\ln(2)}{\\theta}$$ *Trading Metric:* A small $\\theta$ implies a long half-life (e.g. 6 months), which is highly operationally difficult to trade due to capital lockup and carry risk.
