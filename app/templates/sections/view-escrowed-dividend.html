Escrowed Dividend Model

Pricing options on stocks with known cash dividends: strip the dividends out, price on what remains — and understand the volatility distortion this convention creates.

1. The Problem with Cash Dividends

Black-Scholes wants a lognormal underlying, but a stock that drops by a fixed cash amount $D$ on the ex-dividend date is not lognormal: a proportional process cannot have an additive jump. Continuous yield $q$ (Merton) is fine for indices, but single stocks pay discrete, discretely-timed cash amounts. Some convention is needed, and the industry's oldest is the escrowed dividend model.

2. The Escrowed Construction

Split the stock into a riskless escrow holding the present value of dividends paid before option expiry, and a risky remainder:

$$S_t = \underbrace{\sum_{t_i \le T} D_i e^{-r(t_i - t)}}_{\text{escrow}} + \tilde{S}_t, \qquad \tilde{S}_0 = S_0 - \sum_{t_i \le T} D_i e^{-r t_i}.$$

Assume the net price $\tilde{S}$ follows GBM with volatility $\tilde\sigma$ and price European options by plain Black-Scholes on $\tilde{S}_0$:

$$c = \tilde{S}_0\, N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2), \qquad d_1 = \frac{\ln(\tilde{S}_0/K) + (r + \tilde\sigma^2/2)T}{\tilde\sigma\sqrt{T}}.$$

At expiry $\tilde{S}_T = S_T$ (all pre-expiry dividends have been paid), so the payoff is correct; the model simply chooses which part of the stock is random.

3. The Volatility Distortion

Here is the catch: market volatility is observed on the full price $S$, but the model applies $\tilde\sigma$ to the smaller base $\tilde{S}$. Using the same number for both understates the dollar variance of the stock — increasingly so for long maturities with many dividends. The naive fix scales volatility:

$$\tilde\sigma \approx \sigma \cdot \frac{S_0}{\tilde{S}_0},$$

but this overshoots for dividends paid late in the option's life. Two standard refinements:

  • Haug-Haug-Lewis: time-weight each dividend's contribution to effective variance — dividends paid early reduce the risky base for almost the whole life; late dividends barely matter.
  • Bos-Vandermark (2002): split each dividend into "near" and "far" parts ($D_i \frac{T - t_i}{T}$ adjusts spot, $D_i \frac{t_i}{T}$ adjusts strike) — a simple rule reproducing accurate prices to within basis points for typical schedules.

4. Alternatives Landscape

ConventionDynamicsPros / Cons
Escrowed (spot-adjusted)GBM on $S - PV(D)$Simple, closed-form; vol distortion, strike-dependent inconsistency
Forward/yield (Merton $q$)GBM with continuous yieldClean for indices; wrong timing for single stocks
Piecewise GBM with jumps$S$ drops by $D_i$ at $t_i$"True" model; no closed form — trees/FDM with node shifts
Proportional dividends$S$ drops by $\delta_i S$Preserves lognormality exactly; misstates fixed cash amounts

Different conventions give different implied vols from the same option price — a real interoperability hazard when desks exchange vol marks. Long-dated single-stock books (and dividend derivatives desks) often blend: cash dividends for near years, proportional thereafter.

5. Interaction with Early Exercise

The escrowed model is the substrate of Roll-Geske-Whaley: its lognormal net-of-dividend price is exactly what makes the compound-option mathematics work. For American puts, dividends delay exercise (holding the stock collects the drop), while for calls they create the only exercise dates — both effects priced on lattices built over the escrowed process with the dividend added back at node dates.

Key Takeaways

  • Escrowed model: GBM on $S_0 - PV(\text{dividends})$, plain BS thereafter — payoff-consistent and closed-form.
  • Applying full-stock vol to the reduced base understates risk; scale or use HHL / Bos-Vandermark adjustments.
  • Convention choice changes implied vols — document which dividend model your numbers assume.
  • Foundation for RGW Americans; lattices handle the general discrete-dividend case.
