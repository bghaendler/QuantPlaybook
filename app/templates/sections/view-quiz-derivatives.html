Question 1 Multiple Choice

1. Which of the following statements about the futures rate process $R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$ is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Explanation:

Interest rate futures contracts are traded on exchanges and marked-to-market daily. In the absence of arbitrage, the present value of the cash flows received by holding the contract from $t$ to $t+\Delta t$ must be 0:

$$ 0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left[ e^{-\int_t^{t+\Delta t} r(s) ds} \Delta P_{futures}(t + \Delta t) \right] $$

Approximating the discount factor over the short period $\Delta t$ by $e^{-r(t)\Delta t}$, we can factor it out of the expectation because it is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable:

$$ 0 = e^{-r(t)\Delta t} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left[ \Delta P_{futures}(t+\Delta t) \right] \implies \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left[ P_{futures}(t+\Delta t, T_0, T_1) \right] = P_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) $$

This shows that the futures price process $P_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$ is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

Since the futures rate is linearly related to the futures price by $P_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) = 100 \times (1 - R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1))$, the futures rate process $R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$ must also be a martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

Correct Answer: It is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

Question 2 Fill-in-the-Blank

2. Consider the Vasicek short rate model with the parameters from the last slide of the session: $\kappa = 0.86$, $\theta = 0.08$, $\sigma = 0.01$, $r(0) = 0.06$. Compute the convexity adjustment $R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) - F(t, T_0, T_1)$ for $T_1 - T_0 = \frac{1}{4}$ and time to maturity $T_0 - t = 1$. Express your answer in basis points and round to 2 decimal places.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

In the Gaussian Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) framework, the convexity adjustment $\gamma(t, T_0, T_1)$ can be calculated in two ways depending on whether you use the exact formula or the common approximation:

Approach 1: Exact HJM Convexity Adjustment (Recommended)

The exact double-integral formula in HJM incorporates the first inner integral limit up to $T_1$:

$$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \left( \exp \left( \int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_1} \sigma(s, u) du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \right)^\top ds \right) - 1 \right) $$

Plugging in the Vasicek volatility $\sigma(s, T) = \sigma e^{-\kappa (T - s)}$ yields the analytical closed-form exponent:

$$ \text{Exponent}_{\text{exact}} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\kappa^3} (1 - e^{-\kappa \delta}) \left[ (1 - e^{-\kappa (T_0 - t)}) - \frac{1}{2} e^{-\kappa\delta} (1 - e^{-2\kappa(T_0 - t)}) \right] $$

Using the given parameters:

  • $\kappa = 0.86$, $\theta = 0.08$, $\sigma = 0.01$, $r(t) = 0.06$
  • $\delta = T_1 - T_0 = 0.25$, $T_0 - t = 1.0$

We calculate the zero-coupon bond prices using the Vasicek affine formulas:

$$ B(\tau) = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa \tau}}{\kappa}, \quad A(\tau) = \left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(B(\tau) - \tau) - \frac{\sigma^2}{4\kappa}B(\tau)^2 $$

$$ P(t, T_0) \approx 0.93559, \quad P(t, T_1) \approx 0.91882 \implies \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \approx 1.01825 $$

Evaluating the exact exponent:

$$ \text{Exponent}_{\text{exact}} \approx 7.475 \times 10^{-6} $$

$$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) \approx \frac{1}{0.25} \times 1.01825 \times \left( e^{7.475 \times 10^{-6}} - 1 \right) \approx 3.045 \times 10^{-5} \text{ (or } \mathbf{0.30 \text{ bps}} \text{)} $$

Approach 2: Common Approximation (Inner Limit $T_0$)

If the first inner integral limit is approximated up to $T_0$ instead of $T_1$:

$$ \gamma_{\text{approx}}(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \left( \exp \left( \int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_0} \sigma(s, u) du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \right)^\top ds \right) - 1 \right) $$

This yields the simplified analytical exponent:

$$ \text{Exponent}_{\text{approx}} = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^3} (1 - e^{-\kappa \delta})(1 - e^{-\kappa(T_0 - t)})^2 $$

Evaluating this approximate exponent with our parameters:

$$ \text{Exponent}_{\text{approx}} \approx 5.060 \times 10^{-6} $$

$$ \gamma_{\text{approx}}(t, T_0, T_1) \approx \frac{1}{0.25} \times 1.01825 \times \left( e^{5.060 \times 10^{-6}} - 1 \right) \approx 2.061 \times 10^{-5} \text{ (or } \mathbf{0.21 \text{ bps}} \text{)} $$

Accepted Answers: 0.30 or 0.21

Question 3 Multiple Select

3. Consider a Gaussian HJM model with nonnegative forward rate volatility function $\sigma(t, T) \ge 0$. Which of the following statements are true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Explanation:

In a general Gaussian Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) model, the convexity adjustment is given by:

$$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \left( \exp \left( \int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_0} \sigma(s, u) du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \right)^\top ds \right) - 1 \right) $$

Let's analyze the properties of this expression:

  • Nonnegativity of Convexity Adjustment: Since the forward rate volatility function is nonnegative ($\sigma(t, T) \ge 0$), we have: $$ \int_s^{T_0} \sigma(s, u) du \ge 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \ge 0 $$ Therefore, their inner product and the outer integral are also nonnegative: $$ \int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_0} \sigma(s, u) du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \right)^\top ds \ge 0 $$ This implies that the exponential term is greater than or equal to 1, and since bond prices are positive: $$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) \ge 0 \implies R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) \ge F(t, T_0, T_1) $$ Hence, Statement 1 is true.
  • Equality Conditions: The convexity adjustment $\gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = 0$ for all maturities if and only if the exponential argument is 0: $$ \int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_0} \sigma(s, u) du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, v) dv \right)^\top ds = 0 $$ Because the integrand is a product of nonnegative integrals, this holds for all $T_0, T_1$ if and only if: $$ \sigma(t, T) = 0 \quad \text{for all } t \le T $$ This shows that futures rates equal simple forward rates if and only if the volatility is identically zero (deterministic interest rates). Hence, Statement 4 is also true.

Correct Answers:
• Futures rates are larger than or equal to simple forward rates $R_{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) \ge F(t, T_0, T_1)$.
• Futures rates are equal to simple forward rates if and only if the forward rate volatility is zero.
