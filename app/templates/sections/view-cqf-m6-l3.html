Yield Curve Calibration, Ho-Lee/Hull-White Drift Calibrations, and Three-Step Empirical Data Analysis

The Calibration Dilemma

Interest rate models produce a theoretical yield curve that will generally not align perfectly with the yield curve observed in the market [PDF1: 3].

Pricing vs. Arbitrage:

  • Pricing Complex Products: If the model fails to match liquid benchmark instruments exactly, pricing exotics introduces immediate hedging and netting errors [PDF1: 4]. Exact curve fitting is required.
  • Arbitrage Hunting: If the model is structurally correct, any deviation from market yields indicates mispricings and trading opportunities [PDF1: 4].

Snapshot vs. Historical

To achieve exact curve fitting, models require infinite degrees of freedom, which is attained by making parameters time-dependent [PDF1: 7]. Snapshot Calibration: Fitting these time-dependent parameters to the market prices at one specific instant in time [PDF1: 5]. divided differences Note: Dr. Ahmad recommends numerical divided differences over fitting continuous splines for discretized market pricing calibrations [PDF1: 14].

Ho & Lee Calibration Mathematics

The Ho & Lee model features a time-dependent drift $\eta(t)$ and constant volatility $c$ [PDF1: 8]: $$\mathrm{d}r = \eta(t)\mathrm{d}t + c \, \mathrm{d}W_t$$ The ZCB analytical pricing solution is $Z(r, t; T) = e^{A(t; T) - r(T-t)}$ where [PDF1: 9]: $$A(t; T) = -\int_t^T \eta(s)(T-s) \, \mathrm{d}s + \frac{1}{6}c^2(T-t)^3$$

Extracting Drift via Leibniz's Rule

Let today be $t^*$ with spot rate $r^*$. Setting theoretical price equal to the market discount factors $Z_M(t^*; T)$ yields the integral equation: $$\int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s) \, \mathrm{d}s = -\log\left(Z_M(t^*; T)\right) - r^*(T-t^*) + \frac{1}{6}c^2(T-t^*)^3$$ Applying Leibniz's Rule for differentiation under the integral sign twice w.r.t maturity $T$: $$\text{First Derivative: } \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}T}\int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s) \, \mathrm{d}s = \int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s) \, \mathrm{d}s$$ $$\text{Second Derivative: } \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2}{\mathrm{d}T^2}\int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s) \, \mathrm{d}s = \eta^*(T)$$ This isolates the calibrated parameter $\eta^*(t)$ in terms of the initial market discount curve [PDF1: 14]: $$\eta^*(t) = c^2(t - t^*) - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\log\left(Z_M(t^*; t)\right)$$

Hull-White (Extended Vasicek) Calibration Mathematics

To combine mean reversion with exact yield curve fitting, the Hull-White model introduces a time-dependent drift $\eta(t)$ to the Vasicek framework [PDF1: 17]: $$\mathrm{d}r = \left(\eta(t) - \gamma r\right)\mathrm{d}t + c \, \mathrm{d}W_t$$ The analytical zero-coupon bond pricing solution is $Z(r, t; T) = e^{A(t; T) - r B(t; T)}$ where [PDF1: 18]: $$B(t; T) = \frac{1}{\gamma}\left( 1 - e^{-\gamma(T-t)} \right), \quad A(t; T) = -\int_t^T \eta(s)B(s; T) \, \mathrm{d}s + \frac{c^2}{2\gamma^2}\left[ T-t + \frac{2}{\gamma}e^{-\gamma(T-t)} - \frac{1}{2\gamma}e^{-2\gamma(T-t)} - \frac{3}{2\gamma} \right]$$

Derivation of Hull-White Drift $\eta^*(t)$

By equating theoretical prices to the initial market discount curve $Z_M(t^*; T)$ and differentiating twice w.r.t maturity $T$: $$\eta^*(t) = -\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\log\left( Z_M(t^*; t) \right) - \gamma \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\log\left( Z_M(t^*; t) \right) + \frac{c^2}{2\gamma}\left( 1 - e^{-2\gamma(t-t^*)} \right)$$ Piecewise Constant Calibration Case Study: In practice, because market data consists of a finite number of bonds at discrete maturities, $\eta(t)$ is assumed to be piecewise constant. This allows the parameter to be pulled outside the integrals, simplifying the recursive matrix solutions [PDF1: 20].

Step 1: Volatility Structure (Power Law)

We assume the spot rate is time-homogeneous and governed by: $\mathrm{d}r = u(r)\mathrm{d}t + \nu r^\beta \mathrm{d}W_t$ [PDF1: 29]. Taking expectations of daily changes and taking logarithms yields a linear relationship [PDF1: 36]: $$\log\left( \mathbb{E}[(\delta r)^2] \right) = 2\beta \log(r) + \log(\nu^2 \delta t)$$ Performing linear regression on US LIBOR (1985-1997) buckets yields $\beta \approx 1.13$ and $\nu \approx 0.126$. This rules out Vasicek and Ho-Lee ($\beta = 0$), indicating volatility is roughly linear in rate level.

Step 2: Steady-State Drift Structure

Direct historical drift estimation is unstable. Instead, we extract $u(r)$ by integrating the steady-state Fokker-Planck equation [PDF1: 41]: $$u(r) = \nu^2 \beta r^{2\beta - 1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\left( \log(p_\infty(r)) \right)$$ Fitting a lognormal probability density $p_\infty(r)$ (with parameters $a = 0.4$ and $\bar{r} = 0.08$) to LIBOR data solves the drift analytically [PDF1: 44]: $$u(r) = \nu^2 r^{2\beta - 1} \left( \beta - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2a^2} \log(r/\bar{r}) \right)$$ This provides strong non-linear mean reversion and mathematically prevents rates from breaching zero.

Step 3: Market Price of Risk $\lambda(r)$

Expanding the zero-coupon bond price in a Taylor series near maturity: $$Y = -\frac{\ln Z}{T - t} \sim r + \frac{1}{2}(u - \lambda w)(T - t) + \dots$$ The slope at the short end is $\text{Slope} = \frac{u - \lambda w}{2}$ [PDF1: 50]. Using the known $u(r)$ and $w(r)$ along with the yield curve slope, we isolate $\lambda(r)$. In practice, plotting $\lambda(r)$ w.r.t. rate levels yields an extremely noisy, unstable, and chaotic time series, showing the immense difficulty of identifying a stable market price of risk [PDF1: 55].

Exercise 1. Leibniz's Rule Calibration Proof

Question: Show step-by-step how Leibniz's rule is applied twice to isolate the time-dependent drift $\eta^*(t)$ under the Ho-Lee calibration equation.

Solution:
Under Leibniz's rule: $\frac{\partial}{\partial T} \int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s) \mathrm{d}s = \int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s) \mathrm{d}s$.
Differentiating the integral term again: $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial T^2} \int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s)\mathrm{d}s = \frac{\partial}{\partial T} \int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)\mathrm{d}s = \eta^*(T)$.
Differentiating the right-hand side of the Ho-Lee calibration equation: $$\frac{\partial^2}{\partial T^2}\left[ -\log(Z_M(t^*; T)) - r^*(T-t^*) + \frac{1}{6}c^2(T-t^*)^3 \right] = c^2(T-t^*) - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial T^2}\log(Z_M(t^*; T))$$ Thus, the calibrated drift parameter is isolated exactly: $$\eta^*(t) = c^2(t - t^*) - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\log\left(Z_M(t^*; t)\right)$$

Exercise 2. Piecewise Constant Calibration

Question: Explain how piecewise constant parameter assumptions facilitate calibration in the presence of discrete market data maturities.

Solution:
Because real market data consists of a finite number of bond prices at discrete maturities $T_1, T_2, \dots, T_n$, we model $\eta(t)$ as piecewise constant within each interval $[T_{j-1}, T_j]$: $\eta(t) = \eta_j$ for $t \in [T_{j-1}, T_j]$.
This allows us to pull the parameter outside the integral for each interval, converting the integral equations into a system of algebraic equations solved recursively: $$\int_{t^*}^T \eta^*(s)(T-s)\mathrm{d}s = \sum_{i=1}^j \eta_i \int_{T_{i-1}}^{T_i} (T-s)\mathrm{d}s$$ This avoids the need for continuous numerical differentiation on splines, ensuring direct calibration to market benchmarks.

Exercise 3. steady-state Fokker-Planck Drift Isolation

Question: Prove how integrating the steady-state Fokker-Planck equation once w.r.t $r$ yields the direct relationship between the SDE drift $u(r)$ and the probability density function $p_\infty(r)$.

Solution:
Under the steady-state condition $\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = 0$, the Fokker-Planck equation is: $$\frac{1}{2}\nu^2 \frac{\mathrm{d}^2}{\mathrm{d}r^2}\left( r^{2\beta} p_\infty \right) - \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\left( u(r) p_\infty \right) = 0$$ Integrating once w.r.t $r$: $$\frac{1}{2}\nu^2 \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\left( r^{2\beta} p_\infty \right) - u(r) p_\infty = \text{Const}$$ Assuming the probability density and its derivatives vanish at the boundaries ($r \to 0, \infty$), the constant is $0$. Differentiating the first term using the product rule: $$\frac{1}{2}\nu^2 \left( 2\beta r^{2\beta-1} p_\infty + r^{2\beta} \frac{\mathrm{d}p_\infty}{\mathrm{d}r} \right) - u(r) p_\infty = 0$$ Dividing by $p_\infty$ and rearranging: $$u(r) = \nu^2 \beta r^{2\beta-1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta} \frac{1}{p_\infty}\frac{\mathrm{d}p_\infty}{\mathrm{d}r} = \nu^2 \beta r^{2\beta-1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\left(\log(p_\infty(r))\right)$$
