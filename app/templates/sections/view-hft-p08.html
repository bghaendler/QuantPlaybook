High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Volatility-Based Execution Optimization

Making schedules volatility-aware: regime-conditional urgency, intraday vol forecasting for execution, and the vol-liquidity feedback that dominates stressed tapes.

1. Volatility Enters the Schedule Twice

In the Almgren-Chriss frontier (Part 07), σ appears in the timing-risk term and, empirically, inside the impact coefficients:

  • Direct: $\kappa = \sqrt{\lambda\sigma^2/\eta}$ — doubling σ raises urgency by ~√2× at fixed risk aversion: trade faster when the world is noisier.
  • Indirect: spreads widen and depth thins with σ (liquidity providers re-price adverse selection, Part 01) — so $\eta$ rises too, pushing back. The net schedule shift depends on the ratio $\sigma^2/\eta$; empirically urgency still rises in high vol, but less than the naive √ rule.
  • Alpha decay interaction: signals decay faster in high vol — execution for alpha-driven flow front-loads even harder than risk aversion alone implies.

2. Forecasting Vol at Execution Horizons

  • Baseline: intraday seasonal profile (Part 05) × daily-level forecast (EWMA/GARCH or implied) — covers most of the variance at horizons of minutes-to-hours.
  • Dynamic residual: HAR-RV style regressions on realized vol over the last 5/30/390 minutes capture intraday persistence; event dummies (scheduled macro prints) add the spikes the U-shape misses.
  • Real-time signals: trade intensity (Hawkes activity, Part 02), spread state and book-depletion rates nowcast vol shifts seconds-to-minutes ahead — the inputs adaptive algos actually consume.
  • Discipline: evaluate forecasts in deseasonalized units and at the schedule's decision frequency; a 1-day GARCH forecast is the wrong object for a 20-minute slice decision.

3. Regime-Conditional Policies

RegimeSymptomsPolicy shift
Low vol / thick bookTight spreads, deep queuesPatient: high passive ratio, harvest spread, longer horizon
Elevated vol, orderlyWider spreads, active tapeRaise urgency (κ↑), more midpoint/dark, cap passive queue time
Stressed / dislocatingQuote flickering, depth vanishing, fast marketsSchedule suspension logic: limit-price collars, participation floors/ceilings, auction deferral — pre-programmed, not improvised

Regime detection: rolling vol ratios, spread/depth z-scores, or a simple 2–3-state HMM on deseasonalized RV — what matters is hysteresis (don't flip-flop) and pre-committed actions per state.

4. The Vol-Liquidity Feedback (Why Stress Is Different)

  • Vol ↑ ⇒ makers widen/withdraw ⇒ impact ↑ ⇒ each trade moves price more ⇒ measured vol ↑ — a feedback loop; flash events (May 2010) are its extreme expression.
  • Consequence for cost models: $(\sigma, \eta, \gamma)$ calibrated in calm regimes jointly understate stressed costs — stress TCA needs regime-conditional parameters (Part 03's regime note), not scaled-up calm ones.
  • Consequence for risk: timing-risk variance in the AC objective is itself stochastic — vol-of-vol argues for conservative λ when the forecast is uncertain.
  • Circuit breakers, LULD bands and auction fallbacks are the market's own vol-conditional execution policy — algos must encode their mechanics (halt resumption auctions reprice everything).

Key Takeaways

  • σ raises optimal urgency (√ rule) but also raises impact — schedule on the ratio, and front-load more when alpha decays fast.
  • Execution-horizon vol = seasonal profile × daily forecast + HAR residual + event dummies + book-state nowcasts.
  • Codify regime policies with hysteresis: passive-heavy in calm, urgent in elevated, pre-committed collars in stress.
  • The vol-liquidity feedback makes stressed costs nonlinear — calibrate per regime and respect the market's own circuit-breaker mechanics.
