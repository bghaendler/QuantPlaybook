Radon-Nikodym & Change of Measure

The machinery that lets us re-weight probabilities — from the real world $\mathbb{P}$ to the risk-neutral world $\mathbb{Q}$ — without changing what is possible.

1. Equivalent Measures

Two probability measures $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ on the same measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ are equivalent, written $\mathbb{P} \sim \mathbb{Q}$, when they agree on what is impossible:

$$\mathbb{P}(A) = 0 \iff \mathbb{Q}(A) = 0 \quad \text{for all } A \in \mathcal{F}.$$

Changing measure re-weights the likelihood of scenarios but never creates or destroys scenarios. This is exactly the freedom needed in pricing: the risk-neutral measure must agree with the market about which events can happen, while assigning them different odds.

2. The Radon-Nikodym Theorem

If $\mathbb{Q}$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mathbb{P}$ ($\mathbb{Q} \ll \mathbb{P}$), there exists a non-negative random variable $Z$, the Radon-Nikodym derivative

$$Z = \frac{d\mathbb{Q}}{d\mathbb{P}}, \qquad \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[Z] = 1,$$

such that expectations transform by re-weighting:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[X] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[Z\,X].$$

Intuitively, $Z(\omega)$ is the factor by which scenario $\omega$ is made more or less likely under $\mathbb{Q}$. On a discrete space it is literally the ratio of probabilities $q_i / p_i$.

3. The Density Process & Conditional Expectations

In a dynamic setting with filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \le T}$, define the density process

$$Z_t = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}\!\left[\frac{d\mathbb{Q}}{d\mathbb{P}} \,\Big|\, \mathcal{F}_t\right],$$

which is a strictly positive $\mathbb{P}$-martingale with $Z_0 = 1$. Conditional expectations change measure via the abstract Bayes formula:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[X \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[Z_T X \mid \mathcal{F}_t]}{Z_t}.$$

A process $M_t$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale if and only if $Z_t M_t$ is a $\mathbb{P}$-martingale — the workhorse identity behind numeraire changes.

4. Girsanov in One Line

For Brownian motion, the canonical change of measure uses the exponential martingale (Doléans-Dade exponential):

$$Z_t = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^t \lambda_s\, dW_s - \tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^t \lambda_s^2\, ds\right),$$

under which $\widetilde{W}_t = W_t + \int_0^t \lambda_s ds$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-Brownian motion. Choosing $\lambda = (\mu - r)/\sigma$ — the market price of risk — removes the real-world drift $\mu$ and replaces it with the risk-free rate $r$:

$$\frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t \;\;\xrightarrow{\;\mathbb{P} \to \mathbb{Q}\;}\;\; \frac{dS_t}{S_t} = r\, dt + \sigma\, d\widetilde{W}_t.$$

Note the volatility $\sigma$ is untouched: a change of measure can only shift drifts, never the diffusion coefficient.

5. Worked Example: Two-State Economy

One period, two states: up with $\mathbb{P}(\text{up}) = 0.6$, down with $\mathbb{P}(\text{down}) = 0.4$. Suppose no-arbitrage pricing requires risk-neutral probabilities $q_u = 0.45$, $q_d = 0.55$. The Radon-Nikodym derivative is state-by-state re-weighting:

$$Z(\text{up}) = \frac{0.45}{0.6} = 0.75, \qquad Z(\text{down}) = \frac{0.55}{0.4} = 1.375.$$

Check: $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[Z] = 0.6 \times 0.75 + 0.4 \times 1.375 = 1$. ✔ Bad states are over-weighted ($Z > 1$) — the mathematical expression of risk aversion embedded in market prices.

Key Takeaways

  • $d\mathbb{Q}/d\mathbb{P}$ re-weights scenario probabilities; equivalence preserves null sets.
  • $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[X] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}[ZX]$ — pricing under $\mathbb{Q}$ is weighted averaging under $\mathbb{P}$.
  • The density process $Z_t$ is a positive $\mathbb{P}$-martingale; Bayes' rule links conditional expectations across measures.
  • Girsanov supplies the explicit $Z_t$ for diffusions: drifts move, volatilities stay.
  • Risk-neutral probabilities overweight bad states — they bundle probability and risk preference into one object.
