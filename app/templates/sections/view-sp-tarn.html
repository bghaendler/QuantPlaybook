Target Redemption Note (TARN)

A note that dies when it has paid enough: coupons accumulate toward a target cap, redemption triggers on reaching it — and the path-dependent tail has a famous habit of biting.

1. Mechanics

The note pays structured coupons $c_i$ (typically inverse floaters, FX-linked, or steepeners). A running total tracks them:

$$Q_i = \sum_{j \le i} c_j, \qquad \text{redeem at par at the first } t_i \text{ with } Q_i \ge Q^{target}.$$

Variants on the trigger payment: exact (final coupon trimmed to hit the target exactly), capped (no excess), or full (pay the whole final coupon). If the target is never reached, the note runs to maturity — usually repaying par but having paid meager coupons for years. The target (e.g. 10% cumulative on a 10-year note) is the marketing hook: "high coupons until you've earned 10%, then your money back early."

2. The Embedded Bet

The investor is long the fast-redemption scenario: big early coupons → quick par redemption → high IRR. The bad state mirrors the autocallable's: coupons shrink (the linked rate moves against), the note extends to full maturity, paying nearly nothing while capital is trapped — duration explodes exactly in the adverse regime. The structure is a knock-out on cumulative coupons: each coupon payment burns down the remaining optionality, making the value strongly path-dependent (the running sum $Q$ is a state variable, like an Asian average or swing take).

3. The FX-TARF Episode

The traded-product cousin — the Target Redemption Forward (TARF) — books a strip of FX forwards at better-than-market rates that knocks out once cumulative gains hit the target, but with leveraged downside (2:1 notional on losing fixings) and no target protection on losses: gains are capped by the knock-out, losses are not. Corporate hedgers in Asia (USD/CNH 2014-15, again 2022) and exporters worldwide accumulated TARFs for the rate enhancement; when spot trended through the strikes, losses compounded on doubled notionals for the full remaining schedule. The asymmetry — knock-out on the profitable side only — is the entire economics: the "enhanced rate" is premium from selling a strip of leveraged options that survive precisely when losing.

4. Pricing & Risk

  • State variables: (market factor, cumulative coupon $Q$, time) — priced by Monte Carlo (industry default) or PDE/lattice with a $Q$-grid; LSMC if issuer-callable features coexist.
  • Models: rate TARNs need full term-structure dynamics with smile (LMM-SABR / Cheyette); FX TARFs need stochastic-local-vol FX surfaces — the knock-out reads barrier-style smile dynamics on the cumulative process.
  • Greeks: discontinuous at the target boundary (a digital on $Q$); vega and gamma concentrate just below target; delta can flip sign as the knock-out probability dominates the coupon value.
  • XVA matters: extension scenarios coincide with the counterparty being deep out-of-the-money — wrong-way credit exposure is intrinsic to TARF books.

Key Takeaways

  • TARN: structured coupons + automatic redemption when cumulative coupons hit a target.
  • The running coupon total is a genuine state variable — Asian-style path dependence with a digital boundary.
  • Good states end early (capped upside); bad states extend fully (uncapped duration/loss) — the TARF asymmetry.
  • FX TARFs with leveraged downside are the canonical corporate-hedging blow-up case study.
  • Simulation on smile-dynamic models with the $Q$-state, plus wrong-way XVA, is the pricing reality.
