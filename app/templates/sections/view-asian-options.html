Asian Options: Geometric & Arithmetic Approximations

Options on the average price: cheaper, harder to manipulate, beloved by corporates — and analytically awkward because sums of lognormals aren't lognormal.

1. Why Average?

Payoff on the average $A_T$ of fixings $S_{t_1}, \dots, S_{t_n}$: average-price call $= \max(A_T - K, 0)$ (also: average-strike variants $\max(S_T - A_T, 0)$). Motivations:

  • Cheaper: averaging shrinks variance — roughly $\sigma_A^2 \approx \sigma^2/3$ for continuous averaging over the whole life — cutting premium 35–45% vs. vanilla.
  • Manipulation-resistant: no single fixing decides the payoff (commodity benchmarks, thin underlyings, FX fixes).
  • Matches exposure: a corporate buying fuel monthly cares about the average price, not the terminal one. Most commodity options (oil swaps/Asians on Platts averages) are Asian by default.

2. Geometric Asians: The Exactly Solvable Case

The geometric average $G_T = \left(\prod_i S_{t_i}\right)^{1/n}$ of lognormals is lognormal — a sum of normals in logs. For continuous averaging under GBM, $G_T$ behaves like a lognormal asset with

$$\sigma_G = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{3}}, \qquad b_G = \frac{1}{2}\left(b - \frac{\sigma^2}{6}\right),$$

so the price is plain generalized Black-Scholes with $(\sigma_G, b_G)$. Discrete fixings: replace with the exact mean/variance of $\frac{1}{n}\sum \ln S_{t_i}$ (Kemna-Vorst 1990). Geometric Asians barely trade — their value is as a control variate and approximation anchor for the arithmetic contract.

3. Arithmetic Asians: Moment-Matching Approximations

$A_T = \frac{1}{n}\sum S_{t_i}$ has no closed-form law. The classical analytic answers match moments of $A_T$ — computable exactly under GBM — to a tractable density:

  • Turnbull-Wakeman (1991): lognormal matched to $M_1, M_2$ (optionally Edgeworth-corrected with $M_3, M_4$). With continuous averaging and carry $b \ne 0$: $$M_1 = \frac{e^{bT} - 1}{bT}S, \qquad M_2 = \frac{2e^{(2b+\sigma^2)T}}{(b+\sigma^2)(2b+\sigma^2)T^2}S^2 + \frac{2S^2}{bT^2}\left(\frac{1}{2b+\sigma^2} - \frac{e^{bT}}{b+\sigma^2}\right),$$ then $\sigma_A^2 = \frac{1}{T}\ln(M_2/M_1^2)$, $b_A = \frac{1}{T}\ln(M_1/S)$ into Black-76-style formulas.
  • Levy (1992): same idea, slightly different packaging; the FX market's habitual quick Asian.
  • Curran (1994): condition on the geometric average and integrate — among the most accurate analytic methods for discrete fixings.

Accuracy: excellent for typical parameters (σ ≤ 30%, moderate T); degrades for high vol/long maturity where $A_T$'s right tail outgrows the lognormal fit (true Asian is worth less than lognormal-matched for calls — positive skew mismatch).

4. Exact & Numerical Benchmarks

  • Geman-Yor (1993): exact Laplace transform in maturity via Bessel processes; inversion is delicate but definitive.
  • PDE in $(t, S, A)$ or the one-dimensional reduction of Rogers-Shi/Večeř — fast and accurate for continuous averaging.
  • Monte Carlo with geometric control variate: the workhorse — ρ ≈ 0.99 with the geometric twin gives 50–150× variance reduction; handles smiles, discrete fixings, in-progress averaging.
  • In-progress seasoning: once some fixings are known, the remaining option has an adjusted strike $K' = \frac{nK - \sum_{known} S_i}{\#\text{remaining}}$ and shrinking effective vol — late-life Asians converge to cash positions.

5. Risk Character

Asian vega and gamma decay as fixings pass — risk is front-loaded relative to a vanilla. Delta hedging is smoother (each fixing settles a slice), which is exactly why desks like selling them. The effective vol scale $\sigma/\sqrt{3}$ explains pricing intuition: a 1-year Asian behaves roughly like a 4-month vanilla in vol terms.

Key Takeaways

  • Averaging cuts variance (~σ²/3 continuous) → cheaper, manipulation-resistant, corporate-friendly.
  • Geometric Asians are lognormal ⇒ exact BS with $(\sigma/\sqrt{3},\ \frac{1}{2}(b - \sigma^2/6))$ — and the perfect control variate.
  • Arithmetic: Turnbull-Wakeman/Levy moment matching, Curran conditioning; MC+control as benchmark.
  • Seasoned Asians: adjust strike for known fixings; risk decays as the average locks in.
  • Approximations weaken at high vol/long maturity — check against the control-variate MC.
