High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Limit Order Books, Spreads & Liquidity Flows

The central data structure of modern markets: how the book is built, what the spread compensates, and the liquidity metrics execution algorithms consume.

1. Anatomy of the Limit Order Book (LOB)

A double auction maintained continuously: bids (buy limits) stacked below, asks (sell limits) above. Key vocabulary:

  • Best bid/ask: highest buy / lowest sell — their difference is the quoted spread $s = a - b$; the mid-price $m = (a + b)/2$ is the standard reference value.
  • Depth: visible quantity at each price level; L1 data = best quotes only, L2/L3 = full ladder / per-order detail.
  • Order types: limit (joins the book — supplies liquidity, earns the spread, risks non-execution), market (crosses the spread — demands liquidity, pays it, executes now), cancel (the most common message: cancel-to-trade ratios of 10–50× are normal in equities).
  • Priority: price first, then time (FIFO) on most equity venues; pro-rata on many futures (Eurodollars) — priority rules reshape optimal quoting entirely.

2. What the Spread Compensates

Classical decomposition of the spread earned by a liquidity provider:

  • Order-processing costs — infrastructure, exchange fees, capital.
  • Inventory risk (Stoll/Ho-Stoll) — holding unwanted positions against adverse price moves; quotes skew to shed inventory.
  • Adverse selection (Glosten-Milgrom, Kyle) — limit orders get filled precisely when the counterparty knows more; the spread is the break-even price of trading against information. Kyle's λ (price impact per unit flow) and the Glosten-Milgrom sequential model are the canonical formalizations.

Empirical spread measures: quoted ($a - b$), effective ($2|p_{trade} - m|$ — captures inside-spread executions), realized (effective minus subsequent mid move — what the maker actually kept after adverse selection).

3. Liquidity Flows and Imbalance

The book is a queueing system: limit arrivals ↔ cancellations ↔ market-order departures. The most-used state variable is order-flow imbalance:

$$\mathrm{OFI}_t = \frac{Q^{bid}_t - Q^{ask}_t}{Q^{bid}_t + Q^{ask}_t} \in [-1, 1],$$

(best-level or depth-weighted). Empirically, imbalance is among the strongest short-horizon predictors of the next mid-price move — queue depletion on one side precedes the tick. Related diagnostics: arrival intensities per level, queue position (expected time-to-fill), and the "microprice" $\,m^* = \frac{Q^{bid}a + Q^{ask}b}{Q^{bid} + Q^{ask}}$ — an imbalance-weighted fair value that leads the mid.

4. Why This Layer Matters to Every Quant

  • Execution costs are a strategy's tax: a 5 bps alpha traded through a 10 bps round-trip cost is a losing strategy — microstructure decides which signals are monetizable (the theme of Parts 02–08).
  • Microstructure noise contaminates estimates: bid-ask bounce induces negative autocorrelation in trade-price returns (Roll's model: spread $= 2\sqrt{-\mathrm{cov}(r_t, r_{t-1})}$) and biases realized volatility at high sampling frequencies — the reason RV uses 5-minute, not tick, returns.
  • The book is the ground truth of supply/demand: models in Parts 09–10 (diffusion limits, stochastic control) are built on these queue dynamics.

Key Takeaways

  • The LOB is a priority queue: price-time (or pro-rata) rules + limit/market/cancel flows define the game.
  • Spread = processing + inventory + adverse selection; realized vs. effective spread measures what makers truly earn.
  • Order-flow imbalance and the microprice are the workhorse short-horizon state variables.
  • Microstructure sets the cost floor for every strategy and the noise floor for every high-frequency estimate.
