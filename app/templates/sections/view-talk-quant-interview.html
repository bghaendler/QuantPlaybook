Mastering the Quant Finance Interview

What quant interviews actually test, the canonical question types with worked approaches, and how this curriculum maps directly to the questions you'll face.

1. The Five Question Categories

  • Probability & brainteasers: conditional probability, expectation, random walks — testing clear probabilistic thinking (MM Part 05).
  • Stochastic calculus / derivatives: Itô, Black-Scholes derivation, Greeks, measure changes (MM Parts 06–13, the pricing engine).
  • Coding: implement a pricer/simulation, manipulate data, algorithmic problems (the Python lab).
  • Statistics / ML: regression, distributions, overfitting, time series (ARM, the ML masterclass).
  • Markets & behavioral fit: how markets work, why you, how you think about risk — and your projects (the portfolio workshop).

2. Canonical Questions & Approaches

  • "Derive Black-Scholes." Two routes (know both): the PDE/hedging argument (delta-hedge, drift cancels, MM Part 13) and the risk-neutral expectation (lognormal integral / completing the square, MM Part 04). Mention put-call parity as a check.
  • "Compute $\mathbb{E}[W_t^4]$ / show $W_t^2 - t$ is a martingale." Itô on $W^2$ (MM Part 10): $d(W^2) = 2W\,dW + dt$ — the $dW$ term is a martingale, leaving the drift.
  • "What's the value of $\int_0^t W_s\,dW_s$?" $= \tfrac12(W_t^2 - t)$ by Itô — the classic that separates Itô from ordinary calculus.
  • "Price a digital / why does delta-hedging it fail near expiry?" Discontinuous payoff ⇒ exploding gamma (the kink discussion, AD Part 07) — smooth with a call spread.
  • "Expected number of coin flips to get HH vs HT?" Markov-chain / first-step analysis (HH: 6, HT: 4) — a probability-reasoning staple.
  • "How would you estimate volatility?" Realized vs. implied, EWMA/GARCH, the bias-variance window trade (the historical-vol and ARM material).

3. How to Answer (Not Just What)

  • Think aloud: interviewers grade your process — state assumptions, set up the framework, then compute. Silence followed by a wrong number is worse than a clear wrong path.
  • Sanity-check everything: dimensions, limiting cases (σ→0, T→0), sign and magnitude — the validation reflex the whole curriculum drills.
  • Know your projects cold: every line of your CV is fair game; be able to defend modeling choices and state limitations (portfolio-workshop).
  • It's fine to not know — recover well: reason from fundamentals, propose how you'd find out; intellectual honesty beats bluffing (the auditor's virtue).

4. Preparation Plan

  • Fundamentals first: the math-methods and pricing core (this curriculum) — interviews probe depth, not memorized formulas.
  • Practice out loud with a peer; do timed coding (implement a Monte Carlo pricer from scratch in 20 minutes).
  • Standard references: Wilmott, Hull, Joshi's Quant Job Interview Questions and Answers, Zhou's Green Book for brainteasers.
  • Tailor to the seat: HFT roles probe microstructure and low-latency coding; risk roles probe VaR/backtesting; desk roles probe Greeks and hedging — match prep to target (the "day in the life" talks map the seats).
  • The meta-lesson: interviews reward clear thinking validated against sanity checks — exactly the discipline this entire curriculum builds.

Key Takeaways

  • Five categories: probability/brainteasers, stochastic calculus, coding, stats/ML, markets & fit.
  • Master the canonical set (derive BS two ways, Itô on $W^2$, $\int W\,dW$, digital hedging, coin-flip expectations).
  • Think aloud, sanity-check limiting cases, know your projects cold, recover honestly from gaps.
  • Prepare fundamentals over formulas, practice out loud and timed, and tailor to the target seat — the curriculum's validation discipline is the interview skill.
