Complex Analysis for Quants

Taylor vs. Laurent Expansions

Series with negative powers: expanding around singularities, annuli of convergence, and the principal part that carries all the integration information.

1. Two Series, Two Geometries

Taylor (holomorphic at $z_0$): $f(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} a_n(z - z_0)^n$ on a disk $|z - z_0| < R$, $R$ = distance to nearest singularity.

Laurent (holomorphic on an annulus $r < |z - z_0| < R$):

$$f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n(z - z_0)^n, \qquad a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{(z - z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz.$$

Negative powers (the principal part) encode behavior at the singularity; non-negative powers (the analytic part) the smooth remainder. The coefficient $a_{-1}$ — the residue — is the only one that survives loop integration (Part 09's $\oint z^n dz$), which is why Laurent series are the bridge to the residue theorem.

2. The Same Function, Different Annuli

$f(z) = \dfrac{1}{(z-1)(z-2)} = \dfrac{1}{z-2} - \dfrac{1}{z-1}$ has three distinct expansions around $z_0 = 0$:

  • $|z| < 1$ (Taylor): $\;f = \sum_{n\ge0}\left(1 - 2^{-n-1}\right)z^n$ — geometric series both ways "outward".
  • $1 < |z| < 2$ (Laurent): $\;f = -\sum_{n\ge1} z^{-n} - \sum_{n\ge0}\frac{z^n}{2^{n+1}}$ — expand $\frac{1}{z-1}$ in powers of $1/z$, $\frac{1}{z-2}$ in powers of $z$.
  • $|z| > 2$: $\;f = \sum_{n\ge1}\frac{2^{n-1} - 1}{z^n}$ — both factors in powers of $1/z$.

One function, three series, three regions — always state the annulus. The mechanical skill: rewrite each factor as $\frac{1}{a}\cdot\frac{1}{1 - z/a}$ (for $|z| < |a|$) or $\frac{1}{z}\cdot\frac{1}{1 - a/z}$ (for $|z| > |a|$) and use geometric series.

3. Reading Off Structure

  • Finite principal part (terminates at $a_{-m}$): pole of order $m$ — e.g. $\frac{\cos z}{z^2} = \frac{1}{z^2} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{z^2}{24} - \dots$ (double pole, residue 0!).
  • Infinite principal part: essential singularity — $e^{1/z} = 1 + \frac{1}{z} + \frac{1}{2!z^2} + \dots$ (residue 1).
  • No principal part: removable — $\frac{\sin z}{z} = 1 - \frac{z^2}{6} + \dots$ extends holomorphically.

This classification (formalized next part) is read directly off the series — and the residue often falls out without any limit-taking: divide/multiply known Taylor series and collect the $z^{-1}$ coefficient.

4. Quant Sightings of Laurent Structure

  • Generating functions on annuli: two-sided sequences (signal autocovariances $\gamma_k$, $k \in \mathbb{Z}$) have two-sided transforms $\sum \gamma_k z^k$ converging on an annulus around $|z| = 1$ — the Z-transform of time-series analysis is a Laurent series; causal/invertible factorizations (Wold, spectral factorization) are statements about which annulus.
  • Partial fractions for pricing transforms: rational approximations of characteristic exponents (e.g. CIR bond ODE solutions) are expanded around their poles to locate damping limits.
  • Asymptotic wings: the $|z| > 2$-type "expansion at infinity" is how one extracts large-strike behavior from transforms — the Lee moment formula's machinery in miniature.

Key Takeaways

  • Laurent = Taylor + negative powers, convergent on annuli; always declare which annulus.
  • Principal part = the singularity's fingerprint; $a_{-1}$ (the residue) is all that loop integrals see.
  • Build expansions from geometric series of partial fractions; read pole order/essentialness off the series.
  • Z-transforms of time series are Laurent series — annulus choices = causality/invertibility of ARMA models.
