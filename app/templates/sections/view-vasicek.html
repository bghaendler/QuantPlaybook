2. The Vasicek Model

The Vasicek model specifies the $\mathbb{Q}$-dynamics of the short rate as an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process:

$$ dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma dW^*(t) $$

  • $\theta$: mean-reversion level
  • $\kappa$: mean-reversion speed
  • $\sigma$: volatility

Using the variation of constants formula, the explicit solution shows it is a Gaussian process:

$$ r(t) = e^{-\kappa t}r(0) + \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\kappa\theta ds + \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\sigma dW^*(s) $$

The limiting distribution for $t \to \infty$ is normal with mean $\theta$ and variance $\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Interest rates don't grow forever; they tend to stay around a "normal" level. The Vasicek model mathematically captures this "rubber band" effect.

  • Mean Reversion: Think of $\theta$ as the thermostat setting in a room, and $\kappa$ as how strongly the AC kicks in to push the temperature back to $\theta$.
  • Gaussian Flaw: Because the noise is normally distributed, the rate can technically become negative (a flaw in older models, though sometimes realistic today!).
  • Example: If current rates are at 6% but the long-term average ($\theta$) is 8%, the "drift" will constantly pull the rate upwards toward 8%, while the noise ($\sigma$) makes it zig-zag along the way.

Practice Exercise: The Gaussian Flaw

Let the short rate follow a Vasicek process with parameters $\kappa = 0.5$, $\theta = 0.04$ (4%), and $\sigma = 0.03$. Assuming $t \to \infty$ so the distribution reaches its limiting state, what is the probability that the short rate is negative?

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution
  1. The limiting distribution of $r(t)$ in the Vasicek model is Normal: $r(\infty) \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa})$.
  2. Calculate the variance: $\frac{0.03^2}{2 \times 0.5} = \frac{0.0009}{1} = 0.0009$.
  3. Calculate the standard deviation: $\sqrt{0.0009} = 0.03$ (3%).
  4. We want to find $P(r < 0)$. Since $r(\infty) \sim \mathcal{N}(0.04, 0.03^2)$, we standardize to find the Z-score:
    $$ Z = \frac{0 - 0.04}{0.03} = -1.333 $$
  5. Using a standard normal table, $P(Z < -1.33) \approx 0.0918$.

Answer: There is a 9.18% probability that the short rate is negative in the long run. This demonstrates the "Gaussian Flaw" of the Vasicek model, which allows for negative interest rates.
