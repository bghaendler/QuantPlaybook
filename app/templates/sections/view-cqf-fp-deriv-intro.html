Derivatives: Introduction

An Analogy to Bets, Long vs. Short Positions, and Exchange vs. OTC Venues.

What are Derivatives? The Racehorse Analogy

Derivatives are financial instruments that derive their value from an underlying asset (equities, bonds, commodities, currencies). Neil Graham illustrates this with a simple sports betting analogy:

🐎

The Sports Bet

You like the look of a racehorse and bet on its performance. Instead of buying the physical horse, you place a bet with a bookmaker. Neither the horse nor its owner have anything to do with your bet, and there is no limit to the number of bets that can be placed on a single race.

📈

The Derivative Contract

You like the look of Vodafone stock and expect it to rise. Instead of buying the physical shares, you enter into a Future, Option, or Swap contract. The derivative is an agreement solely between you and your counterparty. The corporation has nothing to do with it, and there is no limit to the number of contracts that can be written.

Market Structures: Over-the-Counter vs. Exchange-Traded

Derivatives are transacted in two fundamentally distinct environments. Their margin, settlement, and contract flexibility models are mapped below:

☁️ Over-the-Counter (OTC) Markets

  • Flexible Terms: Parameters (notionals, dates, strikes) are entirely bespoke and privately negotiated.
  • ISDA Documentation: Governed globally by standardized International Swaps and Derivatives Association (ISDA) templates.
  • Bilateral Counterparty Risk: Subject to default risks of the other party. The less creditworthy party commonly posts daily collateral (Credit Support Annex - CSA) to offset this exposure.

🏛️ Exchange-Traded Derivatives (ETD)

  • Rigid Standardization: Terms like sizing, expiries, and ticks are locked into exchange rules (e.g., standard lot contracts).
  • Zero Bilateral Credit Risk: A Central Clearinghouse sits between buyers and sellers, becoming the seller to every buyer and buyer to every seller.
  • Standardized Margin: Both parties must post initial collateral and daily variation margin with the clearinghouse to absorb price swings.
