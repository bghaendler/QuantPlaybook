Advanced Finite Difference Systems, Crank-Nicolson, Thomas LU Factorization, and Successive Over-Relaxation

The Fully Implicit Finite Difference Method

Unlike the explicit scheme (where three known option prices at time step $k$ resolve to one price at step $k+1$), the Fully Implicit Method reverses the relationship: a single known option price at step $k$ relates to three unknown option prices at the next backward step $k+1$.

This is achieved by evaluating all spatial derivative approximations (Delta and Gamma) at the advanced, unknown time step $k+1$ rather than the current time step $k$. This produces a linear system of simultaneous equations.

Unconditional Stability

The fully implicit scheme is accurate to first order in time and second order in space: $\mathcal{O}(\delta t, \delta S^2)$. Crucially, the scheme is unconditionally stable, meaning that error perturbations decay exponentially regardless of the grid spacing step sizes $\delta t$ and $\delta S$. No Von Neumann stability constraints exist!

Implicit Matrix Formulation

Collecting the coefficients of the unknown option prices at step $k+1$ results in a linear system of equations: $$a_i^{k+1} V_{i-1}^{k+1} + (1 + B_i^{k+1}) V_i^{k+1} + C_i^{k+1} V_{i+1}^{k+1} = V_i^k$$

Tridiagonal Coefficient Matrix

The simultaneous system forms a tridiagonal matrix $\mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}^{k+1} = \mathbf{v}^k$: $$A = \begin{pmatrix} b_0 & c_0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ a_1 & b_1 & c_1 & & \vdots \\ 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 \\ \vdots & & a_{n-1} & b_{n-1} & c_{n-1} \\ 0 & \dots & 0 & a_n & b_n \end{pmatrix}$$ Because non-zero coefficients exist only on the diagonal, super-diagonal, and sub-diagonal, we can solve this system extremely quickly without performing direct matrix inversions.

The Crank-Nicolson Method

Developed by John Crank and Phyllis Nicolson, this method takes an equally weighted average of the explicit and fully implicit schemes, effectively evaluating spatial derivatives at the half time step $k + 1/2$. It uses three known option prices at step $k$ and three unknown prices at step $k+1$ simultaneously:

Crank-Nicolson Discrete Formulation

$$\frac{V_i^k - V_i^{k+1}}{\delta t} + \frac{a_i^{k+1}}{2} \Delta^2 V_i^{k+1} + \frac{a_i^k}{2} \Delta^2 V_i^k + \frac{b_i^{k+1}}{2} \Delta V_i^{k+1} + \frac{b_i^k}{2} \Delta V_i^k + \frac{c_i^{k+1}}{2} V_i^{k+1} + \frac{c_i^k}{2} V_i^k = 0$$ where $\Delta^2 V_i$ is the second-order central difference and $\Delta V_i$ is the first-order spatial difference.

Second-Order Accuracy

Because of its symmetric temporal centering, Crank-Nicolson achieves second-order accuracy in both variables: $\mathcal{O}(\delta t^2, \delta S^2)$. This is a significant improvement over the first-order temporal accuracy of both the explicit and fully implicit schemes.

Stability & Solvers

Crank-Nicolson is unconditionally stable (no step constraints). Like the implicit method, it produces a tridiagonal matrix system $\mathbf{M}_L \mathbf{v}^{k+1} = \mathbf{M}_R \mathbf{v}^k$ at each marching step, which must be solved using specialized linear algebra.

1. LU Factorization: Direct Thomas Algorithm

To solve the tridiagonal matrix equation $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{q}$, computing the inverse $\mathbf{M}^{-1}$ is highly inefficient and should never be done in practice. Instead, we use LU Decomposition (Thomas Algorithm), factoring $\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{U}$ into lower bidiagonal $\mathbf{L}$ (unit diagonal) and upper bidiagonal $\mathbf{U}$:

Thomas Algorithm Factorization Steps:
- First, set the starting diagonal element: $d_1 = 1 + B_1$.
- For $2 \le i \le I-1$, compute bidiagonal values recursively: $$l_i = \frac{A_i}{d_{i-1}}, \quad u_{i-1} = C_{i-1}, \quad d_i = 1 + B_i - l_i u_{i-1}$$ Once $\mathbf{L}$ and $\mathbf{U}$ are resolved, we solve $\mathbf{L}\mathbf{U}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{q}$ in two fast sweeps:
1. Forward Substitution (solve $\mathbf{L}\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{q}$): $$w_1 = q_1, \quad w_i = q_i - l_i w_{i-1}$$ 2. Backward Substitution (solve $\mathbf{U}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w}$): $$v_{I-1} = \frac{w_{I-1}}{d_{I-1}}, \quad v_i = \frac{w_i - u_i v_{i+1}}{d_i}$$

2. Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR)

SOR is an indirect iterative technique that accelerates convergence. It builds upon two foundational iterative methods:

- Jacobi Method: Each new iteration uses exclusively the values from the previous iteration step $n$.
- Gauss-Seidel Method: Incorporates recently updated values from the current iteration $n+1$ as they become available.

Strict Diagonal Dominance (SDD):
Gauss-Seidel is guaranteed to converge to the exact solution if the matrix is strictly diagonally dominant: $|M_{ii}| > \sum_{j \neq i} |M_{ij}|$ for all rows.

SOR Parameter $\omega$: SOR accelerates Gauss-Seidel using a relaxation parameter $\omega$: $$v_i^{n+1} = (1-\omega)v_i^n + \frac{\omega}{M_{ii}} \left( q_i - \sum_{j < i} M_{ij} v_j^{n+1} - \sum_{j > i} M_{ij} v_j^n \right)$$ - $0 < \omega < 1$: Under-relaxation (stabilizes unstable systems). - $1 < \omega < 2$: Over-relaxation (accelerates convergence).

Advanced Time-Stepping Schemes

The general $\theta$-method represents time-stepping as a weighted average between the explicit and fully implicit schemes using parameter $\theta \in [0, 1]$:

$$\frac{V_i^{k+1} - V_i^k}{\delta t} = \theta \Delta^2 V_i^{k+1} + (1-\theta) \Delta^2 V_i^k$$
  • $\theta = 0$: Recovers the Explicit Scheme (conditionally stable).
  • $\theta = 1/2$: Recovers the Crank-Nicolson Scheme (unconditionally stable, $\mathcal{O}(\delta t^2)$).
  • $\theta = 1$: Recovers the Fully Implicit Scheme (unconditionally stable, $\mathcal{O}(\delta t)$).

Douglas Schemes: By choosing a specific $\theta$ weight, Douglas schemes achieve fourth-order accuracy in space and second-order in time ($\mathcal{O}(\delta S^4, \delta t^2)$) for the exact same computational effort as Crank-Nicolson.

Two-Factor Pricing Models

When options are exposed to both a stochastic stock price $S$ and a stochastic interest rate $r$ (e.g. convertible bonds), the pricing equation expands to a 3D grid surface ($S, r, t$):

Mixed Derivatives 9-Point Stencil

The mixed partial derivative term is approximated using central differences in both directions: $$\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \partial r} \approx \frac{V_{i+1, j+1}^k - V_{i+1, j-1}^k - V_{i-1, j+1}^k + V_{i-1, j-1}^k}{4 \delta S \delta r}$$ This requires a 9-point grid stencil rather than the standard 3-point or 5-point stencils. If correlation is zero, this mixed term vanishes, reducing the grid stencil requirements back to 5 points.

Stochastic interest rates in this multi-factor framework are typically modeled using the Vasicek or Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) models.

Problem 1.1 Q1: Crout and Doolittle LU Decompositions
Question:
Solve the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ using Crout and Doolittle LU decompositions: $$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 4 & -5 & -10 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}; \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
Detailed Solution:

Doolittle Decomposition ($L$ has unit diagonal): $$L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{22} & 1 \end{pmatrix}; \quad U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 0 & -11 & -18 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{2}{11} \end{pmatrix}$$ Solve $L\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{b}$: $$w_1 = 2, \quad w_2 = 4 - 2(2) = 0, \quad w_3 = 1 - 0.5(2) - (-1/22)(0) = 0$$ Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{w}$: $$z = 0, \quad y = 0, \quad x = 1 \implies \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Crout Decomposition ($U$ has unit diagonal): $$L = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 4 & -11 & 0 \\ 1 & 0.5 & \frac{2}{11} \end{pmatrix}; \quad U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1.5 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & \frac{18}{11} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$ Following forward substitution $L\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{b}$ and backward substitution $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{w}$ yields: $$\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Problem 1.1 Q3: Jacobi vs. Gauss-Seidel Iterative Convergence
Question:
Solve using the Gauss-Seidel method correct to 3 decimal places with initial guess $\mathbf{x}^{(0)} = (0, 0, 0)^T$: $$\begin{pmatrix} 8 & -1 & 1 \\ 2 & 10 & -1 \\ -1 & -1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$ Verify strict diagonal dominance (SDD) and calculate the infinite norm error ratio.
Detailed Solution:
Gauss-Seidel equations: $$x^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{8} \left( 1 + y^{(k)} - z^{(k)} \right), \quad y^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{10} \left( 4 - 2x^{(k+1)} + z^{(k)} \right), \quad z^{(k+1)} = \frac{1}{5} \left( -3 + x^{(k+1)} + y^{(k+1)} \right)$$ - Iteration 1: $x = 0.125, \, y = 0.375, \, z = -0.500$
- Iteration 2: $x = 0.234, \, y = 0.303, \, z = -0.492$
- Iteration 3: $x = 0.224, \, y = 0.306, \, z = -0.494$
- Iteration 4: $x = 0.225, \, y = 0.306, \, z = -0.494$
SDD Verification: Row 1: $|8| > |-1| + |1| = 2$ (SDD). Row 2: $|10| > |2| + |-1| = 3$ (SDD). Row 3: $|5| > |-1| + |-1| = 2$ (SDD). The matrix is strictly diagonally dominant, guaranteeing convergence.
Infinite Norm Error Ratio (It 3 to 4): $$\frac{\|\mathbf{x}^{(4)} - \mathbf{x}^{(3)}\|_{\infty}}{\|\mathbf{x}^{(3)}\|_{\infty}} = \frac{\|(0.001, 0, 0)\|_{\infty}}{\|0.494\|_{\infty}} = \frac{0.001}{0.494} \approx 0.00202$$
Problem 1.2 Q1: Fully Implicit Difference Scheme & Doolittle Solver
Question:
1. Derive the fully implicit difference scheme $a_n V_{n-1}^{m+1} + b_n V_n^{m+1} + c_n V_{n+1}^{m+1} = V_n^m$ for the Black-Scholes PDE. 2. Solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ using Doolittle's method: $$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -2 \\ 3 & 2 & 2 \\ 5 & 4 & 3 \end{pmatrix}; \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$
Detailed Solution:

Part 1: Implicit Difference Derivation: Substituting backward marching derivatives evaluated at step $m+1$: $$\frac{V_n^m - V_n^{m+1}}{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 n^2 (V_{n-1}^{m+1} - 2V_n^{m+1} + V_{n+1}^{m+1}) + \frac{(r-D)n}{2}(V_{n+1}^{m+1} - V_{n-1}^{m+1}) - rV_n^{m+1} = 0$$ Multiplying by $\delta t$ and isolating $V_n^m$ gives: $$a_n = -\frac{1}{2}\left( \sigma^2 n^2 - n(r-D) \right)\delta t, \quad b_n = 1 + \left( \sigma^2 n^2 + r \right)\delta t, \quad c_n = -\frac{1}{2}\left( \sigma^2 n^2 + n(r-D) \right)\delta t$$

Part 2: Doolittle Method: Factoring $A = LU$: $$L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1.5 & 1 & 0 \\ 2.5 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}; \quad U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -2 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 \end{pmatrix}$$ Solve $L\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{b}$ via forward substitution: $$w_1 = 10, \quad w_2 = 1 - 1.5(10) = -14, \quad w_3 = 4 - 2.5(10) - 3(-14) = 21$$ Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{w}$ via backward substitution: $$x_3 = \frac{21}{-7} = -3, \quad 0.5 x_2 + 5(-3) = -14 \implies x_2 = 2$$ $$2x_1 + 2 - 2(-3) = 10 \implies x_1 = 1 \implies \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$
