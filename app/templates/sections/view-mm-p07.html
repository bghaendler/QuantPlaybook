Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Solving First-Order ODEs with Boundary Values

Separable and linear first-order equations, the integrating factor, and how boundary/terminal conditions select the financially meaningful solution.

1. Separable Equations

$\frac{dy}{dt} = g(t)h(y)$: separate and integrate, $\int \frac{dy}{h(y)} = \int g(t)\,dt + C$. The compounding account:

$$\frac{dB}{dt} = r(t)B \;\Rightarrow\; B_t = B_0 \exp\!\left(\int_0^t r(s)\,ds\right),$$

i.e. the discount factor $D(0,T) = e^{-\int_0^T r(s)ds}$ — the time-value of money is one separable ODE. Logistic-style saturating growth $\dot{y} = ky(M - y)$ handles adoption/AUM models; even GBM is "separable after Itô": $d\ln S = (\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)dt + \sigma dW$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Separable ODEs are equations where we can group variables on opposite sides of the equals sign to integrate them.

  • Asset Growth: Used to model deterministic decay or growth models, such as continuous inflation decay.
  • Integration: Solved by directly integrating both sides, adding a constant of integration.

2. Linear First Order: The Integrating Factor

For $\frac{dy}{dt} + p(t)y = q(t)$, multiply by $I(t) = e^{\int p\,dt}$ so the left side becomes $(Iy)'$:

$$y(t) = \frac{1}{I(t)}\left[\int I(s)q(s)\,ds + C\right].$$

Marquee financial application — the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck/Vasicek solution: $dr = \kappa(\theta - r)dt + \sigma dW$ treated pathwise with integrating factor $e^{\kappa t}$:

$$r_t = \theta + (r_0 - \theta)e^{-\kappa t} + \sigma\int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t - s)}\,dW_s,$$

giving mean $\theta + (r_0 - \theta)e^{-\kappa t}$ and variance $\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa t})$ — mean reversion solved exactly with a first-order technique. The same trick solves expected values of most affine models.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Non-separable linear ODEs are solved using an integrating factor, which is an algebraic trick to make integration possible.

  • Integrating Factor: We multiply by e^{\int p(x)dx} to turn the equation into a simple product-rule derivative.
  • Term Structure: Crucial for interest rate models where the rate of change is linear in the short rate.

3. Boundary & Terminal Conditions

The general solution carries a constant $C$; conditions pin it down — and in finance the condition is the contract:

  • Initial value: $B_0$ = today's account value; $r_0$ = today's short rate.
  • Terminal value (finance's habit): bonds solve backwards from $P(T, T) = 1$; options from the payoff. The affine bond exponent $B(t,T)$ solves $\frac{dB}{dt} = \kappa B - 1$, $B(T,T) = 0$ ⇒ $B = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa(T-t)}}{\kappa}$ — integrating factor + terminal condition.
  • Boundary values in state: for ODEs in the spatial variable (perpetual options), conditions at $S \to 0$ / $S \to \infty$ and smooth pasting select among power solutions — Part 08's subject.
  • Existence/uniqueness (Picard-Lindelöf) requires Lipschitz $f$: CIR's $\sqrt{r}$ fails it at zero, which is precisely why CIR's boundary behavior (Feller condition $2\kappa\theta \ge \sigma^2$) needs special care.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Differential equations have infinite solutions; boundary conditions pin down the single unique solution matching a specific contract.

  • Terminal Value: A zero-coupon bond must pay exactly par (P(T,T)=1) at expiration, which serves as the terminal condition.
  • Initial Value: Model calibration uses today's interest rate as the initial condition to solve rate paths.

4. Worked Example: Continuous Annuity

An account earning rate $r$ with continuous withdrawals $w$: $\frac{dA}{dt} = rA - w$, $A_0$ given. Integrating factor $e^{-rt}$:

$$A_t = \frac{w}{r} + \left(A_0 - \frac{w}{r}\right)e^{rt}.$$

Read the threshold: if $A_0 > w/r$ the account grows forever; if $A_0 < w/r$ it hits zero at $t^* = \frac{1}{r}\ln\frac{w/r - A_0 e^{0}}{w/r - A_0}$... solving $A_{t^*} = 0$ gives the ruin time — retirement planning, endowment spending rules, and the deterministic skeleton of ALM in one linear ODE.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: A continuous annuity pays out cash continuously over time. We solve it as a first-order ODE with boundary values.

  • Present Value: The ODE models how the annuity fund balance decays over time as cash is withdrawn.
  • Closed-Form Solution: Yields the exact deposit required today to fund a lifetime of continuous payouts.

Key Takeaways

  • Separable: discount factors and growth laws. Linear + integrating factor: mean reversion solved exactly.
  • OU/Vasicek's full distribution drops out of one integrating-factor computation.
  • Finance fixes constants with terminal conditions — solve backwards from the contract.
  • Check Lipschitz/regularity before trusting uniqueness (CIR at zero is the standard counterexample).
