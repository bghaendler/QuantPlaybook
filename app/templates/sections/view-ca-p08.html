Complex Analysis for Quants ‚Äî Part 08

Cauchy-Riemann in Polar Coordinates

The CR equations rewritten for $z = re^{i\theta}$: derivation by chain rule, the polar Laplacian, and why log and power functions are best handled in polar form.

1. The Polar CR Equations

With $f = u(r, \theta) + iv(r, \theta)$ and $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, the chain rule transforms $u_x = v_y$, $u_y = -v_x$ into:

$$\boxed{\;\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} = \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial v}{\partial \theta}, \qquad \frac{\partial v}{\partial r} = -\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\;} \qquad (r \ne 0)$$

with derivative $f'(z) = e^{-i\theta}\left(u_r + iv_r\right)$. The $1/r$ factors are the price of curvilinear coordinates (arc length along $\theta$ is $r\,d\theta$); the structure ‚Äî each component's radial derivative tied to the other's angular derivative ‚Äî is unchanged.

2. The Functions Born Polar

  • Logarithm: $\ln z = \ln r + i\theta$, so $u = \ln r$, $v = \theta$. Check: $u_r = 1/r = \frac{1}{r}v_\theta$ ‚úì, $v_r = 0 = -\frac{1}{r}u_\theta$ ‚úì ‚Äî holomorphic wherever $\theta$ is single-valued, i.e. off a branch cut; $f' = e^{-i\theta}/r = 1/z$.
  • Powers: $z^\alpha = r^\alpha e^{i\alpha\theta}$ ‚áí $u = r^\alpha\cos\alpha\theta$, $v = r^\alpha\sin\alpha\theta$ ‚Äî CR verified in two lines, for any real Œ± (the fractional powers of MM Part 08's Cauchy-Euler solutions). In Cartesian coordinates the same check is a mess.
  • $1/z$, $z^n$: immediate by the same pattern ‚Äî polar CR is the natural habitat of everything with circular symmetry.

3. The Polar Laplacian

Combining the polar CR pair reproduces harmonicity in polar form:

$$\nabla^2 u = u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r}u_r + \frac{1}{r^2}u_{\theta\theta} = 0.$$

Separable solutions $u = R(r)\Theta(\theta)$ give $\Theta = \cos n\theta, \sin n\theta$ and the radial Cauchy-Euler equation $r^2R'' + rR' - n^2R = 0$ ‚áí $R = r^{\pm n}$ (and $\ln r$ for $n = 0$) ‚Äî the same equidimensional ODE that priced perpetual options. The general disk solution $u = a_0 + \sum r^n(a_n\cos n\theta + b_n\sin n\theta)$ is a Fourier series in the angle with powers in the radius: summing it against boundary data yields the Poisson kernel of Part 07.

4. Branch Cuts: The Practical Lesson

$\theta$ is only defined mod $2\pi$ ‚Äî so $\ln z$ and $z^\alpha$ (Œ± non-integer) need a chosen branch: conventionally $\theta \in (-\pi, \pi]$ with a cut along the negative real axis. Crossing the cut jumps $v = \theta$ by $2\pi$ ‚Äî the function is holomorphic on the cut plane, not on any loop around the origin. Consequences a quant actually hits:

  • Heston's "little trap": the model's characteristic function contains a complex log; naive principal-branch evaluation makes the integrand discontinuous in $u$ and corrupts prices ‚Äî the well-known fix (Albrecher et al.) reformulates to keep the argument off the cut.
  • Library behavior: log/sqrt of complex arrays in NumPy use the principal branch ‚Äî track winding manually when integrating along paths that circle singularities.

Key Takeaways

  • Polar CR: $u_r = \frac{1}{r}v_\theta$, $v_r = -\frac{1}{r}u_\theta$; $f' = e^{-i\theta}(u_r + iv_r)$.
  • $\ln z$ and $z^\alpha$ verify holomorphy trivially in polar form ‚Äî and expose their branch cuts honestly.
  • The polar Laplacian separates into Fourier modes √ó Cauchy-Euler radial powers ‚Äî disk problems solved.
  • Branch-cut discipline is production-relevant: the Heston complex-log discontinuity is the canonical pricing bug.
