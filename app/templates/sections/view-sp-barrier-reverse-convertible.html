Barrier Reverse Convertible (BRC)

The best-selling structured product: a reverse convertible whose short put only activates if the underlying crashes through a barrier — conditional protection, unconditional coupon.

1. Structure

Like the classic RC — unconditional coupon $c$, term 6–24 months — but the equity risk is a down-and-in put with strike $K$ (≈ 100%) and barrier $H$ (typically 55–75% of spot):

$$V_T = \begin{cases} N(1 + c), & \text{barrier never breached, or } S_T \ge K \\[4pt] N\frac{S_T}{K} + Nc, & \text{barrier breached and } S_T < K \end{cases}$$

Decomposition: ZCB + short down-and-in put (DIP). The DIP costs less than the vanilla put (only crash paths activate it), so the coupon is somewhat lower than a classic RC's — the difference buys the "conditional capital protection". Barrier styles: continuous (American, touch any time), daily close, or European (checked at maturity only — materially safer for the investor and increasingly the retail standard).

2. The Cliff Risk Profile

  • While $H$ holds: bond-like, full coupon, par redemption — in backtests the barrier "never" breaks, which is exactly how it is sold.
  • On breach: the position becomes a vanilla short ATM-strike put — value gaps down by the protection's worth at the worst moment. Loss runs from $K$ down, not from $H$: a 61% barrier touch with $S_T = 65$ still loses 35% (plus coupon back).
  • The investor is short crash correlation with their own worst-case: barriers break in market-wide sell-offs, so BRC portfolios lose jointly, precisely when the holder's other assets fall.
  • Worst-of BRCs (2–3 underlyings, breach on any) dominate issuance: the down-and-in worst-of put is cheap (short correlation + short skew + barrier), funding headline coupons of 8–15% — and concentrating dispersion risk on the buyer.

3. Pricing & Hedging (Desk View)

  • Model: the DIP is smile-dynamics-sensitive — local vol overprices, pure stochastic vol underprices barrier hits; desks use calibrated SLV with barrier shifts for hedge slippage. Worst-of adds a (stochastic) correlation mark.
  • Greeks near $H$: the short DIP gives the issuer's hedge book exploding gamma/delta at the barrier — hedge unwinds at breach ("barrier defense" flows) can accelerate the very move, a known market-microstructure effect in names with large BRC open interest.
  • Vega sign flips with spot: far above the barrier the investor is mildly short vol; approaching it, violently so.
  • Autocallable cousin: add early-redemption triggers and the BRC becomes the Phoenix/autocall family (next sections) — same risk core, plus callability.

Key Takeaways

  • BRC = ZCB + short down-and-in put: conditional protection that vanishes exactly in crashes.
  • Loss on breach measures from the strike, not the barrier — the cliff is the defining risk.
  • Worst-of + continuous barriers + volatile names = highest coupon = most options sold.
  • Desk pricing needs SLV + correlation + barrier shifts; investor analysis needs only the decomposition.
  • Barrier-defense hedging flows make large BRC strikes/barriers visible in the underlying's behavior.
