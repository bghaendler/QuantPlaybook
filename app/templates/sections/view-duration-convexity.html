1.4 Duration and Convexity

Risk management in bond portfolios using first and second-order price sensitivity measures, duration matching, and convexity immunization.

Bond Price Sensitivity & Duration

When interest rates shift, bond prices fluctuate. To study how a bond's price $p$ reacts to parallel changes in the yield curve ($y_i \mapsto y_i + s$), we differentiate $p(s)$ with respect to the shift $s$.

The **duration** $D$ is defined as the negative relative first-order sensitivity of the bond price at $s = 0$:

$$ D = -\frac{1}{p} \frac{\partial p(s)}{\partial s} \Big|_{s=0} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n T_i c_i e^{-y_i T_i}}{p} $$

This shows that the duration is an annuity-weighted average of payment dates $T_i$. For a zero-coupon bond with maturity $T$, the Macaulay duration is simply $T$.

Taylor Series Approximations

The first-order approximation for relative bond price changes with respect to a parallel shift $\Delta y$ is:

$$ \frac{\Delta p}{p} \approx -D \Delta y $$

Example 1: A 10-year 6% coupon bond under flat 3% yield curve has a price of $125.14$ and duration $D = 8.06$.

Example 2: A 5-year 3% coupon bond under flat 3% yield curve has a price of $99.79$ and duration $D = 4.72$.

Convexity

Due to the positive curvature of bond prices (convexity), duration underestimates price increases and overestimates price falls. To correct this, we define the **convexity** $C$ as the relative second-order sensitivity:

$$ C = \frac{1}{p} \frac{\partial^2 p(s)}{\partial s^2} \Big|_{s=0} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n T_i^2 c_i e^{-y_i T_i}}{p} $$

Combining duration and convexity, we obtain the **second-order Taylor approximation**:

$$ \frac{\Delta p}{p} \approx -D \Delta y + \frac{1}{2} C (\Delta y)^2 $$

Duration & Convexity Immunization

To hedge a portfolio $\Pi$ with two instruments $H_1, H_2$ against both first and second-order yield twists, we solve for quantities $q_1, q_2$ such that the hedged portfolio's sensitivities are zero:

$$ -q_1 D_{H_1} H_1(0) - q_2 D_{H_2} H_2(0) = D_{\Pi} \Pi(0) $$ $$ q_1 C_{H_1} H_1(0) + q_2 C_{H_2} H_2(0) = -C_{\Pi} \Pi(0) $$

This linear system is solved by inverting the $2\times2$ duration/convexity matrix.
