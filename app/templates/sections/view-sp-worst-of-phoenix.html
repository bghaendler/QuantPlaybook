Worst-Of Autocallable Phoenix

The maximum-coupon configuration: every Phoenix trigger reads the worst of several underlyings — stacking correlation and dispersion premia on top of the autocall machine.

1. Structure

A Phoenix autocallable (memory coupons at barrier $B$, autocall at $A$, European protection barrier $H$) where every observation evaluates the worst performer of a basket of $m$ underlyings (usually 2–4 stocks or indices):

$$W_{t} = \min_{k=1..m} \frac{S^k_{t}}{S^k_0},$$

coupon iff $W_{t_i} \ge B$, autocall iff $W_{t_i} \ge A$, and at maturity redemption $= N \cdot W_T$ if $W_T < H$ — typically settled physically in the worst share. One bad name out of three decides everything.

2. Why the Coupon Is So High

The investor sells, simultaneously:

  • Volatility (each name's puts/digitals),
  • Skew (the protection barrier loads on downside smiles),
  • Correlation/dispersion: the worst-of is worth less (to the holder) the more the basket disperses; expected min of $m$ lognormals falls roughly with $\sigma\sqrt{(1-\rho)}$ — every notch of correlation not realized is investor loss, dealer gain. The implied correlation embedded at issue is typically above historical — the investor sells correlation cheap insurance-style,
  • Forward volatility & extension risk (the autocall kills good paths early).

Stacked premia explain coupons of 10–18% p.a. on baskets of liquid names — and why realized outcomes have fat left tails: all the sold risks correlate in stress.

3. Selection & Behavioral Pitfalls

  • Basket construction: issuers favor high-vol, low-correlation mixes (e.g. a bank + a tech + an energy name) — optically diversified, but for a worst-of, diversification hurts: more independent names = more lottery tickets for one to crash.
  • "All large caps" comfort: the relevant statistic is the worst single-name drawdown over the term — for 3 volatile names over 3 years, a 40%+ drawdown in at least one is closer to likely than rare.
  • Survivorship marketing: backtests start after crashes; memory coupons mask interim stress until the barrier event converts the note into the worst stock at its lows.

4. Dealer-Side Risk

  • Correlation books: dealers accumulate long-dispersion/short-correlation inventory from issuance and recycle via dispersion swaps, correlation swaps and index-vs-single-name vega — capacity limits feed back into issue terms.
  • Cross-gamma between names: hedging the worst-of near barrier requires trading each name conditioned on the others — model risk concentrates in the joint smile (copula/SLV-correlation assumptions), not the marginals.
  • Stress behavior: in a broad sell-off, correlation → 1: the dealer's long-dispersion hedge underperforms exactly as barriers approach, while vega flips — the 2020 autocall hedging losses across several houses were precisely this configuration.

Key Takeaways

  • All triggers read the worst performer; one crashing name controls coupons, autocall and redemption.
  • The coupon stacks vol + skew + correlation + forward-vol premia — the most options sold per unit notional in retail.
  • For worst-of products, a "diversified" basket is riskier, not safer.
  • Pricing: Monte Carlo on multi-asset SLV with a correlation mark; the joint smile is the model risk.
  • Dealer dispersion inventory and stress correlation-convergence shape both terms and crisis dynamics.
