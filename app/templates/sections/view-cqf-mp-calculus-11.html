1.1 Mathematical Shorthand & Number Systems

Rigorous mathematical notation, logic symbols, and set descriptions used in quantitative finance.

1.1.1 Logic Shorthand Notation

Mathematical proofs and derivations utilize concise symbolic logic shorthand to represent statements efficiently. In continuous asset path modeling and probability fields, these symbols form the building blocks of rigorous theorems:

∃ : there exists (existential)
∀ : for all (universal)
∴ : therefore
∵ : because
⇒ : implies
s.t. : such that
⇔ : if and only if
∈ : element of

Example statement "For all real positive volatilities $\sigma$, there exists a unique derivative value $V$ such that the Black-Scholes PDE holds" is written as:

$\forall \sigma \in \mathbb{R}^+, \exists ! V \text{ s.t. } \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + r S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - rV = 0$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Mathematical shorthand symbols act like programming code variables and operators. They allow complex, wordy logical rules to be condensed into short, unambiguous lines of mathematical code.

  • Why it matters: Reading derivatives math can feel like learning a foreign language. Recognizing symbols like $\forall$ (for all) and $\exists$ (there exists) helps you map out boundary conditions quickly.
  • Usage in Proofs: Allows quants to state assumptions, boundaries, and model definitions cleanly in options and derivative theorems.

1.1.2 Number Systems & Fields

Real-world quant modeling relies on specific number fields designed to represent different financial concepts:

  • Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$): $\{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ — Used to index discrete monitoring dates or steps $t_k$ in Monte Carlo simulations.
  • Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$): $\{0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \dots\}$ — Represents contract sizes or portfolio position units.
  • Rationals ($\mathbb{Q}$): $\{p/q : p,q \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ — Represents discrete price grid spaces in Finite Difference Schemes.
  • Irrationals ($\mathbb{Q}$'): $\{\sqrt{2}, \pi, e\}$ — Drive continuous distributions. Euler's number $e$ forms the foundation of continuous compounding.
  • Reals ($\mathbb{R}$): Union of all rational and irrational fields. Volatilities, spot prices, and rates reside here.
  • Complex ($\mathbb{C}$): $\{x + iy\}$ — Backbone of Characteristic Functions and FFT pricing methods (e.g. Heston Model).

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Different fields correspond to different financial scenarios. We don't just use decimals (reals); we use integers for counting contracts, rationals for grid divisions, and complex numbers for advanced option pricing models.

  • Euler's Constant ($e$): Forms the baseline for all continuous compounding calculations and stochastic asset paths.
  • Complex Numbers in Finance: Even though stock prices aren't imaginary, complex numbers ($\mathbb{C}$) are essential for solving options under stochastic volatility (like the Heston model) because they simplify the integration of probability density curves using Fourier transforms.
