4. Model Calibration Example (HJM Calibration to Caps)

Two-Factor Gaussian HJM Model Calibration

Having developed the framework for forward measures and options, we now have all the ingredients to calibrate a term structure model to market data. In this module, we calibrate a two-factor Gaussian Heath–Jarrow–Morton (HJM) model to market Swiss swap rates and at-the-money (ATM) cap quotes.

To do this, we price caps analytically within the Gaussian HJM model, evaluate the market price sensitivities (Vegas) under the Black and Bachelier frameworks, and formulate an elegant, fast, and robust vega-weighted least squares calibration problem.

Calibration Workflow

  1. Discount Curve Estimation: Construct the semi-annual discount factors $P(0, T_k)$ using the pseudoinverse method from Swiss swap rates.
  2. ATM Strike Curve Calculation: Determine the 6-month forward swap rates to define at-the-money cap strikes.
  3. Model Caplet Pricing: Price caplets using HJM integrated return volatilities.
  4. Vega-Weighted Minimization: Minimize implied volatility errors approximated efficiently via analytical Cap Vegas.

Two-Factor Gaussian HJM Specification

We consider a two-factor Gaussian HJM model with the volatility structure:

$$ \sigma(t, T) = \left( e^{-\beta_1(T-t)}v_1, \ e^{-\beta_2(T-t)}v_2 \right) $$

where $v_1, v_2, \beta_1, \beta_2$ are positive parameters to be calibrated to market data.

By elementary integration, the integrated volatility term inside the option pricing formula yields:

$$ \int_0^{T_{i-1}} \| v(s, T_{i-1}) - v(s, T_i) \|^2 ds = \sum_{k=1}^2 \frac{v_k^2}{\beta_k^2} (e^{-\beta_k T_{i-1}} - e^{-\beta_k T_i})^2 \frac{e^{2\beta_k T_{i-1}} - 1}{2\beta_k} $$

*Note: For very small $\beta_k \approx 0$, the limits are resolved analytically to avoid numerical divide-by-zero errors.

HJM Caplet Pricing Formula

Recall that the $i$-th caplet (resets at $T_{i-1}$ and settles at $T_i$) with strike rate $\kappa$ is equivalent to $1 + \delta\kappa$ put options on the $T_i$-bond expiring at $T_{i-1}$. In the Gaussian HJM model, the time-0 price is:

$$ Cpl(T_{i-1}, T_i) = P(0, T_{i-1})\Phi(-d_2(i)) - (1 + \delta \kappa) P(0, T_i)\Phi(-d_1(i)) $$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution, and the parameters $d_1, d_2$ are given by:

$$ d_{1,2}(i) = \frac{\log\left( \frac{P(0, T_i)}{P(0, T_{i-1})} (1 + \delta \kappa) \right) \pm \frac{1}{2} \sigma_i^2}{\sigma_i} $$

with $\sigma_i^2 = \int_0^{T_{i-1}} \| v(s, T_{i-1}) - v(s, T_i) \|^2 ds$ as the integrated variance.

Solving Implied Vol Errors via Vega-Weighting

Directly minimizing the mean squared error of implied volatilities ($\sum (\hat{\sigma}_n^\theta - \sigma_n)^2$) is standard because implied vols standardize prices and are comparable. However, computing $\hat{\sigma}_n^\theta$ in real-time requires numerical root-finding (inverting the Black/Bachelier formula) for each step of the optimizer, which is computationally expensive.

The Solution: A first-order Taylor expansion of the pricing function $C_n(\sigma)$ around the market volatility $\sigma_n$ yields:

$$ \hat{C}_n^\theta \approx C_n + \text{vega}_n (\hat{\sigma}_n^\theta - \sigma_n) \implies (\hat{\sigma}_n^\theta - \sigma_n)^2 \approx \frac{1}{(\text{vega}_n)^2} (\hat{C}_n^\theta - C_n)^2 $$

Thus, by weighting the squared price errors with the inverse squared market vega ($\frac{1}{\text{vega}_n^2}$), the problem is reduced to a fast, analytical, weighted least-squares minimization that runs instantly!

Black Cap Vega ($\text{vega}_{Black}$)

Black caplet vega with respect to Black relative volatility $\sigma_B$ is: $$ \text{vega}_{i, Black} = \delta P(0, T_i) F_i \sqrt{T_{i-1}} \phi(d_1) $$ where $\phi(x)$ is the normal density. The Black cap vega is the sum of caplet vegas.

Bachelier Cap Vega ($\text{vega}_{Bachelier}$)

Bachelier caplet vega with respect to Normal absolute volatility $\sigma_N$ is: $$ \text{vega}_{i, Bachelier} = \delta P(0, T_i) \sqrt{T_{i-1}} \phi(D_i) $$ where $D_i = \frac{F_i - \kappa}{\sigma_N \sqrt{T_{i-1}}}$. The Bachelier cap vega is the sum of caplet vegas.

Market spot swap curve context

The calibration starts from Switzerland market swap rates paying annual fixed coupons with maturities ranging from 1 year to 30 years. The very first rate is the 6-month simple spot rate ($0.343\%$). Because the caps have semi-annual cash flows ($\delta = 0.5$), we estimate the discount curve $P(0, T_k)$ at the semi-annual frequency.

We apply the exact method based on the pseudoinverse matrix to reconstruct the discount factors consistently for all 60 periods ($T_{59} = 30$ years) matching the EPFL paper bootstrap results.
