Module 4: Interest Rate Derivatives

1. Interest Rate Futures & Convexity Adjustment

Interest Rate Futures

Similar to a Forward Rate Agreement (FRA), an interest rate futures contract allows one to manage exposure to the simple spot rate $L(T_0, T_1)$ prevailing over a future period $[T_0, T_1]$ with length $\delta = T_1 - T_0$.

In contrast to FRAs, interest rate futures are daily marked to market (resettled). At any date $t \le T_0$, the futures price is quoted in terms of the futures rate $R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$:

$$ P_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) = 100 \times \bigl(1 - R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)\bigr) $$

When the futures price changes, the holder receives the daily cash flow:

$$ \Delta P_\text{futures}(t + \Delta t) := P_\text{futures}(t + \Delta t, T_0, T_1) - P_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) $$

This cash flow can be positive or negative — it is the key feature distinguishing futures from forwards.

Key Rules for the Futures Rate

  • At delivery $T_0$: The futures rate equals the underlying spot rate: $R_\text{futures}(T_0, T_0, T_1) = L(T_0, T_1)$.
  • At any $t < T_0$: The present value of the daily cash flow from holding the contract must equal zero:

$$ 0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t\!\left[e^{-\int_t^{t+\Delta t} r(s)ds} \cdot \Delta P_\text{futures}(t + \Delta t)\right] \approx e^{-r(t)\Delta t}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t\!\left[\Delta P_\text{futures}(t + \Delta t)\right] $$

Consequence: The conditional expectation of the change in futures price must be zero, i.e., $P_\text{futures}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale. Since the futures rate is linear in the futures price, the futures rate is also a $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale.

Futures Rate Formula

Since the futures rate process $R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale, at any date $t$ it equals the conditional expectation of its terminal value:

$$ R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t\!\left[L(T_0, T_1)\right] $$

In contrast, the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is a $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$-martingale (under the $T_1$-forward measure), so:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}}_t\!\left[L(T_0, T_1)\right] $$

The two rates use different measures — hence they generally differ. The gap between them is the convexity adjustment:

$$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1) - F(t, T_0, T_1) $$

The convexity adjustment is model-dependent. Under $\mathbb{Q}$, futures holders are compensated daily in a way that pushes the futures rate systematically above the forward rate.

Intuition: Why Futures Rate > Forward Rate?

The daily mark-to-market of futures creates an asymmetry:

  • When rates rise, futures holders receive a positive cash flow immediately — and they can reinvest at the now-higher short rate.
  • When rates fall, futures holders pay a negative cash flow — but they finance this at the now-lower short rate.
  • This asymmetry benefits futures holders vs. FRA holders, so the futures rate must be set higher to be equally attractive. The difference is the convexity adjustment $\gamma$.
Rule of thumb: $\gamma \ge 0$ always (in Gaussian models), and it grows with time to maturity and with volatility $\sigma$.

Convexity Adjustment in Gaussian HJM Models

Consider a Gaussian HJM model with deterministic forward rate volatility $\sigma(t, T)$, and $T$-bond return volatility $v(t, T) = -\int_t^T \sigma(t, u)\,du$.

Step 1: Bond Price Ratio

Stochastic calculus shows the ratio of $T_0$-bond to $T_1$-bond can be written as:

$$ \frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)} = \frac{P(0,T_0)}{P(0,T_1)}\,\underbrace{\mathcal{E}\!\left(\int_0^t \Delta v(s)\,dW^*(s)\right)}_{\mathbb{Q}\text{-martingale}} \cdot \underbrace{e^{\int_0^t \Delta v(s)\,-v(s,T_1)^\top ds}}_{\text{deterministic}} $$

where $\Delta v(s) = v(s, T_0) - v(s, T_1) = \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s, u) du$.

Step 2: Conditional Expectation

Since $v$ is deterministic, the conditional $\mathbb{Q}$-expectation of the terminal ratio equals:

$$ \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t\!\left[\frac{P(T_0,T_0)}{P(T_0,T_1)}\right] = \frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)} \cdot e^{\int_t^{T_0} \Delta v(s)\,-v(s,T_1)^\top ds} $$

The exponential correction factor is purely deterministic — this is the Gaussian model advantage.

Step 3: Convexity Formula

Since $L(T_0,T_1) = \tfrac{1}{\delta}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{P(T_0,T_1)}-1\right)$, the exact convexity adjustment is:

$$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)}\left(e^{\int_t^{T_0} \left( \int_s^{T_1} \sigma(s,u)du \right) \left( \int_{T_0}^{T_1} \sigma(s,v)^\top dv \right) ds} - 1\right) $$

Exact Formula vs. Approximation

Depending on whether the first inner integral limit goes up to $T_1$ or is approximated to $T_0$, there are two formulas:

  • Approach 1: Exact HJM Convexity Adjustment

    $$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)}\!\left(\exp\!\left\{\int_t^{T_0}\!\!\left(\int_s^{T_1}\!\sigma(s,u)\,du\right)\!\left(\int_{T_0}^{T_1}\!\sigma(s,v)\,dv\right)^\top\!ds\right\} - 1\right) $$

  • Approach 2: Common Approximation

    $$ \gamma_{\text{approx}}(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)}\!\left(\exp\!\left\{\int_t^{T_0}\!\!\left(\int_s^{T_0}\!\sigma(s,u)\,du\right)\!\left(\int_{T_0}^{T_1}\!\sigma(s,v)\,dv\right)^\top\!ds\right\} - 1\right) $$

The convexity adjustment is always nonnegative $\gamma(t, T_0, T_1) \ge 0$ whenever $\sigma(s,u)\sigma(s,v)^\top \ge 0$ for all $s, u, v$ — which holds automatically in a 1-factor Gaussian model such as Vasicek.

Worked Example: Vasicek Model

The Vasicek short rate model is a Gaussian HJM model with volatility $\sigma(t, T) = e^{-\kappa(T-t)}\sigma$. Integrating both formulations gives their explicit analytical expressions:

  • Exact Closed-form Convexity Adjustment (yielding 0.30 bps):

    $$ \gamma(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)}\left[\exp\!\left\{\frac{\sigma^2}{\kappa^3}\left(1-e^{-\kappa(T_1-T_0)}\right)\!\left[ (1 - e^{-\kappa(T_0-t)}) - \frac{1}{2} e^{-\kappa(T_1-T_0)} (1-e^{-2\kappa(T_0-t)}) \right] \right\} - 1\right] $$

  • Approximate Closed-form Convexity Adjustment (yielding 0.21 bps):

    $$ \gamma_{\text{approx}}(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(t,T_0)}{P(t,T_1)}\left[\exp\!\left\{\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^3}\left(1-e^{-\kappa(T_1-T_0)}\right)\!\left(1-e^{-\kappa(T_0-t)}\right)^2\right\} - 1\right] $$

Key Results from Lecture

  • For $T_0 - t$ up to 5 years, the convexity adjustment is less than 1 basis point (i.e., < 0.01%).
  • At fixed $T_0 - t = 6$ months, the adjustment grows quadratically in $\sigma$.
  • At $\sigma = 2\%$, $\gamma \approx 0.6$ bps — small but important in high-precision pricing contexts.
  • $\gamma$ is always non-negative in the Vasicek model since $\sigma(s,u)\sigma(s,v) = e^{-\kappa(u-s)}e^{-\kappa(v-s)}\sigma^2 > 0$.

Forward Rate vs. Futures Rate: Key Comparison

Feature Forward Rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ Futures Rate $R_\text{futures}(t, T_0, T_1)$
Martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$ (forward measure) $\mathbb{Q}$ (risk-neutral)
Expectation formula $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}}_t[L(T_0, T_1)]$ $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t[L(T_0, T_1)]$
Cash flow timing Single payment at $T_1$ Daily mark-to-market
Collateral / margin None (bilateral contract) Daily variation margin
Relative size Smaller (in Gaussian models) $R_\text{futures} = F + \gamma \ge F$

Practice: Compute the Convexity Adjustment

Using the Vasicek formula, compute the convexity adjustment $\gamma(0, T_0, T_1)$ for the following parameters:

  • $\kappa = 0.86$, $\sigma = 0.01$, $r(0) = 0.06$, $\theta = 0.08$
  • Tenor $\delta = T_1 - T_0 = \tfrac{1}{4}$ (3-month period)
  • Time to maturity $T_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (6 months)
Show Solution

First compute the bond price ratio $P(0,T_0)/P(0,T_1)$ using affine bond pricing. Then compute:

$$ \text{Exponent} = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^3}\left(1-e^{-\kappa\delta}\right)\!\left(1-e^{-\kappa T_0}\right)^2 $$

Plugging in: $\frac{(0.01)^2}{2(0.86)^3}(1-e^{-0.86/4})(1-e^{-0.86/2})^2 \approx 7.95 \times 10^{-6}$

So $e^\text{Exponent} - 1 \approx 7.95 \times 10^{-6}$ (very small!)

Multiplying by $\frac{1}{\delta}\frac{P(0,T_0)}{P(0,T_1)} \approx 4 \times 1.016 \approx 4.06$, we get:

$\gamma(0, T_0, T_1) \approx 3.23 \times 10^{-5} \approx 0.32$ basis points

This confirms the lecture's result: the convexity adjustment is less than 1 basis point for short maturities.
