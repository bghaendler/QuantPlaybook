Applied Stochastic Calculus II

Itô's Hierarchy, SDE Solutions, and Advanced Stochastic Integration

Itô I & II (1D BM)

Evaluates functions of time and standard Brownian motion $F(t, X_t)$:

$$dF = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial X^2}\right)dt + \frac{\partial F}{\partial X} dX$$

Itô III & IV (GBM Stock)

Evaluates functions of time and GBM asset price $V(t, S_t)$ where $dS = \mu S dt + \sigma S dX$:

$$dV = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}\right)dt + \sigma S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dX$$

Itô V & VI (Multi-Factor)

Generalizes to general processes and multi-factor contract structures $V(t, S_1, S_2)$ with correlation $\rho$:

$$dV = \left(\dots + \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 S_1 S_2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S_1 \partial S_2}\right)dt + \dots$$

Closed-Form Analytical Solutions

Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)

Derived using Itô III on $V(S) = \log S$:

$$S_t = S_0 \exp\left( \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t \right)$$

Vasicek Interest Rate Model

Derived via integrating factor $e^{\gamma t}$ on $dr = \gamma(\bar{r}-r)dt + \sigma dW_t$:

$$r_t = \bar{r} + (r_0 - \bar{r})e^{-\gamma t} + \sigma \int_0^t e^{\gamma(s-t)} dW_s$$

Problem A: Stochastic Differential Equation Calibration

Calibrate a smooth function $g(S)$ of a stochastic process $dS_t = B(S_t, t)dt + A(S_t, t)dW_t$ to have zero drift but active volatility, and derive the governing ODE.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Apply standard Itô's Lemma to $g(S)$: Since $g$ is a function of a stochastic process $S_t$, we write: $$dg(S_t) = \left( B(S_t, t) \frac{dg}{dS} + \frac{1}{2} A^2(S_t, t) \frac{d^2g}{dS^2} \right) dt + A(S_t, t) \frac{dg}{dS} dW_t$$
  2. Enforce Zero-Drift Condition: For the transformed process $dg(S)$ to have zero deterministic drift, we must force the coefficient of the $dt$ component to zero: $$B(S_t, t) \frac{dg}{dS} + \frac{1}{2} A^2(S_t, t) \frac{d^2g}{dS^2} = 0$$
  3. Reformulate as a Second-Order Linear ODE: Letting $g'(S) = \psi(S)$ simplifies the equation into a separable first-order ODE: $$B(S) \psi(S) + \frac{1}{2} A^2(S) \psi'(S) = 0 \implies \frac{\psi'(S)}{\psi(S)} = -\frac{2B(S)}{A^2(S)}$$
  4. Integrate to find the Calibration Function $g(S)$: $$\ln \psi(S) = -2\int \frac{B(S)}{A^2(S)} dS \implies \psi(S) = C_1 \exp\left( -2\int \frac{B(S)}{A^2(S)} dS \right)$$ Integrating once more yields the final spatial calibration formula: $$g(S) = C_1 \int \exp\left( -2\int \frac{B(u)}{A^2(u)} du \right) dS + C_2$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why apply Itô's Lemma first? The asset price moves randomly. If we want to construct a derivative contract or transform the price ($g(S)$), we need to see how this new contract changes. Itô's Lemma is the mathematical tool that splits these changes into a smooth conveyor belt (drift) and a random shaking (volatility).

2. Why force the drift to zero? We want to design a contract that is a "fair bet" (a martingale). In a fair game, there is no smooth conveyor belt pushing your average returns up or down over time, so we set the drift terms to zero.

3. What does the ODE represent? It is a balance condition. It states that the smooth drift ($B$) of the asset must be perfectly balanced by the curvature of our contract ($g''$) multiplied by the volatility ($A^2$). This is a classic "variance drag" balancing act.

4. Why solve for $g(S)$? Solving this ODE tells us the exact shape or formula our contract must have to remain driftless. This is extremely useful for risk-management and pricing structured financial contracts.

Problem B: Advanced Stochastic Integration

Evaluate the stochastic integral $\int_{0}^{t} W_s (1 - e^{-W_s^2}) dW_s$ in closed analytical form, and verify its statistical convergence numerically.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Define target function $F(W, t)$ to match integrand: We seek $F$ such that $\frac{\partial F}{\partial W} = W(1 - e^{-W^2}) = W - W e^{-W^2}$. Integrating with respect to $W$: $$F(W, t) = \frac{1}{2}W^2 + \frac{1}{2}e^{-W^2}$$
  2. Compute Necessary Derivatives of $F$: $$\frac{\partial F}{\partial t} = 0 \quad , \quad \frac{\partial F}{\partial W} = W - W e^{-W^2}$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial W^2} = 1 - \left( e^{-W^2} - 2W^2 e^{-W^2} \right) = 1 - e^{-W^2} + 2W^2 e^{-W^2}$$
  3. Apply Stochastic Integral Formula: $$\int_{0}^{t} \frac{\partial F}{\partial W} dW_s = F(W_t, t) - F(W_0, 0) - \int_{0}^{t} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial s} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial W^2} \right) ds$$ Substituting our derivatives: $$\int_{0}^{t} W_s(1 - e^{-W_s^2}) dW_s = \left(\frac{1}{2}W_t^2 + \frac{1}{2}e^{-W_t^2}\right) - \left(0 + \frac{1}{2}\right) - \int_{0}^{t} \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - e^{-W_s^2} + 2W_s^2 e^{-W_s^2}\right) ds$$
  4. State the final analytical exact closed form: $$\int_{0}^{t} W_s(1 - e^{-W_s^2}) dW_s = \frac{1}{2}W_t^2 + \frac{1}{2}e^{-W_t^2} - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{t} \left(1 - e^{-W_s^2} + 2W_s^2 e^{-W_s^2}\right) ds$$ This is validated numerically in the laboratory dashboard to high precision.
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why choose that specific starting function? In ordinary calculus, the integral of $x(1-e^{-x^2})dx$ is evaluated by parts or substitution to yield $\frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{2}e^{-x^2}$. We use this exact antiderivative function to serve as our base blueprint.

2. Why calculate the second derivative? Because stochastic calculus requires the second-order Itô expansion, we must evaluate the concavity (curvature) of our target function. This generates the unique stochastic correction term.

3. What does the final integral represent? The remaining time integral is non-stochastic (meaning it integrates over standard time $ds$, not $dW_s$). It represents the smooth variance drag that accumulates along the specific path the random walker takes.

4. Why verify it numerically? Advanced integrals are difficult to trust without empirical verification. The SDE laboratory dashboard simulates these paths in real-time and sums up thousands of daily steps, proving that the numerical Riemann sum perfectly converges to our analytical formula.

Problem C: Steady-State Fokker-Planck Relation

Prove that at steady state ($\frac{\partial p_\infty}{\partial t} = 0$), the Fokker-Planck (Forward Kolmogorov) equation integrates to yield the drift parameter relation: $u(r) = \nu^2\beta r^{2\beta-1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta}\frac{d}{dr}(\log p_\infty)$.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the Fokker-Planck Equation: For a general SDE $dr_t = u(r)dt + w(r)dW_t$ where $w(r) = \nu r^\beta$, the transition density $p$ satisfies: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left( u(r) p \right) + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2}\left( w^2(r) p \right)$$
  2. Set to Steady State and Integrate: At steady state, the temporal derivative is zero ($\frac{\partial p_\infty}{\partial t} = 0$). Integrating both sides once with respect to $r$ (setting boundary constants to zero): $$-u(r) p_\infty + \frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dr}\left( w^2(r) p_\infty \right) = 0 \implies u(r) p_\infty = \frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dr}\left( \nu^2 r^{2\beta} p_\infty \right)$$
  3. Expand the derivative via product rule: $$u(r) p_\infty = \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 \left( 2\beta r^{2\beta-1} p_\infty + r^{2\beta} \frac{dp_\infty}{dr} \right)$$ Dividing both sides by the probability density $p_\infty$: $$u(r) = \nu^2 \beta r^{2\beta-1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta} \frac{1}{p_\infty}\frac{dp_\infty}{dr}$$
  4. Substitute Logarithmic Derivative: Noting that $\frac{1}{p}\frac{dp}{dr} = \frac{d}{dr}(\log p)$ yields the final elegant drift relationship: $$u(r) = \nu^2 \beta r^{2\beta-1} + \frac{1}{2}\nu^2 r^{2\beta} \frac{d}{dr}(\log p_\infty)$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What does "steady state" mean? If you leave a random system running for a very long time, it eventually settles into a stable probability distribution. The probability peak stops moving around, meaning time changes are zero ($\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = 0$).

2. Why integrate once? The Fokker-Planck equation is a second-order PDE. Integrating it once simplifies it into a first-order equation representing a zero net-flow balance of probability.

3. Why use the product rule? Volatility depends on the rate level ($r^{2\beta}$). As the rate changes, the volatility strength changes. The product rule captures both the change in the rate's probability and the change in volatility strength itself.

4. Why substitute $\log p$? The ratio of probability slope to absolute probability level ($\frac{1}{p}\frac{dp}{dr}$) is exactly the derivative of the log probability. This mathematical substitution simplifies a messy ratio into a clean, elegant relation that is easy to analyze.
