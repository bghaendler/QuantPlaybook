Guest Lecture

The AI Revolution in Risk Management

Where machine learning genuinely improves risk practice, where it adds new risks, and how to deploy it without surrendering the governance discipline of the risk courses.

1. Where AI Adds Real Value

  • Nonlinear risk-factor modeling: ML captures interactions and regime structure that linear factor models miss — gradient-boosted trees and neural nets for PD estimation, fraud detection, and conditional volatility.
  • Faster risk computation: neural-network surrogates ("deep pricers") learn a calibrated pricing/XVA map, replacing expensive revaluation in VaR/CCR grids — orders-of-magnitude speedups (complementing the AAD/GPU work).
  • Alternative data: NLP on news/filings for early credit deterioration, satellite/transaction data for nowcasting — signals invisible to traditional models.
  • Anomaly detection: unsupervised methods flag unusual trading/positions — operational and rogue-trading risk (ARM Part 04) surfaced earlier.

2. The New Risks AI Brings

  • Model opacity: a black-box PD model fails SR 11-7 explainability — SHAP/LIME and surrogate models are partial fixes; regulators increasingly require interpretability for credit/capital decisions.
  • Overfitting & non-stationarity: ML excels at fitting the past; markets regime-shift (ARM Part 06). The 2007 quant quake is the cautionary tale — crowded learned signals correlate in stress.
  • Data & feedback risk: garbage-in at scale; and models that trade can move the very markets they predict (reflexivity).
  • Concentration/systemic risk: if everyone trains on the same data with the same architectures, models herd — a new channel for synchronized behavior.

3. Governance for ML Models

  • Extend model risk management (ARM Part 01): validation now covers training data, feature stability, and out-of-distribution behavior, not just parameters.
  • Challenger/champion frameworks and continuous monitoring of feature drift — ML models decay faster than parametric ones.
  • Benchmarking against interpretable baselines: if a logistic regression nearly matches the neural net, the explainability cost of the net isn't justified.
  • Human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions — the model informs, accountable humans decide (ARM Part 02's principle).

Key Takeaways

  • AI's wins: nonlinear factor modeling, deep-pricer surrogates, alternative-data signals, anomaly detection.
  • AI's risks: opacity, overfitting to non-stationary markets, feedback/reflexivity, and model herding.
  • Governance must extend to data, features and OOD behavior — with interpretable benchmarks and human accountability.
  • Net: a powerful complement to the quantitative risk toolkit, not a replacement for its discipline.
