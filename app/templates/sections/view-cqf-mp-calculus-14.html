1.4 Exponentials, Logarithms & Limit Rules

Exponential growth, inverse logarithms, limits of continuous compounding, and L'Hôpital's Rule.

1.4.1 Exponentials & Continuous Compounding

The transcendental number $e$ is defined as the continuous limit: $e = \lim_{n \to \infty} (1 + 1/n)^n \approx 2.718281828$. This constant describes continuous rate compounding in assets: $S_t = S_0 e^{rt}$.

The natural logarithm $\ln(x)$ is the inverse of $e^x$, satisfying $\ln(e^x) = e^{\ln(x)} = x$. Key algebraic identities include:

exey = ex+y, e⁰ = 1
log(xy) = log x + log y
log(x/y) = log x - log y
log(1/x) = -log x

Limits & Continuity: A function $f(x)$ is continuous at $x_0$ if the limit exists and equals $f(x_0)$. If the limit evaluates to an indeterminate form (e.g. $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$), apply L'Hôpital's Rule: $$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The exponential constant $e \approx 2.718$ is the natural limit of compounding interest as the number of compounding periods per year approaches infinity.

  • Natural Logarithm ($\ln(x)$): The mathematical inverse of $e^x$. If $e^{rt}$ scales money forward in time, taking the logarithm lets us solve for the time ($t$) or the interest rate ($r$) required to reach a specific target value.
  • Limit Intuition: Limits describe the height a function is heading towards as you get closer and closer to a target input, even if the function has a hole or is undefined at that exact point.

Worked Solutions: Limit Proofs

Exercise 1: L'Hôpital's Rule Indeterminate Form

Evaluate the limit $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x + 2x - 1}{3x}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule.

  1. Indeterminate form check: Substituting $x = 0$ yields: $$\text{Numerator: } \cos(0) + 2(0) - 1 = 0 \quad , \quad \text{Denominator: } 3(0) = 0$$ This results in the indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$. L'Hôpital is applicable!
  2. Differentiate: Let $f(x) = \cos x + 2x - 1 \implies f'(x) = -\sin x + 2$. Let $g(x) = 3x \implies g'(x) = 3$.
  3. Evaluate the Limit: $$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{-\sin x + 2}{3} = \frac{2}{3}$$
Exercise 2: Derivation of Continuous Compounding Limit

Prove that the continuous compounding limit holds: $\lim_{n \to \infty} P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} = P e^{rt}$ using logarithmic limits.

  1. Let $L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$. Take natural logarithm: $$\ln(L) = \lim_{n \to \infty} nt \ln\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)$$
  2. Substitute $x = 1/n \implies x \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$: $$\ln(L) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{t \ln(1 + rx)}{x}$$ Plugging in $x = 0$ yields $\frac{0}{0}$. Differentiate numerator and denominator: $$\ln(L) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\frac{tr}{1+rx}}{1} = tr$$
  3. Apply exponential inverse to yield $L = e^{rt}$, proving: $P e^{rt}$. $\blacksquare$
