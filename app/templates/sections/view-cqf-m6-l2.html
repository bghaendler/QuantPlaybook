Module 06: Fixed Income & Credit Lecture 02

L2: Stochastic Interest Rate Modeling

Spot Rate SDEs, One-Factor Bond Pricing Equation, Affine Pricing Theorems, and Two-Factor Systems

The Stochastic Spot Rate ($r$)

The instantaneous spot rate $r$ is a mathematical construct representing the yield on a bond of infinitesimal maturity [PDF1: 2]. Real-world proxies are short-maturity instruments, such as a one-month bond yield [PDF1: 2]. We govern the spot rate's random walk via a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) [PDF1: 2]: $$\mathrm{d}r = u(r, t)\mathrm{d}t + w(r, t)\mathrm{d}W_t$$ Where $u(r, t)$ is the real-world drift rate, and $w(r, t)$ represents the local interest rate volatility.

One-Factor Bond Pricing Equation

Because the spot rate $r$ is not a traded asset, we cannot hedge a bond with the underlying rate itself [PDF1: 2]. Instead, we construct a risk-free portfolio $\Pi = V_1 - \Delta V_2$ hedging a bond of maturity $T_1$ against another of maturity $T_2$ [PDF1: 2]. Applying Itô's Lemma, choosing $\Delta = \frac{\partial V_1}{\partial r} / \frac{\partial V_2}{\partial r}$ to eliminate $\mathrm{d}W_t$, and invoking no-arbitrage ($\mathrm{d}\Pi = r \Pi \mathrm{d}t$) separates maturities [PDF1: 3]: $$\frac{\frac{\partial V_1}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}w^2 \frac{\partial^2 V_1}{\partial r^2} - r V_1}{\frac{\partial V_1}{\partial r}} = \frac{\frac{\partial V_2}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}w^2 \frac{\partial^2 V_2}{\partial r^2} - r V_2}{\frac{\partial V_2}{\partial r}} = a(r, t)$$ This maturity-independent separation leads directly to the Bond Pricing Equation (BPE) [PDF1: 4]: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}w(r, t)^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial r^2} + \left( u(r, t) - \lambda(r, t) w(r, t) \right) \frac{\partial V}{\partial r} - r V = 0$$

The Market Price of Interest Rate Risk ($\lambda$)

The separation variable $a(r, t)$ is written without loss of generality in terms of the market price of interest rate risk $\lambda(r, t)$ [PDF1: 4]: $$a(r, t) = \lambda(r, t)w(r, t) - u(r, t)$$ Applying Itô's Lemma to an unhedged bond, its price return decomposes into the risk-free rate plus excess compensation per unit of stochastic risk [PDF1: 5]: $$\mathrm{d}V - r V \mathrm{d}t = w \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\left( \mathrm{d}W_t + \lambda(r, t)\mathrm{d}t \right)$$ Because bond prices are inversely related to interest rates ($\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} < 0$), the market price of risk $\lambda$ is typically negative.

Equities vs. Fixed Income Risk Neutrality

- For Traded Assets (Equities $S$): The real-world drift is replaced by the risk-free rate $r$ in the pricing PDE. The market price of risk is the Sharpe ratio: $$\lambda_S = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma}$$ - For Non-Traded Quantities (Interest Rates $r$): All pricing is done under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, where the real-world drift $u(r, t)$ is replaced by the risk-neutral drift: $$\text{Risk-Neutral Drift} = u(r, t) - \lambda(r, t)w(r, t)$$ $$\lambda_r = \frac{a(r,t) + u(r,t)}{w(r,t)}$$

Tractable Affine Pricing Term Structures

An interest rate model belongs to the affine class if the price of a zero-coupon bond (ZCB) can be expressed as an exponential linear function of the short rate [PDF1: 11]: $$V(r, t; T) = \exp\left( A(t; T) - r B(t; T) \right)$$ Where $A(t; T)$ and $B(t; T)$ are maturity-dependent functions.

The Affine Pricing Theorem

Substituting $V = \exp(A - r B)$ into the Bond Pricing Equation and differentiating twice with respect to $r$ yields the necessary and sufficient structural condition: * A spot rate model is affine if and only if both its risk-adjusted drift $(u - \lambda w)$ and local variance $w^2$ are linear in $r$ [PDF1: 12]: $$w^2 = \alpha(t)r + \beta(t)$$ $$u - \lambda w = \eta(t) - \gamma(t)r$$ Under these conditions, $A(t; T)$ and $B(t; T)$ are governed by two coupled ordinary differential equations (ODEs): $$\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} = \eta(t) B - \frac{1}{2}\beta(t) B^2$$ $$\frac{\partial B}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2}\alpha(t) B^2 + \gamma(t) B - 1$$ With the boundary conditions at maturity $T$ satisfying $V(r, T; T) = 1$: $A(T; T) = 0$ and $B(T; T) = 0$ [PDF1: 12].

A. The Vasicek Model (1977)

The Vasicek spot rate is mean-reverting and governed by [PDF1: 9]: $$\mathrm{d}r = (\eta - \gamma r)\mathrm{d}t + \beta^{1/2} \mathrm{d}W_t$$ Its analytical functions are solved directly from the affine ODEs [PDF1: 10]: $$B(t; T) = \frac{1}{\gamma}\left( 1 - e^{-\gamma(T-t)} \right)$$ $$A(t; T) = \frac{1}{\gamma^2}\left( B(t; T) - T + t \right)\left( \eta \gamma - \frac{1}{2}\beta \right) - \frac{\beta B(t; T)^2}{4\gamma}$$ Disadvantage: Under the Fokker-Planck steady-state distribution, $r_T$ is Normally distributed, meaning the spot rate can breach zero and become negative with positive probability [PDF1: 11].

B. The Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model (1985)

To resolve the negative rate problem, the CIR model scales local variance by the spot rate itself [PDF1: 47]: $$\mathrm{d}r = (\eta - \gamma r)\mathrm{d}t + \sqrt{\alpha r} \mathrm{d}W_t$$ The spot rate remains strictly positive and bounded if the Feller Condition is satisfied [PDF1: 47]: $$\eta > \alpha/2$$ The lower boundary $r=0$ is non-attainable if the steady-state parameter $k > 1$ [PDF1: 67]: $$k = \frac{2\eta}{\alpha} + \frac{2\beta\gamma}{\alpha^2} > 1$$

C. The Ho & Lee Model (1986)

The first no-arbitrage model, designed to perfectly fit the initial market yield curve $Z_M(t^*; T)$ by using a time-dependent drift $\eta(t)$ [PDF1: 18]: $$\mathrm{d}r = \eta(t)\mathrm{d}t + \beta^{1/2}\mathrm{d}W_t$$ Applying Leibniz's integration rule twice on the market curve logs solves the inverse problem, yielding the calibrated parameter [PDF1: 20]: $$\eta^*(t) = -\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\log\left( Z_M(t^*; t) \right) + \beta(t - t^*)$$

Multi-Factor Systems & Two-Factor PDEs

One-Factor Limitations: A single spot rate model assumes all yields are perfectly correlated to a single random source $r$, which fails to capture non-parallel shifts, tilts, or independent long/short-rate dynamics [PDF1: 21]. Two-Factor SDEs: We model two correlated stochastic variables (short-rate $r$ and consol long-rate or variance $l$) [PDF1: 21]: $$\mathrm{d}r = u \mathrm{d}t + w \mathrm{d}W_1, \quad \mathrm{d}l = p \mathrm{d}t + q \mathrm{d}W_2 \quad (\text{with } \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{d}W_1\mathrm{d}W_2] = \rho\mathrm{d}t)$$

Two-Factor Bond Pricing PDE

To eliminate both sources of randomness ($\mathrm{d}W_1, \mathrm{d}W_2$), we construct a portfolio using three bonds of different maturities [PDF1: 21]. Under no-arbitrage, the consistency determinant of the 3x2 matrix must equal zero, yielding two market prices of risk ($\lambda_r, \lambda_l$) and the Two-Factor Bond Pricing PDE [PDF1: 23, 24]: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}w^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial r^2} + \rho w q \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial r \partial l} + \frac{1}{2}q^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial l^2} + (u - \lambda_r w)\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} + (p - \lambda_l q)\frac{\partial V}{\partial l} - r V = 0$$ * Brennan & Schwartz Model (1982): Mean-reverts short rate $r$ to consolidations long-rate $l$ [PDF1: 25]. Due to lognormal form, no analytical pricing solutions exist, and simulations can blow up. * Fong & Vasicek (1991): Short-rate variance $\xi$ follows its own mean-reverting square-root process. * Longstaff & Schwartz (1992): Models two independent square-root factors ($x, y$), expressing the spot rate linearly: $r = c x + d y$ [PDF1: 25].
