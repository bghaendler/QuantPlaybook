4. Integrals & Complex Analysis

Definite integrals, substitution pitfalls, complex numbers conjugates, and Euler's series expansion proofs.

1.10 Definite Integrals & Substitution Pitfalls

The definite integral $\int_a^b f(x)dx$ represents the signed area under $f(x)$ between boundaries. Since the variable of integration is a "dummy" variable:

$$\int_a^b f(x)dx = \int_a^b f(y)dy$$

Substitution Rule for Definite Integrals: Must transform boundary limits. When letting $z = g(x) \implies dz = g'(x)dx$:

$$\int_a^b f(g(x))g'(x)dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(z)dz$$

λ CRITICAL WARNING: Substitutions like $z = x^2$ fail on intervals containing $x=0$ (e.g. $[-1,1]$) because $dz/dx = 2x = 0$ at the origin, breaking invertibility and changing signs! Always verify $dz/dx \ne 0$ across the integration range.

1.11 Complex Algebra & Euler Integral Proofs

For any complex number $z = x + iy \in \mathbb{C}$, its complex conjugate is $\bar{z} = x - iy$, representing a reflection in the real axis. Key algebraic identities:

Re z = (z + z̄)/2        Im z = (z - z̄)/(2i)
z z̄ = |z|² = x² + y²     |z₁ z₂| = |z₁||z₂|

Euler's Series Expansion Proof: Substituting $x = i\theta$ in the Maclaurin series of $e^x$ and grouping real/imaginary parts proves Euler's formula:

$$e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \sum_{even} \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k}}{(2k)!} + i\sum_{odd} \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Application to Integrals: We can solve complex trigonometric integrals like $\int e^x \sin x \, dx$ cleanly by taking the imaginary component: $\int e^x \text{Im}[e^{ix}]dx = \text{Im}\left[\frac{e^{(1+i)x}}{1+i}\right] = \frac{1}{2}e^x(\sin x - \cos x)$.
