Question 1 Multiple Choice

Consider two CIR-type processes $X_1(t)$ and $X_2(t)$ with dynamics of the form $$dX_i(t) = \kappa_i(\theta_i - X_i(t)) dt + \sigma_i\sqrt{X_i(t)} dW_i(t)$$ for mean-reversion level and speed parameters $\theta_i$ and $\kappa_i$, respectively, volatility parameters $\sigma_i$, and where $W_1(t)$ and $W_2(t)$ are two independent standard Brownian motions. Which of the following statements about the sum $X(t) = X_1(t) + X_2(t)$ is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

Let's define the sum process $X(t) = X_1(t) + X_2(t)$. Applying the linearity of stochastic differentials, the SDE of the sum is:

$$ dX(t) = dX_1(t) + dX_2(t) = \left[ \kappa_1(\theta_1 - X_1(t)) + \kappa_2(\theta_2 - X_2(t)) \right] dt + \sigma_1 \sqrt{X_1(t)} dW_1(t) + \sigma_2 \sqrt{X_2(t)} dW_2(t) $$

For $X(t)$ to be a valid CIR-type process, it must satisfy two conditions:

  1. Drift Condition: The drift must be of the form $\kappa_X(\theta_X - X(t)) = \kappa_X(\theta_X - (X_1(t) + X_2(t)))$. Comparing this to the actual drift: $$\kappa_1\theta_1 + \kappa_2\theta_2 - \kappa_1 X_1(t) - \kappa_2 X_2(t) \equiv \kappa_X\theta_X - \kappa_X X_1(t) - \kappa_X X_2(t)$$ This identity holds for all values of $X_1(t)$ and $X_2(t)$ if and only if the speed parameters are identical: $$\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = \kappa_X$$ Under this restriction, the drift simplifies to $\kappa_1((\theta_1 + \theta_2) - X(t))$, which matches the CIR drift form with speed $\kappa_1$ and long-term mean $\theta_1 + \theta_2$.
  2. Diffusion Condition (Quadratic Variation): The diffusion term must be of the form $\sigma_X \sqrt{X(t)} dW^*(t)$ for a new Brownian motion $W^*(t)$. Its quadratic variation must satisfy: $$ d[X, X]_t = \sigma_X^2 X(t) dt = \sigma_X^2 (X_1(t) + X_2(t)) dt $$ Using the independence of $W_1(t)$ and $W_2(t)$ ($d[W_1, W_2]_t = 0$), the actual quadratic variation of the sum's diffusion is: $$ d[X, X]_t = \sigma_1^2 X_1(t) dt + \sigma_2^2 X_2(t) dt $$ Comparing the coefficients of $X_1(t)$ and $X_2(t)$, we must have: $$\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2 = \sigma_X^2 \implies \sigma_1 = \sigma_2$$

Thus, $X(t)$ is a CIR process if and only if the speed parameters are identical ($\kappa_1 = \kappa_2$) and the volatility parameters are identical ($\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$).

Correct Answer: $X(t)$ is a CIR-type process if $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2$ and $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$.

Question 2 Multiple Choice

Consider the Vasicek model for the short rate $r(t)$: $$dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t)) dt + \sigma dW^*(t)$$ where $W^*(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion under the risk-neutral probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\kappa > 0$. Which of the following statements about the infinite maturity yield is correct?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

Under the Vasicek model, zero-coupon bond prices are exponential affine: $P(t, T) = \exp(A(t, T) - B(t, T)r(t))$, where $\tau = T-t$ and the pre-calculated sensitivity functions are:

$$ B(t, T) = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa\tau}}{\kappa} $$ $$ A(t, T) = \left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(B(t, T) - \tau) - \frac{\sigma^2}{4\kappa}B(t, T)^2 $$

The continuously compounded yield to maturity $y(t, T)$ is given by:

$$ y(t, T) = -\frac{\ln P(t, T)}{T - t} = \frac{B(t, T)r(t) - A(t, T)}{\tau} $$

Now, let's analyze the limit of each term as maturity goes to infinity ($\tau \to \infty$):

  • Short Rate Term: Since $\lim_{\tau \to \infty} B(t, T) = \frac{1}{\kappa}$, the short rate coefficient vanishes: $$\lim_{\tau \to \infty} \frac{B(t, T)r(t)}{\tau} = \lim_{\tau \to \infty} \frac{r(t)}{\kappa \tau} = 0$$
  • Yield Factor Term: Substituting the definition of $A(t, T)$: $$\lim_{\tau \to \infty} \frac{-A(t, T)}{\tau} = \lim_{\tau \to \infty} \left[ -\left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)\left(\frac{B(t,T)}{\tau} - 1\right) + \frac{\sigma^2}{4\kappa} \frac{B(t,T)^2}{\tau} \right]$$ Substituting $\lim_{\tau \to \infty} \frac{B(t, T)}{\tau} = 0$ and $\lim_{\tau \to \infty} B(t, T)^2 = \frac{1}{\kappa^2}$: $$\lim_{\tau \to \infty} \frac{-A(t, T)}{\tau} = -\left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(-1) + 0 = \theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}$$

Combining the terms yields the infinite maturity yield limit:

$$ \lim_{T \to \infty} y(t, T) = \theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2} $$

Correct Answer: $\lim_{T \to \infty} y(t, T) = \theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}$

One-Period Forward Curve Arbitrage Construction (Q3, Q4, Q5)

Consider the following one-period model for the forward curve: $$f(0, T) = 0.04, \quad T \ge 0$$ $$f(\omega, 1, T) = \begin{cases} 0.06, & T \ge 1, \omega = \omega_1 \\ 0.02, & T \ge 1, \omega = \omega_2 \end{cases}$$ where $\Omega = \{\omega_1, \omega_2\}$ with objective probabilities $\mathbb{P}[\omega_i] > 0, i = 1, 2$.

Show that this model allows for arbitrage. Construct a portfolio consisting of three zero-coupon bonds maturing in 1, 2, and 3 years with initial value zero and such that its value at time $t = 1$ equals 1 in both states $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$. Answer the following three questions (Q3, Q4, and Q5).

Question 3 Numerical Input

a) How many units of the zero-coupon bond maturing in 1 year do you need? Round your answer to the nearest integer.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Portfolio Solver:

First, let's calculate the pricing of the zero-coupon bonds $P(t, T)$ using the forward rate relationship $P(t, T) = \exp\left( -\int_t^T f(t, s) ds \right)$:

  • At time $t = 0$: $$P(0, 1) = e^{-0.04 \times 1} = e^{-0.04} \approx 0.960789$$ $$P(0, 2) = e^{-0.04 \times 2} = e^{-0.08} \approx 0.923116$$ $$P(0, 3) = e^{-0.04 \times 3} = e^{-0.12} \approx 0.886920$$
  • At time $t = 1$ (where the 1-year bond matures: $P(1, 1) = 1$):
    • State $\omega_1$: $$P(\omega_1, 1, 2) = e^{-0.06 \times (2 - 1)} = e^{-0.06} \approx 0.941765$$ $$P(\omega_1, 1, 3) = e^{-0.06 \times (3 - 1)} = e^{-0.12} \approx 0.886920$$
    • State $\omega_2$: $$P(\omega_2, 1, 2) = e^{-0.02 \times (2 - 1)} = e^{-0.02} \approx 0.980199$$ $$P(\omega_2, 1, 3) = e^{-0.02 \times (3 - 1)} = e^{-0.04} \approx 0.960789$$

Let $h_1, h_2, h_3$ be the units of 1-year, 2-year, and 3-year bonds respectively. We form a system of 3 linear equations based on the portfolio conditions:

  1. Initial Cost is Zero: $$ h_1 P(0, 1) + h_2 P(0, 2) + h_3 P(0, 3) = 0 \implies h_1 e^{-0.04} + h_2 e^{-0.08} + h_3 e^{-0.12} = 0 \quad \text{--- (Eq 1)} $$
  2. Portfolio Value at $t=1$ in State $\omega_1$ is 1: $$ h_1 \cdot 1 + h_2 e^{-0.06} + h_3 e^{-0.12} = 1 \quad \text{--- (Eq 2)} $$
  3. Portfolio Value at $t=1$ in State $\omega_2$ is 1: $$ h_1 \cdot 1 + h_2 e^{-0.02} + h_3 e^{-0.04} = 1 \quad \text{--- (Eq 3)} $$

To solve this system:

  • Subtract Eq 2 from Eq 3 to eliminate $h_1$: $$ h_2 \left( e^{-0.02} - e^{-0.06} \right) + h_3 \left( e^{-0.04} - e^{-0.12} \right) = 0 $$ $$ h_2 (0.038434) + h_3 (0.073869) = 0 \implies h_2 \approx -1.921963 h_3 $$
  • From Eq 1, express $h_1$ in terms of $h_2$ and $h_3$: $$ h_1 = -h_2 e^{-0.04} - h_3 e^{-0.08} $$
  • Substitute $h_1$ into Eq 2: $$ -h_2 e^{-0.04} - h_3 e^{-0.08} + h_2 e^{-0.06} + h_3 e^{-0.12} = 1 \implies h_2 \left( e^{-0.06} - e^{-0.04} \right) + h_3 \left( e^{-0.12} - e^{-0.08} \right) = 1 $$
  • Substitute $h_2 \approx -1.921963 h_3$: $$ h_3 \left[ -1.921963 \left( e^{-0.06} - e^{-0.04} \right) + \left( e^{-0.12} - e^{-0.08} \right) \right] = 1 $$ $$ h_3 \left[ -1.921963 \times (-0.019025) - 0.036196 \right] = 1 $$ $$ h_3 \left[ 0.036565 - 0.036196 \right] = 1 \implies 0.000369 \times h_3 = 1 \implies h_3 \approx 2708.13 $$
  • Solve for $h_2$ and $h_1$: $$ h_2 \approx -1.921963 \times 2708.13 \approx -5204.92 $$ $$ h_1 = -(-5204.92) e^{-0.04} - 2708.13 e^{-0.08} \approx 2500.92 $$

Rounding to the nearest integer, we find:

$h_1 = 2501$ units

Correct Answer: 2501

Question 6 Numerical Input

Consider the following Vasicek model for the short rate $r(t)$: $$dr(t) = 0.86(0.08 - r(t))dt + 0.01 dW^*(t), \quad r(0) = 6\%,$$ where $W^*(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion under the risk-neutral probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$. Compute the time-0 price of a put option on the 6-month bond ($T_1 = 1/2$) with expiry date in 3 months ($T_0 = 1/4$) and ATM strike price $K_1 = P(0, T_1)/P(0, T_0)$. Express your answer in basis points and round to 2 decimal places.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Pricing Derivation:

The price of a European put option with expiry $T_0$ and strike $K$ on a zero-coupon bond maturing at $T_1$ under the Vasicek model is given by the standard pricing formula:

$$ Put(0, T_0, T_1, K) = K P(0, T_0) N(-d_2) - P(0, T_1) N(-d_1) $$

where the standardized limits are:

$$ d_1 = \frac{\ln\left( \frac{P(0, T_1)}{K P(0, T_0)} \right) + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_p}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma_p $$

Because the option is struck exactly at-the-money (ATM), we are given $K = \frac{P(0, T_1)}{P(0, T_0)}$. This simplifies the equations dramatically:

  1. Strike Product: $K P(0, T_0) = \frac{P(0, T_1)}{P(0, T_0)} P(0, T_0) = P(0, T_1)$.
  2. Log Term: $\ln\left( \frac{P(0, T_1)}{K P(0, T_0)} \right) = \ln(1) = 0$.
  3. Standardized Limits: $d_1 = \frac{1}{2}\sigma_p$ and $d_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\sigma_p$.

Substituting these back into the put option pricing formula yields:

$$ Put(0, T_0, T_1, K) = P(0, T_1) \left( N\left( \frac{1}{2}\sigma_p \right) - N\left( -\frac{1}{2}\sigma_p \right) \right) = P(0, T_1) \left( 2 N\left( \frac{1}{2}\sigma_p \right) - 1 \right) $$

Now, let's compute the parameter values step-by-step using $\kappa = 0.86, \theta = 0.08, \sigma = 0.01, r(0) = 0.06, T_0 = 0.25, T_1 = 0.50$:

  • Bond Volatility Component ($B(T_0, T_1)$): Since Vasicek is time-homogeneous, $B(T_0, T_1) = B(0, T_1 - T_0) = B(0.25)$: $$ B(0.25) = \frac{1 - e^{-0.86 \times 0.25}}{0.86} = \frac{1 - e^{-0.215}}{0.86} \approx 0.224952 $$
  • Integrated Option Volatility ($\sigma_p$): The variance of the short rate at $T_0$ is: $$ Var_0(r(T_0)) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}\left( 1 - e^{-2\kappa T_0} \right) = \frac{0.01^2}{1.72}\left( 1 - e^{-0.43} \right) \approx 0.000020319 $$ $$ \sigma_p = B(T_0, T_1) \sqrt{Var_0(r(T_0))} \approx 0.224952 \times 0.0045077 \approx 0.00101401 $$
  • Zero-Coupon Bond Price ($P(0, T_1)$): We evaluate $P(0, 0.50) = e^{A(0.50) - B(0.50)r(0)}$: $$ B(0.50) = \frac{1 - e^{-0.43}}{0.86} \approx 0.406385 $$ $$ A(0.50) = \left(0.08 - \frac{0.01^2}{2 \times 0.86^2}\right)(B(0.50) - 0.50) - \frac{0.01^2}{4 \times 0.86}B(0.50)^2 \approx -0.007488 $$ $$ P(0, 0.50) \approx e^{-0.007488 - 0.406385 \times 0.06} = e^{-0.031871} \approx 0.968632 $$
  • Put Option Price: $$ Put = 0.968632 \times \left( 2 N(0.000507) - 1 \right) \approx 0.968632 \times 0.0004045 \approx 0.00039184 \text{ of par} $$
  • Converting to Basis Points (bps): $$ Put \text{ in bps} = 0.00039184 \times 10,000 \approx 3.9184 \text{ bps} $$

Rounding to 2 decimal places, we get $3.92$ basis points.

Correct Answer: 3.92
