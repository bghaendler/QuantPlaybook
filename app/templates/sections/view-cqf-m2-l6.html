Origin and Nature of the Basel Accords

The Basel Framework is the primary global standard for the regulation of banks. Formulated by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), the framework is housed inside the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) in Basel, Switzerland—a historic city strategically situated at the intersection of Switzerland, France, and Germany. The BIS acts as the coordinate center for central banks worldwide.

📌 Important Distinction: The Basel Committee has no formal legislative authority or treaty mandate. Its guidelines are not legally binding laws; rather, they are highly influential regulatory recommendations. Member countries are expected to adapt and codify these rules into local statutory law (such as the Capital Requirements Regulation CRR/CRR2 in the European Union, or Dodd-Frank rules in the United States).

A defining characteristic of financial regulation is its ever-increasing complexity. The first capital accord (Basel I, 1988) was approximately 30 pages long. The newest ruleset (Basel IV) exceeds 1,900 pages, representing a massive expansion in administrative requirements:

"European banks must observe approximately 40,000 legally binding requirements... No single banking supervisor or practitioner can fully explain all the rules and their consequences."
— Prof. Dr. Herman Schulte-Mathier, PwC Basel IV study

Brief History of Regulation

Regulating financial operations is a historical cycle of speculative excesses followed by restrictive government response:

  • 1610: Dutch Short-Selling Ban: The earliest recorded ban on short selling, enacted by Dutch authorities to protect speculative East India Company pricing, which did not prevent the subsequent Tulip Mania.
  • 1933: Glass-Steagall Act: Enacted in the US after the 1929 stock market crash to legally separate commercial banks (which accept deposits) from investment banks (which underwriting/trade securities).
  • 1999: Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act: Repealed Glass-Steagall's investment restrictions, paving the way for massive financial conglomerates, cited by many economists as a contributor to the 2007-08 global financial crisis.

Bank Balance Sheets, Leverage, and Risk-Weights

Under the theoretical Modigliani-Miller Theorem (1958), in frictionless markets (no taxes, default costs, or asymmetric information), a firm's value is completely independent of its capital structure. In the real world, however, leverage amplifies insolvency risk. Equity serves as the critical loss-absorbing buffer that stands between a bank and default.

The Transition to Risk-Weighted Assets (RWAs)

The Basel Committee argues that a simple leverage ratio is insufficient because it treats a safe cash asset the same as a speculative derivative. Basel requires banks to apply regulatory risk-weights to their assets to calculate Risk-Weighted Assets (RWAs):

  • Sovereign Sovereign Debt: 0% weight
  • Residential Mortgages: 35% weight
  • Corporate Loans: 75% - 100% weight
  • High-risk Securitizations: 1,200% weight

Pillar 1 RWA Allocation: Where is Bank Risk Concentrated?

Under Pillar 1, risk-weighted assets are split among Credit Risk, Operational Risk, and Market Risk. In typical commercial banks, credit risk is by far the largest contributor, followed surprisingly by operational risk in second place, with market risk third. Operational risk captures a broad range of events including lawsuits, compliance fines, computer crashes, and internal/external fraud.

Historical Case Studies of Bank Failures

Understanding the diverse paths to collapse illustrates why multi-faceted regulatory standards (incorporating leverage, operational controls, market risks, and liquidity maturity profiles) are vital:

1. Continental Illinois (1984)

Mechanism: Credit Defaults + Liquidity Run.
A rapid accumulation of bad commercial oil/gas loans severely compromised the bank's asset base. Once corporate depositors realized the scale of the loss, they initiated a massive electronic run, requiring a $5 billion US federal intervention. Coined the phrase "Too Big to Fail."

2. Barings Bank (1995)

Mechanism: Operational Risk & Rogue Trading.
Nick Leeson, a futures trader operating in Singapore, exploited internal control gaps to hide massive speculative losses in account `88888`. He bet on the Nikkei index recovering; after the Kobe earthquake hit, losses reached £827 million, bankrupting the historic 233-year-old bank, sold for £1.

3. Lehman Brothers (2008)

Mechanism: Leverage + Mortgage Asset Writedowns.
Lehman operated with an extreme leverage ratio of 31 (meaning assets exceeded equity 31-fold). A small drop in their subprime-backed real estate assets wiped out their thin equity base, making them insolvent and incapable of answering overnight repo margin calls.

4. Silicon Valley Bank (2023)

Mechanism: Duration Risk + Social Media Run.
SVB parked tech deposits into long-term US Treasuries at ultra-low rates. When the Fed aggressively hiked rates, these bonds fell in value. After SVB announced a capital raise, venture capitalists triggered a viral, social-media-fueled depositor bank run.

Credit Risk & Vasicek's Asymptotic Single Risk Factor (ASRF)

Under the Basel Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach, banks calculate required regulatory credit capital using formulas derived directly from Vasicek's ASRF model. The model assumes a highly diversified loan portfolio exposed to a single common systematic macroeconomic factor.

Step 1 Asset Value Dynamics & Latent Factor Model

Let the standardized asset value of borrower $i$ be driven by a systematic global macroeconomic factor $Z \sim N(0,1)$ and an independent idiosyncratic shock $Y_i \sim N(0,1)$:

$$X_i = \sqrt{\rho} Z + \sqrt{1 - \rho} Y_i$$

Here, $\rho \in (0,1)$ represents the Asset Correlation (the proportion of variance explained by the systemic macroeconomy). A borrower defaults if their asset value $X_i$ falls below an unconditional default threshold $\Phi^{-1}(p)$, where $p$ is the unconditional Expected Default Probability (PD).

Step 2 Conditional Probability of Default $\tilde{p}(z)$

Conditioning on the realized macroeconomic state $Z = z$, the event of default becomes independent across borrowers. The conditional probability of default for borrower $i$ is:

$$\tilde{p}(z) = P(X_i < \Phi^{-1}(p) \mid Z = z) = \Phi\left( \frac{\Phi^{-1}(p) - \sqrt{\rho}z}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}} \right)$$

As the portfolio size $N \to \infty$, the Law of Large Numbers ensures idiosyncratic shocks $Y_i$ completely diversify away. The aggregate portfolio loss converges almost surely to the conditional default probability $\tilde{p}(Z)$, transforming the credit loss from a discrete binomial sum to a smooth continuous limiting distribution.

Step 3 Unconditional Loss Distribution (Vasicek CDF)

By mapping the monotonic relationship between $Z$ and the portfolio loss $x$, we derive the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of portfolio credit losses:

$$F(x) = \Phi\left( \frac{\sqrt{1 - \rho}\Phi^{-1}(x) - \Phi^{-1}(p)}{\sqrt{\rho}} \right)$$

To ensure solvency at a strict $99.9\%$ confidence interval, Basel evaluates the worst-case default rate (WCDR) under a severe systematic economic contraction ($Z = -\Phi^{-1}(0.999) \approx -3.090$):

$$PD_i^{99.9\%} = \Phi\left( \frac{\Phi^{-1}(p) + \sqrt{\rho}\Phi^{-1}(0.999)}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}} \right)$$
Basel Capital Charge Calculation:
Subtracting the Expected Loss ($p$) gives the unexpected loss cushion. Multiplying by Loss Given Default (LGD) and a Maturity Adjustment (MA) yields the formal regulatory capital charge: $$\text{Capital} = \left[ PD_i^{99.9\%} - p \right] \times \text{LGD} \times \text{MA}$$

Basel III Liquidity Standards

The 2007-2008 global financial crisis proved that capital buffers are insufficient if a bank experiences a sudden, acute drain on liquid cash. Basel III introduced two specific quantitative liquidity requirements to prevent systemic insolvency:

1. Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

Horizon: 30 calendar days.
Ensures a bank has enough High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) to survive an acute stress scenario.

$$\text{LCR} = \frac{\text{Stock of HQLA}}{\text{Net cash outflows over 30 days}} \ge 100\%$$
  • Level 1 HQLA (0% Haircut, No Cap): Central bank reserves, cash, sovereign debt.
  • Level 2A HQLA (15% Haircut, Max 40% of HQLA): Highly rated corporate/covered bonds.
  • Level 2B HQLA (50% Haircut, Max 15% of HQLA): Speculative equities, lower-rated corporate debt.

2. Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

Horizon: 1 year.
Ensures a bank funds long-term assets (like mortgages and commercial loans) using stable, long-term liabilities.

$$\text{NSFR} = \frac{\text{Available Stable Funding (ASF)}}{\text{Required Stable Funding (RSF)}} \ge 100\%$$

ASF Tiers: Regulatory capital and Tier 1 equity count at 100%. Customer retail deposits count at 90-95%. Wholesale deposits count at 0-50%.
RSF Tiers: Long-term loans (>1yr) require 100% stable funding. Residential mortgages require 65%. Highly liquid cash requires 0%.

Real-World Liquidity Example (Barclays plc):
Barclays reports fully compliant and conservative liquidity indicators, demonstrating the practical application of Basel standards. In its recent disclosures, Barclays maintains a Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) of approximately 150% (comfortably exceeding the 100% regulatory floor) and a Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) of approximately 121%, signaling highly robust short-term cash reserves and stable, long-term asset funding coverage.
