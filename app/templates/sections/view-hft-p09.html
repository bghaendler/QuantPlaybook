High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Stock Diffusion Modeling with Numeric Boundaries

From discrete ticks and queues to diffusions — and back: tick-size boundaries, price bands, and the reflected/absorbed processes that respect them.

1. Discreteness Meets Diffusion

Microstructure prices live on a grid (tick size $\delta$), yet models and limits are continuous. The reconciliation:

  • Diffusion limits of queues: rescaled order-book queue sizes converge to (reflected) Brownian motions; mid-price changes — sums of many ±tick moves — aggregate to diffusions by CLT. The art is matching the diffusion's σ to tick-level dynamics: $\sigma^2_{eff} = \delta^2 \cdot f(\text{queue intensities})$ (Cont-de Larrard-type formulas relating price volatility to order-flow rates).
  • Tick-size regimes: large-tick names (spread ≈ 1 tick, queues deep) behave like queueing systems; small-tick names like diffusions with sparse books — one model family rarely fits both.
  • Microstructure noise: observed price = efficient diffusion + rounding/bounce noise; signature plots (RV vs. sampling frequency) expose the noise floor (Part 01's Roll logic).

2. Boundaries: Absorbing, Reflecting, and Banded

Real prices face hard numeric boundaries; the right boundary condition is a modeling decision:

BoundaryConditionMicrostructure instance
Absorbing$V(B) = $ value at stopQueue depletion (level disappears), stop-out levels, default-style exits
Reflecting$\partial_x V(B) = 0$LULD price bands while pegged, inventory limits of a market maker
Sticky / partially reflectingMixed (Robin)Limit-up states that trade at the band before resuming

The first-passage toolkit carries over verbatim from barrier options: reflection principle, method of images, and the exit-probability formulas — for driftless BM on $[0, B]$ started at $x$, $\mathbb{P}[\text{hit } B \text{ before } 0] = x/B$ (the gambler's-ruin identity used for queue-race odds: which side of the book depletes first).

3. Numerics That Respect Boundaries

  • Finite differences: impose the condition in the scheme — Dirichlet rows for absorbing, ghost-node mirror for reflecting; mis-typed boundaries are the classic source of "leaking probability" (check: total mass conservation under reflecting BCs).
  • Monte Carlo with barriers: discrete monitoring overestimates survival — apply the Brownian-bridge crossing probability $p = \exp(-2\Delta_1\Delta_2/(\sigma^2\Delta t))$ per step (the same correction as barrier-option pricing), or the Broadie-Glasserman-Kou $0.5826\,\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$ barrier shift.
  • Reflected paths: simulate via $|X_t|$ (driftless) or the Skorokhod construction $X_t = Y_t + L_t$ with local-time pushes; naive clipping at the boundary biases occupation times.
  • Grid alignment: place FD nodes on the boundary and on tick multiples — interpolating a band between nodes degrades convergence from $O(h^2)$ to $O(h)$.

4. Applications in the HFT Stack

  • Queue-race probabilities (fill vs. adverse tick) price the value of queue position — the core quantity behind join/cancel decisions in large-tick names.
  • Band-aware risk: LULD/limit-band proximity changes short-horizon return distributions from ~Gaussian to censored — position risk and option hedging near bands must use the truncated laws.
  • Inventory control previews Part 10: a market maker's inventory is a controlled diffusion between reflecting limits — the stochastic-control formulation needs exactly these boundary conditions.

Key Takeaways

  • Diffusions emerge from tick/queue dynamics; calibrate $\sigma_{eff}$ from order-flow intensities and respect tick-regime differences.
  • Absorbing vs. reflecting vs. sticky boundaries are modeling choices with distinct PDE conditions and distinct microstructure meanings.
  • Use barrier-pricing numerics: Brownian-bridge corrections, ghost nodes, Skorokhod reflection — never naive clipping.
  • Gambler's-ruin exit odds price queue races; banded diffusions underpin maker inventory control (next part).
