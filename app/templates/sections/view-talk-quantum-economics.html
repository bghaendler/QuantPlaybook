Let's Do Quantum Economics!

A provocative frontier: borrowing the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics — not quantum computers — to model decision-making, markets, and the genuinely non-classical behavior of human choice.

1. Quantum Formalism ≠ Quantum Hardware

"Quantum economics" mostly does not mean running economics on quantum computers (that's the quantum-pricing/ML talks). It means using the mathematical structures of quantum theory — superposition, interference, non-commuting observables, contextuality — as models for phenomena where classical probability fails to fit human behavior. The claim: some decision-making is better described by Hilbert-space probability than by Kolmogorov's.

2. Where Classical Probability Breaks (the Motivation)

  • Order effects: ask survey questions A-then-B vs B-then-A and answers differ — classically $P(A \cap B) = P(B \cap A)$, but human responses don't commute. Quantum models use non-commuting projectors naturally.
  • The conjunction fallacy & disjunction effect: Tversky-Kahneman violations of classical probability axioms that quantum-probability models (Busemeyer, Pothos) reproduce as interference effects.
  • Preference construction: people don't have fixed preferences they reveal — they're "measured into" a choice (superposition collapsing on decision), echoing measurement in QM.
  • Ellsberg/ambiguity: behavior under ambiguity (not just risk) fits quantum-like uncertainty better than expected utility.

3. Applications to Markets

  • Behavioral asset pricing: interference terms as a model for sentiment-driven mispricing and bubbles — beliefs in superposition until "measured" by events (loosely connecting to the sentiment-portfolio talk).
  • Quantum game theory: entangled strategies can change equilibria — a theoretical curiosity with speculative market-design implications.
  • Path-integral finance: Feynman's path-integral formalism (a quantum tool) recast for option pricing — mathematically a re-expression of the same expectations (Feynman-Kac, MM Part 06), occasionally a useful computational lens rather than new economics.

4. Critical Assessment

  • The honest case: quantum-probability models do fit certain cognitive-bias datasets better than classical ones, with fewer free parameters — a genuine descriptive contribution to behavioral economics.
  • The skeptical case: "fits the data with extra machinery" isn't "is the right theory"; much of it is metaphor or relabeling, and predictive (vs. retrodictive) success is thin. Occam's razor cuts hard here.
  • The quant takeaway: a stimulating reminder that human decision-making violates the rational-agent and classical-probability assumptions our models lean on (the AI-safety/behavioral talk's theme) — valuable as a critique of EMH/rationality more than as a deployable pricing tool. Hold it as an intriguing frontier, not a method to bet on.

Key Takeaways

  • Quantum economics borrows QM's math (superposition, non-commuting observables, interference), not quantum hardware.
  • It targets where classical probability fails human behavior: order effects, conjunction fallacy, constructed preferences, ambiguity.
  • Market applications (behavioral pricing, quantum games, path-integral finance) are mostly theoretical/speculative.
  • Genuine descriptive value for cognitive biases, but thin predictive power — an intriguing critique of rationality, not a trading method.
