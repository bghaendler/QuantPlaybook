Risk-Sensitive Investing — Part 04

Black-Litterman Benchmarking with Jumps

Taming the input-sensitivity of optimal allocation: equilibrium priors, view blending, and the jump-aware extension for tail-conscious benchmarking.

1. The Problem Black-Litterman Solves

Mean-variance and Kelly both invert $\Sigma$ and multiply by $\mu$ — a notorious error amplifier: tiny changes in expected-return estimates produce wild, often un-investable corner portfolios (the equities course's "Markowitz curse"). Black-Litterman (1992) fixes the inputs, not the optimizer.

2. The Two-Step Construction

  • Reverse-optimize for the equilibrium prior: assume the market-cap portfolio $w_{mkt}$ is optimal ⇒ implied returns $\Pi = \delta\Sigma w_{mkt}$ ($\delta$ = market risk aversion). This is the "neutral" anchor — hold the market if you have no views.
  • Blend in views ($P\mu = Q + \epsilon$, view uncertainty $\Omega$) via Bayesian updating:
$$\mu_{BL} = \left[(\tau\Sigma)^{-1} + P^\top\Omega^{-1}P\right]^{-1}\left[(\tau\Sigma)^{-1}\Pi + P^\top\Omega^{-1}Q\right].$$

The posterior shrinks toward equilibrium where views are weak (large Ω) and toward views where confident. Feeding $\mu_{BL}$ into Merton/Kelly (Parts 01–03) gives stable, intuitive tilts that collapse to the market when views vanish — the property plain mean-variance lacks.

3. Adding Jumps: Tail-Aware Benchmarking

  • Why: Gaussian BL prices only the covariance; benchmarks (and the liabilities of Part 05) are exposed to co-jumps — assets crashing together (ARM Part 06's tail-dependence). A jump-diffusion return model $dS/S = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW + (e^J - 1)dN$ makes the equilibrium and the views tail-conscious.
  • Equilibrium with jumps: reverse-optimization under a risk-sensitive/CRRA investor who fears jumps yields an implied $\Pi$ that already embeds a jump-risk premium — assets with systematic crash exposure carry higher implied returns, matching the empirical equity/credit premia (ASV's jump-premium theme).
  • Views on tails: express opinions not just on means but on jump intensity/correlation (e.g. "crash co-movement will rise") — the posterior tilts allocations away from crowded co-jump exposures even when Gaussian correlations look benign.
  • Objective: optimize the risk-sensitive criterion (Part 02) on the jump-aware posterior — the higher-cumulant penalties and the jump model together discipline tail concentration that mean-variance + Gaussian BL would happily build.

4. Practice Notes

  • Calibrating δ, τ, Ω: δ from the market Sharpe (~2–3); τ small (0.025–0.05) — it scales prior confidence; Ω from view conviction (often $\Omega = \mathrm{diag}(P\tau\Sigma P^\top)$ for "as confident as the prior").
  • Jump parameters from option-implied tails (the ASV machinery) or historical jump filters — and stress them: jump correlation is the least stable input exactly when it matters.
  • Benchmark-relative framing: run BL in active-return space (views relative to benchmark) so the output is a tracking-error-budgeted tilt — the institutional mandate form (cf. talk-max-diversification, talk-hrp for alternative robustifications).
  • Sanity: with no views, output must equal the (jump-adjusted) market portfolio; any drift signals a prior/optimizer inconsistency.

Key Takeaways

  • Black-Litterman fixes optimizer input-sensitivity: equilibrium prior $\Pi = \delta\Sigma w_{mkt}$ blended with Bayesian views.
  • Posterior collapses to the market absent views, tilts confidently where views are strong — investable, stable allocations.
  • Jump-aware BL embeds a crash-risk premium in equilibrium and allows views on tail co-movement — tail-conscious benchmarking.
  • Pair with the risk-sensitive objective; stress jump correlations; frame relative to benchmark for mandate use.
