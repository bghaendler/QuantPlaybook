Compound & Chooser Options (Geske, Rubinstein)

Options on options: two layers of decision, bivariate normals, and the cheapest way to buy time before committing to a hedge.

1. Compound Options: The Four Types

A compound option (Geske 1979) gives the right at $T_1$ to pay $K_1$ for an underlying option with strike $K_2$ expiring at $T_2 > T_1$: call-on-call, call-on-put, put-on-call, put-on-put. Exercise the outer call iff the inner option is then worth more than $K_1$, i.e. iff $S_{T_1} > S^*$ where $S^*$ solves

$$c_{BS}(S^*, K_2, T_2 - T_1) = K_1.$$

The call-on-call price needs the joint probability of exercise at both dates — a bivariate normal with correlation $\rho = \sqrt{T_1/T_2}$:

$$C_{CoC} = S e^{(b-r)T_2} M(a_1, b_1; \rho) - K_2 e^{-rT_2} M(a_2, b_2; \rho) - K_1 e^{-rT_1} N(a_2),$$ $$a_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(S/S^*) + (b \pm \sigma^2/2)T_1}{\sigma\sqrt{T_1}}, \qquad b_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(S/K_2) + (b \pm \sigma^2/2)T_2}{\sigma\sqrt{T_2}}.$$

2. Why Compounds Matter Beyond the Product

  • Corporate finance is compound: in Merton's structural model, equity of a levered firm is a call on assets — so an equity option is a compound option on assets (Geske's stochastic-leverage extension generates realistic skew).
  • Sequential investment: R&D phases, staged ventures, licenses: each stage is a call on the next — the real-options workhorse.
  • American calls with one dividend: Roll-Geske-Whaley is exactly compound mathematics.
  • Hedging contingent exposure: bid on a contract you may not win → buy a call on the FX hedge rather than the hedge itself (cheaper; pay the second premium only if needed).
  • Vol convexity: compounds are sensitive to forward vol and vol-of-vol — the inner option's future vega is part of today's underlying.

3. Chooser Options (Rubinstein 1991)

A simple chooser ("as-you-like-it") lets the holder declare at $T_1$ whether the contract is a call or a put, both with strike $K$ and expiry $T_2$. By put-call parity the time-$T_1$ choice value is

$$\max(c, p) = c + \max(0, p - c) = c + \max\left(0, K e^{-r(T_2 - T_1)} - S_{T_1} e^{(b-r)(T_2-T_1)}\right),$$

so the chooser decomposes exactly into: one call (K, T₂) + one put with adjusted strike $Ke^{-r(T_2-T_1)}$ expiring at $T_1$ (on the carry-adjusted asset). Hence a closed form with only univariate normals:

$$V = c_{BS}(S, K, T_2) + p_{BS}\big(S e^{(b-r)(T_2-T_1)},\ K e^{-r(T_2-T_1)},\ T_1\big).$$

A complex chooser (different strikes/expiries for the call and put branches) loses this trick and prices like a compound, with a critical $S^*$ and bivariate normals.

4. Chooser vs. Straddle

A straddle holds call and put to expiry; the chooser must abandon one at $T_1$:

$$\text{Chooser}(T_1 = 0) = \text{single option} \quad\le\quad \text{Chooser}(T_1) \nearrow \quad\le\quad \text{Chooser}(T_1 = T_2) = \text{straddle}.$$

Price is increasing in the choice date. Typical numbers ($S = K = 100$, σ = 25%, T₂ = 1y): single option ≈ 12, chooser with $T_1$ = 6m ≈ 17–18, straddle ≈ 20. Use case: event risk of known timing but unknown direction (elections, verdicts, FDA decisions) at a discount to the straddle — you pay only for optionality up to the decision date.

Key Takeaways

  • Compound options: two exercise dates ⇒ critical price $S^*$ + bivariate normal with $\rho = \sqrt{T_1/T_2}$.
  • Geske's formula underlies equity-as-option corporate finance, staged real options, and RGW Americans.
  • Simple choosers decompose by parity into a call + maturity-adjusted put — univariate closed form.
  • Chooser value rises with the decision date, bounded by single option and straddle.
  • Both products load on forward volatility — quote them off a model with credible vol dynamics, not flat BS.
