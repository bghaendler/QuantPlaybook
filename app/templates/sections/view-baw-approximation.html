Barone-Adesi & Whaley Quadratic Approximation

The 1987 workhorse: approximate the early-exercise premium by an ODE you can solve, bolt it onto the European price, and recover American values in microseconds.

1. The Idea: Decompose and Approximate

Write the American price as European plus early-exercise premium, $V_{Am} = V_{Eur} + \varepsilon(S, t)$. Since both $V_{Am}$ and $V_{Eur}$ satisfy the BS equation (in their regions), so does $\varepsilon$. Change variables to $\tau = T - t$, $h(\tau) = 1 - e^{-r\tau}$ and write $\varepsilon = h(\tau) f(S, h)$. The PDE for $f$ contains a term $\propto (1-h)\, \partial f/\partial h$ that is small both near expiry ($h \to 0$ multiplies it) and far from expiry ($\partial f/\partial h \to 0$). BAW's quadratic approximation drops it, leaving an Euler ODE in $S$:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 f'' + b S f' - \frac{r}{h} f = 0.$$

2. Solving the ODE

Solutions are powers $f = a S^q$ with $q$ from the quadratic (hence the name):

$$q_{1,2} = \frac{-(N-1) \mp \sqrt{(N-1)^2 + 4M/h}}{2}, \qquad M = \frac{2r}{\sigma^2},\; N = \frac{2b}{\sigma^2}.$$

For a call keep the growing root $q_2 > 1$ (premium increasing in $S$); for a put the negative root $q_1 < 0$. The American call approximation:

$$C(S) = \begin{cases} c_{BS}(S) + A_2 \left(\dfrac{S}{S^*}\right)^{q_2}, & S < S^* \\[4pt] S - K, & S \ge S^* \end{cases} \qquad A_2 = \frac{S^*}{q_2}\left(1 - e^{(b-r)T} N(d_1(S^*))\right).$$

3. The Critical Price $S^*$

$S^*$ comes from value matching and smooth pasting at the boundary, condensed into one nonlinear equation:

$$S^* - K = c_{BS}(S^*) + \frac{S^*}{q_2}\left(1 - e^{(b-r)T} N(d_1(S^*))\right),$$

solved by Newton iteration (BAW give a good analytical seed; convergence in 3–5 iterations). The put case mirrors with $S^{**}$, $q_1$, and $K - S$. Note for $b \ge r$ (e.g. no dividends) the call boundary is infinite — the formula correctly returns the European value.

4. Accuracy Profile

Short (< 3m)~0.01–0.05% Excellent — dropped term vanishes as $h\to0$
Medium (1y)a few cents on $100Acceptable for risk, marginal for pricing
Long (> 3y, LEAPS)can exceed 1%Weakest regime — use Bjerksund-Stensland 2002 or Ju-Zhong

Errors are largest at intermediate maturities/moneyness where the dropped $\partial f/\partial h$ term peaks. The approximation is not a bound — it can over- or under-price.

5. Why It Mattered (and Still Does)

  • Pre-1987, American pricing meant slow binomial trees; BAW made real-time American quotes and Greeks feasible on 1980s hardware — it powered commodity/futures option desks (where $b < r$ makes calls genuinely American).
  • Still the default "fast American" in many libraries and risk engines; Greeks come analytically by differentiating the two-piece formula.
  • Successors: Ju-Zhong (1999) corrects the dropped term to second order; Bjerksund-Stensland takes a different (lower-bound) route — see next section.

Key Takeaways

  • American = European + premium; the premium's PDE is reduced to a solvable Euler ODE by dropping one provably-small term.
  • Power solutions $S^{q}$ with $q$ from a quadratic; coefficient fixed by smooth pasting at a critical price $S^*$ found by Newton.
  • Very accurate short-dated, weakest for long maturities.
  • Speed is the point: closed-form-fast Americans for pricing, Greeks and calibration loops.
