Stylised Facts of Asset Returns

The robust empirical regularities observed across asset classes, markets and time periods — and why they break the textbook Gaussian random-walk model.

1. What Is a Stylised Fact?

A stylised fact is a statistical property of returns so persistent across instruments, markets and decades that any candidate model must reproduce it. Defining the log-return over horizon $\tau$ as

$$r_t(\tau) = \ln S_{t+\tau} - \ln S_t,$$

the benchmark Geometric Brownian Motion model predicts that $r_t(\tau)$ is i.i.d. Gaussian with variance proportional to $\tau$. Empirical data systematically violates this in at least five well-documented ways.

2. Fat Tails (Excess Kurtosis)

Daily equity returns exhibit kurtosis far above the Gaussian value of 3 — typically 5 to 30 for indices, higher for single stocks. The tails decay approximately as a power law:

$$\mathbb{P}(|r| > x) \sim x^{-\alpha}, \qquad \alpha \approx 3 \text{ to } 5,$$

the so-called cubic law of returns. Practical consequence: a "25-sigma" event under the normal distribution (essentially impossible) is merely rare under a fat-tailed law. Tail risk measures and option smiles both originate here.

As the sampling horizon $\tau$ increases (daily → weekly → monthly), the distribution slowly approaches Gaussianity — aggregational Gaussianity — consistent with the Central Limit Theorem acting on weakly dependent increments.

3. Volatility Clustering & Long Memory

While returns themselves are nearly uncorrelated, their magnitudes are strongly autocorrelated:

$$\operatorname{corr}(r_t, r_{t+k}) \approx 0, \qquad \operatorname{corr}(|r_t|, |r_{t+k}|) > 0 \text{ for } k \text{ up to months.}$$

"Large changes tend to be followed by large changes — of either sign — and small changes by small changes" (Mandelbrot, 1963). The autocorrelation of $|r_t|$ decays slowly, roughly as $k^{-\beta}$ with $\beta \in (0.2, 0.4)$, a signature of long memory in volatility. This is the empirical foundation of ARCH/GARCH and stochastic-volatility models.

4. Leverage Effect & Gain/Loss Asymmetry

Volatility is negatively correlated with past returns:

$$\operatorname{corr}(r_t, \sigma_{t+k}^2) < 0, \quad k > 0.$$

Falling prices raise volatility more than rising prices of the same size — partly a financial-leverage story (equity becomes riskier as firm value drops), partly risk-premium feedback. In option markets this asymmetry manifests as the persistent negative skew of equity implied volatility. Drawdowns are also steeper than rallies: large negative returns occur more often than equally large positive ones.

5. Absence of Linear Autocorrelation

Beyond very short timescales (minutes, where microstructure effects dominate), linear autocorrelations of returns are statistically negligible — otherwise simple linear strategies would arbitrage them away. This is consistent with the (weak-form) efficient market hypothesis, yet emphatically does not imply independence: nonlinear functions of returns ($|r|$, $r^2$) are predictable.
