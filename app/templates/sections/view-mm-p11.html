Lognormal Stock Price Evolution Derivation

From the GBM postulate to the explicit distribution of $S_T$: the complete derivation, its moments, and what the lognormal does and doesn't capture.

1. Solve the SDE

Postulate proportional dynamics (returns, not levels, are stationary): $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$. Apply Itô to $\ln S$:

$$d\ln S = \left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)dt + \sigma\,dW,$$

whose right side no longer involves $S$ — integrate directly:

$$S_T = S_0\,\exp\!\left[\left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T + \sigma W_T\right], \qquad W_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, T).$$

So $\ln S_T \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\ln S_0 + (\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T,\ \sigma^2 T\right)$: prices are lognormal, log-returns are normal, and the solution is exact (the basis of exact simulation).

2. Moments & the Three "Means"

$$\mathbb{E}[S_T] = S_0e^{\mu T}, \qquad \operatorname{Median}(S_T) = S_0e^{(\mu - \sigma^2/2)T}, \qquad \operatorname{Mode}(S_T) = S_0e^{(\mu - \tfrac{3}{2}\sigma^2)T},$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(S_T) = S_0^2e^{2\mu T}\left(e^{\sigma^2T} - 1\right).$$

Mean > median > mode: the right-skewed lognormal's signature. The arithmetic-vs-geometric gap ($\sigma^2/2$) explains why a volatile asset can have positive expected return yet a typical (median) path that loses money — the quantitative core of "volatility drag" and of Kelly-style log-growth thinking. Density, for reference:

$$f(s) = \frac{1}{s\,\sigma\sqrt{2\pi T}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{\left[\ln(s/S_0) - (\mu - \sigma^2/2)T\right]^2}{2\sigma^2 T}\right), \quad s > 0.$$

3. Why Lognormal Is the Right First Model

  • Positivity: $S_T > 0$ always — limited liability respected (Bachelier's normal model fails this).
  • Scale invariance: returns don't depend on the price level; a 2:1 split changes nothing — proportional dynamics is the economically sensible invariance.
  • Multiplicative CLT: compound many small independent relative changes and the log converges to normal — the same argument that makes the normal universal for sums makes the lognormal universal for products.
  • Closure: products/powers of lognormals are lognormal — why geometric Asian and basket approximations work.

4. Where It Breaks (and What Fixes It)

Fat tails (kurtosis ≫ 3)Jumps (Merton), Lévy, Student-t innovations

Skew/leverage effectNegative spot-vol correlation, local vol
Implied smile ≠ flatAll of the above, calibrated to options

Under the risk-neutral measure, set $\mu \to r$ (or $r - q$): the same lognormal with the drift replaced — every Black-Scholes quantity ($N(d_2)$ probabilities, truncated means) is a direct lognormal computation from this section's formulas.

Key Takeaways

  • Itô on $\ln S$ linearizes GBM; the exact solution makes prices lognormal with variance $\sigma^2T$ in logs.
  • Mean, median, mode separate by powers of $e^{\sigma^2T/2}$ — volatility drag quantified.
  • Positivity, scale invariance and multiplicative CLT justify the model; tails, clustering and skew indict it.
  • Risk-neutral version ($\mu \to r$) + truncated lognormal integrals = Black-Scholes (Part 04's computation).

Plain English Notes

1. Solve the SDE

The Big Picture: We find the exact stock price equation by applying Itˆ's Lemma to the logarithm of the asset price.

  • Log Transformation: Converts multiplicative asset price growth into additive log-returns.
  • Analytical Solution: Yields the exact formula showing that the asset price grows exponentially over time.

2. Moments & the Three "Means"

The Big Picture: Lognormal stock prices have three different 'averages': the median, the mode, and the mean.

  • Median & Mode: The median represents the 50% landing probability threshold; the mode is the most likely peak value.
  • Expected Value: The mean matches the risk-free rate growth, reflecting the volatility drift correction.

3. Why Lognormal Is the Right First Model

The Big Picture: Lognormal is the foundation of option pricing because it matches basic financial rules: stock prices stay positive and returns compound.

  • Positive Boundaries: Since the price is an exponential function, the stock can never drop below zero.
  • Compounding: Multiplicative price changes match the continuous compounding of interest rates.

4. Where It Breaks (and What Fixes It)

The Big Picture: Lognormal models assume constant volatility, which fails to match real-world volatility smiles and crashes.

  • Constant Vol Flaw: Markets exhibit volatility smiles (out-of-the-money options have higher implied vol than ATM options).
  • Fixes: Quants use local volatility (Dupire) or stochastic volatility (Heston) to capture realistic smiles.
