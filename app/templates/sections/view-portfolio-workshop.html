Final Project Portfolio Workshop I & II

The capstone: scoping, building and presenting an original quant project — what makes a strong submission, the common pitfalls, and how to demonstrate end-to-end competence.

1. Choosing a Project That Works

  • Scope to completable depth: one well-executed question beats five half-built modules. "Calibrate and stress-test SABR on SPX options" finishes; "build a full XVA system" does not.
  • Pick where you can validate: projects with a benchmark (closed form, published result, market data) let you prove correctness — the single biggest differentiator.
  • Project archetypes that succeed: a pricing/calibration study (a model on real smiles), a strategy backtest with honest costs (HFT/RSI), a risk engine with backtesting (ARM), a numerical-methods comparison (PDE vs. MC vs. tree convergence), or an AAD/GPU acceleration with speed+accuracy benchmarks.
  • Data realism: use real (or realistically simulated) data; toy data hides the problems that demonstrate real skill.

2. The Build (Workshop I)

  • Reproducible pipeline: data → model → results → figures, runnable end-to-end with a seed (the Python-lab standards). A grader who can't re-run it can't trust it.
  • Validation layer first: establish the benchmark before the novel part — MC against analytic, calibration against a known surface — so every later number has a reference.
  • Error budgeting: separate model error, numerical error and estimation error (the ARM Part 01 lens); report standard errors and convergence.
  • Version control & structure: modular code, tests on the core functions (the dot-product/benchmark tests of the AD/lab work), a README that states assumptions and conventions.

3. The Presentation (Workshop II)

  • Lead with the question and the answer: what did you ask, what did you find, why does it matter — before the methodology.
  • Show validation explicitly: the "my MC matches Black-Scholes to 4 digits" slide buys credibility for everything after it.
  • Visualize the result: a fitted smile over market points, a convergence plot, a P&L distribution, a VaR backtest exception chart — one clear figure per claim.
  • State limitations honestly: what the model can't do, where estimation error bites, what you'd build next — examiners reward awareness over false completeness (the governance maturity of ARM Part 02).
  • Connect to the curriculum: name the techniques (Itô, Fourier pricing, Cholesky, AAD) — demonstrate you know why each tool, not just that it ran.

4. Pitfalls That Sink Projects

  • No benchmark: impressive-looking numbers with nothing to check them against — the most common weakness.
  • Backtest overfitting / look-ahead: a 4-Sharpe strategy that used future information; in-sample tuning presented as out-of-sample skill (the deadly research sin).
  • Ignoring transaction costs & capacity on trading projects — the HFT cost stack turns paper-alpha into real losses.
  • Scope creep producing a broad, shallow, half-working system instead of a narrow, deep, validated one.
  • Unexamined model risk: presenting one model's output as truth without the "what if the model is wrong" stress (ASV's identical-calibration-divergent-extrapolation lesson).
  • Polished slides, broken code: the pipeline must actually run; a non-reproducible result is a non-result.

Key Takeaways

  • Narrow, deep, validated beats broad and shallow — scope to something you can finish and prove.
  • Build the benchmark/validation layer first; every result needs a reference and an error bar.
  • Present question→answer→evidence→limitations; one clear figure per claim, validation shown explicitly.
  • Avoid the killers: no benchmark, look-ahead bias, ignored costs, scope creep, unexamined model risk, non-reproducible code.
