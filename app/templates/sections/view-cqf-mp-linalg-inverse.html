The Matrix Inverse

Identity matrices, 2x2 inverse formulas, cofactors and minors, and adjoint representations.

1. Matrix Inverse Definition

The inverse of a square matrix $\mathbf{A}$, denoted as $\mathbf{A}^{-1}$, satisfies:

$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{A}^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I}$$

where $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix. If the inverse exists, the solution to the linear system $\mathbf{A}\underline{x} = \underline{p}$ is uniquely found via:

$$\underline{x} = \mathbf{A}^{-1}\underline{p}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Inverting a matrix is the linear algebra equivalent of division. If a matrix represents a forward transformation (like moving or stretching coordinates), the inverse matrix represents the exact step-by-step instructions to return everything to its original place.

  • Implicit PDEs: When solving Option Pricing grids implicitly (e.g. Crank-Nicolson), we transition the system forward using a matrix. To find today's prices from tomorrow's boundary conditions, we solve the system by computing the matrix inverse.
  • Identity: Multiplying a matrix by its inverse returns the "Identity" matrix, which does nothing (the matrix equivalent of multiplying a number by its reciprocal to get 1).

2. Simple 2x2 Matrix Inverse

For a standard $2 \times 2$ matrix, the inverse formula is defined as:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{provided } ad-bc \neq 0$$

The term $ad - bc$ is the determinant. If the determinant is zero, the matrix is singular and cannot be inverted.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The determinant ($ad - bc$) measures how much the matrix scales area. If the determinant is zero, the transformation squashes the entire 2D plane flat into a single 1D line or point.

  • Lost Information: Once 2D space is compressed into a 1D line, information is lost forever. You cannot determine where points originally were along the collapsed dimension, which is why a determinant of zero means no inverse exists.
  • Singular Matrices: In finance, a singular covariance matrix means some assets are perfect linear combinations of others, leading to redundant risk factors.

3. General N x N Matrix Inverse

For larger $n \times n$ matrices, the inverse is defined using determinants and adjoint configurations:

$$\mathbf{A}^{-1} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{A}|} \text{adj}(\mathbf{A})$$

where $|\mathbf{A}|$ is the determinant of $\mathbf{A}$, and $\text{adj}(\mathbf{A})$ is the adjoint (transpose of the cofactor matrix):

$$\text{adj}(\mathbf{A}) = \left[ (-1)^{i+j} |M_{ij}| \right]^{\mathrm{T}}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Calculating inverses for large matrices using cofactors is extremely slow and numerically unstable for computers. In practice, quants use factorization methods (LU, Cholesky, or QR decomposition) to solve systems.

  • Cholesky Decomposition: Used extensively to generate correlated random numbers in Monte Carlo simulations by splitting the covariance matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^{\mathrm{T}}$.
  • Row Operations: Swapping rows or scaling equations changes the determinant's value but doesn't change whether the matrix is invertible, preserving the core solvability of the system.
