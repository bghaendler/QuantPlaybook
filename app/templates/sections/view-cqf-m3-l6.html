The Four Volatilities, the Inconsistencies of Vega, Term Structure Calibration, and Volatility Arbitrage Hedging

The Volatility Taxonomy

Dr. Paul Wilmott frames volatility as the central unknown parameter in options pricing. Black-Scholes is an elegant model precisely because it has exactly one unknown parameter. With zero unknowns, trade would cease due to perfect agreement; with too many, the model becomes unusable.

1. Actual / Local Volatility ($\sigma_a$)

The instantaneous random "noise" in a financial instrument's SDE: $dS = \mu S dt + \sigma_a S dX$. It exists purely at an instant, has no associated time scale, cannot be directly observed, and must be forecast over the option's entire life.

2. Historical / Realized Volatility

The calculated standard deviation of returns over a past window. It requires two timescales: a lookback window (e.g. 60 days) and a return interval (e.g. daily). Realized volatility serves as a baseline forecast for future actual volatility.

3. Implied Volatility ($\sigma_i$)

The volatility backed out of current option market prices using the Black-Scholes formula. It is associated with a single timescale: expiration. It is influenced by supply/demand crash insurance motives, rather than reflecting a pure physical forecast.

4. Forward Volatility

The implied volatility over a future window (e.g. between month 1 and month 2) extracted by comparing current implied volatilities of different maturities. It serves as an arbitrage-free forecast of future options pricing.

Vega: The "Bastard Greek"

Vega measures the sensitivity of an option's theoretical value to changes in volatility: $$\text{Vega} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma} = S \sqrt{T-t} N'(d_1)$$

Dr. Paul Wilmott labels Vega a "bastard Greek" because it is mathematically inconsistent: it involves differentiating the Black-Scholes formula with respect to a parameter ($\sigma$) that the formula's very SDE derivation assumes to be constant.

Analogy: Differentiating the Black-Scholes formula with respect to volatility $\sigma$ is conceptually equivalent to differentiating the area of a circle ($A = \pi r^2$) with respect to $\pi$. Despite this inconsistency, Vega is a critical tool used to manage residual volatility risks in exotic books.

Greeks & Volatility Rules of Thumb

Rule 1: Asset Scaling

Doubling volatility $\sigma$ is mathematically equivalent to quadrupling the time to expiration $T$ in the pricing formulas. They scale as $\sigma\sqrt{T}$.

Rule 2: Delta & Gamma Smoothing

Increasing volatility smooths out Delta and Gamma curves across asset space. This acts as a physical diffusion process, flattening highly peaked profiles.

Rule 3: Gamma Peak Fading

Low volatility creates a very steep, dangerously severe Gamma peak right at the strike price $E$. High volatility flattens and distributes this risk safely.

Term Structure Calibration & "Un-averaging"

If we assume the market is right, we can reconcile different implied volatilities for different expirations by modeling a time-dependent, deterministic actual volatility $\sigma_a(t)$. Under this framework, the implied volatility is the root mean square average of the time-varying variance: $$\sigma_i(T; t^*)^2 = \frac{1}{T - t^*} \int_{t^*}^{T} \sigma_a(\tau)^2 d\tau$$

Differentiating the term structure (Leibniz Rule Proof)

To solve for the local variance $\sigma_a(T)^2$, we multiply by the time interval and differentiate both sides with respect to expiration $T$: $$(T - t^*)\sigma_i(T; t^*)^2 = \int_{t^*}^{T} \sigma_a(\tau)^2 d\tau$$ $$\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\left[ (T - t^*)\sigma_i(T; t^*)^2 \right] = \sigma_a(T)^2$$ Applying the product rule on the left yields the closed-form un-averaging formula: $$\sigma_a(T) = \sqrt{\sigma_i(T; t^*)^2 + 2(T - t^*)\sigma_i(T; t^*)\frac{\partial \sigma_i(T; t^*)}{\partial T}}$$

Numerical Instability

Because implied volatility represents a smoothed average, backing out local volatility is an inverse problem. Differentiation amplifies noise, causing mild ripples in market quotes to produce extremely jagged, unstable, or negative local volatility curves.

Temporal Recalibration Inconsistency

The calibrated function $\sigma_a(t)$ is supposed to be a fixed deterministic forecast of calendar time. In practice, recalibrating the model a week later ($t^*$) yields a completely different function. This mismatch demonstrates that a deterministic model of time is physically incorrect.

Volatility Skews, Smiles & Frowns

Implied volatility is not flat; it varies significantly with strike $E$. In equity markets, we observe a downward sloping skew (fear of sudden crashes). In FX markets, we often observe a symmetric smile (expectations of large jumps in either direction) or a frown.

The Supply & Demand Insurance Analog

Just like car insurance premiums cover profit margins, business costs, and crash probability (making premium costs higher than actuarial replication), deep out-of-the-money puts are priced by supply, demand, crash hedging, and fat-tail risk. We cannot extract a pure physical probability from implied volatility because Black-Scholes packs all market frictions into a single parameter: volatility.

Dupire Local Volatility

To handle strikes and expiries simultaneously, Dupire developed the local volatility surface $\sigma_a(S, t)$ from market prices of European options: $$\sigma_a(E, T)^2 = \frac{\frac{\partial C}{\partial T} + (r - D)E\frac{\partial C}{\partial E} + D C}{\frac{1}{2} E^2 \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial E^2}}$$

This equation takes the second derivative with respect to strike (which represents the risk-neutral probability density) in the denominator and the first derivative with respect to time in the numerator.

Volatility Calibration worked Solutions

Problem 6.1: Piecewise Constant "Un-averaging" Solution
Question:
Given the following term structure of monthly implied volatilities:
  • Month 1 ($T_1 = 1/12$): Implied Volatility $\sigma_i(T_1) = 30\%$
  • Month 2 ($T_2 = 2/12$): Implied Volatility $\sigma_i(T_2) = 25\%$
  • Month 3 ($T_3 = 3/12$): Implied Volatility $\sigma_i(T_3) = 26\%$
Calculate the piecewise constant actual volatilities $\sigma_1$, $\sigma_2$, and $\sigma_3$ during each month.
Detailed Solution:

Step 1: First Month actual Volatility ($\sigma_1$)
Since the first period starts at zero, the average matches the local: $$\sigma_1 = \sigma_i(T_1) = 30\%$$

Step 2: Second Month actual Volatility ($\sigma_2$)
The variance over the first two months is time-weighted: $$T_2 \sigma_i(T_2)^2 = T_1 \sigma_1^2 + (T_2 - T_1) \sigma_2^2$$ Substitute values: $$\frac{2}{12} (0.25)^2 = \frac{1}{12} (0.30)^2 + \frac{1}{12} \sigma_2^2$$ Multiply through by $12$: $$2(0.0625) = 0.0900 + \sigma_2^2$$ $$0.1250 = 0.0900 + \sigma_2^2 \implies \sigma_2^2 = 0.0350$$ $$\sigma_2 = \sqrt{0.0350} \approx 18.71\%$$

Step 3: Third Month actual Volatility ($\sigma_3$)
The variance over the first three months: $$T_3 \sigma_i(T_3)^2 = T_1 \sigma_1^2 + (T_2 - T_1) \sigma_2^2 + (T_3 - T_2) \sigma_3^2$$ Substitute values: $$\frac{3}{12} (0.26)^2 = \frac{1}{12} (0.30)^2 + \frac{1}{12} (0.1871)^2 + \frac{1}{12} \sigma_3^2$$ Multiply through by $12$: $$3(0.0676) = 0.0900 + 0.0350 + \sigma_3^2$$ $$0.2028 = 0.1250 + \sigma_3^2 \implies \sigma_3^2 = 0.0778$$ $$\sigma_3 = \sqrt{0.0778} \approx 27.89\%$$

Results Summary: $\sigma_1 = 30\%$, $\sigma_2 = 18.71\%$, $\sigma_3 = 27.89\%$
