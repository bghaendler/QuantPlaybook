Asymptotics of Stochastic Volatility — Part 05

Exponential Lévy Option Asymptotics

Jump models in the asymptotic lens: small-time smiles that don't vanish, wing slopes from exponential moments, and the diagnostic that separates jumps from diffusion in market data.

1. Exponential Lévy Models

$S_t = S_0e^{X_t}$ with $X$ a Lévy process: characteristic exponent via Lévy-Khintchine,

$$\ln\mathbb{E}[e^{iuX_t}] = t\Big(iub - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2u^2 + \int\big(e^{iux} - 1 - iux\mathbf{1}_{|x|<1}\big)\nu(dx)\Big),$$

with the Lévy measure ν carrying the jumps. The cast: Merton (Gaussian jumps + diffusion), Kou (double-exponential), Variance Gamma and CGMY (infinite-activity pure jump). Pricing runs on Part CA-17's Fourier machinery — these models exist because their characteristic functions are simple while their densities aren't.

2. Small-Time Asymptotics: Jumps Change Everything

Compare with diffusion/SV (Part 03's $T\to0$ regime):

  • OTM option prices: diffusive models ⇒ $C(K,T) \sim e^{-c/T}$ (exponentially small — Varadhan); Lévy models ⇒ $C(K,T) \sim T\cdot\int(\,\cdot\,)\nu(dx)$ — linear in T: one jump suffices to cross the strike, and the probability of one jump is $O(T)$.
  • Implied vol smiles: consequently the smile does not flatten as $T \to 0$ — it steepens: $\sigma_{BS}(k, T) \sim \frac{|k|}{\sqrt{2T\ln(1/T)}}$-type blowup at fixed OTM strike. Empirical short-dated equity/index smiles are extremely steep — the classic evidence for a jump component (and, alternatively explained by rough vol — the live debate).
  • ATM behavior: with a diffusion part, ATM vol → σ; pure-jump infinite-variation (CGMY Y > 1) mimics diffusion ATM; finite-variation VG has ATM vol → 0 like $\sqrt{T\ln(1/T)}$ — fine structure of ν visible at the money.

3. Wings: Lee's Moment Formula Is Exact Here

Exponential moments $\mathbb{E}[S_T^u] = e^{T\kappa(u)}$ exist iff $\int_{|x|>1}e^{ux}\nu(dx) < \infty$ — for Kou/VG/CGMY this caps $u$ at the exponential tail rates of ν (e.g. Kou's $\eta_1, \eta_2$; CGMY's G, M). Lee's formula then pins the wing slopes of total implied variance:

$$\limsup_{k\to\infty}\frac{\sigma_{BS}^2(k)T}{|k|} = \psi(u^* - 1), \qquad \psi(x) = 2 - 4(\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x),$$

so wing slopes are time-stationary and read off ν's tails directly — calibrating G and M (or η₁, η₂) is calibrating the wings. Contrast SV: critical moments move with T (Part 04). This stationarity — same smile shape every maturity, just $\sqrt{T}$-scaled — is also Lévy models' weakness: they cannot fit the term-structure of skew (floating smiles), motivating SV-Lévy hybrids (Part 07).

4. Diagnostics and Use

  • Jump vs. diffusion test in data: plot OTM option prices vs. T at fixed strike on log axes — linear-in-T decay flags jumps; $e^{-c/T}$ flags diffusion; realized-variance-based tests (bipower variation) corroborate from the time series.
  • Calibration division of labor: short-dated steep smiles identify ν (jump intensity/tails); long-dated smiles need the SV layer — same regime-matching principle as Part 03.
  • Risk reading: linear-in-T OTM prices mean short-dated wing options are jump insurance, almost pure ν-risk — hedge with strikes, not with delta.
  • Numerics: all of the above is testable in the Carr-Madan pricer with Kou/VG characteristic functions — the small-T linearity emerges by 1–2 week maturities.

Key Takeaways

  • Lévy OTM prices decay linearly in T (one-jump events) vs. exponentially for diffusions — smiles steepen, not flatten, as T→0.
  • Wing slopes are exact functions of ν's exponential tails via Lee's formula — and time-stationary.
  • Stationary smile shape is the fingerprint and the flaw: jumps fit short-dated steepness, fail skew term-structure.
  • Log-log price-vs-maturity plots separate jumps from diffusion in market data; calibrate ν short, SV long.
