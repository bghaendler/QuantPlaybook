Advanced Risk Management — Part 01

Calibration & Modeling Risk Overview

The risk before the risks: every number a risk system produces is conditional on a model and a calibration — this part maps how those choices fail and how to manage them.

1. The Model Risk Taxonomy

  • Specification risk: the model family is wrong — Gaussian where fat-tailed (Part 03/06), constant correlation where regime-dependent, diffusion where jumps. The error survives perfect calibration.
  • Calibration/estimation risk: the family is fine but parameters are noisy or unstable — short samples, regime mixing, overfitting to the quote of the day.
  • Implementation risk: correct model, wrong code/data — unit errors, stale curves, look-ahead in backtests.
  • Usage risk: correct numbers, wrong application — a 1-day VaR steering a 1-month illiquid book, or a pricing measure used for risk forecasts.

Regulatory frame: SR 11-7 defines a model as data→assumptions→output and requires validation, monitoring and effective challenge for each — the taxonomy above is essentially its checklist.

2. Calibration: Fitting Is Not Knowing

  • Two calibration cultures: time-series/historical (P-measure: GARCH, EWMA on returns) vs. cross-sectional/implied (Q-measure: vols, correlations from today's option quotes). They answer different questions; mixing them silently is a classic usage error.
  • Ill-posedness: many parameter sets fit equally well (Heston's κ-σ ridge; copula correlation from few tranche quotes) — report the set, not the point: profile likelihoods, bootstrap bands, identifiability checks.
  • Instability as signal: parameters that lurch at re-calibration are absorbing specification error — track parameter time series as a monitoring metric in their own right.
  • Regularization trade-off: penalties/priors stabilize calibration at the cost of bias — choose by out-of-sample risk forecasting, not in-sample fit.

3. Measuring Model Risk

  • Model reserves: price/risk under a benchmark set of defensible models; reserve the spread (worst-vs-booked) — standard for exotics desks.
  • Sensitivity mapping: ∂(output)/∂(assumption) — which assumptions move VaR/price most? Those get monitoring and challenge first.
  • Backtesting & benchmarking: Kupiec/Christoffersen exception tests for VaR (Part 07), pricing vs. consensus services, P&L-attribution tests (FRTB's eligibility test institutionalizes this).
  • Worst-case bounds: robust/uncertainty-set approaches (e.g. price under all models within a divergence ball of the baseline) quantify "unknown model" exposure formally.

4. Course Roadmap

This series builds the toolkit bottom-up: risk frameworks and taxonomies (Parts 02, 04), the non-normal reality of returns (Parts 03, 06), VaR — historical, parametric, Monte Carlo (Parts 05, 07–10) — with the calibration-risk lens of this part applied at every step: each method's numbers will be presented with the assumptions that can break them.

Key Takeaways

  • Model risk = specification + calibration + implementation + usage; perfect fits don't cure wrong families.
  • Keep P-measure and Q-measure calibrations distinct; report parameter uncertainty, not just points.
  • Parameter instability is diagnostic data; sensitivity mapping prioritizes validation effort.
  • Reserves, backtests, benchmarking and robust bounds turn "the model might be wrong" into managed numbers.
