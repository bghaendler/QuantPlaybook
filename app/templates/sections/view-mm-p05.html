Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Random Variables & Probability Moments

From sample spaces to the four moments every risk report quotes — mean, variance, skew, kurtosis — and the distributions that carry them in finance.

1. Random Variables, CDF, PDF

A random variable $X$ maps outcomes to numbers; its law is captured by the CDF $F(x) = \mathbb{P}(X \le x)$ (non-decreasing, right-continuous, limits 0 and 1) and, when it exists, the density $f = F'$ with $\mathbb{P}(a < X \le b) = \int_a^b f$. Quantiles invert it: $\text{VaR}_\alpha$ is precisely $F^{-1}_{loss}(\alpha)$ — risk management is applied CDF inversion. Expectations of transforms: $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g(x)f(x)dx$ (LOTUS) — option pricing is $g = $ discounted payoff under the risk-neutral $f$.

2. The Four Moments

MomentDefinitionFinance reading
Mean $\mu$$\mathbb{E}[X]$Expected return / forward
Variance $\sigma^2$$\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]$Volatility² — the unit of risk
Skewness $\gamma_1$$\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^3]/\sigma^3$Crash asymmetry; smile slope
Kurtosis $\gamma_2$ (excess)$\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^4]/\sigma^4 - 3$Fat tails; smile curvature

Useful algebra: $\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mu^2$; $\operatorname{Var}(aX + bY) = a^2\sigma_X^2 + b^2\sigma_Y^2 + 2ab\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ — the two-line foundation of portfolio theory. Moment generating function $M(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ packages all moments: $\mathbb{E}[X^n] = M^{(n)}(0)$.

3. The Cast of Distributions

  • Normal $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$: closed under sums, CLT-blessed, MGF $e^{\mu t + \sigma^2t^2/2}$ — log-returns' default model and its known understatement of tails.
  • Lognormal: $e^{\mathcal{N}}$ — prices; mean $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$ (note the convexity bump), all moments finite but the MGF doesn't exist.
  • Poisson/exponential: jump counts and waiting times (default arrival in intensity models: $\mathbb{P}(\tau > t) = e^{-\lambda t}$).
  • Student-t: power tails, kurtosis from $\nu$; the GARCH-residual standard; variance infinite for $\nu \le 2$.
  • Empirical daily equity returns: $\gamma_1 \approx -0.5$ to $-1$, $\gamma_2 \approx 5$–$30$ — the numbers any candidate model must reproduce (cf. Stylised Facts).

4. Moments → Prices and Risk

  • Variance prices vanillas (BS needs only $\sigma$); skew and kurtosis price the smile (Gram-Charlier: slope ∝ $-\gamma_1$, curvature ∝ $\gamma_2$).
  • Cornish-Fisher adjusts VaR quantiles for $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$: $z^{CF} = z + \frac{\gamma_1}{6}(z^2 - 1) + \frac{\gamma_2}{24}(z^3 - 3z) - \frac{\gamma_1^2}{36}(2z^3 - 5z)$.
  • Sample moments are noisy in the tails: kurtosis estimates need thousands of observations and are dominated by a handful of extreme days — quote them with suspicion, prefer robust/quantile-based diagnostics.

Key Takeaways

  • CDF inversion = quantiles = VaR; LOTUS = pricing integrals.
  • Variance algebra with covariance is the mathematical core of diversification.
  • Skew and kurtosis are priced objects (smiles), not statistical curiosities.
  • Know each distribution's tail and which moments exist before trusting any formula on it.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Random variables model the uncertainty of future asset returns. Moments (Mean, Variance, Skewness, Kurtosis) describe the shape, width, and tail-risks of the market.

  • Mean & Variance: Mean is the average return we expect; Variance is the risk or spread of returns around that average.
  • Skewness & Kurtosis: Skewness captures asymmetric risk (like stock crashes); Kurtosis measures fat tails (extreme events happening more often than normal).
  • Moment Generating Functions: A mathematical shortcut that bundles all moments into a single equation, making complex return distributions easy to calculate.
