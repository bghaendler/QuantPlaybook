2. The Square-Root Law

Across asset classes, decades, and venues, the cost of executing a metaorder of size $Q$ empirically follows:

$$\Delta p \approx Y\,\sigma\sqrt{\frac{Q}{V}},$$

Notable features: concavity (the second half of an order costs less per share than the first), near-universality of the exponent ($\approx 0.4$â€“$0.6$), and dependence on $Q/V$ (participation), not $Q$ alone. Key Takeaways

  • Separate temporary (decaying, mechanical) from permanent (informational) impact; propagator models tie them together with power-law decay.
  • The square-root law $\Delta p \sim \sigma\sqrt{Q/V}$ is the most robust empirical fact in execution â€” cost scales with participation.
  • Heavy-tailed sizes, long-memory signs, Hawkes-clustered arrivals: the statistical environment any execution model must inhabit.
  • Linear (Almgren-Chriss) for tractability, concave-with-decay for realism â€” and respect no-arbitrage constraints on impact shapes.
