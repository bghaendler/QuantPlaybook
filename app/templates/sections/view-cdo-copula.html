CDO Pricing & Copula Correlation

Tranching a portfolio of credits and pricing the slices: the Gaussian copula, base correlation, and the model that became a byword for model risk.

1. The CDO Structure

A portfolio of $n$ credits (bonds, loans, or CDS — a "synthetic" CDO) is sliced by cumulative loss. A tranche with attachment $a$ and detachment $d$ absorbs portfolio losses in $[a, d]$:

$$L_{tranche}(t) = \frac{\min(L(t), d) - \min(L(t), a)}{d - a}, \qquad L(t) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (1 - R_i)\,\mathbb{1}_{\{\tau_i \le t\}}.$$

Equity (0–3%) takes first losses for a fat coupon; mezzanine (3–7%) next; senior/super-senior last. Pricing a tranche = computing the loss distribution of the portfolio at each payment date — which depends overwhelmingly on default correlation: single-name spreads pin expected loss, correlation decides how it is shared across tranches.

2. The Gaussian Copula (Li, 2000)

Glue marginal survival curves $Q_i(t)$ (bootstrapped from CDS) into a joint law via latent Gaussian factors. One-factor version:

$$X_i = \sqrt{\rho}\, M + \sqrt{1 - \rho}\, Z_i, \qquad \tau_i \le t \iff X_i \le \Phi^{-1}\big(1 - Q_i(t)\big),$$

with common factor $M$ and idiosyncratic $Z_i$. Conditional on $M$, defaults are independent with probability

$$p_i(t \mid M) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\Phi^{-1}(1 - Q_i(t)) - \sqrt{\rho}\, M}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}}\right),$$

so the loss distribution is a one-dimensional integral over $M$ of (almost) binomial mixtures — computed by recursion or, in the large homogeneous portfolio (Vasicek) limit, in closed form. The same mathematics is the Basel IRB formula; tractability made it the universal market standard for index tranches (iTraxx/CDX).

3. Correlation Smile & Base Correlation

Inverting market tranche prices for the single ρ that reprices each tranche (implied compound correlation) yields different ρ per tranche — a correlation smile, the smoking gun that the Gaussian copula is mis-specified (its tail dependence is zero: extreme joint defaults are too unlikely). The market's workaround, base correlation: quote ρ for equity tranches $[0, d]$ at each detachment $d$ and price any tranche as a difference of two base tranches. It interpolates cleanly but is a smile-quoting convention, not a model — it can even imply negative expected tranche losses if interpolated carelessly.

4. What Went Wrong (2007-2008)

  • Tail dependence: Gaussian factors → joint extreme defaults essentially impossible; real mortgage defaults were driven by one factor (house prices) with near-total dependence in the tail. Student-t or Clayton copulas (with tail dependence) price senior risk very differently.
  • Correlation as a constant: ρ calibrated to benign history; realized correlation in stress → 1, exactly when senior tranches matter.
  • CDO-squared opacity: tranches of tranches amplified parameter sensitivity beyond any calibration's resolution.
  • The lesson is general: marginals can be perfectly calibrated while the dependence structure — the actual product — is guessed. Copula choice is the price of a correlation product.

5. The Mathematics Worth Keeping

  • Sklar's theorem: any joint law = marginals + copula $C(u_1, \dots, u_n)$ — the clean separation of calibration concerns; still the right framework, with better copulas.
  • Conditional-independence factor trick: integrate out $M$ — reused in CVA, mortgage, and insurance portfolio models.
  • Vasicek large-pool loss density: $f(x)$ with $\Phi^{-1}$ algebra — Basel capital, loan-book stress testing.
  • Post-crisis tranche markets (index tranches, bespoke CSOs are back since 2017) still quote Gaussian base correlation — now understood as a quoting convention with model risk explicitly reserved.

Key Takeaways

  • Tranches are options on portfolio loss; correlation allocates a fixed expected loss across the capital structure.
  • Gaussian copula: marginals from CDS + one-factor latent Gaussians ⇒ semi-analytic loss distributions.
  • Tranche-implied correlation smiles ⇒ base-correlation quoting, mirroring vol-smile practice.
  • Zero tail dependence was the fatal flaw: dependence structure, not marginals, is the product.
  • Sklar separation, factor conditioning and the Vasicek limit remain core tools across portfolio risk.
