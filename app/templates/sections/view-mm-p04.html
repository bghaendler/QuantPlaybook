Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Functional Integration Techniques

The integration toolkit finance actually uses: substitution, parts, Gaussian integrals — and the handful of canonical computations behind closed-form prices.

1. The Core Techniques

  • Substitution (chain rule backwards): $\int f(g(x))g'(x)dx = \int f(u)du$. The standardization $z = \frac{\ln(S/S_0) - \mu T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$ that turns every lognormal expectation into a Gaussian integral is one substitution.
  • Integration by parts: $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$. Behind: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty (1 - F(x))dx$ for positive $X$, the put-call decomposition of any payoff into vanillas (Carr-Madan static replication: $f(S_T)$ = bonds + forwards + $\int f''(K)\,\text{options}(K)dK$ — parts twice!), and expected shortfall formulas.
  • Differentiation under the integral (Leibniz): how Greeks are derived — differentiate the pricing integral in $S$ or $\sigma$ before integrating; also the Breeden-Litzenberger relation $\partial^2 C/\partial K^2 = e^{-rT}q(K)$.
  • Improper integrals & convergence: tails must decay fast enough — lognormal moments all exist, power-law tails ($t$-distributions) lose moments above the tail index; variance-of-variance integrals in vol models live on this edge.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Integration in finance is used to calculate the present value of continuous cash flows and expected payoffs.

  • Substitution: Standardizes variable asset distributions into standard normal shapes to make integration solvable.
  • Integration by Parts: The mathematical engine behind static replication (valuing any payoff using puts and calls).

2. The Gaussian Integral Family

Everything reduces to:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-z^2/2}\,dz = \sqrt{2\pi}, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{az}\phi(z)\,dz = e^{a^2/2},$$

the second being the normal MGF — i.e. $\mathbb{E}[e^{aZ}] = e^{a^2/2}$, the identity that prices everything lognormal. The completing-the-square move:

$$\int_d^{\infty} e^{az}\phi(z)\,dz = e^{a^2/2}\,N(a - d)$$

is, in one line, the entire derivation of the $S N(d_1)$ term of Black-Scholes: the truncated lognormal mean splits into MGF × shifted normal probability. Master this single computation and BS, Margrabe, exchange and quanto formulas are bookkeeping.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The Gaussian integral is the core equation of all normal and lognormal asset pricing models.

  • Normal MGF: The identity 𝔼[e^{aZ}] = e^{a^2/2} is why stock drift terms resolve into simple analytical exponents.
  • Completing the Square: The calculus trick that allows us to integrate normal distributions over partial ranges.

3. Worked Example: The BS Call in Four Lines

With $S_T = S_0 e^{(r - \sigma^2/2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T}Z}$:

$$C = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[(S_T - K)^+] = e^{-rT}\int_{-d_2}^{\infty}\left(S_0e^{(r-\sigma^2/2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T}z} - K\right)\phi(z)\,dz$$

where $-d_2$ is the exercise threshold. The second piece is $-Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ by symmetry $\int_{-d}^\infty \phi = N(d)$; the first applies the completed square with $a = \sigma\sqrt{T}$:

$$e^{-rT}S_0 e^{(r - \sigma^2/2)T} e^{\sigma^2 T/2} N(\sigma\sqrt{T} + d_2) = S_0\,N(d_1). \;\;\blacksquare$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The Black-Scholes formula is derived by integrating the lognormal stock distribution over the range where the option is in-the-money.

  • Integral Splitting: Separates option pricing into stock delivery (S N(d1)) and cash payment (K e^{-rT} N(d2)).
  • Threshold Boundary: The integration limit -d2 represents the boundary where the option finishes in-the-money.

4. Numerical Integration (When Closed Forms Stop)

  • Trapezoid/Simpson: $O(h^2)$/$O(h^4)$ on smooth integrands — fine for densities; split the domain at payoff kinks to keep the rate.
  • Gauss-Hermite quadrature: built for $\int f(z)e^{-z^2}dz$ — 20 nodes price smooth payoffs under Gaussians to near machine precision; the inner loop of many stochastic-vol and conditioning methods (e.g. spread options, Asian conditioning).
  • FFT (Carr-Madan): integrate characteristic functions across all strikes at once — the production method for Heston/Lévy calibration.
  • Dimension > 3 ⇒ Monte Carlo: quadrature cost is exponential in dimension; MC error is $O(N^{-1/2})$ regardless.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: When options cannot be solved analytically, quants use numerical integration grids or simulations.

  • Gauss-Hermite Quadrature: Prices options under normal distributions with high accuracy using only a few grid points.
  • FFT: Prices options at hundreds of different strikes in a single millisecond, making model calibration fast.

Key Takeaways

  • Substitution standardizes; parts decomposes payoffs (Carr-Madan); Leibniz generates Greeks.
  • $\mathbb{E}[e^{aZ}] = e^{a^2/2}$ + completing the square = the Black-Scholes derivation, reusable everywhere.
  • Breeden-Litzenberger: the option surface integrates/differentiates into the risk-neutral density.
  • Quadrature for low dimensions (Gauss-Hermite for Gaussians, FFT for char. functions), Monte Carlo beyond.
