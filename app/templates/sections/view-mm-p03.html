Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Taylor Series Approximations

Local polynomial pictures of nonlinear functions — the engine behind Greeks, duration/convexity, Itô's lemma and every risk report you will ever read.

1. The Expansion

$$f(x + h) = f(x) + f'(x)h + \frac{f''(x)}{2!}h^2 + \frac{f'''(x)}{3!}h^3 + \dots + \frac{f^{(n)}(x)}{n!}h^n + R_n,$$

with Lagrange remainder $R_n = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}h^{n+1}$ for some $\xi$ between $x$ and $x+h$ — the error is controlled by the next term's size. Workhorse series worth memorizing:

$$e^h = 1 + h + \tfrac{h^2}{2} + \dots, \qquad \ln(1 + h) = h - \tfrac{h^2}{2} + \tfrac{h^3}{3} - \dots, \qquad \frac{1}{1-h} = 1 + h + h^2 + \dots \;(|h| < 1).$$

$\ln(1+h) \approx h$ is why small simple returns ≈ log returns; the $-h^2/2$ correction is why they diverge for big moves (and where "volatility drag" comes from).

2. Finance Is Second-Order Taylor

  • Option P&L: $\delta V \approx \Delta\,\delta S + \tfrac{1}{2}\Gamma\,\delta S^2 + \Theta\,\delta t + \text{Vega}\,\delta\sigma + \dots$ — the desk risk report is a truncated multivariate Taylor expansion; the cross-term $\partial^2 V/\partial S\partial\sigma$ is vanna.
  • Bonds: $\frac{\delta P}{P} \approx -D\,\delta y + \tfrac{1}{2}C\,\delta y^2$ — duration and convexity are nothing but the first two Taylor coefficients in yield.
  • Delta-gamma VaR: approximate the portfolio as quadratic in risk factors, then compute the quantile (Cornish-Fisher) — fast, and accurate exactly while $h$ stays small. 2008's lesson: the remainder term is a market regime, not a footnote.
  • Itô preview: for $dX = \mu dt + \sigma dW$, the second-order term $\frac{1}{2}f''\,dX^2$ does not vanish because $dW^2 = dt$ — Itô's lemma is Taylor to second order plus that one substitution (Part 10).

3. Worked Example: ATM Straddle Rule

Expand the BS call around the money with $b = 0$, small $\sigma\sqrt{T}$:

$$C_{ATM} \approx 0.4\, S\, \sigma\sqrt{T},$$

(precisely $S\sigma\sqrt{T}\,\phi(0)\cdot$ correction, $\phi(0) = 0.3989$). The ATM straddle ≈ $0.8 S\sigma\sqrt{T}$: a one-line vol-to-price converter used on every options desk, and the Brenner-Subrahmanyam implied-vol seed inverted. Accuracy check: σ = 20%, T = 0.25 → $\sigma\sqrt{T} = 0.10$, error of the truncation ~0.1% — third-order terms are $O((\sigma\sqrt{T})^3)$.

4. When Taylor Fails

  • Kinks and digitals: no derivatives at the kink — expansions across a strike near expiry are meaningless (gamma explodes; the remainder dominates).
  • Radius of convergence: $\ln(1+h)$ diverges for $|h| > 1$; geometric series likewise — extrapolating fitted polynomials beyond their data is the classic smile-fitting accident (SVI's linear wings exist precisely to avoid it).
  • Large moves: delta-gamma approximations understate losses for convex-short books in jumps; full revaluation in stress tests is the institutional remedy.

Key Takeaways

  • Greeks, duration/convexity and delta-gamma VaR are Taylor coefficients wearing work clothes.
  • $\ln(1+h) \approx h - h^2/2$: returns arithmetic, volatility drag, and log-vs-simple in one line.
  • $C_{ATM} \approx 0.4 S\sigma\sqrt{T}$ — the most used back-of-envelope formula in options.
  • Respect the remainder: kinks, jumps and convergence radii are where truncation bites.

Plain English Notes

1. The Expansion

The Big Picture: Taylor series allow us to approximate a complex, curvy pricing function using simple lines and parabolas.

  • Local Approximation: The approximation is highly accurate near the starting point but degrades as the stock price moves further away.
  • Polynomials: Simplifies differential equations by converting transcendental functions into basic algebraic terms.

2. Finance Is Second-Order Taylor

The Big Picture: Option risk management is fundamentally a second-order Taylor expansion using Delta, Gamma, and Theta.

  • Delta & Gamma: Delta is the first derivative (linear slope) and Gamma is the second derivative (curvature) of the option price.
  • Theta: Captures the time-decay of the option, representing the derivative with respect to time.

3. Worked Example: ATM Straddle Rule

The Big Picture: The Taylor expansion explains the trading-desk rule of thumb for valuing At-The-Money (ATM) straddles.

  • Straddle Value: ATM straddle prices scale linearly with volatility and the square root of time (σ√T).
  • Desk Rule: Traders use this approximation to instantly quote straddles without running the full Black-Scholes formula.

4. When Taylor Fails

The Big Picture: Taylor series are local approximations; they fail during sudden market jumps or when options are near expiration.

  • Payoff Kinks: At expiration, the option payoff has a sharp kink, where derivatives do not exist and Taylor approximations break down.
  • Jump Risk: Large market crashes violate the assumption of small, continuous price changes.
