1.6 Bivariate Derivatives & Chain Rules

Partial derivatives of multi-variable functions, total derivatives, and chain rules.

1.6.1 Multivariable Derivatives & Chain Rules

In multivariate systems, partial derivatives measure rates of change holding other independent variables constant:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{\delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\delta x, y) - f(x, y)}{\delta x} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{\delta y \to 0} \frac{f(x, y+\delta y) - f(x, y)}{\delta y}$$

The Chain Rule (Case I): If $x = x(s)$ and $y = y(s)$, the total derivative with respect to $s$ is:

$$\frac{dF}{ds} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{ds} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{ds}$$

The Chain Rule (Case II): If $x = x(u,v)$ and $y = y(u,v)$, the partials are:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial u} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial F}{\partial v} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial v} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v}$$

Total Differential ($df$): Signed change along the local tangent surface:

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}dy$$

Exercise 1: Bivariate Chain Rule Proof

Let $F(x,y) = \ln(x^2 + y^2)$ where $x = \sin t$ and $y = \cos t$. Find the total derivative $\frac{dF}{dt}$ and prove it is identically zero.

Step-by-Step Proof:
  1. Calculate partial derivatives of $F$ with respect to $x$ and $y$: $$\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = \frac{2x}{x^2 + y^2} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} = \frac{2y}{x^2 + y^2}$$
  2. Calculate derivatives of parameter functions with respect to $t$: $$\frac{dx}{dt} = \cos t = y \quad , \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = -\sin t = -x$$
  3. Assemble using Total Chain Rule Case I: $$\frac{dF}{dt} = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}$$ $$\frac{dF}{dt} = \left(\frac{2x}{x^2 + y^2}\right)(y) + \left(\frac{2y}{x^2 + y^2}\right)(-x)$$ $$\frac{dF}{dt} = \frac{2xy - 2xy}{x^2 + y^2} = 0 \quad \blacksquare$$
  4. Note: Geometrically, since $x^2 + y^2 = \sin^2 t + \cos^2 t = 1$, the function is evaluating along a constant circular path $F(t) = \ln(1) = 0$. Its derivative is naturally zero.
