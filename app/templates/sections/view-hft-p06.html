High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Multi-Venue Execution: Dark Pools vs. Lit Markets

Fragmented liquidity: the venue taxonomy, the routing problem, dark-pool selection effects, and the regulatory rails the whole game runs on.

1. The Venue Landscape

  • Lit exchanges: displayed books, pre-trade transparency, protected quotes (NYSE, Nasdaq, ~16 US exchanges + European MTFs). Maker-taker or taker-maker (inverted) fee schedules — rebates shape queue economics.
  • Dark pools (ATSs): no displayed quotes; typically match at the midpoint of the lit NBBO. Variants: continuous crossing, scheduled crosses, block venues with size thresholds.
  • Wholesalers / internalizers: retail flow executed off-exchange against market-maker capital (PFOF channel) — a large share of US retail volume.
  • Auctions: open/close (and the EU's growing periodic auctions) — discrete-time matches inside a continuous day.

2. The Routing Problem

A child order can rest on any of dozens of venues; a smart order router (SOR) optimizes over:

  • Fill probability per venue — queue lengths, historical fill rates, venue market share by name and time of day.
  • All-in price — fees/rebates net of price; an inverted venue paying takers can beat a maker rebate after queue-time cost.
  • Latency & sequencing — spraying simultaneously vs. sequentially; stale quotes get faded ("phantom liquidity") if routing is slow.
  • Information leakage — every venue touched reveals intent; pinging many pools advertises the parent order. Leakage cost is real but only measurable in aggregate (markouts by venue).

Formally a multi-armed-bandit / stochastic-assignment problem: exploration (learning venue fill curves) vs. exploitation, with venue states correlated through the common NBBO.

3. Dark Pool Economics: Selection Is the Story

  • The draw: midpoint execution saves half the spread, with zero pre-trade footprint — ideal for patient, uninformed flow.
  • Adverse selection in the dark: your resting midpoint order fills fastest exactly when the market is about to move through it (informed counterparties sweep). Measure with markouts: mid-price drift at +1s/+10s/+60s after fills, per venue — persistent negative markouts identify toxic pools.
  • Quote fading & latency arbitrage: midpoint pegs reference the NBBO; participants with faster SIP/direct feeds pick off stale pegs around quote changes — why venues add speed bumps and randomized matching.
  • Counterparty curation: block venues and trajectory crosses restrict participation to reduce toxicity — the product is the selection effect.

4. Regulatory Rails & Practical Allocation

  • US: Reg NMS — order protection (no trade-throughs of protected lit quotes), access rules, sub-penny restrictions; dark midpoint executions are compliant because they price-improve.
  • EU: MiFID II dark-volume caps (double volume cap → single cap) pushed flow to periodic auctions and SIs — venue mix is jurisdiction-specific.
  • Practical allocation heuristic: urgency low + size large ⇒ dark/midpoint and blocks first; urgency high ⇒ lit sweep with marketable slices; always recycle through venues by measured markout quality, not advertised fill rates.
  • Monitoring: per-venue realized spread, fill-rate-conditional markouts, and reversion after your own fills — the SOR's objective function should be re-fitted from this telemetry continuously.

Key Takeaways

  • Liquidity is fragmented across lit books, dark midpoint pools, internalizers and auctions — routing is an optimization, not plumbing.
  • Dark pools trade pre-trade opacity for adverse selection; markout analysis per venue is the toxicity meter.
  • Fees/rebates, queue position, latency and leakage all enter the router's objective; bandit framing fits the learning problem.
  • Reg NMS / MiFID II shape what's allowed where — venue strategy is inseparable from its regulatory regime.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Trading is split between public exchanges (Lit markets) and private matching pools (Dark pools). Execution algorithms must route orders to minimize leakage and maximize fill rates.

  • Lit Markets: Publicly display orders and prices, providing price discovery but exposing your trading intentions.
  • Dark Pools: Do not display bids/asks; orders match blindly at the mid-market price, eliminating market impact but risking non-execution.
  • Smart Order Routers (SOR): Algorithms that split an order and route pieces to different venues to get the best overall fill price.
