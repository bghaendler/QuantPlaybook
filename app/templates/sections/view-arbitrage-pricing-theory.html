5. Arbitrage Pricing Theory & Option Valuation

Arbitrage Pricing Theory & Risk-Neutrality

By combining Itô's formula and Girsanov's theorem, we arrive at the cornerstone of modern quantitative finance: the **Arbitrage Pricing Theory** and risk-neutral pricing framework.

Suppose a risky asset (stock) $S(t)$ has real-world dynamics:

$$ \frac{dS(t)}{S(t)} = \mu(t) dt + \sigma(t) dW(t) = \left( r(t) + \sigma(t) \lambda(t) \right) dt + \sigma(t) dW(t) $$

where $r(t)$ is the risk-free short rate, $\sigma(t)$ is stock volatility, and $\lambda(t)$ is the **market price of risk** (risk premium per unit of volatility).

The Risk-Neutral Measure $\mathbb{Q}$: Switching to the equivalent probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ defined by Girsanov removes the risk premium. Under $\mathbb{Q}$, all traded assets grow exactly at the risk-free rate:

$$ \frac{dS(t)}{S(t)} = r(t) dt + \sigma(t) dW^*(t) $$

Consequently, the discounted asset price process $S^*(t) = e^{-\int_0^t r(s) ds} S(t)$ is a **pure $\mathbb{Q}$-martingale**.

This allows us to value any derivative payoff $V(T)$ at time $T$ as the discounted expectation of its future value under the risk-neutral measure: $V(0) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^T r(s) ds} V(T) \right]$. To hedge this option, a trader maintains a **replicating portfolio** holding $\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$ shares of stock.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: How do you price a complex option whose value depends on a random stock? Real investors hate risk, and their risk aversion is impossible to measure. The breakthrough of Black, Scholes, and Merton was showing that we can pretend *everyone is risk-neutral* if we hedge our risk away using stock and bonds. Under this "fake" probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$, all assets grow at the interest rate, making option valuation a simple exercise in averaging.

  • No Arbitrage: The "no free lunch" rule. If you can replicate the option's payoff perfectly by constantly buying and selling stock ($\Delta$ shares) and depositing cash in the bank, the option *must* cost exactly the same as the replicating portfolio today.
  • Risk-Neutral Valuation: You do not need to guess if the stock will rise or fall in the real world. You just simulate it growing at the bank rate ($r$), find the average option payout, and discount it back to today.
  • Delta ($\Delta$): The sensitivity of the option price to stock price moves. If $\Delta = 0.60$, the option price moves by $0.60 for every $1.00 move in the stock. To perfectly hedge, you buy exactly $0.60 shares of stock.
  • Example: Pricing a Call option with strike $100. We simulate risk-neutral stock paths. At expiry $T$, if a path ends at $120, the payoff is $20. If it ends at $90, the payoff is $0. The discounted average of these payoffs is the option's fair price.

Practice Exercise: Pricing Binary Options

A **Binary (Digital) Call Option** pays out exactly $1.00 if the stock price $S(T) \ge K$ at expiry $T$, and $0.00 otherwise. Using the risk-neutral pricing formula, write down the pricing formula for this digital option in terms of the standard normal CDF $N(d_2)$, and calculate its price if $S_0 = 100$, $K = 100$, $r = 5\%$, $\sigma = 20\%$, and $T = 1$ year.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution
  1. Under the risk-neutral pricing framework, the value of the digital option is the discounted expected payoff under measure $\mathbb{Q}$:
    $$V(0) = e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \mathbf{1}_{\{S(T) \ge K\}} \right] = e^{-rT} \mathbb{Q}\left( S(T) \ge K \right)$$
  2. In the Black-Scholes model, the stock price at maturity $T$ is:
    $$S(T) = S_0 \exp\left( \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T + \sigma \sqrt{T} Z \right) \quad \text{where } Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$
  3. Solve the inequality $S(T) \ge K$ for $Z$:
    $$S_0 \exp\left( \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T + \sigma \sqrt{T} Z \right) \ge K \iff Z \ge \frac{\ln(K/S_0) - (r - 0.5\sigma^2)T}{\sigma \sqrt{T}}$$
  4. This is exactly equivalent to $Z \ge -d_2$, where:
    $$d_2 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r - 0.5\sigma^2)T}{\sigma \sqrt{T}}$$
    By symmetry of the standard normal distribution, $\mathbb{Q}(Z \ge -d_2) = N(d_2)$.
  5. Thus, the Black-Scholes digital option price formula is:
    $$V(0) = e^{-rT} N(d_2)$$
  6. Substitute the given values ($S_0=100$, $K=100$, $r=0.05$, $\sigma=0.20$, $T=1$):
    $$d_2 = \frac{\ln(100/100) + (0.05 - 0.5 \times 0.20^2) \times 1}{0.20 \times \sqrt{1}} = \frac{0 + (0.05 - 0.02)}{0.20} = \frac{0.03}{0.20} = 0.15$$
  7. Compute $N(d_2) = N(0.15) \approx 0.5596$ (55.96% probability of expiring in the money).
  8. Discount back to today:
    $$V(0) = e^{-0.05 \times 1} \times 0.5596 = 0.9512 \times 0.5596 \approx 0.5323$$

Answer: The digital call option price formula is **$e^{-rT} N(d_2)$**. Under the given parameters, its price is **$0.5323$** (or **$53.23** cents per $1 payout).
