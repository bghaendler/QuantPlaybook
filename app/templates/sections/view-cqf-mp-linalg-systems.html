Solving Linear Systems

Augmented matrices, Gaussian elimination, elementary row operations, and classification of linear system solutions.

1. Augmented Matrix Notation

A system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ unknowns can be compactly represented and solved using an augmented matrix, grouping the coefficient matrix $\mathbf{A}$ and the constant vector $\underline{b}$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1n} & \big| & b_1 \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{2n} & \big| & b_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \big| & \vdots \\ a_{n1} & a_{n2} & \dots & a_{nn} & \big| & b_n \end{pmatrix}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: An augmented matrix is just a shorthand way to write a system of equations without repeating the variable names ($x, y, z$). It lines up the coefficients on the left and the answers on the right, separated by a vertical bar.

  • Clean Bookkeeping: Isolating the numbers from the symbols allows us to perform purely arithmetic operations on the grid grid layout.
  • Computer Friendly: This standardized array format is how algorithms parse, store, and solve systems of equations.

2. Elementary Row Operations

To solve the system, we apply elementary row operations to reduce the augmented matrix into echelon form (where the number of leading zeros increases row-by-row). These row operations are guaranteed not to alter the solution of the linear system:

[ER₁] Row Interchange: $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$
[ER₂] Scalar Multiplication: $R_i \to k R_i \quad (k \ne 0)$
[ER₃] Row Addition: $R_i \to R_i + k R_j$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Think of row operations as legal moves in a game. You can swap equations, scale them, or combine them. As long as you perform these operations uniformly, the final solution coordinates remain completely identical.

  • Gaussian Elimination: A systematic recipe that uses these three moves to clear out variables row by row, working from bottom-left to top-right.
  • Back Substitution: Once the system is in upper-triangular echelon form, we immediately solve for the last variable, then plug it back in to solve for the previous ones.

3. Classification of Linear Systems

Let $E$ be the number of non-zero rows in echelon form (equations) and $n$ the number of unknowns:

  • Inconsistent (No Solution): Occurs when the equations contradict each other, resulting in an echelon line containing zeros on the left but a non-zero constant on the right (e.g. $0 = -3$).
  • Consistent:
    • Unique Solution: Occurs when $E = n$ (exactly one unique coordinate intersection).
    • Infinite Solutions: Occurs when $E < n$, introducing $n - E$ free variables (degrees of freedom).

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: In two dimensions, linear equations represent straight lines. Solving the system is simply finding where these lines meet.

  • Unique Solution: The lines cross at exactly one intersection point.
  • No Solution: The lines are perfectly parallel and will never touch.
  • Infinite Solutions: The lines lie exactly on top of each other, meaning every point along the line is a valid solution.
