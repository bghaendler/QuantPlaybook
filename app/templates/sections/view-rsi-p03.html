Risk-Sensitive Investing — Part 03

Fractional Kelly Capital Growth Strategies in Practice

Kelly's growth-optimal bet sized for the real world: why full Kelly is too aggressive, how fractional Kelly trades growth for drawdown control, and the estimation-error correction that makes it usable.

1. The Kelly Criterion

Maximize the expected log growth rate $g = \mathbb{E}[\ln(1 + f\cdot R)]$. For a continuous asset (μ−r excess, σ vol), the optimal fraction is the γ = 1 Merton weight:

$$f^* = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma^2}, \qquad g(f^*) = r + \frac{(\mu - r)^2}{2\sigma^2} = r + \frac{\text{SR}^2}{2}.$$

Growth-optimality: over a long horizon, full-Kelly wealth beats any other strategy's wealth with probability → 1, and it minimizes expected time to reach wealth targets. The classic discrete form for a bet with edge: $f^* = \frac{p\cdot b - q}{b}$ (win prob $p$, payoff odds $b$). Notice $f^*$ is the squared Sharpe over 2 — the same object the risk-sensitive criterion (Part 02) optimizes at θ → 0.

2. Why Full Kelly Hurts

  • Brutal drawdowns: full Kelly has a ~1/2 chance of a 50% drawdown at some point; the long-run-optimal path is a roller coaster few can hold through.
  • Estimation error is fatal: $f^*$ depends on μ — the least estimable quantity (ARM/equities theme). Overestimating the edge overbets, and the growth curve $g(f)$ is asymmetric: betting above $f^*$ destroys growth far faster than betting below (past $2f^*$, growth goes negative — you lose money with a positive-edge strategy).
  • Higher moments: the log-optimal derivation assumes the model; fat tails and jumps (Part 06) make realized Kelly riskier than Gaussian Kelly suggests.

The growth curve is a downward parabola in $f$ peaking at $f^*$: $g(f) = r + f(\mu-r) - \frac{1}{2}f^2\sigma^2$. Half-Kelly captures 3/4 of the growth ($g$ at $f^*/2$) with half the volatility — the asymmetry that makes fractional Kelly the practitioners' default.

3. Fractional Kelly

Bet $f = c\cdot f^*$ for $c \in (0, 1)$. Connections:

  • = CRRA with γ = 1/c: half-Kelly is exactly relative-risk-aversion 2 (Part 01's Merton with γ = 2); fractional Kelly is the utility-maximizing solution for a more risk-averse investor — not an ad hoc haircut.
  • = risk-sensitive θ = 2(1/c − 1) (Part 02's bridge) — three vocabularies, one knob.
  • Bayesian Kelly: integrating over parameter uncertainty (a posterior on μ) endogenously shrinks $f$ below the plug-in $f^*$ — fractional Kelly approximates the optimal response to estimation error. Rule of thumb: with realistic μ-uncertainty, the certainty-equivalent fraction lands near $c \approx 0.3$–$0.5$.

4. Practice

  • Multi-asset Kelly = $\Sigma^{-1}(\mu - r)$ (the unlevered tangency direction, then scaled) — same $\Sigma^{-1}\mu$ fragility as Markowitz; shrink/BL the inputs (Part 04) before sizing.
  • Drawdown-constrained Kelly (Grossman-Zhou, Cvitanić-Karatzas): impose a floor (never below αW_max) ⇒ a state-dependent fraction that de-risks as you approach the floor — the formal version of "cut size in a drawdown."
  • Practitioners who use it: Thorp, PIMCO-style growth accounts, and many systematic CTAs size at quarter-to-half Kelly; the discipline is to size on conservatively estimated edge, not the in-sample one.
  • Diagnostic: plot realized growth vs. fraction on backtests — the peak is almost always left of the plug-in $f^*$, quantifying the estimation-error haircut directly (talk-kelly-ziemba covers the empirical record).

Key Takeaways

  • Kelly: $f^* = (\mu - r)/\sigma^2 = $ SR²/2 growth — long-run wealth-dominant but drawdown-brutal.
  • The growth parabola is asymmetric: overbetting punishes far more than underbetting; half-Kelly keeps 3/4 growth at half vol.
  • Fractional Kelly = CRRA(1/c) = risk-sensitive θ — and approximates the Bayesian response to μ-uncertainty.
  • Size on conservative edge estimates with drawdown constraints; the realized-growth peak sits left of the plug-in optimum.
