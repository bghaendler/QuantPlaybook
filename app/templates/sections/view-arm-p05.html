Advanced Risk Management

Calculating Value-at-Risk via Historical Simulation

The most-used VaR method in banking: full-revaluation on historical scenarios, step by step, with its weighting refinements and known blind spots.

1. The Method in Five Steps

  1. Window: collect the last $n$ (typically 250–500) daily risk-factor returns (prices, rates, vols).
  2. Scenarios: apply each historical day's return vector to today's factor levels — relative shifts for prices/vols, absolute for rates (choice matters near zero rates).
  3. Revalue: reprice today's portfolio under each scenario — full revaluation for nonlinear books (options through pricing models), sensitivities-based approximation only where linearity is verified.
  4. P&L vector: $n$ hypothetical one-day P&Ls — the empirical distribution.
  5. Quantile: 99% VaR = the 1% worst point (e.g. ~2.5th–3rd worst of 250; interpolate or take the order statistic per house convention). ES = average of losses beyond it.

No distributional assumption, no correlation matrix — co-movements enter through the historical vectors themselves, fat tails and all (Part 03's problem partially self-solves). That is the method's entire appeal.

2. Worked Miniature

Portfolio: $10m in an index, 250-day window. Worst five scenario P&Ls (applying each day's index return to $10m): −$612k, −$498k, −$431k, −$396k, −$370k. Then 99% VaR ≈ the 2.5th worst ≈ $465k (interpolating 2nd–3rd), and ES₉₉ ≈ mean of the worst 2.5 ≈ $540k. Mechanics identical for 1,000 positions — the work is in the revaluation layer, not the statistics.

3. Refinements

  • Exponential weighting (BRW): weight scenario $i$ (age $a_i$) by $\lambda^{a_i}(1-\lambda)/(1-\lambda^n)$, λ ≈ 0.99 — recent regimes dominate; cures the "stale calm window" lag at the cost of effective sample size.
  • Filtered HS (Hull-White/Barone-Adesi): devolatilize returns by their day's GARCH/EWMA σ, then rescale by today's σ — combines empirical tail shape with current vol level; materially better backtests, now widespread.
  • Stressed VaR / stressed ES: run the same engine on a fixed crisis window (e.g. 2008–09) — Basel 2.5/FRTB requirement; removes the procyclicality of rolling windows.
  • Overlapping 10-day P&Ls vs. √10 scaling: overlap induces autocorrelation in the sample but avoids the scaling fallacy (Part 03) — regulators accept both with care.

4. Blind Spots (Know What You Bought)

  • The window is the model: nothing worse than the worst day in the window can appear — 250 calm days produce a confident, useless VaR ("driving by the rear-view mirror"). Complement: stress tests + stressed ES.
  • Quantile noise: the 99% point from 250 observations rests on ~2–3 order statistics — sampling error of ±30–50% is normal; report bands (bootstrap the scenario set).
  • New risk factors (IPOs, new issues) lack history — proxying choices are silent model risk (Part 01).
  • Ghost effects: a crisis day entering/leaving the window steps VaR discontinuously — explain the steps before the desk does.

Key Takeaways

  • HS-VaR = today's book, yesterday's moves: empirical co-movements and tails for free, no covariance matrix.
  • Full revaluation for nonlinear books; the engineering lives in the repricing layer.
  • Filtered HS (devolatilize/revolatilize) is the highest-value refinement; stressed windows fix rear-view myopia.
  • Quote the sampling band — a 250-day 99% quantile is a 2-observation statistic, not a constant of nature.
