1.7 Derivatives & Standard Calculus Results

Fundamental derivative limits, rigorous rules, standard results tables, and implicit differentiation.

1.7.1 Definitions & Linearity

The derivative of $f(x)$ at $x$ represents its local slope and is defined via the fundamental limit:

$$f'(x) = \frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

Algebraic & Exp:
• d/dx(xⁿ) = n xⁿ⁻¹
• d/dx(eᵃˣ) = a eᵃˣ
• d/dx(ln x) = 1/x
Trigonometric:
• d/dx(sin x) = cos x
• d/dx(cos x) = -sin x
• d/dx(tan x) = sec² x

Linearity: $\frac{d}{dx}\left[\alpha f(x) + \beta g(x)\right] = \alpha f'(x) + \beta g'(x)$.
Implicit Differentiation: Differentiate each term of $F(x, y) = 0$ with respect to $x$ (applying the chain rule to $y$ terms) to solve for $y' = \frac{dy}{dx}$ directly.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The derivative is the exact slope of a function's curve at any given point, measuring how fast the outputs respond to tiny changes in inputs.

  • Linearity: If you scale two functions and add them together, their combined derivative is simply the sum of their individual derivatives scaled by the same factor.
  • Implicit Differentiation: If you can't isolate $y$ on one side of an equation (e.g. $y^5 + xy = 3$), you can still find its slope by differentiating everything with respect to $x$, using the Chain Rule to handle the $y$ variables.

Exercise 1: Bivariate Implicit Derivation

Find the equation of the tangent slope $y'$ for the Folium of Descartes: $x^3 + y^3 - 3xy = 0$ at the point $(3/2, 3/2)$.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution:
  1. Differentiate term-by-term with respect to $x$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) + \frac{d}{dx}(y^3) - \frac{d}{dx}(3xy) = 0$$ $$3x^2 + 3y^2 y' - 3(y + x y') = 0$$
  2. Factor out and solve for $y'$: $$3x^2 + 3y^2 y' - 3y - 3x y' = 0 \implies (3y^2 - 3x)y' = 3y - 3x^2$$ $$y' = \frac{y - x^2}{y^2 - x}$$
  3. Evaluate at $(3/2, 3/2)$: $$y' = \frac{3/2 - 9/4}{9/4 - 3/2} = \frac{-3/4}{3/4} = -1$$ The local tangent slope is strictly diagonal and negative.
