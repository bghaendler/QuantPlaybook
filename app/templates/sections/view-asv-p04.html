Asymptotics of Stochastic Volatility — Part 04

SABR vs. CEV vs. Heston: Large-Time Limit Comparisons

Three model families pushed to long maturities: which smiles flatten, which collapse, and what the comparison teaches about choosing models per book.

1. The Contenders' Long-Run Anatomy

  • CEV ($dF = \alpha F^\beta dW$): no stochastic vol at all — the "smile" is pure local-vol skew. Long-time behavior is degenerate: for β < 1 the origin is attainable (absorption = default-like mass at 0) — the left wing's density mass grows with T; effective smile becomes pure downward skew, no curvature.
  • SABR: vol is a GBM ($d\alpha = \nu\alpha dW_2$) — not mean-reverting, not ergodic: $\alpha_T$ is lognormal with exploding variance. Total variance $\int\alpha^2$ has fat (lognormal-like) tails forever ⇒ the smile does not flatten correctly; moment explosions are immediate ($\mathbb{E}[S_T^{1+\epsilon}] = \infty$ for ρ ≥ 0 cases and generally for large u) — SABR is a short-maturity asymptotic device, not a global model.
  • Heston ($dv = \kappa(\theta - v)dt + \xi\sqrt{v}dW_2$): CIR variance is ergodic (Gamma invariant law) ⇒ genuine large-time limit: fixed-strike smile flattens to $\sqrt{\theta}$-level vol, with the scaled limiting smile computable via the explicit limiting cumulant function $\Lambda(u)$ (Forde-Jacquier) — the only one of the three with honest long-run behavior.

2. Moment Explosions: The Sharp Comparison Tool

Define $T^*(u) = \sup\{T: \mathbb{E}[S_T^u] < \infty\}$. Lee's moment formula ties wings to moments: right-wing slope of total variance $= \psi(u^* - 1)$ where $u^*$ is the critical moment.

  • CEV: all positive moments finite (β < 1) — wings eventually Gaussian-thin on the right; left wing dominated by absorption mass.
  • SABR (β = 1): moments $u > 1$ explode at finite (often zero) time for ρ ≤ 0...> precise conditions in ρ, ν — wings stay steep at all maturities; large-T smile in scaled strikes stays non-degenerate wide.
  • Heston: $T^*(u)$ finite and explicitly computable (the Andersen-Piterbarg ODE-blowup criterion); critical moments → constants as $T \to \infty$, wing slopes settle — consistent with ergodic flattening at fixed strikes.

The practical use: moment explosions decide whether CMS convexity, quanto and power-payoff integrals even converge under each model — checked before any pricing.

4. Choosing by Book

  • Short-dated smile quoting/risk (FX options, listed equity): SABR-class asymptotics — unbeatable speed/stability where they're valid.
  • Long-dated/hybrid books (CMS, PRDC, autocall back-ends): mean-reverting SV (Heston-class or LSV) — ergodic structure and finite-moment control are requirements, not luxuries.
  • Default-sensitive equity (low strikes): CEV/local-vol absorption behavior is a feature (links to credit, cf. structural models) — but recognize it's modeling default mass, not smile curvature.
  • Mixed desks mark short ends on SABR quotes and long ends on Heston/LSV surfaces — the handover maturity is itself a governance choice; document it.

Key Takeaways

  • CEV: no SV, absorption-dominated left wing; SABR: non-ergodic lognormal vol — short-time tool only; Heston: ergodic, honest large-T limits.
  • Moment explosion times $T^*(u)$ are the sharp cross-model comparison — and a convergence pre-check for convex payoffs.
  • Identical 6m calibrations diverge wildly at 10y: large-time behavior is pure model risk, invisible in short-dated fits.
  • Match the model's asymptotic regime to the book's maturity profile; document the SABR→SV handover point.
