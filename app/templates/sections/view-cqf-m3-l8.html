Jensen's Inequality & Convexity

Jensen's Inequality states that for any convex function $f(\cdot)$ and random variable $x$: $$E[f(x)] \ge f(E[x])$$ This mathematical principle explains the fundamental value of options. If stock price $S_T$ at expiry is random, and we have an option with a convex payoff function $P(S)$, Jensen's inequality guarantees that options have value strictly above the function of their expected stock price: $$E[P(S_T)] \ge P(E[S_T])$$

Taylor Series Proof (Convexity Adjustment)

Let $S = E[S] + \epsilon$, where $E[\epsilon] = 0$. Expanding $f(S)$ about $E[S]$: $$E[f(S)] \approx E\left[ f(E[S]) + \epsilon f'(E[S]) + \frac{1}{2}\epsilon^2 f''(E[S]) \right] = f(E[S]) + \frac{1}{2} f''(E[S]) E[\epsilon^2]$$ The expected value of the function is greater than the function of the expected value by the convexity adjustment: $$\text{Convexity Adjustment} \approx \frac{1}{2} f''(E[S]) \sigma^2$$ This is the origin of the $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma$ term in the Black-Scholes PDE!

Discrete Die-Rolling Example

Consider a game where you roll a fair 6-sided die, square the spots rolled, and receive that payout:

  • Convex function: $f(x) = x^2$
  • Expected Roll: $E[x] = \frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = 3.5$
  • Function of Expected Roll: $f(E[x]) = (3.5)^2 = 12.25$
  • Expected Payout: $$E[f(x)] = \frac{1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + 4^2 + 5^2 + 6^2}{6} = \frac{91}{6} \approx 15.167$$

Jensen's Gap: $15.167 > 12.25$
The fair price of the game is $\$15\frac{1}{6}$, not $\$12\frac{1}{4}$!

The Kelly Criterion (Optimal Bet Sizing)

Formulated by John Kelly Jr. in the context of information theory, the Kelly Criterion solves the question: *what fraction of wealth should be wagered repeatedly to maximize long-term geometric wealth growth?* It contrasts with Markowitz's static modern portfolio theory by prioritizing repeated compounding growth over one-period risk/reward.

Geometric Wealth Growth SDE

If you bet a fraction $f$ of your wealth repeatedly on returns $\psi$ over step $\tau$ ($\mu \tau$ mean, $\sigma \sqrt{\tau}$ volatility), your wealth grows geometrically: $$E[\ln(W_N)] \approx N E[\ln(1 + f\psi)] \approx N \tau \left( f\mu - \frac{f^2\sigma^2}{2} \right)$$ To find the growth rate maximizer, we differentiate with respect to fraction $f$ and set to zero: $$\frac{d}{df} \left( f\mu - \frac{f^2\sigma^2}{2} \right) = \mu - f\sigma^2 = 0 \implies f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$$ At the optimal bet fraction $f^*$, the maximum geometric growth rate is: $$\text{Max Geometric Growth Rate} = \frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}$$ *This rate is directly proportional to the square of the Sharpe Ratio!*

Geometric Drag & Asymmetry

If you bet too much (even with favorable odds), you will lose money. The asymmetry of percentages dictates that a $10\%$ gain followed by a $10\%$ loss leaves you with $99\%$ of your starting capital ($1.1 \times 0.9 = 0.99$). Over-betting ($f > 2f^*$) leads to eventual geometric bankruptcy.

"Half-Kelly" Risk Reduction

Because parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are highly uncertain, many quant practitioners implement the "Half-Kelly" strategy: betting exactly half of the Kelly optimal fraction ($f = 0.5 f^*$). This retains $75\%$ of the optimal geometric growth rate while dramatically reducing volatility by $50\%$.

Behavioral Happiness & Prospect Theory

Kahneman & Tversky's foundational Prospect Theory demonstrates that the human response to gains and losses is highly asymmetric: losses are felt approximately twice as strongly as equivalent gains ($\alpha = 2$ pain-aversion coefficient).

If we define daily or monthly happiness $H(R)$ as $R$ (for gains $R>0$) and $\alpha R$ (for losses $R<0$), evaluating the expected happiness over a viewing interval $\tau$ yields the closed-form integral solution: $$E[H] = \sigma \sqrt{\tau} \left( \bar{\lambda} + (\alpha - 1)\left( \bar{\lambda}(1 - \Phi(\bar{\lambda})) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\bar{\lambda}^2 / 2} \right) \right)$$ where $\bar{\lambda} = \frac{\mu\sqrt{\tau}}{\sigma}$ is the time-scaled Sharpe Ratio.

The Misery of High-Frequency Viewing

Because short-term noise dominates over the drift on short time horizons, investors who observe their portfolios at high frequency (daily/weekly) will on average be unhappy, even when the portfolio generates positive annual returns!

For typical S&P 500 parameters ($\mu = 8\%$, $\sigma = 15\%$), a positive expected happiness requires: $$\tau > 0.27 \text{ years (approx. 3 months)}$$ * Key Rule: A typical investor should only look at their P&L quarterly to remain emotionally positive. If they insist on viewing monthly, the portfolio Sharpe Ratio must exceed $0.97$!

Discrete Hedging Errors & Transaction Costs

In a real-world setting, continuous rebalancing is impossible, forcing hedging to occur at discrete steps $\delta t$. Under discrete steps, the stock move is $\delta S = \sigma S \phi \sqrt{\delta t}$. The difference between the actual realized Gamma profit $\frac{1}{2}\delta S^2 \Gamma$ and the expected Black-Scholes profit $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma \delta t$ creates the discrete hedging error: $$\text{Error} \approx \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma \delta t (\phi^2 - 1) \quad \text{where } \phi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$ Since $E[\phi^2] = 1$, the Black-Scholes model works on average, but the error is stochastic at any single step.

Leland's Non-linear Transaction Costs PDE

If rehedging at steps $\delta t$ incurs proportional stock transaction costs $\kappa |\nu| S$ (where $\kappa$ is cost percent), subtracting these costs from the expected portfolio change yields Leland's Non-linear PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - \kappa \sigma S^2 \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi \delta t}} \left| \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right| + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0$$ For a single-signed Gamma book, this non-linear term can be internalized by using a modified Leland Volatility: $$\check{\sigma}^2 = \sigma^2 \left( 1 - 2 K \text{ sgn}(\Gamma) \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \right) \quad \text{where } K = \frac{\kappa}{\sigma\sqrt{\delta t}}$$ - Long Gamma ($\Gamma > 0$): Leland Volatility is lower ($\check{\sigma} < \sigma$), option value is reduced. - Short Gamma ($\Gamma < 0$): Leland Volatility is higher ($\hat{\sigma} > \sigma$), option value is increased (compensation for transaction costs).

Extreme Markets & The Crash Coefficient

During severe market events and crashes, traditional portfolio diversification models fail completely. Two core behaviors characterize extreme markets:

1. Correlation Convergence

Asset correlations rapidly approach $1$ during a crash. Under extreme panic, all individual multi-factor asset exposures collapse, and the entire diversified portfolio behaves as if it were exposed to a single underlying index factor.

2. Linear Benchmark Relationships

The relationship between individual asset returns and index returns becomes strictly linear in the tails. We define the crash coefficient ($\kappa_i$) to measure individual movements: $$\text{If index moves by } x\%, \text{ the } i\text{-th asset moves by } \kappa_i x\%.$$

Collapse of Dimensions

In normal markets, the portfolio value is a function of multiple independent variables: $$\Pi_{\text{Normal}} = F(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N)$$ In extreme markets, this collapses to a function of a single variable $x$ (index return) weighted by crash coefficients: $$\Pi_{\text{Crash}} = \bar{F}(x; \kappa_1, \kappa_2, \dots, \kappa_N)$$
