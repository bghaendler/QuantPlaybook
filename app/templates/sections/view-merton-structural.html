Merton Structural Default Model

Credit risk from the balance sheet: equity as a call on firm assets, debt as a risk-free bond minus a put — and default as an economic event, not a statistical one.

1. The Capital Structure as Options

Firm asset value $V_t$ follows GBM; the firm owes a single zero-coupon debt $D$ at $T$. At maturity, equity holders receive $\max(V_T - D, 0)$ — they own a call on the firm's assets:

$$E_0 = V_0 N(d_1) - D e^{-rT} N(d_2), \qquad d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(V_0/D) + (r \pm \sigma_V^2/2)T}{\sigma_V\sqrt{T}}.$$

Debt holders receive $\min(V_T, D) = D - \max(D - V_T, 0)$: risky debt = risk-free bond − put on assets. The credit spread is the put premium amortized over maturity — lenders have sold default insurance to shareholders.

2. Default Probability & Distance to Default

Default occurs iff $V_T < D$:

$$PD = N(-d_2) = N\!\left(-\frac{\ln(V_0/D) + (\mu_V - \sigma_V^2/2)T}{\sigma_V\sqrt{T}}\right),$$

(risk-neutral with $r$; physical with the asset drift $\mu_V$). The argument is the distance to default:

$$DD = \frac{\ln(V_0/D) + (\mu_V - \sigma_V^2/2)T}{\sigma_V \sqrt{T}} \approx \frac{\text{asset cushion in } \sigma\text{'s}}{}$$

— how many asset-volatility standard deviations the firm sits above its default point. Leverage and asset vol are the two levers: a stable utility at 80% leverage can be safer than a volatile tech firm at 40%.

3. Implementation: Backing Out Unobservables

$V$ and $\sigma_V$ are not observed; equity value $E$ and equity vol $\sigma_E$ are. Solve the system

$$E = V N(d_1) - D e^{-rT} N(d_2), \qquad \sigma_E E = N(d_1)\, \sigma_V V,$$

(the second from Itô on $E(V)$). Two equations, two unknowns — Newton iteration. The KMV/Moody's industrial version: default point = short-term debt + ½ long-term debt; map DD to default frequencies empirically (EDF) rather than through $N(\cdot)$, because Gaussian tails are far too thin at short horizons.

4. Strengths, Failures, Extensions

  • Strength: economically causal — links credit to equity markets in real time; explains why credit spreads widen as equity falls and vol rises (the put gains). Basis of capital-structure arbitrage and the Basel IRB asset-correlation framework (Vasicek is Merton + one factor).
  • Failure 1 — short-spread problem: a diffusion cannot default suddenly: model spreads → 0 as $T \to 0$, while real 1-year spreads are tens of bps. Fixes: jumps in $V$, or incomplete-information models (Duffie-Lando).
  • Failure 2 — default only at maturity: Black-Cox (1976) adds a continuous default barrier (first passage); Leland (1994) makes the barrier endogenous via tax shields vs. bankruptcy costs, delivering optimal capital structure in the same framework.
  • Failure 3 — flat term structures and recovery: stochastic rates and barriers (Longstaff-Schwartz 1995) refine both.

5. Worked Example

$V_0 = 100$, $D = 70$, $T = 1$, $r = 4\%$, $\sigma_V = 25\%$:

$$d_2 = \frac{\ln(100/70) + (0.04 - 0.03125)}{0.25} = \frac{0.3567 + 0.0088}{0.25} = 1.462, \qquad PD = N(-1.462) \approx 7.2\%.$$ $$\text{Debt} = 70e^{-0.04} - p(V_0, 70) \approx 67.25 - 1.62 = 65.63 \;\Rightarrow\; y = \ln(70/65.63) = 6.45\% \;\Rightarrow\; \text{spread} \approx 245\text{ bp}.$$

Implied recovery-given-default and spread both fall out of the same put price — internally consistent credit pricing from four balance-sheet numbers.

Key Takeaways

  • Equity = call on assets; risky debt = risk-free debt − put; spread = put premium.
  • $PD = N(-DD)$: leverage and asset vol determine credit through distance to default.
  • Calibrate $(V, \sigma_V)$ from equity price and vol via the two-equation system (KMV in industry).
  • Known failure: vanishing short spreads — cured by jumps, barriers (Black-Cox) or incomplete information.
  • The structural lens powers equity-credit relative value and the Basel IRB formula itself.
