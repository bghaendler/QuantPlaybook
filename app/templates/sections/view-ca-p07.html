Complex Analysis for Quants — Part 07

Harmonic Conjugates & Laplace Solutions

Real and imaginary parts of holomorphic functions solve Laplace's equation — and CR lets you reconstruct one from the other. Steady states, perpetual claims, and the mean-value property.

1. Harmonic Functions

$u(x,y)$ is harmonic if $\nabla^2 u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$. From Part 06, the real and imaginary parts of any holomorphic $f = u + iv$ are harmonic; conversely, every harmonic function on a simply connected domain is the real part of some holomorphic function. $v$ is called the harmonic conjugate of $u$ (unique up to a constant). Where Laplace appears in finance:

  • Time-independent expected values: for 2-D Brownian motion, $h(x,y) = \mathbb{P}[\text{exit through boundary segment }A]$ is harmonic with boundary data $\mathbf{1}_A$ — exit probabilities solve the Dirichlet problem.
  • Perpetual/steady-state claims: infinite-horizon problems shed their time derivative (cf. MM Part 08) and become elliptic — Laplace/Poisson-type equations in the state variables.
  • The heat equation's equilibrium: as $t \to \infty$, parabolic problems relax to harmonic profiles.

2. Reconstructing the Conjugate (Worked)

Given harmonic $u = x^2 - y^2 + x$. CR demands $v_y = u_x = 2x + 1$ and $v_x = -u_y = 2y$:

$$v = \int (2x + 1)\,dy = 2xy + y + g(x), \qquad v_x = 2y + g'(x) \overset{!}{=} 2y \Rightarrow g' = 0.$$

So $v = 2xy + y + C$ and $f = u + iv = z^2 + z + iC$ — recognizing the pattern is the quick check (replace $x \to z$, $y \to 0$ heuristic). Always verify the candidate $u$ is harmonic first: a non-harmonic $u$ has no conjugate, and the integration produces an inconsistency in $g$.

3. Mean Value & Maximum Principles

$$u(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} u(z_0 + re^{i\theta})\,d\theta$$

— a harmonic function's value at a point equals its average on any surrounding circle (a corollary of Cauchy's formula, Part 11). Probabilistically: Brownian motion started at $z_0$ exits a centered disk uniformly in angle, so $u(z_0) = \mathbb{E}[u(\text{exit point})]$ — harmonic functions are martingales of BM. Consequences:

  • Maximum principle: no interior maxima/minima — extremes live on the boundary. The PDE backbone of arbitrage-bound arguments and a free sanity test for any elliptic solver ("interior hot spot ⇒ bug").
  • Uniqueness: two solutions of the Dirichlet problem with equal boundary data differ by a harmonic function that is 0 on the boundary ⇒ 0 everywhere.

4. Solving Laplace by Conformal Transport

Harmonicity is conformally invariant: if $\varphi$ is harmonic and $f$ holomorphic, $\varphi \circ f$ is harmonic. Recipe: map your domain to a standard one (disk, half-plane) with a conformal $f$ (Part 04), solve there, pull back.

  • On the half-plane, the bounded harmonic function with boundary data jumping from $0$ to $1$ at the origin is $\frac{1}{\pi}\arg z = \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan(y/x)$ — exit probabilities by inspection.
  • On the disk, the Poisson kernel $\frac{1 - |z|^2}{|e^{i\theta} - z|^2}$ integrates boundary data to the interior solution — the closed-form Dirichlet solver.

This transport trick — solve where the geometry is easy — is the same philosophy as changing numéraire or coordinates in pricing: move the problem, not the difficulty.

Key Takeaways

  • Re and Im of holomorphic functions are harmonic; conjugates reconstruct via CR integration (check harmonicity first).
  • Mean-value property = BM exits circles uniformly: harmonic functions are the martingales of diffusion.
  • Maximum principle: extremes on the boundary — a free correctness test for elliptic/steady-state solvers.
  • Conformal invariance solves Dirichlet problems by transport to the disk/half-plane (Poisson kernel, $\frac{1}{\pi}\arg z$).
