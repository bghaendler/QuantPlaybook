Swing Contracts: Flexible Volume Take-up

Options where the choice variable is quantity, not just timing: gas and power supply contracts with daily and annual volume flexibility — priced by stochastic control.

1. The Contract

A buyer (utility, industrial) contracts with a supplier for delivery over a period (month/year) at a fixed or indexed price $K$, with volume flexibility:

  • Daily constraints: take $q_t \in [q_{min}, q_{max}]$ each day (Daily Contract Quantity bounds).
  • Global constraints: cumulative take $Q_T = \sum q_t \in [Q_{min}, Q_{max}]$ (Annual/Total Contract Quantity, often with take-or-pay penalties for under-take).
  • Sometimes framed as a limited number $N$ of "swing rights": days on which the buyer may deviate from a baseline nomination.

Each day the holder compares the spot price $S_t$ with $K$: take maximum volume when $S_t > K$ (the contract is cheap), minimum when $S_t < K$ — except that global constraints couple today's decision to the future. Burning flexibility today may forfeit more valuable optionality later.

2. The Stochastic-Control Formulation

The value with remaining flexibility is a dynamic program over an extra state variable — cumulative take $Q$ (or remaining rights $n$):

$$V_t(S, Q) = \max_{q \in [q_{min}, q_{max}]} \left\{ q\,(S - K) + e^{-r\Delta t}\, \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[V_{t+1}(S_{t+1}, Q + q) \mid S_t = S\right] \right\},$$

with terminal condition encoding penalties: $V_T(S, Q) = -\text{penalty}(Q)$ if $Q < Q_{min}$. A key structural result (Bardou-Bouthemy-Pagès): with linear penalties the optimal policy is bang-bang — take $q_{min}$ or $q_{max}$, never interior — which shrinks the action space dramatically.

3. Bounds and Intuition

A swing contract is sandwiched between known objects:

$$\underbrace{\text{strip of forwards}}_{\text{no flexibility}} \;\le\; \text{Swing} \;\le\; \underbrace{\text{strip of } N \text{ independent daily options}}_{\text{flexibility without global constraints}}.$$

The gap to the upper bound is the cost of the volume coupling. With loose global constraints, swing → multiple-exercise option ≈ sum of the $N$ best daily options; with tight constraints ($Q_{min} = Q_{max}$), it collapses to a forward strip. Multiple-exercise options are themselves a ladder: an $N$-rights swing equals an $(N-1)$-rights swing plus one marginal Bermudan — valued recursively.

Underlying price models matter: gas spot is mean-reverting with jumps and strong seasonality (e.g. $d\ln S = \kappa(\theta_t - \ln S)dt + \sigma_t dW + J\,dN$); using GBM materially misprices swing because mean reversion caps how long spikes — the main source of swing value — persist.

5. Hedging & Practice

  • Delta: the expected take profile $\partial V/\partial F_t$ per delivery month — hedged with forward strips, rebalanced as the take/price evolves.
  • Extrinsic value: reported as swing value minus intrinsic (optimal take against today's curve); it decays like theta and is realized only through disciplined re-optimization.
  • Take-or-pay renegotiations (e.g. European gas post-2009, post-2021) are disputes over exactly this optionality — mispriced flexibility becomes corporate P&L.
  • Storage contracts are the same mathematics with inventory in place of cumulative take (inject/withdraw = negative/positive swing).

Key Takeaways

  • Swing = daily volume options coupled by global min/max take constraints — an optimal control problem with state $(S, Q)$.
  • Optimal policies are bang-bang under linear penalties; value sits between a forward strip and a strip of independent options.
  • Longstaff-Schwartz with the cumulative-take state is the practical pricing standard.
  • Mean-reverting, jumpy, seasonal spot models are essential — GBM overstates spike persistence and swing value.
  • Storage, take-or-pay, and multiple-exercise Bermudans are the same problem in different clothes.
