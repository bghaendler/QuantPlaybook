3. Itô's Formula & Calculus

Itô's Formula & Stochastic Calculus

An Itô Process $X(t)$ is a stochastic process that can be written as the sum of a smooth deterministic trend (drift) and a rough stochastic integral (diffusion):

$$ X(t) = X(0) + \int_0^t \mu(s) ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s) dW(s) \quad \iff \quad dX(t) = \mu(t) dt + \sigma(t) dW(t) $$

Because Brownian motion paths are rough ($dW^2 = dt$), standard calculus rules fail. If we apply a smooth function $f(t, x)$ to an Itô process, the expansion must include the second-order Taylor term. This is known as Itô's Lemma:

Theorem (Itô's Formula): Let $X(t)$ be an Itô process. For any twice-differentiable function $f(t, x)$, the differential is given by:

$$ df(t, X(t)) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dX(t) + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} dX(t)^2 $$

Substituting $dX(t)^2 = \sigma(t)^2 dt$ yields:

$$ df(t, X(t)) = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \mu(t) + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\sigma(t)^2}_{\text{Stochastic Correction}} \right) dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\sigma(t) dW(t) $$

The highlighted term is the Itô correction. Ignoring it (i.e. using ordinary chain rule) leads to massive cumulative errors in derivative pricing and risk management.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: In normal math, if you change $x$ a tiny bit, $dx^2$ is so microscopic it vanishes. In random math, because Brownian motion jumps around so wildly, the squared change $dW^2$ is actually a real, measurable time tick $dt$. Itô's formula is just a regular Taylor series that includes this extra second-order term.

  • Why a Correction is Needed: Consider $f(x) = x^2$. If $x$ fluctuates symmetrically by $\pm \epsilon$, the average of $f(x \pm \epsilon)$ is $x^2 + \epsilon^2$. The function value goes *up* on average just from the volatility! The Itô correction measures this exact convexity bonus.
  • $f(x) = x^2$ Correction: $df = 2x dW + dt$. The ordinary chain rule would predict just $2x dW$. The extra $+dt$ is a steady upward drift created entirely by randomness!
  • Example: In stock modeling (Geometric Brownian Motion), the stock price has drift $\mu$. But if you look at the *logarithm* of the stock price, $\ln(S)$, Itô's formula says the drift of the log price is actually smaller: $\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$. pure volatility acts as a "drag" on compounding growth.

Practice Exercise: Geometric Brownian Motion SDE

Consider the Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) model used for stocks: $dS(t) = \mu S(t) dt + \sigma S(t) dW(t)$ with $S(0) > 0$. Apply Itô's Lemma to the function $f(x) = \ln(x)$ to solve for the explicit path $S(t)$.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution

We want to find $d(\ln S(t))$. Let $f(s) = \ln(s)$:

  1. Calculate the derivatives of $f(s) = \ln(s)$:
    $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = \frac{1}{s}, \quad \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial s^2} = -\frac{1}{s^2}, \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = 0$$
  2. Apply Itô's formula:
    $$d(\ln S(t)) = \frac{1}{S(t)} dS(t) + \frac{1}{2} \left( -\frac{1}{S(t)^2} \right) dS(t)^2$$
  3. Recall that $dS(t)^2 = \sigma^2 S(t)^2 dt$. Plug in $dS(t)$ and $dS(t)^2$:
    $$d(\ln S(t)) = \frac{1}{S(t)} \left( \mu S(t) dt + \sigma S(t) dW(t) \right) - \frac{1}{2 S(t)^2} \left( \sigma^2 S(t)^2 dt \right)$$
  4. Simplify the terms:
    $$d(\ln S(t)) = \mu dt + \sigma dW(t) - \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 dt = \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) dt + \sigma dW(t)$$
  5. Integrate both sides from $0$ to $t$:
    $$\ln S(t) - \ln S(0) = \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) t + \sigma W(t)$$
  6. Exponentiate to get the final solution:
    $$S(t) = S(0) \exp\left( \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) t + \sigma W(t) \right)$$

Conclusion: The explicit solution shows that the log-price grows at the modified rate $\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$, proving that volatility acts as a "drag" on actual wealth growth due to convexity effects.
