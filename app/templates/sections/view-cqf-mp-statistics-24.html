2.4 Time Series Analysis

Moving averages (SMA, EMA), alpha multiplier derivations, and autoregressive models AR(1), AR(2), AR(infinity).

2.4.1 Moving Average Models (SMA vs. EMA)

A time series is a sequence of data points, typically measured at successive times spaced at uniform time intervals. Moving averages smooth price data to form trend-following indicators by filtering high-frequency noise.

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): Formed by computing the average over a specific number of periods. As new data becomes available, the oldest data point is dropped: $$SMA_t = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} P_{t-i}$$
  • Exponential Moving Average (EMA): Reduces the lag by applying more weight to recent prices. The recursive definition is: $$E_{i+1} = \alpha (P_{i+1} - E_i) + E_i$$

    Note: In textbook notations, this is mathematically equivalent to $E_{i+1} = 2^{-(n+1)}(P_{i+1} - E_i) + E_i$ where the derived multiplier maps to standard periods.

Smoothing Weight Multiplier ($\alpha$) and Period Lag Equivalents:

The weighting applied to the most recent price depends on the number of periods in the moving average:

$$\text{Weight } \alpha = \frac{2}{n+1}$$
  • A 10-period exponential moving average applies an 18.18% weighting to the most recent price: $\alpha = \frac{2}{10+1} \approx 0.1818$.
  • A 20-period EMA applies a 9.52% weighting to the most recent price: $\alpha = \frac{2}{20+1} \approx 0.0952$.

The weighting for a shorter time period is greater than the weighting for a longer time period. The weighting drops by half every time the moving average period doubles.

2.4.2 Autoregressive (AR) Models

Autoregressive models describe random processes (denoted here as $e_t$) by a weighted sum of their previous values and a white noise error $u_t$:

  • First-Order Autoregressive Process, AR(1): Only the immediately previous value has a direct effect on the current value: $$e_t = r e_{t-1} + u_t$$

    where $r$ is a constant with an absolute value less than one ($|r| < 1$), and $u_t$ is a white noise process drawn from a distribution with mean zero and finite variance.

  • Second-Order Autoregressive Process, AR(2): Values depend on the last two observations: $$e_t = r_1 e_{t-1} + r_2 e_{t-2} + u_t$$
  • Autoregressive Process of Order Infinity, AR($\infty$): Modelled across an infinite lag hierarchy: $$e_t = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} r_i e_{t-i} + u_t$$
