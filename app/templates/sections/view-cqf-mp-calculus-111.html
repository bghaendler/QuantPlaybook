1.11 Complex Algebra & Euler Proofs

Complex conjugates, polar expansions, Euler's formula proofs, and complex trigonometric integration.

1.11.1 Complex Algebra & Identity

For any complex number $z = x + iy \in \mathbb{C}$ (where $i = \sqrt{-1}$), its complex conjugate is defined as $\bar{z} = x - iy$. Key algebraic relations include:

• Re z = (z + z̄) / 2     • Im z = (z - z̄) / (2i)
• z z̄ = |z|² = x² + y²   • |z₁ z₂| = |z₁| |z₂|

Euler's Series Expansion Proof: Substituting $x = i\theta$ in the Maclaurin series of $e^x$ and grouping real/imaginary parts proves Euler's formula:

$$e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \sum_{even} \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k}}{(2k)!} + i\sum_{odd} \frac{(-1)^k \theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Complex numbers expand coordinates into a two-dimensional grid (real vs. imaginary) which lets us model wave rotation, frequency, and circular cycles with ease.

  • Conjugate & Distance: The complex conjugate ($\bar{z}$) is the vertical mirror reflection across the real line. Multiplying any complex number by its conjugate yields a real number equal to its absolute square distance from the origin ($r^2$).
  • Euler's Expansion: By substituting imaginary exponents into the infinite series for $e^x$, the even powers group to form the formula for $\cos\theta$ and the odd powers group to form the formula for $\sin\theta$.
  • Polar Coordinate Rotation: Representing numbers as $r e^{i\theta}$ simplifies multiplication enormously: you simply multiply the lengths ($r$) and add the rotation angles ($\theta$).

Exercise 1: Complex Trigonometric Integration

Evaluate the integral $I = \int e^x \sin x \, dx$ cleanly using the complex exponential method.

Step-by-Step Derivation:
  1. Use Euler's identity: $$\sin x = \text{Im}[e^{ix}]$$ $$I = \int e^x \text{Im}[e^{ix}] dx = \text{Im}\left[ \int e^{(1+i)x} dx \right]$$
  2. Integrate complex exponential: $$\int e^{(1+i)x} dx = \frac{e^{(1+i)x}}{1+i} = \frac{e^x e^{ix}(1-i)}{(1+i)(1-i)} = \frac{e^x(\cos x + i\sin x)(1-i)}{2}$$
  3. Isolate Imaginary component: $$\text{Im}\left[ \frac{e^x}{2} ((\cos x + \sin x) + i(\sin x - \cos x)) \right] = \frac{1}{2} e^x (\sin x - \cos x) + C$$
