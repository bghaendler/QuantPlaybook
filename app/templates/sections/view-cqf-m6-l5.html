L5: Heath, Jarrow & Morton (HJM) Model

Modeling the Entire Forward Curve, Multi-Factor Dynamics, and Jacobi PCA Diagonalization

Modeling the Entire Yield Curve

The Heath, Jarrow, and Morton (HJM) framework models the yield curve as a whole by focusing on the evolution of the entire forward rate curve rather than a single short-rate point. Instantaneous forward rates $f(t, T)$ are continuously compounded rates contracted today for borrowing/lending over an infinitesimal period starting at a future maturity date $T$.

Discount Factors & Forward Rates: Zero-coupon bond prices are linked to the instantaneous forward rate curve via: $$Z(t, T) = \exp\left( -\int_t^T f(t, s) \, \mathrm{d}s \right) \implies f(t, T) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial T} \log Z(t, T)$$

Derivation Methods

The instantaneous forward rate is formally derived through three consistent mathematical methods:

  • Integral over Forward Curve: Directly differentiating log ZCB prices.
  • Present Value of Forward Investment: Taking the limit of discrete-tenor forward contracts.
  • Self-Financing Arbitrage Portfolio: Formulating an arbitrage portfolio of bonds that matures at $T$ and $T+\delta t$.

Zero-Coupon Bond Price Dynamics

We assume the ZCB price follows a generic lognormal SDE under the physical measure $\mathbb{P}$: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}Z(t, T)}{Z(t, T)} = \mu(t, T)\mathrm{d}t + \sigma(t, T)\mathrm{d}W_t$$ By constructing a self-financing risk-free portfolio hedging two bonds of different maturities, we isolate the market price of interest rate risk $\lambda(r, t)$: $$\frac{\mu(t, T) - r(t)}{\sigma(t, T)} = \lambda(r, t) \implies \mu(t, T) = r(t) + \lambda(r, t)\sigma(t, T)$$ Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, setting $\lambda = 0$ simplifies the drift to the short rate $r(t)$: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}Z(t, T)}{Z(t, T)} = r(t)\mathrm{d}t + \sigma(t, T)\mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$

Differentiating log ZCB Prices (Itô's Lemma)

Applying Itô's Lemma to $F = \log Z(t,T)$: $$\mathrm{d}(\log Z(t, T)) = \left( r(t) - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(t, T) \right) \mathrm{d}t + \sigma(t, T) \mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ By differentiating partially w.r.t maturity $T$ and applying the definition of $f(t,T)$: $$\mathrm{d}f(t, T) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left( \mathrm{d}\log Z(t,T) \right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial T}\left[ \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(t, T) - r(t) \right] \mathrm{d}t - \frac{\partial \sigma(t, T)}{\partial T} \mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$

No-Arbitrage Drift Constraint

Since the spot rate $r(t)$ is independent of maturity $T$, its derivative vanishes ($\frac{\partial r}{\partial T} = 0$). By defining the forward rate volatility as $\nu(t, T) \equiv -\frac{\partial \sigma(t, T)}{\partial T}$, we obtain: $$\sigma(t, T) = -\int_t^T \nu(t, s) \, \mathrm{d}s$$ Substituting this back yields the HJM Drift Condition SDE under $\mathbb{Q}$: $$\mathrm{d}f(t, T) = \left( \nu(t, T)\int_t^T \nu(t, s)\mathrm{d}s \right) \mathrm{d}t + \nu(t, T)\mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ This is a critical result: in an arbitrage-free model, the drift of the forward rate is completely determined by its volatility structure.

The Non-Markovian Nature of HJM

Because the spot rate is $r(t) = f(t, t)$, integrating the forward SDE yields a drift that contains the history of volatility and Brownian movements from the initial time $t^*$ to the current time $t$: $$\mathrm{d}r(t) = \left[ \frac{\partial f(t^*, t)}{\partial t} - \frac{\partial \mu(t, s)}{\partial s}\Big|_{s=t} + \int_{t^*}^t \left( \sigma(s, t)\frac{\partial^2 \sigma(s, t)}{\partial t^2} + \left(\frac{\partial \sigma(s, t)}{\partial t}\right)^2 \right)\mathrm{d}s - \int_{t^*}^t \frac{\partial^2 \sigma(s, t)}{\partial t^2}\mathrm{d}W_s \right]\mathrm{d}t - \frac{\partial \sigma(t, s)}{\partial s}\Big|_{s=t} \mathrm{d}W_t$$ This structural "memory" means the spot rate is non-Markovian. A system shock cannot be undone by an equal and opposite shock. Binomial trees grow exponentially ("bushy"), making finite differences unfeasible and requiring Monte Carlo simulation for pricing exotics.

Musiela Parameterization (Constant Tenor)

In calendar time $T$, the maturity date is constant, so the modeled rate represents a shorter-term asset every day. The Musiela Parameterization shifts maturity to constant tenor (time-to-maturity) $\tau = T - t$. Using the multi-variable chain rule: $$\mathrm{d}\bar{f}(t, \tau) = \left( \bar{\nu}(t, \tau)\int_0^\tau \bar{\nu}(t, s) \, \mathrm{d}s + \frac{\partial \bar{f}(t, \tau)}{\partial \tau} \right) \mathrm{d}t + \bar{\nu}(t, \tau) \mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ The term $\frac{\partial \bar{f}}{\partial \tau}$ represents the slope of the yield curve, correcting for "rolling down the curve" as calendar time passes.

Multi-Factor Yield Curve Modeling

To capture complex curve twists and bending, HJM is extended to $k$ independent Brownian motions $W_{i,t}$: $$\mathrm{d}\bar{f}(t, \tau) = \left( \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{\nu}_i(t, \tau) \int_0^\tau \bar{\nu}_i(t, s) \, \mathrm{d}s + \frac{\partial \bar{f}(t, \tau)}{\partial \tau} \right) \mathrm{d}t + \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{\nu}_i(t, \tau) \mathrm{d}W_{i, t}$$ Principal Component Analysis (PCA) decomposes historical yield curve movements, revealing that three factors (PC1 Level Shift, PC2 Slope Twist, PC3 Curvature Bend) explain over $95\%$ of yield curve variations.

Special Case: The Ho & Lee Model

The SDE of the Ho & Lee short-rate model maps directly into the HJM framework by setting $\nu(t,T) = c$ (constant forward rate volatility): $$\mathrm{d}f(t, T) = c^2(T-t)\mathrm{d}t + c \, \mathrm{d}W_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ This demonstrates that traditional affine models are nested as specific, restricted parameterizations within the general Heath-Jarrow-Morton framework.

Exercise 1. The Jacobi Rotation Angle Derivation

Question: Prove that the rotation angle $\phi$ required to cancel the off-diagonal element $a_{p, q}$ in a symmetric matrix $\mathbf{A}$ satisfies $\tan(2\phi) = \frac{2 a_{p, q}}{a_{q, q} - a_{p, p}}$.

Solution:
Under rotation matrix $\mathbf{P_{p, q}}$, the transformed element is: $$a'_{p, q} = a_{p, q}(\cos^2 \phi - \sin^2 \phi) + (a_{p, p} - a_{q, q})\sin \phi \cos \phi = 0$$ Using the trigonometric identities $\cos^2 \phi - \sin^2 \phi = \cos(2\phi)$ and $2\sin \phi \cos \phi = \sin(2\phi)$: $$a'_{p, q} = a_{p, q}\cos(2\phi) + \frac{1}{2}(a_{p, p} - a_{q, q})\sin(2\phi) = 0$$ Rearranging the terms: $$\frac{1}{2}(a_{q, q} - a_{p, p})\sin(2\phi) = a_{p, q}\cos(2\phi)$$ Dividing both sides by $\cos(2\phi)$ and $\frac{1}{2}(a_{q, q} - a_{p, p})$: $$\frac{\sin(2\phi)}{\cos(2\phi)} = \frac{2 a_{p, q}}{a_{q, q} - a_{p, p}} \implies \tan(2\phi) = \frac{2 a_{p, q}}{a_{q, q} - a_{p, p}}$$ This proves the Jacobi rotation angle.

Exercise 2. Jacobi Off-Diagonal Sum Decay

Question: Show that each Jacobi rotation reduces the sum of squares of the off-diagonal elements by exactly $2a_{p, q}^2$: $S(\mathbf{A'}) = S(\mathbf{A}) - 2a_{p, q}^2$.

Solution:
For any orthogonal transformation, the Frobenius norm (total sum of squares of all elements) is invariant: $$\sum_{i, j} (a'_{i, j})^2 = \sum_{i, j} a_{i, j}^2$$ Splitting this sum into diagonal and off-diagonal terms: $$\sum_{i=1}^n (a'_{i, i})^2 + S(\mathbf{A'}) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_{i, i}^2 + S(\mathbf{A})$$ The rotation only affects elements in rows and columns $p$ and $q$. For the diagonal terms, algebraic expansion reveals: $$(a'_{p, p})^2 + (a'_{q, q})^2 = a_{p, p}^2 + a_{q, q}^2 + 2 a_{p, q}^2$$ Substituting this back into the norm invariance equation: $$\sum_{i \neq p, q} a_{i, i}^2 + a_{p, p}^2 + a_{q, q}^2 + 2 a_{p, q}^2 + S(\mathbf{A'}) = \sum_{i \neq p, q} a_{i, i}^2 + a_{p, p}^2 + a_{q, q}^2 + S(\mathbf{A})$$ Canceling the identical diagonal terms: $$2 a_{p, q}^2 + S(\mathbf{A'}) = S(\mathbf{A}) \implies S(\mathbf{A'}) = S(\mathbf{A}) - 2a_{p, q}^2$$ This proves the off-diagonal sum decay.
