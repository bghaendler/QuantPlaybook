Case Study 1: Speculating via Leveraged Amazon Calls

An investor is highly bullish on Amazon (AMZN), currently trading at $198.00 / share. Instead of allocating $198.00 upfront to buy one share, they purchase a 90-day $200 Strike Call Option for an upfront premium of only $7.00 (yielding immense leverage).

Suppose that 30 business days later, AMZN surges by 18.18% to $234.00. Drag the slider to adjust the new call option premium and compare our percentage profit against direct stock purchases:

$34.00
Direct Stock Investment Return (Buy at $198 ➔ Sell at $234): +18.18%
Option Speculator Return (Buy at $7 ➔ Sell at current premium): +385.71%

Why Buying Call Options Provides Structural Leverage:

By paying the $7.00 premium, the speculator caps their absolute maximum downside to exactly $7.00. However, because they participate in the identical dollar-for-dollar upside above the $200 strike, their rate of return is exponentially magnified:

Return = (Premium_sold - Premium_paid) / Premium_paid
(34.00 - 7.00) / 7.00 = 3.857 ➔ 385.71% Profit!

Case Study 2: Hedging Wheat Crops via Put Option Insurance

A farming corporation sows a wheat crop at a cost of 1,100 cents / bushel. While futures protect against downside by locking in a selling rate, they also eliminate any upside. To enjoy upside market performance while establishing a safe floor, the farmer buys a 6-month ATM 1,100 Strike Put Option for a premium of 46.75 cents.
