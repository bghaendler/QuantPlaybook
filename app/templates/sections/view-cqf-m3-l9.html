The Diversification Paradox in Banking

In classical portfolio theory, diversification is the ultimate "free lunch"—it reduces variance without sacrificing expected return. However, Dr. Paul Wilmott demonstrates that when trading with Other People's Money (OPM) under typical institutional incentive structures, diversification is actually against a trader's personal financial interest.

Consider the coin-tossing bonus model:

  • All traders in your bank are betting on the same unbiased coin $C_1$ ($50\%$ win probability).
  • To diversify and show your quantitative edge, you bet on an uncorrelated, biased coin $C_2$ ($75\%$ win probability).
  • A bonus is only paid if both you individually make money AND the broader trading floor makes money. If the floor collapses, the bank pays no bonuses regardless of your performance.

Conventional Failure

Let's calculate the probabilities of receiving a bonus:

  • Herding Strategy (Correlated): You bet on the same coin as your colleagues. You win if and only if the floor wins. Since $P(\text{Floor Wins}) = 50\%$, your bonus probability is: $$P(\text{Bonus}_{\text{Herd}}) = 50\%$$
  • Uncorrelated Strategy (Diversified): You bet on your $75\%$ coin. Because it is independent, the joint probability of you and the floor winning together is: $$P(\text{Bonus}_{\text{Div}}) = P(\text{You Win}) \times P(\text{Floor Wins}) = 0.75 \times 0.50 = 37.5\%$$

Bonus Probability: $50\% \text{ (Herd)} > 37.5\% \text{ (Diversified)}$

Supply, Demand, and the Cow/Milk Fallacy

In classical Black-Scholes pricing, volatility $\sigma$ is treated as the only free parameter, and markets are assumed to be complete. Quants regularly calibrate models by backing out implied volatility from market prices. Paul argues that this is fundamentally flawed due to supply and demand:

1. Systemic Downside Demand

The global aggregate of all equity derivatives nets down to shares, meaning the world is structurally net long equities. To protect against market crashes, investors eagerly buy out-of-the-money puts. This massive imbalance in supply/demand drives put prices up, creating the famous negative volatility skew. The skew is a reflection of greed and fear, not a stationary volatility parameter.

2. The Cow & Milk Analogy

A pint of milk is a scalar price representing a complex vector of input variables: production costs, logistics, retail competition, supply/demand, and perhaps loss-leader pricing. You cannot back out the cost of a cow from a pint of milk. Similarly, you cannot back out a pure volatility parameter from an option price that is governed by transaction costs, hedging errors, and supply and demand.

Volatility Sensitivity & "Bastard Greeks"

If you increase volatility, does the option value always go up? The answer depends entirely on the sign of Gamma ($\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}$) across the option's range.

In the Black-Scholes PDE, volatility sensitivity is mathematically coupled to Gamma: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma + rS \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0$$ If $\Gamma > 0$ everywhere (as in vanilla options), increasing volatility increases option value. If $\Gamma < 0$ everywhere (such as short options), it decreases the value. If Gamma changes sign (as in barrier options), vega becomes highly unstable.

Legitimacy of Greeks

Paul Wilmott terms vega ($\text{vega} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}$) a "bastard Greek" because it is mathematically illegitimate:

A legitimate Greek measures sensitivity to a variable (like $S$ or $t$). Vega measures sensitivity to a parameter ($\sigma$) that was assumed to be a constant during the PDE's derivation! Varying a constant parameter to measure risk is mathematically inconsistent.

Moreover, vega approximates a Gamma-weighted time integral: $$\text{vega} \approx \sigma S^2 (T-t) \Gamma$$ If Gamma changes sign, the standard constant-volatility assumption collapses completely.

The Correlation Nightmare

Quants often treat correlation as a single stationary parameter ($\rho = 0.6$). In reality, correlation in SDEs acts at the infinitesimal timescale ($dW_1 dW_2 = \rho dt$) and is highly unstable across time and regimes.

Perfect Correlation $\rho = 1.0$ Trending Apart

If $dS_1 = \mu_1 S_1 dt + \sigma S_1 dW$ and $dS_2 = \mu_2 S_2 dt + \sigma S_2 dW$, they share the exact same Brownian noise ($\rho = 1.0$). However, if $\mu_1 = 10\%$ and $\mu_2 = -10\%$, they will diverge completely over a macro horizon, showing that short-term correlation is irrelevant for macro asset allocation.

Perfect Negative Correlation $\rho = -1.0$ Growing Together

If $dS_1 = 0.15 S_1 dt + \sigma S_1 dW$ and $dS_2 = 0.15 S_2 dt - \sigma S_2 dW$, they have perfectly opposite noise ($\rho = -1.0$). Yet, because both have high positive drift ($15\%$), over the long run both assets will trend strongly upwards together!

Two-Regime Competition Model

Consider competing running shoe companies: in Regime 1 (growth), they rise together ($\rho > 0$). In Regime 2 (market saturation), they compete fiercely, moving inversely ($\rho < 0$). A single average correlation coefficient fails to capture this regime-switching complexity.

Discrete Hedging, Feedback Loops, and Closed-Form Obsessions

Three major areas showcase the disconnect between quantitative elegant mathematics and real-world finance:

1. Optimal Hedging Efficiency

Instead of rehedging at fixed intervals $\delta t$ (which is highly inefficient), quants can hedge using optimal boundary triggers. Rebalancing only when Delta deviates beyond a dynamic barrier reduces standard deviation of hedging errors dramatically. You get the same risk reduction from 10 optimal hedges as 25 fixed ones!

2. Positive Feedback Loops

Replicating a put option (dynamic portfolio insurance) requires selling stock as prices fall. When many players implement similar models simultaneously, a positive feedback loop triggers: falling prices cause dynamic selling, which accelerates the crash (as seen in the 1987 crash, the 2010 flash crash, and option strike pinning).

3. Closed-Form Obsession

Quants choose models (e.g., Vasicek, CIR, Hull-White) not because they represent reality, but because they yield closed-form solutions that make calibration easy. Paul compares this to an airplane designer who removes all non-linear terms from fluid dynamics simply to make the paper plane equations solvable!
