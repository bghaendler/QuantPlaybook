Basel Accord Evolution (I → IV)

Four decades of bank capital regulation: from a single crude ratio to a multi-metric framework of risk-weighted capital, leverage, liquidity and output floors.

1. The Core Idea: Risk-Weighted Capital

All accords revolve around one ratio:

$$\text{Capital Ratio} = \frac{\text{Eligible Capital}}{\text{Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA)}} \ge \text{minimum}.$$

The story of Basel is the progressive refinement of both numerator (what counts as loss-absorbing capital) and denominator (how risk is measured) — punctuated by crises that exposed each generation's blind spots.

2. Basel I (1988): The Blunt Instrument

  • Credit risk only; five crude risk-weight buckets (0% sovereigns, 20% banks, 50% mortgages, 100% corporates).
  • Minimum 8% of RWA (the "Cooke ratio"), half in Tier 1.
  • 1996 Market Risk Amendment: trading books charged via 10-day 99% VaR × multiplier (≥3) — the regulatory debut of internal models.
  • Fatal flaw: a 100% weight for both AAA and CCC corporates invited regulatory arbitrage — securitize the good loans, keep the bad.

3. Basel II (2004): Risk Sensitivity & Three Pillars

Pillar 1 minimum capital, Pillar 2 supervisory review (ICAAP), Pillar 3 market discipline/disclosure. Credit risk gained internal-ratings-based (IRB) options built on a single-factor Gaussian copula (Vasicek/ASRF) formula:

$$K = LGD \cdot \left[\Phi\!\left(\frac{\Phi^{-1}(PD) + \sqrt{\rho}\,\Phi^{-1}(0.999)}{\sqrt{1-\rho}}\right) - PD\right] \cdot \text{(maturity adj.)},$$

capital covering the 99.9% unexpected loss given banks' own $PD$, $LGD$, $EAD$ estimates. Operational risk was added (Basic Indicator / Standardised / AMA). Flaw exposed in 2008: model risk, procyclicality, far too little capital against trading books and securitizations — banks levered to 40–50× on thin Tier 1.

4. Basel III (2010–): The Crisis Response

  • Capital quality: Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ≥ 4.5%, Tier 1 ≥ 6%, total ≥ 8% — plus a 2.5% conservation buffer, 0–2.5% countercyclical buffer, and G-SIB surcharges (1–3.5%). Effective CET1 demands ~10%+ for large banks.
  • Leverage ratio: Tier 1 / total exposure ≥ 3% — a non-risk-based backstop against RWA gaming.
  • Liquidity: LCR (30-day stress survival: HQLA ≥ net outflows) and NSFR (one-year stable funding).
  • Counterparty credit: CVA capital charge, central-clearing incentives, margin rules for non-cleared derivatives.
  • Trading book (FRTB, finalized within "Basel IV"): stressed Expected Shortfall at 97.5% with liquidity horizons replaces VaR; strict trading/banking-book boundary; desk-level model approval with P&L attribution tests.

5. "Basel IV" (2017 finalization, phased to 2028)

Officially the completion of Basel III; the industry nickname reflects its impact. Centerpiece: the output floor —

$$\text{RWA} \ge 72.5\% \times \text{RWA}_{\text{standardised}},$$

capping the benefit of internal models. Plus: removal of AMA (op-risk now a single Standardised Measurement Approach), constraints on IRB (no advanced IRB for large corporates/banks, input floors on PD/LGD), revised standardised approaches for credit and CVA. Net effect: convergence of model-based and standardised capital, dramatically reduced RWA variability across banks — at the cost of weaker risk sensitivity.

Key Takeaways

  • Each accord refines Capital / RWA; each crisis exposes the current measurement gap.
  • Basel II introduced internal models (IRB, 99.9% Vasicek formula); Basel III re-armored capital and added leverage + liquidity metrics.
  • FRTB replaces trading-book VaR with stressed 97.5% Expected Shortfall.
  • Basel IV's output floor (72.5% of standardised) reins internal models back in.
  • For quants: regulation now defines much of the demand for risk modeling — and its constraints.
