1. Stochastic Basis & Martingales

Stochastic Basis & Martingale Theory

Random variables and processes are modeled on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$, where $\Omega$ is the set of all possible outcomes, $\mathcal{F}$ is the $\sigma$-algebra of measurable events, and $\mathbb{P}$ is the probability measure.

The flow of information over time is modeled by a Filtration $\mathcal{F}_t$ for $t \ge 0$, which is a family of sub-$\sigma$-algebras that are nested: $\mathcal{F}_s \subseteq \mathcal{F}_t$ for all $s \le t$. This represents that our information grows and past events are never forgotten.

Definition (Martingale): A stochastic process $M(t)$ is a martingale with respect to the filtration $\mathcal{F}_t$ if:
  • $M(t)$ is adapted to $\mathcal{F}_t$ (its value is known at time $t$).
  • $M(t)$ is integrable: $\mathbb{E}[|M(t)|] < \infty$ for all $t \ge 0$.
  • The conditional expectation of future values given current information is the current value:

$$ \mathbb{E}[M(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = M(t) \quad \text{for all } t \le T $$

If the expectation is a drift upwards, i.e., $\mathbb{E}[M(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \ge M(t)$, the process is a submartingale. If it drifts downwards, $\mathbb{E}[M(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \le M(t)$, it is a supermartingale.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: A "stochastic process" is just a system that changes randomly over time—like a stock price, interest rate, or a gambler's wealth. Martingales mathematically model a "fair game."

  • $\Omega$ (Samples): All possible histories. If you toss a coin twice, $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$.
  • $\mathcal{F}_t$ (Filtration): The "news feed" of what has happened up to time $t$. At halftime, you know the score of the game so far ($\mathcal{F}_t$), but not the final score ($\mathcal{F}_T$).
  • Martingale (Fair Game): Your best guess for what you will have tomorrow is exactly what you have in your pocket right now. History does not help you beat the game.
  • Submartingale (Favorable Game): A game where your wealth is expected to grow over time (sub = "going up" in expectation, think *sub*-way going up to the surface).
  • Supermartingale (Unfavorable Game): A game where you expect to lose money over time, like any casino game. The house always wins.

Practice Exercise: Verifying Martingale Properties

Let $W(t)$ be standard Brownian motion. Show that the process $X(t) = W(t)^2 - t$ is a martingale with respect to its natural filtration $\mathcal{F}_t$.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution

To verify that $X(t) = W(t)^2 - t$ is a martingale, we must verify $\mathbb{E}[X(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = X(t)$ for $t \le T$:

  1. Write out the target conditional expectation:
    $$\mathbb{E}[W(T)^2 - T \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \mathbb{E}[W(T)^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t] - T$$
  2. Decompose $W(T)$ into its known part at time $t$ and the independent future increment: $W(T) = W(t) + (W(T) - W(t))$.
    $$\mathbb{E}[W(T)^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \mathbb{E}[(W(t) + (W(T) - W(t)))^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$$
  3. Expand the square:
    $$\mathbb{E}[W(t)^2 + 2W(t)(W(T) - W(t)) + (W(T) - W(t))^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$$
  4. Use the properties of conditional expectation:
    • $W(t)^2$ is known given $\mathcal{F}_t$, so it factors out: $\mathbb{E}[W(t)^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = W(t)^2$.
    • The increment $W(T) - W(t)$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_t$, so its conditional expectation is its unconditional expectation: $\mathbb{E}[W(T) - W(t) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = 0$. This kills the middle cross-product term.
    • The squared increment $(W(T) - W(t))^2$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_t$, so its conditional expectation is its unconditional variance: $\mathbb{E}[(W(T) - W(t))^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = T - t$.
  5. Combine these results:
    $$\mathbb{E}[W(T)^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = W(t)^2 + 0 + (T - t) = W(t)^2 + T - t$$
  6. Subtract $T$:
    $$\mathbb{E}[W(T)^2 - T \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = W(t)^2 + T - t - T = W(t)^2 - t = X(t)$$

Conclusion: Since $\mathbb{E}[X(T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = X(t)$, the process $W(t)^2 - t$ is indeed a martingale. In fact, it compensates for the quadratic variance growth of Brownian motion.
