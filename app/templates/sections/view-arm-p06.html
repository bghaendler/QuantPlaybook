Advanced Risk Management

Return Distribution Fat-Tail Realities

The empirical record of extremes: how often "impossible" days occur, the mechanisms that generate them, and the quantitative consequences for anyone holding risk overnight.

1. The Record of "Impossible" Days

  • October 19, 1987: S&P −20.5% in a day — about 22σ on the prevailing vol estimate; a Gaussian world expects one such day per ~10⁵⁰⁺ universe lifetimes.
  • Repeat offenders since: 2008 (±9–11% days), the 2010 flash crash, Feb 2018's vol spike (VIX +116% in a day), March 2020 (−12%, circuit-breaker cluster), single-name and FX gaps (CHF +30% in minutes, Jan 2015).
  • The pattern is statistical, not anecdotal: daily index returns fit power-law tails $\mathbb{P}(|r| > x) \sim x^{-\alpha}$ with $\alpha \approx 3$ (the "cubic law") — extreme days are regular arrivals, roughly one ≥5σ event every few years per market.

2. Mechanisms That Manufacture Tails

  • Volatility clustering: Gaussian-conditional GARCH already fattens unconditional tails (mixture over σ regimes); but conditional tails are fat too — t-innovations needed on top (Part 03).
  • Feedback trading: portfolio insurance (1987), margin spirals and forced deleveraging (2008), stop-loss cascades and liquidity withdrawal (flash crashes) — mechanical sellers into falling prices generate returns no exogenous-news model produces (the HFT Part 08 vol-liquidity loop at market scale).
  • Jumps and gaps: de-pegs, defaults, halts — discontinuities that diffusive models cannot reach at any σ.
  • Crowding: common positioning converts idiosyncratic shocks into synchronized exits — the quant quake of Aug 2007 as the canonical example.

3. Quantitative Consequences

  • Moments mislead: with $\alpha \approx 3$, the fourth moment barely exists — sample kurtosis never converges (one day dominates any window); prefer quantile/tail-index statistics (Parts 03's EVT) to moment statistics.
  • Diversification weakens in the tail: tail dependence rises in crashes (correlations "go to one") — portfolio tails are fatter than marginal aggregation suggests; copula tail-dependence coefficients, not Pearson ρ, are the right object (Part 10).
  • Risk premia interpretation: part of the equity premium is compensation for negative-jump exposure — selling vol/insurance harvests premia punctuated by tail giveback ("picking up nickels before the steamroller"): strategy Sharpe ratios without tail metrics are incomplete.
  • Sizing under tails: Kelly-type growth criteria with fat-tailed inputs prescribe materially smaller positions than Gaussian Kelly — drawdown distributions, not variance, should drive leverage caps.

4. Living With It (Practice)

  • Quote risk at multiple confidence levels (95/99/99.9) — the ratio between them is the in-house tail-thickness gauge; a 99.9/99 ratio near Gaussian flags an under-modeled tail.
  • Pair every statistical measure with scenario floors: minimum capital against 1987-size index moves regardless of model output.
  • Hold convexity where it's cheap: tail hedges (OTM puts, payer swaptions, vol calls) priced against the historical frequency of 5σ+ days, not the model's.
  • Backtest tail behavior specifically (Part 07): exception clustering matters as much as exception counts — clusters are the regime signature.

Key Takeaways

  • Extreme days are regular: cubic-law tails (α ≈ 3) make 5σ+ events a once-per-few-years item per market.
  • Clustering + feedback trading + jumps + crowding manufacture tails endogenously — no model with exogenous Gaussian news can match them.
  • Use quantile/tail-index statistics over sample moments; assume diversification degrades exactly when needed.
  • Multi-level VaR ratios, scenario floors and cheap convexity are the working defenses.
