1.8 Higher-Order Derivatives & Leibniz Rule

Recursive derivatives, the general Leibniz product formula, factorials, and function singularities.

1.8.1 Higher-Order Derivatives

Higher-order derivatives are obtained by repeatedly applying the differentiation operator: $f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d}{dx}[f^{(n-1)}(x)]$. For an $n$-th degree polynomial, the $(n+1)$-th derivative is zero.

Leibniz Rule (Product Derivatives): Generalizes the single-variable product rule to evaluate the $n$-th derivative of a product of two differentiable functions $y = uv$:

$$D^n(uv) = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} D^r u \, D^{n-r} v \quad \text{where} \quad \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$

If one function is a simple polynomial, its higher derivatives eventually vanish, truncating the infinite sum into a finite sequence of terms.

Singularities: Points where a function is mathematically undefined or its derivatives tend to infinity. For example, $f(x) = \frac{1}{x-2}$ is singular at $x=2$ (representing an essential vertical asymptote).

Exercise 1: Truncated Leibniz Proof

Derive the general $n$-th order derivative of $y(x) = x^2 e^{3x}$ using the Leibniz product formula.

Step-by-Step Proof:
  1. Set up terms: Let $u(x) = x^2$ and $v(x) = e^{3x}$. Find derivatives: $$u' = 2x , \quad u'' = 2 , \quad u^{(r)} = 0 \text{ for } r \ge 3$$ $$v^{(k)} = 3^k e^{3x}$$
  2. Apply Leibniz Rule (only 3 terms remain non-zero): $$D^n(uv) = \binom{n}{0} u D^n v + \binom{n}{1} D^1 u D^{n-1} v + \binom{n}{2} D^2 u D^{n-2} v$$ $$D^n(uv) = 1(x^2)\left(3^n e^{3x}\right) + n(2x)\left(3^{n-1} e^{3x}\right) + \frac{n(n-1)}{2}(2)\left(3^{n-2} e^{3x}\right)$$
  3. Factorize: $$D^n(x^2 e^{3x}) = 3^{n-2} e^{3x} \left[ 9x^2 + 6nx + n(n-1) \right]$$
