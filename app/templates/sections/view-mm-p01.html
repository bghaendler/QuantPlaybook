Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Number Sets, Mappings, Functions

The vocabulary everything else is written in: which numbers we work with, what a function really is, and why finance forces precision about domains.

1. The Tower of Number Sets

$$\mathbb{N} \subset \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R} \subset \mathbb{C}$$
  • $\mathbb{N}$ counts (trading days, coupon dates); $\mathbb{Z}$ adds signed quantities (long/short positions).
  • $\mathbb{Q}$ holds quoted prices (ticks are rational!) — but is full of holes: $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$.
  • $\mathbb{R}$ is complete (every Cauchy sequence converges) — the property calculus, limits and Brownian motion live on. Prices are modeled in $\mathbb{R}_{+}$ even though they trade in $\mathbb{Q}$.
  • $\mathbb{C}$ enters via characteristic functions and Fourier option pricing (Parts 02 and the Complex Analysis masterclass).
  • Interval notation matters in term sheets: a barrier "in $[L, U)$" vs "$[L, U]$" is a legal difference, not a stylistic one.

2. Mappings: Domain, Codomain, Image

A function $f: X \to Y$ assigns each element of the domain exactly one element of the codomain. The distinctions that pay rent:

  • Injective (one-to-one): no two inputs share an output — invertible on its image. The Black-Scholes price as a function of vol, $\sigma \mapsto C_{BS}(\sigma)$, is strictly increasing hence injective: this is why implied vol is well-defined.
  • Surjective (onto): every codomain point is hit. $C_{BS}: (0,\infty) \to (\text{intrinsic}, S)$ is surjective onto the no-arbitrage interval — implied vol exists exactly for prices in that range.
  • Bijective = both ⇒ a true inverse $f^{-1}$ exists. Quantile functions $F^{-1}$ (Monte Carlo sampling, VaR) are the inverse of a CDF made bijective by restricting to where it strictly increases.

3. Function Properties Finance Leans On

  • Monotonicity: prices monotone in vol (vega > 0) and in strike (calls decreasing) — the basis of no-arbitrage bounds and root-finding.
  • Convexity: $f(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \le \lambda f(x) + (1-\lambda)f(y)$. Option payoffs are convex in $S_T$; price convex in strike ⇔ positive risk-neutral density (butterfly no-arbitrage). Jensen's inequality — $\mathbb{E}[f(X)] \ge f(\mathbb{E}[X])$ for convex $f$ — is the value of optionality.
  • Continuity & piecewise definitions: payoffs are continuous but kinked (vanilla) or discontinuous (digitals) — and the kink's location is where all the gamma lives.
  • Composition: $\ln$, $\exp$ as the bridge between returns and prices: $S_T = S_0 e^{r_{log}}$; compose carefully — $\mathbb{E}[e^X] \ne e^{\mathbb{E}[X]}$ (the $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction is Part 10's subject).

Key Takeaways

  • $\mathbb{R}$'s completeness is the licence for calculus-based finance; $\mathbb{R}_+$ hosts prices, $\mathbb{R}$ hosts returns.
  • Injectivity/surjectivity of $\sigma \mapsto C_{BS}$ is precisely existence + uniqueness of implied vol.
  • Convexity is optionality: Jensen's inequality prices the smile before any model does.
  • Read payoffs as piecewise mappings; kinks and jumps locate the risk.

Plain English Notes

1. The Tower of Number Sets

The Big Picture: Mathematical definitions provide the baseline structure for asset pricing, ensuring we only model valid price inputs and map them correctly to option values.

  • Integration by parts is the math behind static replication (valuing any payoff using standard puts and calls).
  • Fourier integration prices options instantly by moving calculations into the frequency domain.

The Big Picture: Mathematical methods provide the foundational framework to convert random market variables into clean, actionable pricing models.

  • Monte Carlo simulations value options by averaging the payoffs of thousands of simulated random paths.
  • Value-at-Risk (VaR) is the maximum loss expected under normal market conditions at a given confidence level.
