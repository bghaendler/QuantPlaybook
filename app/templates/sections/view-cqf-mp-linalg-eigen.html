Maths Primer 2.6 Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

The characteristic equation, diagonalisation of symmetric matrices, invertibility criteria, and strict diagonal dominance.

1. The Eigenvalue Problem

If $\mathbf{A}$ is an $N \times N$ square matrix, the eigenvalue problem is to find a scalar $\lambda$ (eigenvalue) and a non-trivial vector $\underline{v}$ (eigenvector) such that:

$$\mathbf{A}\underline{v} = \lambda\underline{v} \quad \implies \quad (\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})\underline{v} = \underline{0}$$

A non-trivial solution exists if and only if the determinant of $(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})$ is zero, which is the characteristic equation:

$$\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = 0$$

2. Properties of Real Symmetric Matrices

Real symmetric matrices ($\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^{\mathrm{T}}$) possess extremely powerful algebraic properties that are heavily leveraged in quantitative asset pricing and covariance structures:

  1. All eigenvalues of a real symmetric matrix are guaranteed to be real.
  2. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are mutually orthogonal.
  3. The matrix can be diagonalised using an orthogonal matrix $\mathbf{P}$ (formed by placing the normalized eigenvectors of $\mathbf{A}$ as columns): $$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{\mathrm{T}} \quad \text{or} \quad \mathbf{P}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{D}$$ where $\mathbf{D}$ is the diagonal eigenvalue matrix: $$\mathbf{D} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & \lambda_n \end{pmatrix}$$

3. Criteria for Invertibility & Diagonal Dominance

A system of linear equations $\mathbf{A}\underline{x} = \underline{b}$ is uniquely solvable if and only if $\mathbf{A}$ is invertible. A square matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is invertible if:

  • The determinant is non-zero ($|\mathbf{A}| \neq 0$).
  • All eigenvalues are non-zero ($\lambda_i \neq 0$ for all $i$).
  • It is strictly diagonally dominant (s.d.d.).

Strict Diagonal Dominance (s.d.d.):

A matrix $\mathbf{A}$ with entries $A_{ij}$ is strictly diagonally dominant if the absolute value of each diagonal element is strictly greater than the sum of the absolute values of the remaining elements in that row:

$$|A_{ii}| > \sum_{j \neq i} |A_{ij}| \quad \text{for all } i$$
