L1: Fixed Income Products & Analysis

Cash Contracts, Benchmark Rates, Swaps, Multi-Curve Framework, and Bond price-Yield sensitivities

Fixed-Income Cash Contracts

Fixed income represents transactions involving the exchange of known cash flows, distinct from equities and commodities. ZERO-COUPON BONDS (ZCBs) are the simplest building blocks, paying a principal amount $N$ at a single future date $T$.

deposits & Day Counts: deposits are OTC lending structures where principal settles on $t_0$ (typically $T+2$) and pays principal plus interest at maturity $t_1$: $N(1 + \delta R)$ [7]. The accrual factor $\delta$ represents the year fraction [8]:

  • $\text{Act}/360$: standard for USD & EUR money markets.
  • $\text{Act}/365\text{F}$: standard for GBP money markets.
  • $30/360$: Assumes $30$ days per month and $360$ days per year, standard for swaps.

Repos, Bills & Benchmarks

Bills & CDs: Securitized deposits quoted on a *discounted basis*, where interest is subtracted at inception: $N(1 - \delta R)$ [9]. Repurchase Agreements (Repos): Secured short-term cash borrowing using bonds as collateral. The borrower delivers bond quantity $Q = N/B_0$, repurchasing at $N(1+\delta R)$ [12].

Interest Rate Benchmarks: Reference rates published by independent administrators:
  • USD-SOFR: Secured Overnight Financing Rate, based on Treasury repo transactions.
  • EUR-$\text{ESTR}$: Euro Short-Term Rate, unsecured wholesale bank deposits.
  • GBP-SONIA: Sterling Overnight Interbank Average, unsecured overnight sterling rate.

Vanilla Swaps, OIS & Comparative Advantage

Vanilla IRS: A bilateral exchange where one leg pays a fixed rate ($C$) and the other pays a floating rate ($L(t_{i-1})$) [20]. Overnight Indexed Swaps (OIS): A swap where the floating leg pays a compounded overnight rate index (e.g. SOFR or SONIA) [21]: $$\text{OIS Floating Leg} = \prod_{i=1}^n \left(1 + \delta_i I^O(s_{i-1}, s_i)\right) - 1$$ Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs): Locks in interest rate for a single future period. The start-settled FRA discount payment formula is [22]: $$\text{FRA Payment} = \frac{\delta \left(L^j(\theta) - K\right)}{1 + \delta L^j(\theta)}$$

Exploiting Comparative Advantage Swap Case Study

Two firms, A and B, want to borrow USD $10$ million for 2 years. Company B has higher perceived default risk:
Company Fixed Borrowing Cost Floating Borrowing Cost
A (Highly Rated) $3.00\%$ $\text{SOFR} + 30\text{ bps}$
B (Lower Rated) $4.20\%$ $\text{SOFR} + 100\text{ bps}$
- Company A's advantage is larger in the fixed market ($1.20\%$) than floating ($0.70\%$), showing comparative advantage in fixed borrowing. - If A wants floating and B wants fixed, they borrow where they have comparative advantages (A fixed at $3\%$, B floating at $\text{SOFR} + 100\text{ bps}$), generating potential combined savings of **$50\text{ bps}$** ($0.50\%$). - They enter a swap where A pays $\text{SOFR}$ and B pays $2.95\%$. Net payments: A pays $\text{SOFR} + 5\text{ bps}$ (saving $25\text{ bps}$); B pays $3.95\%$ (saving $25\text{ bps}$).

The Multi-Curve Framework Post-2008

Before the 2007 Financial Crisis, interbank basis spreads between different tenors (e.g. 3M vs 6M EURIBOR) were negligible, enabling single-curve discounting and forecasting.

The Crisis Breakdown: On August 9, 2007, liquidity squeezes and credit risk pricing caused basis spreads to jump. The single-curve replication assumption broke down because cancellation of cash flows assumed absolute certainty of receipt. The modern **Multi-Curve Framework** separates curves into a **Discounting Curve** (OIS risk-free curves) and tenor-specific **Forward Index Curves** (for floating forecasting) [49].

Swap Pricing & par Rates

Discount factors $P(s, t)$ represent zero-coupon bond present values. Under multi-curve pricing, the Par swap rate $K$ (making the swap's present value $0$ at inception) is [51]: $$K = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n P(0, t_i) \delta_i F^j(0; \theta_i, t_{i-1}, t_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{\tilde{n}} \tilde{\delta}_i P(0, \tilde{t}_i)}$$ Where $P(0, \cdot)$ is the discounting curve, and $F^j(0; \cdot)$ represents the forward forecasting index.

CCPs, Margins & Collateral Account Discounting

Central Counterparties (CCPs): Interpose themselves in derivative markets to enable multilateral netting, significantly reducing credit risk exposure. Variation Margin (VM): Daily realized cash exchanges reflecting P&L shifts. Initial Margin (IM): Additional posted collateral to buffer potential liquidation losses during the Margin Period of Risk (MPoR), sized at the 99th percentile VAR over 10 days.

Collateral Pricing Theorem

Because cash collateral deposited for variation margin earns daily compounded overnight interest $c(t)$, assets must be discounted at the collateral rate rather than a theoretical risk-free rate. The value $V^c_0$ of a future payoff $V^c_u$ is given by: $$V^c_0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{X}}\left[ \left(N^c_u\right)^{-1} V^c_u \right]$$ Where $N^c_u = \exp\left(\int_0^u c_\tau \mathrm{d}\tau\right)$ is the collateral account. Under collateral discounting, the compounded overnight OIS pseudo-discount factor replication holds perfectly: $$\text{PV}(\text{Floating Overnight Coupon}) = P^c(0, s_0) - P^c(0, s_n)$$

Bond Pricing & Associated Measures

Clean vs. Dirty Prices: Bonds are quoted and traded in *Clean prices*, which exclude accrued interest. However, actual settlement cash flows are executed in *Dirty prices*: $$\text{Clean} = \text{Dirty} - \text{Accrued Interest}$$ Yield to Maturity ($Y$) is the internal rate of return that equates the future cash flows to the dirty price.

Modified Duration & Convexity: Used to approximate price changes w.r.t YTM shifts ($Y_0 + \Delta Y$): $$\text{Duration}_{\text{Mod}} = -\frac{1}{\text{Dirty}}\frac{\partial \text{Dirty}}{\partial Y}, \quad \text{Convexity} = \frac{1}{\text{Dirty}}\frac{\partial^2 \text{Dirty}}{\partial Y^2}$$ $$\text{Dirty}(Y_0 + \Delta Y) \approx \text{Dirty}(Y_0) \left[ 1 - \text{Duration}_{\text{Mod}} \cdot \Delta Y + \frac{1}{2} \text{Convexity} \cdot (\Delta Y)^2 \right]$$

Stochastic Interest Rate Modeling

To simulate yield curves, models represent the continuous evolution of rates:

  • Short-Rate Models: SDEs modeling $r_t$ (e.g. Vasicek, CIR, Hull-White): $$P(s, t) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{X}}\left[ \exp\left(-\int_s^t r_\tau \mathrm{d}\tau\right) \Big| \mathcal{F}_s \right]$$
  • Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM): Models the entire forward rate curve $f(t, u)$ directly: $$\mathrm{d}f(t, u) = \mu(t, u) \mathrm{d}t + \sigma(t, u) \mathrm{d}W_t$$
