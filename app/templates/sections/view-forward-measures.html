2. Forward Measures & Option Pricing

Numeraire & Forward Measure Setup

By the Arbitrage Pricing Theory, discounted asset prices are martingales under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$. When we discount bond price processes by the savings account $B(t) = e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds}$, they are $\mathbb{Q}$-martingales.

If we instead discount by a specific $T$-bond $P(t, T)$, we obtain martingales under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$. This change of numeraire is equivalent to $\mathbb{Q}$ with a Radon-Nikodym density process given by:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} \middle| \mathcal{F}_t \right] = \frac{e^{-\int_0^t r(s) ds} P(t, T)}{P(0, T)}$$

This technique is extremely powerful for pricing interest rate derivatives because it removes the discount factor from inside the expectation operator:

$$p(t) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_t^T r(s) ds} X \middle| \mathcal{F}_t \right] = P(t, T) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^T} [X | \mathcal{F}_t]$$

Key Analytical Properties

Why Numeraire Changes Matter:

  • Fundamental Property: For any time points $T_0$ and $T_1$, the ratio $\frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)}$ is a martingale under the $T_1$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$.
  • Simple Forward Rates: As a direct consequence, simple forward rates $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ follow a martingale under the $T_1$-forward measure.
  • HJM Martingale Representation: In the Gaussian Heath-Jarrow-Morton framework, the $T$-forward rate process $f(t, T)$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^T$, meaning $f(t, T) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^T}[r(T) | \mathcal{F}_t]$.
  • Girsanov Transformation: Under $\mathbb{Q}^T$, the drift-adjusted process $W^T(t) = W^*(t) - \int_0^t v(s, T) ds$ is a standard Brownian motion.

Closed-Form Bond Option Pricing Derivation

Here is the full step-by-step mathematical decomposition that enables options pricing under the HJM framework with deterministic volatilities.

👉 1. Payoff Decomposition & Bayes' Rule Application

The time-0 price of a European call option on a $T_1$-bond with expiry $T_0$ and strike $K$ is:

$$p_{\text{call}} = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^{T_0} r(s)ds} (P(T_0, T_1) - K)^+ \right]$$

Let $A_E = \{P(T_0, T_1) > K\}$ denote the exercise event. We can decompose the payoff $(P(T_0, T_1) - K)\mathbb{1}_{A_E}$ and write the price as the difference of two expectations:

$$p_{\text{call}} = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^{T_0} r(s)ds} P(T_0, T_1) \mathbb{1}_{A_E} \right] - K \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^{T_0} r(s)ds} \mathbb{1}_{A_E} \right]$$

By changing measure to the $T_0$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_0}$ in the second term, we get rid of the discount factor:

$$K \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^{T_0} r(s)ds} \mathbb{1}_{A_E} \right] = K P(0, T_0) \mathbb{Q}^{T_0} [P(T_0, T_1) > K]$$

For the first term, we change measure to the $T_1$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$ by using the Radon-Nikodym density relative to $T_1$:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ e^{-\int_0^{T_0} r(s)ds} P(T_0, T_1) \mathbb{1}_{A_E} \right] = P(0, T_1) \mathbb{Q}^{T_1} [P(T_0, T_1) > K]$$

Thus, we arrive at the beautiful representation in terms of probability distributions:

$$p_{\text{call}} = P(0, T_1) \mathbb{Q}^{T_1} [P(T_0, T_1) > K] - K P(0, T_0) \mathbb{Q}^{T_0} [P(T_0, T_1) > K]$$

👉 2. Gaussian Log-Normal Volatilities (Vasicek Model Example)

In a Gaussian HJM model where the forward rate volatility $\sigma(t, T)$ is deterministic, the ratio of bond prices is log-normally distributed. Under the $T_1$-forward measure, we have:

$$\log \left( \frac{P(T_0, T_1)}{P(T_0, T_0)} \right) = \log P(T_0, T_1) \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \log \frac{P(0, T_1)}{P(0, T_0)} - \frac{1}{2}\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2, \; \Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2 \right)$$

Where the integrated variance of the bond returns ratio $\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2$ is:

$$\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2 = \int_0^{T_0} \|v(s, T_0) - v(s, T_1)\|^2 ds$$

For the Vasicek model, with decay volatility $\sigma(t, T) = \sigma e^{-\kappa(T-t)}$, the difference in bond volatilities is:

$$v(t, T_0) - v(t, T_1) = \frac{\sigma}{\kappa} \left( e^{-\kappa(T_0 - t)} - e^{-\kappa(T_1 - t)} \right)$$

Performing the integration results in the exact closed-form variance:

$$\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^3} \left( 1 - e^{-\kappa(T_1 - T_0)} \right)^2 \left( 1 - e^{-2\kappa T_0} \right)$$

👉 3. Put-Call Parity & Put Option Formula

The put-call parity for bond option prices relates call and put options with strike $K$ and expiry $T_0$ by:

$$p_{\text{call}} - p_{\text{put}} = P(0, T_1) - K P(0, T_0)$$

Solving for $p_{\text{put}}$ and substituting the Gaussian HJM call option formula:

$$p_{\text{put}} = K P(0, T_0) \Phi(-d_2) - P(0, T_1) \Phi(-d_1)$$

where:

$$d_1 = \frac{\log \left( \frac{P(0, T_1)}{K P(0, T_0)} \right) + \frac{1}{2}\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}^2}{\Sigma_{T_0, T_1}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \Sigma_{T_0, T_1}$$
