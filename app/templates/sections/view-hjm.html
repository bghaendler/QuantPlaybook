1. Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) Framework

In short rate models (like Vasicek or CIR), we specify the dynamics of the instantaneous short rate $r(t)$ and deduce bond prices. The Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) framework takes the opposite approach: it specifies the evolution of the entire instantaneous forward curve $f(t, T)$ directly.

As a key benefit, the initial forward curve $f(0, T)$ enters as an exogenous parameter. This guarantees an exact fit to the initial market term structure, eliminating the fitting difficulties encountered with standard short rate models.

The $\mathbb{Q}$-dynamics of the forward rates are specified for each maturity $T$ as an Itô process:

$$ df(t, T) = \alpha(t, T)dt + \sigma(t, T) dW^*(t) $$

where $W^*(t)$ is a $d$-dimensional risk-neutral Brownian motion, and the volatility $\sigma(t, T)$ is a $d$-dimensional row vector process:

$$ \sigma(t, T) = \left( \sigma_1(t, T), \dots, \sigma_d(t, T) \right) $$

The Famous HJM Drift Condition

Arbitrage-free pricing implies that the expected return of any zero-coupon bond under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ must equal the risk-free rate $r(t)$. This requirement completely determines the drift process $\alpha(t, T)$ in terms of the volatility $\sigma(t, T)$:

$$ \alpha(t, T) = \sigma(t, T) \int_t^T \sigma(t, u)^T du $$

This is the HJM Drift Condition. In a risk-neutral world, once you choose the volatility structure $\sigma(t, T)$ of forward rates, the drift $\alpha(t, T)$ is completely locked in! There is no free choice for the drift.

Intuition: A positive shock to a Brownian motion drives forward rates up, which causes bond prices to fall. The drift condition adds exactly the right amount of upward drift to compensate for this volatility risk and keep the expected bond return equal to $r(t)$.

Step-by-Step Derivation of HJM

Let's derive the HJM drift condition in three steps using Itô's lemma:

Step 1: Exponent Dynamics

A zero-coupon bond price is given by $P(t, T) = \exp\left(-\int_t^T f(t, u)du\right)$. Differentiating the exponent process $X(t, T) = -\int_t^T f(t, u)du$ under the integral sign gives:

$$ dX(t, T) = f(t, t)dt - \int_t^T df(t, u) du $$

Substituting the forward rate dynamics and swapping integration order yields:

$$ dX = \left(r(t) - \int_t^T \alpha(t, u)du\right)dt + v(t, T)dW^*(t) $$

where $v(t, T) = -\int_t^T \sigma(t, u)du$ represents the bond price volatility.

Step 2: Bond Return Dynamics

We now apply Itô's formula to the exponential function $P(t, T) = e^{X(t, T)}$:

$$ \frac{dP(t, T)}{P(t, T)} = dX + \frac{1}{2}d\langle X \rangle $$

Plugging in $dX$ and the quadratic variation $d\langle X \rangle = \|v(t, T)\|^2 dt$, we arrive at the return decomposition:

$$ \frac{dP(t, T)}{P(t, T)} = \left(r(t) - \int_t^T \alpha(t, u)du + \frac{1}{2}\|v(t, T)\|^2\right)dt + v(t, T)dW^*(t) $$

Step 3: Drift Condition

No arbitrage requires the risk-neutral expected bond return to be exactly $r(t)dt$. Therefore, the drift term must satisfy:

$$ -\int_t^T \alpha(t, u)du + \frac{1}{2}\|v(t, T)\|^2 = 0 $$

Differentiating both sides with respect to maturity $T$ gives:

$$ -\alpha(t, T) + v(t, T) \frac{\partial v(t, T)^T}{\partial T} = 0 $$

Since $v(t, T) = -\int_t^T \sigma(t, u)du$, its derivative is $\frac{\partial v(t, T)}{\partial T} = -\sigma(t, T)$. Substituting these back gives the drift condition.

Short Rate Model Correspondence

The HJM framework is exceptionally broad: every short rate model driven by a finite number of Brownian motions can be mapped to an equivalent HJM representation.

To see this, let's identify which forward volatility structure $\sigma(t, T)$ corresponds to the classic Vasicek short rate model:

$$ dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma_V dW^*(t) $$

In the Vasicek model, bond prices are affine, which implies forward rates are affine functions of the short rate:

$$ f(t, T) = A'(T-t) + B'(T-t)r(t) $$

where $B(T-t) = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa(T-t)}}{\kappa}$, so its derivative is $B'(T-t) = e^{-\kappa(T-t)}$.

Applying Itô's formula to $f(t, T)$ allows us to find its diffusion coefficient (martingale part):

$$ df(t, T) = (\dots)dt + B'(T-t)\sigma_V dW^*(t) $$

Matching this to the HJM specification shows that the Vasicek short rate model corresponds to the HJM volatility structure:

$$ \sigma(t, T) = e^{-\kappa(T-t)}\sigma_V $$

This reveals that the Vasicek model is an HJM model with an exponentially decaying forward rate volatility.

Comparing Volatility Structures

Let's compare the two simplest HJM models: Ho-Lee and Vasicek.

Feature Ho-Lee (Constant Vol) Vasicek (Decaying Vol)
Volatility $\sigma(t, T)$ $\sigma_0$ $\sigma_0 e^{-\kappa(T-t)}$
HJM Drift $\alpha(t, T)$ $\sigma_0^2(T-t)$ $\sigma_0^2 e^{-\kappa(T-t)} \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa(T-t)}}{\kappa}$
Bond Volatility $v(t, T)$ $-\sigma_0(T-t)$ $-\sigma_0 \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa(T-t)}}{\kappa}$
Interpretation All maturities have the same volatility; curves shift in parallel. Short-term rates fluctuate more than long-term rates (damped curve shifts).

Practice Exercise: The HJM Drift Condition

Assume the volatility of the instantaneous forward rate is constant: $\sigma(t, T) = 0.02$ (2%). Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, what is the drift of the forward rate dynamics for a maturity 5 years from now (i.e. $T - t = 5$)?

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution
  1. This model corresponds to the Ho-Lee volatility structure, where $\sigma(t, T) = \sigma_0 = 0.02$ is constant.
  2. According to the HJM drift condition:
    $$ \alpha(t, T) = \sigma(t, T) \int_t^T \sigma(t, u) du $$
  3. Since $\sigma(t, T)$ is constant, we can simplify this integral:
    $$ \alpha(t, T) = \sigma_0 \times \left( \sigma_0 \times (T - t) \right) = \sigma_0^2 (T - t) $$
  4. We are given $T - t = 5$ years and $\sigma_0 = 0.02$. Plug in the numbers:
    $$ \alpha(t, T) = (0.02)^2 \times 5 = 0.0004 \times 5 = 0.0020 \text{ (or } 0.20\% \text{ per year}^2) $$

Answer: The risk-neutral drift of the forward rate $\alpha(t, T)$ is 0.0020 per year (or 0.20%). Note that this positive drift is required to ensure that the expected return of the 5-year zero-coupon bond matches the risk-free rate, offsetting the volatility risk.
