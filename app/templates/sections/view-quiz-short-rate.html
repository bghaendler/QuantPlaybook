Question 1 (Vasicek Model) Multiple Choice

Consider the Vasicek model for the short rate $r(t)$ under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$: $$dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma dW^*(t)$$ The analytical solution of this SDE is given by: $$r(t) = r(0)e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1 - e^{-\kappa t}) + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)} dW^*(s)$$ What is the long-term variance $\lim_{t \to \infty} \text{Var}(r(t))$ under the risk-neutral measure?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

To find the variance of $r(t)$, note that the first two terms in the analytical solution: $$ r(0)e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1 - e^{-\kappa t}) $$ are purely deterministic. Therefore, the variance of $r(t)$ is entirely determined by the stochastic integral term:

$$ \text{Var}(r(t)) = \text{Var}\left( \sigma \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)} dW^*(s) \right) = \sigma^2 \text{Var}\left( \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)} dW^*(s) \right) $$

Using Itô's Isometry for stochastic integrals, the variance of a stochastic integral $\int_0^t g(s) dW^*(s)$ is the integral of the squared integrand:

\begin{align*} \text{Var}\left( \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)} dW^*(s) \right) &= \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)} dW^*(s) \right)^2 \right] \\ &= \int_0^t \left( e^{-\kappa(t-s)} \right)^2 ds \\ &= \int_0^t e^{-2\kappa(t-s)} ds \\ &= e^{-2\kappa t} \int_0^t e^{2\kappa s} ds \end{align*}

Evaluating the deterministic integral:

$$ \int_0^t e^{2\kappa s} ds = \left[ \frac{e^{2\kappa s}}{2\kappa} \right]_0^t = \frac{e^{2\kappa t} - 1}{2\kappa} $$

Substituting this back into the variance equation yields:

$$ \text{Var}(r(t)) = \sigma^2 e^{-2\kappa t} \left( \frac{e^{2\kappa t} - 1}{2\kappa} \right) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa} \left( 1 - e^{-2\kappa t} \right) $$

Taking the limit as $t \to \infty$:

$$ \lim_{t \to \infty} \text{Var}(r(t)) = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa} \left( 1 - e^{-2\kappa t} \right) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa} $$

This shows that the variance of the short rate in the Vasicek model does not grow indefinitely but converges to a steady-state value, reflecting the stabilizing effect of the mean-reversion force.

Correct Answer: $\lim_{t \to \infty} \text{Var}(r(t)) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}$

Question 2 (CIR Model) Multiple Choice

In the Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) short rate model under $\mathbb{Q}$: $$dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma \sqrt{r(t)} dW^*(t)$$ what parameter restriction (known as the Feller condition) ensures that the short rate $r(t)$ remains strictly positive for all $t > 0$ with probability 1, meaning the process never touches the zero boundary?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Analysis & Explanation:

The CIR model is a square-root process. Its volatility term $\sigma\sqrt{r(t)}$ drops to 0 if the short rate $r(t)$ hits 0, which makes the process temporarily deterministic at the boundary. At $r(t) = 0$, the drift is: $$ \kappa(\theta - 0) = \kappa\theta > 0 $$ This positive drift pulls the process back toward the long-term mean $\theta$.

To determine if the process can touch the zero boundary, we analyze the speed of mean reversion relative to the size of the noise near zero. The condition that prevents the process from touching 0 is the Feller condition:

$$ 2\kappa\theta \geq \sigma^2 $$

Under this condition:

  • The mean reversion force $\kappa\theta$ is strong enough to overpower the random diffusion noise $\sigma\sqrt{r(t)}$ as the rate approaches 0.
  • Mathematically, the boundary 0 is classified as an inaccessible boundary (specifically, an entry boundary), meaning the probability of $r(t)$ ever reaching 0 is exactly 0.

If $2\kappa\theta < \sigma^2$, the boundary is accessible (specifically, a regular reflecting boundary). The process can touch 0 periodically, but it is instantly reflected back into positive territory. Thus, $r(t) \geq 0$ holds always, but strict positivity requires $2\kappa\theta \geq \sigma^2$.

Correct Answer: $2\kappa\theta \geq \sigma^2$

Question 3 (Affine Term Structure) Multiple Choice

Under the Affine Term Structure (ATS) framework, zero-coupon bond prices are represented by: $$P(t, T) = e^{A(t, T) - B(t, T)r(t)}$$ For this affine pricing form to satisfy the term structure PDE without arbitrage, what mathematical property must the short rate drift $\mu(t, r)$ and variance $\sigma(t, r)^2$ possess?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Derivation of Affine Conditions:

In interest rate theory, the term structure PDE for a zero-coupon bond price $P(t, T)$ under the risk-neutral measure is:

$$ \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} + \mu(t, r)\frac{\partial P}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma(t, r)^2\frac{\partial P^2}{\partial r^2} - r P = 0 $$

subject to the boundary condition $P(T, T) = 1$.

Suppose the ZCB price takes the exponential affine form $P(t, T) = \exp(A(t, T) - B(t, T)r)$. Let's calculate the partial derivatives:

$$ \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} = \left( \frac{\partial A}{\partial t} - \frac{\partial B}{\partial t}r \right) P, \quad \frac{\partial P}{\partial r} = -B(t, T) P, \quad \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial r^2} = B(t, T)^2 P $$

Substitute these partial derivatives back into the term structure PDE, and divide the entire equation by $P \neq 0$:

$$ \left( \frac{\partial A}{\partial t} - \frac{\partial B}{\partial t}r \right) - \mu(t, r) B(t, T) + \frac{1}{2}\sigma(t, r)^2 B(t, T)^2 - r = 0 $$

For this equation to hold for any value of the short rate $r$, the SDE coefficients $\mu(t, r)$ and $\sigma(t, r)^2$ must be affine (linear) functions of $r$:

$$ \mu(t, r) = \alpha(t) r + \beta(t) $$ $$ \sigma(t, r)^2 = \gamma(t) r + \delta(t) $$

Substituting these affine expressions back into the equation allows us to collect the coefficients of $r$ and the terms independent of $r$ separately, yielding two coupled ordinary differential equations (Riccati equations) for $A(t, T)$ and $B(t, T)$. Because both Vasicek ($\mu = \kappa(\theta-r)$, $\sigma^2 = \sigma^2$) and CIR ($\mu = \kappa(\theta-r)$, $\sigma^2 = \sigma^2 r$) satisfy this linear requirement, they are both Affine Term Structure Models.

Correct Answer: The drift $\mu(t, r)$ and variance $\sigma(t, r)^2$ must both be affine (linear) functions of the short rate $r(t)$.

Question 4 (Hull-White Model) Multiple Choice

The Hull-White model extends the classical Vasicek model by introducing a time-varying drift parameter: $$dr(t) = (\theta(t) - a r(t))dt + \sigma dW^*(t)$$ What is the primary practical advantage of this extension over the standard Vasicek model?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Practical Explanation:

Standard equilibrium models like Vasicek and CIR are "endogenous" or "internal yield" models, meaning the initial term structure is an output of the model parameters. Under standard Vasicek, once you set the long-term mean $\theta$, reversion speed $\kappa$, and volatility $\sigma$, the model determines the initial yield curve. This theoretical curve almost never matches the actual, irregular yield curve observed in real-world bond markets. This creates a significant problem: the model is inconsistent with current bond prices, which leads to mispricing when valuing derivatives.

The Hull-White model resolves this issue by introducing the time-dependent function $\theta(t)$:

  • The time-dependent function $\theta(t)$ can be mathematically selected to fit exactly the initial market term structure.
  • If the initial forward curve observed in the market is $f(0, T)$, then we choose $\theta(t)$ to satisfy: $$ \theta(t) = \frac{\partial f(0, t)}{\partial t} + a f(0, t) + \frac{\sigma^2}{2a}\left(1 - e^{-2at}\right) $$

By defining $\theta(t)$ in this manner, the theoretical ZCB prices generated by the Hull-White model match the market ZCB prices exactly for all maturities, ensuring the model is calibrated (no-arbitrage consistency with liquid instruments) before it is used to value exotic interest rate derivatives.

Correct Answer: The time-dependent parameter $\theta(t)$ can be chosen to perfectly fit the initial term structure of interest rates observed in the market.
