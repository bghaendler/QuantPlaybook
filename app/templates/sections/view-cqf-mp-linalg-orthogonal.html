Orthogonal Matrices

Geometric rotations and reflections, transpose relations, and structural properties.

1. Orthogonal Matrix Definition

A square matrix $\mathbf{P}$ is defined as orthogonal if and only if:

$$\mathbf{P}\mathbf{P}^{\mathrm{T}} = \mathbf{P}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{I}$$

This implies that the inverse of an orthogonal matrix is simply its transpose:

$$\mathbf{P}^{-1} = \mathbf{P}^{\mathrm{T}}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: An orthogonal matrix represents a rigid motion like a rotation or reflection. It transforms coordinates without stretching, shrinking, or bending space, meaning vector lengths and angles between vectors stay perfectly unchanged!

  • Why Inverse = Transpose: Since the columns are perpendicular unit vectors, transposing the matrix performs the exact reverse projection. You don't need complex inversion division routines; reversing the transformation is virtually free.
  • Invertibility: Orthogonal matrices are always invertible because their columns never merge or flatten any dimensions.

2. Core Geometric & Structural Properties

The algebraic definition leads to several powerful geometric characteristics:

  • The rows and columns of $\mathbf{P}$ are mutually orthogonal unit vectors (their dot product is zero, and norm is exactly $1$).
  • They preserve inner products (dot products): $(\mathbf{P}\underline{u}) \cdot (\mathbf{P}\underline{v}) = \underline{u} \cdot \underline{v}$.
  • In two dimensions, standard orthogonal matrices take the form of rotations or reflections: $$\text{Rotation: } \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{or} \quad \text{Reflection: } \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & -\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Applying an orthogonal transformation is just like turning the camera or rotating the room. The distance between objects remains exactly the same.

  • Preserving Energy/Variance: In quantitative finance, we use orthogonal matrices to isolate uncorrelated risk factors (like in PCA). It rotates the dataset into independent axes without losing or distorting any information.
  • Numerical Stability: Multiplying by an orthogonal matrix does not magnify rounding errors, making it a cornerstone for algorithms like QR decomposition used in curve fitting.
