Range Accrual Corridor Note

Coupons that count the days: interest accrues only while the reference stays inside a corridor — a strip of digital options sold against calm markets.

1. Payoff Mechanics

For each coupon period, the note pays an enhanced rate $C$ scaled by the fraction of days the reference $X_t$ (an index, FX rate, or — classically — a swap/deposit rate) fixes inside the corridor $[L, U]$:

$$\text{Coupon} = N \cdot C \cdot \frac{\#\{t : L \le X_t \le U\}}{\#\text{business days}}.$$

Principal is typically protected (the structure lives on the coupon). Decomposition: each observation day contributes a pair of digital options — the investor is short a digital strip:

$$\text{daily accrual} = \frac{C}{D}\Big(\mathbb{1}_{\{X_t \ge L\}} - \mathbb{1}_{\{X_t > U\}}\Big),$$

so the note = bond + $C/D \times \sum_t$ (digital call at $L$ − digital call at $U$), one pair per day — hundreds of small digitals.

2. What the Investor Has Sold

  • Short volatility/realized range: the corridor pays in quiet regimes; breakouts (either direction) stop accrual. The enhanced coupon is digital-strip premium — i.e. vol premium in disguise.
  • Path risk concentrates late: a breach early in a period costs that period only; structural regime change (rate hiking cycle, FX trend) zeroes coupons for the remaining life — duration of the "dead" note extends exactly when rates rise (negative convexity for rate corridors).
  • Smile exposure: digitals price off the skew at $L$ and $U$ (call-spread replication): the issuer hedges with tight call spreads, and the corridor edges inherit all the pin/skew risk of digitals.

3. Pricing

Each daily digital is a cheap closed-form object — under Black-76 on the relevant forward $F_t$: $e^{-rT_t}[N(d_2(L)) - N(d_2(U))]$ with skew corrections via call spreads. The subtleties:

  • Forward curve through the corridor: value is driven by where forwards sit relative to $[L, U]$ — a steep curve that exits the corridor in year 3 makes far coupons nearly worthless however calm the market.
  • Per-day smiles: strictly, each observation needs vol at its own expiry-moneyness; in practice interpolated surfaces with skew-consistent digital replication.
  • Callable range accruals (issuer can redeem) dominate the rates market: pricing then needs a term-structure model with credible vol dynamics (LMM/SABR-LMM or Bermudan-capable short-rate models) — the product that famously punished dealers hedging Bermudan callability against corridor digitals.
  • Dual-range / range on spreads (e.g. CMS 10y−2y stays positive): adds curve-correlation, the 2008-era "steepener" notes.

4. Risk Vignette

USD 3y note, corridor on 3m rates $[0\%, 4\%]$, coupon 6% (vs. 4% market). Two years of accrual at full rate, then a hiking cycle pushes fixings to 5%: coupons stop entirely; mark-to-market drops both from dead coupons and higher discounting. The investor's realized yield ≈ 4% — market rate — having borne breakout risk for nothing. Reverse scenario (rates stay in range): they earn +2% over market, which was precisely the digital-strip premium. There is no free coupon; there is only the range bet, priced daily by the desk.

Key Takeaways

  • Range accrual = bond + short strip of daily digital pairs at the corridor edges.
  • The enhanced coupon is volatility/breakout premium; calm markets pay it, regimes break it.
  • Forwards relative to the corridor — not spot — drive value; steep curves kill far coupons.
  • Digitals ⇒ skew pricing and call-spread hedging at $L$ and $U$; callable versions need full term-structure models.
  • Negative convexity: the note extends and deadens exactly in adverse rate regimes.
