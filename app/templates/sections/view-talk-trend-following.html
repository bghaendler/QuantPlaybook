De-constructing a Trend-Following Trading System

Anatomy of the CTA workhorse: signal generation, position sizing, risk management, and the convexity that explains why trend-following pays — and when it bleeds.

1. The Signal

  • Core idea: buy what's going up, sell what's going down — momentum persists over weeks-to-months across nearly every liquid market (the most robust anomaly outside the equity premium).
  • Constructions: moving-average crossovers, breakout (Donchian channels), time-series momentum (sign of past 12-month return), all roughly equivalent — the signal is robust to its exact form, which is itself telling.
  • Speed: fast (days) captures more turns but pays more cost (HFT Part 07); slow (months) is cost-efficient but laggier — most CTAs blend horizons.
  • Breadth is the edge: run the same simple rule across 50–200 markets (equities, bonds, FX, commodities) — diversification across uncorrelated trends, not signal cleverness, drives the Sharpe.

2. Position Sizing & Risk

  • Volatility targeting: size each position inversely to its volatility ($w_i \propto 1/\sigma_i$) so each contributes equal risk — the single most important implementation choice; turns ragged raw signals into a controlled return stream.
  • Portfolio vol target: scale the whole book to a target annualized vol (e.g. 10–15%) — dynamic deleveraging in turmoil (the risk-control discipline of ARM).
  • Risk allocation echoes Kelly (RSI Part 03): equal-risk-contribution sizing is fractional-Kelly-like; full-throttle sizing on a noisy edge invites the same overbetting damage.

3. Why It Pays: The Convexity

  • Trend-following is long convexity — its return profile resembles a long straddle / long options position: small losses in choppy, mean-reverting markets (paying "theta"), large gains in sustained trends (the "gamma" payoff). This is why it's prized as a crisis-alpha diversifier: it tends to profit in prolonged sell-offs (2008, 2022) when most strategies suffer.
  • The cost of convexity: like any long-options position, it bleeds in range-bound, whipsaw regimes — multi-year drawdowns are normal and the behavioral test of the strategy.
  • The premium's source: debated — behavioral under-reaction then herding, risk transfer from hedgers, or compensation for the whipsaw pain. The convexity framing (Fung-Hsieh: trend ≈ lookback straddles) is the cleanest model.

4. Building & Evaluating One

  • Honest backtest: realistic costs and slippage (HFT stack), point-in-time data, no parameter mining across the lookback — trend systems are notoriously easy to overfit on the speed parameter (portfolio-workshop's warning).
  • Capacity & costs: the edge is real but thin per-trade; futures liquidity sets capacity, and turnover-times-cost is the make-or-break.
  • Evaluation: judge on diversified, vol-targeted, post-cost equity curve and its drawdown profile and crisis behavior — not in-sample Sharpe; the value is the convexity and correlation to the rest of a portfolio.

Key Takeaways

  • Simple momentum signals run with breadth across many markets — diversification, not signal cleverness, is the edge.
  • Inverse-vol sizing + portfolio vol targeting is the implementation core, echoing fractional-Kelly risk control.
  • Trend-following is long convexity (≈ long straddles): crisis alpha that bleeds in whipsaws — multi-year drawdowns are normal.
  • Evaluate on diversified, vol-targeted, post-cost drawdown/crisis behavior, not in-sample Sharpe; beware speed-parameter overfitting.
