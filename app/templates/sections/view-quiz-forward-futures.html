Question 1 (Forward Rate Calculation) Numeric Input

Suppose we observe the 1-year yield $y(0, 1) = 4\%$ and the 2-year yield $y(0, 2) = 6\%$. Calculate the simple forward rate $F(0, 1, 2)$. Express your answer in percentage points and round to two decimal places.

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

Under continuous compounding, the Zero-Coupon Bond (ZCB) prices maturing in $T=1$ and $T=2$ years are:

$$ P(0, 1) = e^{-1 \times y(0,1)} = e^{-0.04} \approx 0.960789 $$ $$ P(0, 2) = e^{-2 \times y(0,2)} = e^{-0.12} \approx 0.886920 $$

The simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ over the future period $[T_0, T_1] = [1, 2]$ has length $\delta = 2 - 1 = 1$. The pricing relation satisfies:

$$ F(0, 1, 2) = \frac{1}{\delta} \left( \frac{P(0, 1)}{P(0, 2)} - 1 \right) = \frac{1}{1} \left( \frac{e^{-0.04}}{e^{-0.12}} - 1 \right) = e^{0.08} - 1 $$

Evaluating this exponential term:

$$ e^{0.08} - 1 \approx 1.083287 - 1 = 0.083287 \text{ (or } \mathbf{8.33\%}\text{)} $$

Expressed in percentage points, the simple forward rate is exactly 8.33.

Correct Answer: 8.33

Question 2 (Discount Curve & Forward Sign) Multiple Choice

If the discount curve $T \mapsto P(t, T)$ is increasing for $T \in [T_0, T_1]$, can you say something about the sign of the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Proof:

The simple forward rate over $[T_0, T_1]$ is analytically defined as:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \left( \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} - 1 \right) $$

The period length $\delta = T_1 - T_0$ is strictly positive ($\delta > 0$). If the discount curve $T \mapsto P(t, T)$ is strictly increasing over $[T_0, T_1]$:

$$ T_0 < T_1 \implies P(t, T_0) < P(t, T_1) $$

Dividing both sides by the positive price $P(t, T_1)$ yields:

$$ \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} < 1 \implies \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} - 1 < 0 $$

Dividing by the positive $\delta$, we establish that:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) < 0 \text{ (strictly Negative)} $$

Typically, discount curves are strictly decreasing over maturity due to time preference, which corresponds to positive forward rates. An increasing discount curve implies negative interest rates, forcing the forward rate to be strictly **Negative**.

Correct Answer: Negative

Question 3 (Spot Yield vs. Instantaneous Forward) Multiple Choice

Suppose the yield curve $T \mapsto y(t, T)$ is increasing for $T \in [T_0, T_1]$. Which of the following statements is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

The yield curve is related to instantaneous forward rates by taking the integral average over maturity:

$$ y(t, T) = \frac{1}{T - t} \int_t^T f(t, u) du \implies (T - t) y(t, T) = \int_t^T f(t, u) du $$

Differentiating both sides with respect to maturity $T$ using the product rule and the fundamental theorem of calculus:

$$ y(t, T) + (T - t) \frac{\partial y(t, T)}{\partial T} = f(t, T) $$

Rearranging this equation gives the key spot-forward slope relation:

$$ f(t, T) = y(t, T) + (T - t) \frac{\partial y(t, T)}{\partial T} $$

We analyze the signs of the terms on the right-hand side:

  • Since the yield curve $T \mapsto y(t, T)$ is strictly increasing, its slope is positive: $\frac{\partial y(t, T)}{\partial T} > 0$.
  • Since the maturity date is in the future, the time-to-maturity length is positive: $T - t > 0$.

Therefore, their product is positive: $(T-t) \frac{\partial y(t, T)}{\partial T} > 0$, establishing that:

$$ f(t, T) > y(t, T) $$

Thus, the instantaneous forward curve is strictly **above** the spot yield curve when yields are upward-sloping.

Correct Answer: The instantaneous forward curve is above the yield curve.

Question 4 (Yield Curve & Forward Yield Inequality) Multiple Choice

Suppose the yield curve $T \mapsto y(t, T)$ is increasing for $T \in [T_0, T_1]$. Which of the following statements about the forward yield $Y(t, T_0, T_1)$ is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Proof:

The continuously compounded forward rate (forward yield) $Y(t, T_0, T_1)$ is defined by:

$$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0} \log\left(\frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)}\right) $$

Substituting ZCB price in terms of continuously compounded spot yield $P(t, T) = e^{-(T-t)y(t, T)}$:

$$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{(T_1 - t)y(t, T_1) - (T_0 - t)y(t, T_0)}{T_1 - T_0} $$

We rearrange the numerator algebraically by adding and subtracting $(T_1 - t)y(t, T_0)$:

$$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) = y(t, T_1) + \frac{T_0 - t}{T_1 - T_0} \bigl(y(t, T_1) - y(t, T_0)\bigr) $$

Since the spot yield curve $y(t, T)$ is strictly increasing:

  • The yield difference is positive: $y(t, T_1) - y(t, T_0) > 0$.
  • Since $T_1 > T_0 \ge t$, we have $T_1 - T_0 > 0$ and $T_0 - t \ge 0$.

Therefore, the second term is strictly non-negative. This establishes that:

$$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) \geq y(t, T_1) > y(t, T_0) $$

Thus, we establish the chain of inequalities: $y(t, T_0) < y(t, T_1) < Y(t, T_0, T_1)$.

Correct Answer: $y(t, T_0) < y(t, T_1) < Y(t, T_0, T_1)$.

Question 5 (Yield Curve & Simple Forward Inequality) Multiple Choice

Suppose the yield curve $T \mapsto y(t, T)$ is increasing for $T \in [T_0, T_1]$. Which of the following statements about the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Proof:

We connect three distinct interest rate concepts:

  1. Forward Yield vs. Spot Yield: In Question 4, we proved that if the yield curve is strictly increasing over $[T_0, T_1]$, then the continuously compounded forward yield is strictly greater than the spot yield at maturity: $$ y(t, T_1) < Y(t, T_0, T_1) $$
  2. Simple Forward vs. Forward Yield: In our reading module on Forward Yields, we established that due to continuous compounding frequency benefits, the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is strictly larger than the continuously compounded forward yield $Y(t, T_0, T_1)$: $$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) < F(t, T_0, T_1) $$

Combining these two inequalities via the transitive property of ordering:

$$ y(t, T_0) < y(t, T_1) < Y(t, T_0, T_1) < F(t, T_0, T_1) $$

Therefore, we establish the chain: $y(t, T_0) < y(t, T_1) < F(t, T_0, T_1)$.

Correct Answer: $y(t, T_0) < y(t, T_1) < F(t, T_0, T_1)$.

Question 6 (Simple Spot vs. Simple Forward) Multiple Choice

Suppose the term structure of simple spot rates $T \mapsto L(t, T)$ is decreasing for $T \in [T_0, T_1]$. If we assume furthermore that $L(t, T_0)L(t, T_1) > 0$. Which of the following statements about the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is true?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

Under simple spot compounding, the discount factors satisfy:

$$ P(t, T) = \frac{1}{1 + (T-t)L(t, T)} $$

Without loss of generality, let $t = 0$. The discount factors maturing at $T_0$ and $T_1$ are:

$$ P(0, T_0) = \frac{1}{1 + T_0 L(0, T_0)}, \quad P(0, T_1) = \frac{1}{1 + T_1 L(0, T_1)} $$

The simple forward rate is defined as: $F(0, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0}\left(\frac{P(0, T_0)}{P(0, T_1)} - 1\right)$. Substituting the discount factors yields:

$$ F(0, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0} \left( \frac{1 + T_1 L(0, T_1)}{1 + T_0 L(0, T_0)} - 1 \right) = \frac{T_1 L(0, T_1) - T_0 L(0, T_0)}{(T_1 - T_0)(1 + T_0 L(0, T_0))} $$

Since the simple spot curve is decreasing, $L(0, T_1) < L(0, T_0)$. Let's compare $F(0, T_0, T_1)$ to the spot rate at maturity $L(0, T_1)$:

$$ F(0, T_0, T_1) - L(0, T_1) = \frac{T_1 L(0, T_1) - T_0 L(0, T_0) - L(0, T_1)(T_1 - T_0)(1 + T_0 L(0, T_0))}{(T_1 - T_0)(1 + T_0 L(0, T_0))} $$

Expanding and simplifying the numerator:

$$ \text{Numerator} = T_0 \bigl( L(0, T_1) - L(0, T_0) \bigr) - T_0 (T_1 - T_0) L(0, T_0) L(0, T_1) $$

Analyzing the signs of the terms:

  • Since $L$ is strictly decreasing, $L(0, T_1) - L(0, T_0) < 0$.
  • Since simple rates are positive, $L(0, T_0) L(0, T_1) > 0$.
  • With $T_1 > T_0 > 0$, the second term $-T_0(T_1 - T_0) L(0, T_0) L(0, T_1) < 0$.

Thus, the numerator is strictly negative, proving that: $F(0, T_0, T_1) - L(0, T_1) < 0 \implies F(0, T_0, T_1) < L(0, T_1)$.

Since the spot curve is strictly decreasing, we have $L(0, T_1) < L(0, T_0)$, leading directly to the chain:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) < L(t, T_1) < L(t, T_0) $$

Correct Answer: $F(t, T_0, T_1) < L(t, T_1) < L(t, T_0)$.

Question 7 (Futures Price Change) Multiple Choice

Suppose at time $t = 0$ we hold a futures contract with notional 100, reference period $[T_0, T_1]$, and corresponding futures rate $R_{\text{futures}}(0, T_0, T_1) = 5\%$. By how much does the futures price change if the futures rate drops to 3%?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

The quoted exchange price of an interest rate futures contract is defined as:

$$ P_{\text{futures}}(t, T_0, T_1) = 100 \times \bigl( 1 - R_{\text{futures}}(t, T_0, T_1) \bigr) $$

We calculate the initial futures price at $R_{\text{futures}}(0) = 5\% = 0.05$:

$$ P_{\text{futures}}^{\text{initial}} = 100 \times (1 - 0.05) = 100 \times 0.95 = 95 $$

Next, we calculate the new futures price when the futures rate drops to $R_{\text{futures}}^{\text{new}} = 3\% = 0.03$:

$$ P_{\text{futures}}^{\text{new}} = 100 \times (1 - 0.03) = 100 \times 0.97 = 97 $$

The daily mark-to-market change in the futures price received by the contract holder is:

$$ \Delta P_{\text{futures}} = P_{\text{futures}}^{\text{new}} - P_{\text{futures}}^{\text{initial}} = 97 - 95 = \mathbf{+2} $$

Since interest rates dropped, the price of the interest rate contract rose by exactly **2** points, representing a positive cash flow of 2 units to the holder.

Correct Answer: 2
