Monte Carlo Simulation Foundations

Under the risk-neutral measure, options are priced by taking the discounted expectation of the terminal payoff. Instead of solving partial differential equations, Monte Carlo Simulation simulates millions of potential random asset paths to evaluate this expectation directly. The stock price evolves under the risk-neutral drift $r$ (the risk-neutral spot rate model).

The Five-Step Monte Carlo Recipe

  1. Simulate a risk-neutral random asset path starting from today's spot $S_0$ over the horizon $T$.
  2. Calculate the option's payoff at expiry (e.g. $\max(S_T - E, 0)$ for a European Call).
  3. Repeat this process for a massive number of realizations $M$ (hundreds of thousands or millions).
  4. Calculate the average payoff across all simulated paths.
  5. Take the present value of this average to obtain the option price: $V_0 = e^{-rT} \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M \text{Payoff}_m$.

Stochastic Discretization Schemes

- Euler-Maruyama Scheme: An extension of Euler's ODE method to stochastic calculus, providing accuracy order $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\Delta t})$: $$S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \left( 1 + r \Delta t + \sigma \phi \sqrt{\Delta t} \right), \quad \phi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$ - Milstein Scheme: Includes a second-order correction term for high accuracy ($\mathcal{O}(\Delta t)$): $$S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \left( 1 + r \Delta t + \sigma \phi \sqrt{\Delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 (\phi^2 - 1)\Delta t \right)$$ - Exact Discretization (GBM): Derived via log-SDE integration (exact when $\mu, \sigma$ are constant): $$S_T = S_0 \exp\left( \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T + \sigma \phi \sqrt{T} \right)$$

Error Analysis & Industry Preferences

By the Central Limit Theorem, the pricing error decays according to: $$\text{Error} \propto \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{M}}$$ where $M$ is the number of simulations. Solvers determine the required number of paths by establishing a maximum acceptable error tolerance.

Advantages vs. Disadvantages

Advantages:
  • Highly flexible (changing payoff doesn't rewrite engine).
  • Natural extension to path-dependent exotic options.
  • Handles multi-factor correlated asset portfolios.
Disadvantages:
  • Computational cost of random number generation.
  • Slow convergence rate ($\mathcal{O}(M^{-0.5})$).
  • Greeks require multiple full simulation runs.
  • Difficult to incorporate early exercise (American options).

Finite Difference Grid Discretization

The Finite Difference Method (FDM) solves the continuous Black-Scholes PDE deterministically by mapping derivatives onto a 2D uniform grid of time step $\delta t$ and asset step $\delta S$. Since we march backward in time, we define time to expiry as $\tau = T - t$, introducing a sign change in the time derivative ($\partial V / \partial t \to -\partial V / \partial t$).

Black-Scholes discrete difference equation:

Let the PDE be written as $\frac{\partial V}{\partial \tau} = a(S)\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + b(S)\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + c V$. Substituting forward difference in time and central differences in space: $$V_i^{k+1} = \alpha_i V_{i-1}^k + \beta_i V_i^k + \gamma_i V_{i+1}^k$$ where the coefficients depend on the grid boundaries, volatility, and rates: $$\alpha_i = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 i^2 - i(r - d) \right) \delta t$$ $$\beta_i = 1 - \left( r + \sigma^2 i^2 \right) \delta t$$ $$\gamma_i = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma^2 i^2 + i(r - d) \right) \delta t$$

Boundary Conditions

- Lower Boundary ($S=0$ / $i=0$): Diffusion and drift terms vanish, reducing the PDE to exponential decay: $$V_0^{k+1} = (1 - r \delta t) V_0^k$$ - Upper Boundary ($S \to S^*$ / $i=I$): At high spot values, the option value is linear, indicating vanishing gamma ($\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \to 0$): $$V_I^{k} = 2V_{I-1}^k - V_{I-2}^k$$

Advantages & Disadvantages of Explicit FDM

Advantages: Straightforward to code with obvious instability indicators. Handles variable coefficients and dividends beautifully without requiring closed-form solutions. Ideal for American-style early exercise (since we simply take the maximum of the continuation value and intrinsic value at each grid node: $V_i^{k+1} = \max(V_{\text{continuation}}, V_{\text{intrinsic}})$).
Disadvantages: Suffer from strict conditional stability constraints (time step $\delta t$ cannot be chosen independently of spatial grid size $\delta S$). Impractical beyond 3 spatial dimensions (curse of dimensionality).

Von Neumann (Fourier) Stability Proof

Explicit finite-difference schemes can exhibit explosive numerical oscillations if the steps are chosen incorrectly. We analyze the propagation of error waves $E_n^m = \bar{a}^m e^{i n \omega}$ through the linear discrete difference equation:

Mathematical Proof Steps

Substitute the oscillatory disturbance $E_n^m = \bar{a}^m e^{i n \omega}$ into the marching error scheme: $$\bar{a}^{m+1} e^{i n \omega} = \alpha_n \bar{a}^m e^{i(n-1)\omega} + \beta_n \bar{a}^m e^{i n \omega} + \gamma_n \bar{a}^m e^{i(n+1)\omega}$$ Dividing both sides by $\bar{a}^m e^{i n \omega}$ and isolating the second-order diffusion term (letting $r = D = 0$): $$\bar{a} = \frac{1}{2} n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t e^{-i\omega} + (1 - n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t) + \frac{1}{2} n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t e^{i\omega}$$ Using Euler's identity $e^{i\omega} + e^{-i\omega} = 2 \cos \omega$: $$\bar{a} = n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t \cos \omega + 1 - n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t = 1 - n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t (1 - \cos \omega)$$ Using the trigonometric half-angle identity $1 - \cos \omega = 2 \sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2}$: $$\bar{a} = 1 - 2 n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t \sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2}$$ For error waves to decay (stability), the amplitude factor must satisfy: $$|\bar{a}| \le 1 \implies -1 \le 1 - 2 n^2 \sigma^2 \delta t \sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2} \le 1 \implies \delta t \le \frac{1}{n^2 \sigma^2 \sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2}}$$

Taking the worst-case boundary where the spot price is at its maximum grid limit ($n = N$) and the oscillation wave frequency peaks ($\sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2} = 1$): $$\delta t \le \frac{1}{\sigma^2 N^2}$$ Since $N = S^*/\delta S$, this places a direct quadratic constraint: doubling the spatial resolution requires four times as many time steps ($\delta t \propto N^{-2}$).

Problem 1: 1D Heat Equation Discretization & Stability Proof
Question:
Given the 1D heat equation $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$. Derive the forward marching scheme: $$u_n^{m+1} = A u_{n-1}^m + B u_n^m + C u_{n+1}^m$$ and prove the stability condition $r \le 1/2$ where $r = c^2 \frac{\delta t}{\delta x^2}$.
Detailed Solution:
Substituting derivative approximations: $$\frac{u_n^{m+1} - u_n^m}{\delta t} = c^2 \frac{u_{n-1}^m - 2u_n^m + u_{n+1}^m}{\delta x^2} \implies u_n^{m+1} = r u_{n-1}^m + (1-2r)u_n^m + r u_{n+1}^m$$ Comparing coefficients gives: $$A = r, \quad B = 1-2r, \quad C = r$$ Substituting the error wave $E_n^m = \bar{a}^m e^{i n \omega}$: $$\bar{a} = r e^{-i\omega} + (1-2r) + r e^{i\omega} = 1 - 2r(1 - \cos \omega) = 1 - 4r \sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2}$$ For stability $|\bar{a}| \le 1$. Under maximum frequency $\sin^2 \frac{\omega}{2} = 1$: $$-1 \le 1 - 4r \le 1 \implies 4r \le 2 \implies r \le \frac{1}{2} \implies \delta t \le \frac{\delta x^2}{2c^2}$$
Problem 2: Explicit FDM for the Black-Scholes PDE
Question:
Derive the backward marching difference equation $V_n^{m-1} = a_n V_{n-1}^m + b_n V_n^m + c_n V_{n+1}^m$ for the Black-Scholes PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + (r-D)S\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - r V = 0$$
Detailed Solution:
Substituting FDM finite difference derivatives: $$\frac{V_n^m - V_n^{m-1}}{\delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 (n\delta S)^2 \left( \frac{V_{n-1}^m - 2V_n^m + V_{n+1}^m}{\delta S^2} \right) + (r-D)(n\delta S)\left( \frac{V_{n+1}^m - V_{n-1}^m}{2\delta S} \right) - r V_n^m = 0$$ Canceling step size $\delta S$, multiplying by $\delta t$, and isolating $V_n^{m-1}$: $$V_n^{m-1} = V_{n-1}^m \left[ \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 n^2 \delta t - \frac{1}{2}(r-D)n\delta t \right] + V_n^m \left[ 1 - (\sigma^2 n^2 + r)\delta t \right] + V_{n+1}^m \left[ \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 n^2 \delta t + \frac{1}{2}(r-D)n\delta t \right]$$ This defines the coefficients: $$a_n = \frac{1}{2}\left( \sigma^2 n^2 - n(r-D) \right)\delta t$$ $$b_n = 1 - \left( \sigma^2 n^2 + r \right)\delta t$$ $$c_n = \frac{1}{2}\left( \sigma^2 n^2 + n(r-D) \right)\delta t$$
Problem 3: Monte Carlo Pricing of a Binary Call Option
Question:
Outline a Monte Carlo simulation method to price a European Binary Call option.
Detailed Solution:

1. Path Simulation: Under the risk-neutral measure, the spot price at maturity $T$ is simulated using the exact GBM solution in one giant step: $$S_m(T) = S_0 \exp\left( \left( r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)T + \sigma \sqrt{T} \phi_m \right), \quad \phi_m \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$ where $\phi_m$ is drawn using normal distributions.

2. Payoff Evaluation: The payoff of a binary call option is defined by a Heaviside indicator function: $$\text{Payoff}(S_m(T)) = \mathcal{H}(S_m(T) - E) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_m(T) > E \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

3. Present Value Expectation: Discount and average over $M$ paths: $$\text{Binary Option Value} = e^{-rT} \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \mathcal{H}(S_m(T) - E)$$
