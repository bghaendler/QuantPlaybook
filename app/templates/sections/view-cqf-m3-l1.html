L1: The Black-Scholes Option Pricing Framework

Assumptions, Rigorous PDE Derivations, Heat Equation Transformation, Greeks Formulas, and Options Sandbox

Black-Scholes Foundations

Published in their seminal 1973 paper, "The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities", Fisher Black and Myron Scholes (with essential insights from Robert C. Merton) revolutionized derivative valuation. By demonstrating that options can be dynamically replicated using the underlying stock and risk-free cash, they derived a unique, preference-free pricing framework.

Idealized Market Assumptions

  • 1. Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM): Stock prices $S$ follow a continuous lognormal random walk with constant (or deterministic) volatility $\sigma$ and constant drift $\mu$.
  • 2. Constant Risk-Free Rate: The short-term risk-free interest rate $r$ is constant, and borrowing/lending rates are equal.
  • 3. Zero Dividend Yield: The underlying asset pays no dividends (this assumption can be relaxed to a continuous constant dividend yield $D$).
  • 4. Continuous Rebalancing: Rebalancing of the delta-hedged replicating portfolio is performed continuously.
  • 5. Frictionless Markets: No transaction costs, brokerage fees, taxes, or constraints on short-selling. Fractional trading is permitted.
  • 6. Absence of Arbitrage: The market is completely free of arbitrage opportunities.
  • 7. European-Style Exercise: The option can only be exercised at the pre-specified maturity date $T$.
💡 Academic Insights: Under these assumptions, option prices are completely independent of investors' risk preferences (the drift parameter $\mu$ disappears entirely). The Nobel Prize in Economics was awarded to Myron Scholes and Robert Merton in 1997 (Fischer Black passed away in 1995).

Derivation of the pricing equation

1. Replicating Re-hedged Portfolio
We assume the asset price $S$ follows a Geometric Brownian Motion: $$dS = \mu S dt + \sigma S dW$$ Construct a self-financing replicating portfolio $\Pi$ consisting of one long position in the option $V(S, t)$ and a short position in a quantity $\Delta$ of the underlying stock $S$: $$\Pi = V(S, t) - \Delta S$$
2. Stochastic Portfolio Dynamics
Over an infinitesimal time step $dt$, holding the quantity $\Delta$ fixed, the change in portfolio value is: $$d\Pi = dV - \Delta dS$$ Applying Itô's lemma to expand $dV$: $$dV = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right) dt + \sigma S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dW$$ Substituting both SDEs into $d\Pi$: $$d\Pi = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - \Delta \mu S \right) dt + \sigma S \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - \Delta \right) dW$$
3. Dynamic Delta Hedging
To remove all systemic risk from the portfolio, we eliminate the stochastic $dW$ term by setting: $$\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$$ Substituting this definition of delta simplifies the portfolio dynamics to a completely risk-free, deterministic SDE: $$d\Pi = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right) dt$$
4. Solvency and No-Arbitrage
By no-arbitrage, a completely risk-free portfolio must grow at the risk-free cash deposit rate $r$: $$d\Pi = r \Pi dt = r \left( V - S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right) dt$$ Equating the two expressions for $d\Pi$: $$\left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right) dt = r \left( V - S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right) dt$$ Dividing by $dt$ and rearranging yields the legendary Black-Scholes Partial Differential Equation: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + r S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - r V = 0$$
💡 Crucial Observation: The asset drift parameter $\mu$ has completely vanished from the equation! Option pricing depends entirely on volatility $\sigma$, not on expected stock returns.

Transforming the Black-Scholes PDE to the Heat Equation

The Black-Scholes PDE is a variable-coefficient, backward parabolic PDE. We can transform it step-by-step into a standard constant-coefficient 1D Heat Equation:

Step 1: Future Value Transformation
To remove the $-rV$ term, let $V(S, t) = e^{-r(T-t)} U(S, t)$. Differentiating and substituting into the PDE yields: $$\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial S^2} + r S \frac{\partial U}{\partial S} = 0$$
Step 2: Time to Expiry Variable
Since option pricing is a backward-in-time problem from expiry $T$, define the time-to-expiry variable $\tau = T-t$. Using the chain rule: $$\frac{\partial U}{\partial \tau} = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial S^2} + r S \frac{\partial U}{\partial S}$$
Step 3: Logarithmic Coordinate Space
To eliminate the variable coefficients $S$ and $S^2$, let $\xi = \log S$. Substituting the partial spatial derivatives: $$\frac{\partial U}{\partial \tau} = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial \xi^2} + \left( r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) \frac{\partial U}{\partial \xi}$$
Step 4: Translated Moving Coordinates
To eliminate the remaining first-order convective term, translate the spatial coordinate: $$x = \xi + \left( r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)\tau$$ Writing $W(x, \tau) = U(S, t)$ transforms the PDE into the classic 1D Diffusion/Heat Equation: $$\frac{\partial W}{\partial \tau} = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 W}{\partial x^2}$$

Fundamental Solution & Transition Density

The heat equation has a known fundamental solution representing the transition density (Green's function): $$W_f(x, \tau; x') = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2 \pi \tau}} \exp\left( -\frac{(x - x')^2}{2 \sigma^2 \tau} \right)$$ In the limit as $\tau \to 0$ (at expiry), this distribution converges to the Dirac delta function: $$\lim_{\tau \to 0} W_f(x, \tau; x') = \delta(x' - x)$$ This elegant probabilistic mapping confirms that the option price is the discounted expected payoff under a risk-neutral random walk where the drift is replaced by the risk-free rate $r$: $$V(S, t) = e^{-r(T-t)} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \text{Payoff}(S_T) \mid S_t = S \right]$$

Black-Scholes closed-form solutions

For an underlying asset paying a constant continuous dividend yield $D$, the closed-form solutions are:

1. European Call Option Value $$C(S, t) = S e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1) - E e^{-r(T-t)} N(d_2)$$
2. European Put Option Value $$P(S, t) = -S e^{-D(T-t)} N(-d_1) + E e^{-r(T-t)} N(-d_2)$$
Option Variable Terms ($d_1$ and $d_2$) $$d_1 = \frac{\log(S/E) + \left( r - D + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)(T-t)}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}}$$ $$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T-t} = \frac{\log(S/E) + \left( r - D - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)(T-t)}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}}$$

Digital & Approximations

3. Binary (Digital) Options
Digital contracts pay exactly \$1 if the option expires in-the-money: $$\text{Binary Call} = e^{-r(T-t)} N(d_2)$$ $$\text{Binary Put} = e^{-r(T-t)} (1 - N(d_2))$$
4. At-The-Money Forward (ATMF) Linear Approximation
When strike equals the forward price $E = S e^{(r-D)(T-t)}$, there is a simple linear approximation: $$\text{Call} \approx \text{Put} \approx 0.4 \times S e^{-D(T-t)} \sigma \sqrt{T-t}$$
💡 Binary Interpretation: Under risk-neutral pricing, the factor $N(d_2)$ represents the probability that the stock price expires above the strike price $E$. Hence, a digital option price is simply the discounted risk-neutral probability of expiring in-the-money.

Black-Scholes Exercises & Solutions

Test your understanding of the Black-Scholes framework with these mathematical and stochastic exercises:

Exercise 1: Call Option Delta and Vega Derivation
Problem:
Differentiate the European call option pricing formula: $$C(S, t) = S e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1) - E e^{-r(T-t)} N(d_2)$$ to show that: $$\Delta = e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1) \quad \text{and} \quad \nu = S e^{-D(T-t)} \sqrt{\frac{T-t}{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} d_1^2}$$ using the identity: $$S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1) = E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2)$$
Detailed Solution:

Part A: Delta Derivation
Differentiating $C$ with respect to $S$: $$\Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} = e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1) + S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1)\frac{\partial d_1}{\partial S} - E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2)\frac{\partial d_2}{\partial S}$$ Since $\frac{\partial d_1}{\partial S} = \frac{\partial d_2}{\partial S} = \frac{1}{S \sigma \sqrt{T-t}}$, we can group terms: $$\Delta = e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1) + \frac{\partial d_1}{\partial S}\left( S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1) - E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2) \right)$$ Using the given identity, the bracketed term is exactly $0$. Thus: $$\Delta = e^{-D(T-t)} N(d_1)$$

Part B: Vega Derivation
Differentiating $C$ with respect to $\sigma$: $$\nu = \frac{\partial C}{\partial \sigma} = S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1)\frac{\partial d_1}{\partial \sigma} - E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2)\frac{\partial d_2}{\partial \sigma}$$ Substitute $\frac{\partial d_2}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial d_1}{\partial \sigma} - \sqrt{T-t}$ into the equation: $$\nu = S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1)\frac{\partial d_1}{\partial \sigma} - E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2)\left( \frac{\partial d_1}{\partial \sigma} - \sqrt{T-t} \right)$$ $$\nu = \frac{\partial d_1}{\partial \sigma}\left( S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1) - E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2) \right) + E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2) \sqrt{T-t}$$ The first term is zero. Applying the identity $E e^{-r(T-t)} N'(d_2) = S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1)$ yields: $$\nu = S e^{-D(T-t)} N'(d_1) \sqrt{T-t} = \sqrt{\frac{T-t}{2\pi}} S e^{-D(T-t)} e^{-\frac{1}{2} d_1^2}$$

Exercise 2: Option Value SDE and Substitution Solution
Problem:
Given that the asset price $S$ evolves according to: $dS = a(S, t) dt + b(S, t) dW$:
1. Apply Itô's Lemma to show $dV = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + a \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2} b^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right) dt + b \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dW$.
2. Show by substitution that $V(S, t) = e^{-\alpha t} S^2$ is a solution to the customized PDE provided $b^2 = (\alpha - r) S^2$.
Detailed Solution:

Part 1: SDE Expansion
Using Itô's Lemma: $$dV = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dS + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} (dS)^2$$ Since $(dS)^2 = b^2 dt$, substitute $dS$: $$dV = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + a\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2} b^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} \right) dt + b\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dW$$

Part 2: Verification by Substitution
Let $V(S, t) = e^{-\alpha t} S^2$. Calculate the partial derivatives: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = -\alpha e^{-\alpha t} S^2 \quad , \quad \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} = 2 S e^{-\alpha t} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = 2 e^{-\alpha t}$$ Substitute these into the customized Black-Scholes PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}b^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = r \left( V - S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right)$$ $$\text{LHS} = -\alpha e^{-\alpha t} S^2 + \frac{1}{2}b^2(2 e^{-\alpha t}) = e^{-\alpha t} (b^2 - \alpha S^2)$$ $$\text{RHS} = r \left( e^{-\alpha t} S^2 - S (2 S e^{-\alpha t}) \right) = -r e^{-\alpha t} S^2$$ Equating LHS and RHS gives: $$b^2 - \alpha S^2 = -r S^2 \implies b^2 = (\alpha - r)S^2$$

Exercise 3: Analytical Limits of the Black-Scholes Formula
Problem:
Find the value of the European call option in the following mathematical limits:
Limit (a): Time $t \to T^-$ (with $\sigma > 0$)
Limit (b): Volatility $\sigma \to 0^+$ (with $t < T$)
Detailed Solution:

Part A: Limit as $t \to T^-$
Let $\tau = T - t \to 0$. The limits of $d_1$ and $d_2$ are: $$d_{1,2} \approx \frac{\log(S/E)}{\sigma \sqrt{\tau}} \to \begin{cases} +\infty & \text{if } S > E \\ -\infty & \text{if } S < E \\ 0 & \text{if } S = E \end{cases}$$ This maps the cumulative distribution function limits: * If $S > E$: $N(d_{1,2}) \to 1 \implies C(S, t) \to S - E$. * If $S < E$: $N(d_{1,2}) \to 0 \implies C(S, t) \to 0$. * If $S = E$: $N(d_{1,2}) \to 1/2 \implies C(S, t) \to 0$. Thus, at expiry, the option converges to its standard intrinsic payoff: $\max(S-E, 0)$.

Part B: Limit as volatility $\sigma \to 0^+$
$$\lim_{\sigma \to 0^+} d_{1,2} = \frac{\log\left( \frac{S e^{-D(T-t)}}{E e^{-r(T-t)}} \right)}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}} \to \begin{cases} +\infty & \text{if } S e^{-D(T-t)} > E e^{-r(T-t)} \\ -\infty & \text{if } S e^{-D(T-t)} < E e^{-r(T-t)} \end{cases}$$ * If the discounted spot exceeds the discounted strike, the call option converges to: $S e^{-D(T-t)} - E e^{-r(T-t)}$. * Otherwise, it converges to $0$. Thus, as volatility goes to zero, the call option converges to its discounted intrinsic value: $\max\left( S e^{-D(T-t)} - E e^{-r(T-t)}, 0 \right)$.

Exercise 4: Backward Kolmogorov SDE for Option Value
Problem:
Write the option value in the form $V(S, t) = \exp(-r(T-t)) \, q(S, t)$ and show that $q(S, t)$ satisfies the backward Kolmogorov equation: $$\frac{\partial q}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 q}{\partial S^2} + (r - D) S \frac{\partial q}{\partial S} = 0$$
Detailed Solution:
Differentiate $V(S, t) = e^{-r(T-t)} q(S, t)$ with respect to all variables: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = r e^{-r(T-t)} q + e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{\partial q}{\partial t} = r V + e^{-r(T-t)}\frac{\partial q}{\partial t}$$ $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} = e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{\partial q}{\partial S} \quad , \quad \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{\partial^2 q}{\partial S^2}$$ Substitute these back into the standard Black-Scholes PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + (r - D) S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - r V = 0$$ $$r V + e^{-r(T-t)}\frac{\partial q}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{\partial^2 q}{\partial S^2} + (r - D) S e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{\partial q}{\partial S} - r V = 0$$ The $rV$ terms cancel out. Dividing the entire equation by the non-zero discount factor $e^{-r(T-t)}$ yields: $$\frac{\partial q}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 q}{\partial S^2} + (r - D) S \frac{\partial q}{\partial S} = 0$$ Which is the exact Backward Kolmogorov SDE representing the risk-neutral probability density progression.
