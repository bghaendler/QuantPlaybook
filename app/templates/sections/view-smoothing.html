2.3 Smoothing Methods

Prioritizing smooth forward curves using parametric families (Nelson-Siegel, Svensson) and Lorimier smoothing splines.

Parametric Forward Families

Instead of bootstrapping discrete dates, parametric models assume the forward curve $f(T)$ belongs to a smooth, low-dimensional family of functions. The Nelson-Siegel family uses four parameters to represent typical shapes:

$$ f(T) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 e^{-a T} + \beta_2 a T e^{-a T} $$

The Svensson family extends this by adding a second curvature term to accommodate yield curves with multiple humps (using six parameters):

$$ f(T) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 e^{-a_1 T} + \beta_2 a_1 T e^{-a_1 T} + \beta_3 a_2 T e^{-a_2 T} $$

Yield Basis Functions

Integrating the forward curve yields the spot zero-coupon yield curve: $y(T) = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T f(u) du$. Under Nelson-Siegel, this decomposes into three interpretable basis elements:

  • Level ($\beta_0$): Basis is $1.0$ (constant across all maturities).
  • Slope ($\beta_1$): Basis is $\frac{1 - e^{-a T}}{a T}$ (decays from 1 to 0).
  • Curvature ($\beta_2$): Basis is $\frac{1 - e^{-a T}}{a T} - e^{-a T}$ (starts at 0, humps, then decays).

Lorimier Smoothing Splines

Instead of matching rates exactly or assuming a global parametric formula, Lorimier's spline methodology searches for a continuous forward rate $f(T)$ in a Hilbert space $H$ that balances goodness of fit and curve smoothness by minimizing:

$$ \min_{f \in H} \int_0^{T_*} (f'(u))^2 du + \alpha \sum_{i=1}^N \left( T_i y_i - \int_0^{T_i} f(u) du \right)^2 $$

The smoothing parameter $\alpha > 0$ determines the exact trade-off: larger $\alpha$ values force the spline to pass exactly through the market yields, while smaller $\alpha$ values prioritize a smooth curve.

Hilbert Quadratic Basis Splines

Lorimier proved that the unique solution $f(u)$ is a quadratic spline composed of basis elements $h_i(u)$ whose derivatives are flat-capped lines:

$$ h_i'(u) = (T_i - u)^+, \quad h_i(0) = T_i $$

We evaluate the scalar products exactly using the closed-form equation:

⟨h_i, h_j⟩ = T_i T_j (1 + m) - 0.5 (T_i + T_j) m² + 0.333 m³

(where $m = \min(T_i, T_j)$)
