2.4 Principal Component Analysis

Decomposing yield curve fluctuations into three primary risk factors: Level, Slope, and Curvature.

Dimensionality Reduction of Yield Curves

A yield curve has yields across many maturities, representing a high-dimensional vector. However, yield movements are highly correlated. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) decomposes the empirical covariance matrix $Q$ of yield changes using the spectral theorem:

$$ Q = A L A^\top $$

where $L$ is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ (representing the variance explained by each factor) and $A$ contains the eigenvectors (called loadings or factors). Empirically, the first three components explain over $98.5\%$ of all yield curve movements, allowing us to capture almost all risk using just 3 variables.

The Physical Factors

The three primary eigenvectors exhibit stable, intuitive physical shapes across historical markets:

  • PC1 (Level / Parallel Shift): Flat across all maturities. Adjusting this component shifts all yields up or down by a similar amount (explains $\approx 79.68\%$ of variance).
  • PC2 (Slope / Twist): Monotonically increasing or decreasing. Adjusting this component rotates the curve, steepening or flattening it ($\approx 14.87\%$).
  • PC3 (Curvature / Hump): Convex or bowed shape. Adjusting this component changes the medium-term hump ($\approx 4.13\%$).
