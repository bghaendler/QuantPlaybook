Complex Analysis for Quants

Cauchy-Riemann Differentiability Tests

The pair of first-order PDEs that decide holomorphy: derivation, use as a test, and what they say geometrically.

1. Derivation: Two Directions Must Agree

Write $f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$. Compute $f'(z_0)$ approaching horizontally ($h = \Delta x$) and vertically ($h = i\Delta y$):

$$f' = u_x + iv_x \qquad\text{and}\qquad f' = \frac{u_y + iv_y}{i} = v_y - iu_y.$$

Equating real and imaginary parts gives the Cauchy-Riemann equations:

$$\boxed{\;u_x = v_y, \qquad u_y = -v_x\;}$$

Test: if $u, v$ have continuous partials and satisfy CR on an open set, $f$ is holomorphic there (sufficiency needs the continuity); if CR fails anywhere, $f$ is not complex-differentiable there. When they hold: $f' = u_x + iv_x$.

2. Worked Checks

  • $f = z^2$: $u = x^2 - y^2$, $v = 2xy$ ⇒ $u_x = 2x = v_y$, $u_y = -2y = -v_x$ ✓ everywhere; $f' = 2x + i2y = 2z$.
  • $f = e^z$: $u = e^x\cos y$, $v = e^x\sin y$ ⇒ CR holds everywhere; $f' = e^z$ — the exponential really is its own derivative in ℂ.
  • $f = \bar{z}$: $u = x$, $v = -y$ ⇒ $u_x = 1 \ne -1 = v_y$ ✗ — nowhere holomorphic despite being perfectly smooth as a map of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Conjugation is the canonical non-example.
  • $f = |z|^2$: $u = x^2 + y^2$, $v = 0$ ⇒ CR only at the origin — differentiable at one point, holomorphic nowhere (holomorphy needs an open set).

3. The Wirtinger Shortcut: No $\bar{z}$ Allowed

Define $\frac{\partial}{\partial\bar{z}} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + i\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\right)$. The CR equations compress to one line:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \bar{z}} = 0$$

— holomorphic functions are exactly those with no $\bar{z}$-dependence. Practical scanning rule: expressions built from $z$ alone ($z^2$, $e^z$, $1/(z-a)$) pass; anything smuggling in $\bar z$, $|z|$, $\mathrm{Re}\,z$, $\arg z$ fails. This is also the form used in generalized (quasi-conformal) settings and in some SABR/heat-kernel literature.

4. Geometric and Analytic Consequences

  • Orthogonal level curves: $\nabla u \cdot \nabla v = u_xv_x + u_yv_y = -u_xu_y + u_yu_x = 0$ — the families $u = $ const and $v = $ const always cross at right angles (the conformal grid of Part 04).
  • Harmonicity: differentiating CR crosswise: $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0 = v_{xx} + v_{yy}$ — both components solve Laplace's equation (Part 07's subject).
  • Jacobian: $\det J = u_xv_y - u_yv_x = u_x^2 + u_y^2 = |f'(z)|^2 \ge 0$ — holomorphic maps preserve orientation and scale areas by $|f'|^2$.
  • Rigidity: if $f' \equiv 0$ on a connected open set, or if $|f|$ is constant, then $f$ is constant — first hints of the maximum-modulus principle.

Key Takeaways

  • CR: $u_x = v_y$, $u_y = -v_x$ — necessary always; sufficient with continuous partials; then $f' = u_x + iv_x$.
  • $\bar z$, $|z|$, $\mathrm{Re}\,z$ in a formula ⇒ not holomorphic ($\partial f/\partial\bar z \ne 0$).
  • CR ⇒ orthogonal level sets, harmonic components, Jacobian $= |f'|^2$.
  • Holomorphy is a property of open sets, not points — $|z|^2$ is the standard trap.
