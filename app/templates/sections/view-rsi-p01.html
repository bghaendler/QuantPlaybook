Risk-Sensitive Investing

Stochastic Control Models in Asset Allocation

Dynamic portfolio choice as a control problem: Merton's framework, the HJB route to optimal weights, and why dynamic solutions differ from repeated one-period ones.

1. The Merton Problem

Wealth $W_t$ split between a risky asset ($\mu$, σ) and cash ($r$); control = risky fraction $\pi_t$ (and consumption, in the full version):

$$dW = W\big[r + \pi(\mu - r)\big]dt + W\pi\sigma\,dW_t^B.$$

Maximize $\mathbb{E}[U(W_T)]$ for CRRA utility $U(w) = w^{1-\gamma}/(1-\gamma)$. The HJB equation (the same machinery as HFT Part 10) admits the famous closed form:

$$\pi^* = \frac{\mu - r}{\gamma\sigma^2}$$

— constant in time and wealth. With μ−r = 5%, σ = 18%, γ = 3: $\pi^* = 0.05/(3 \times 0.0324) \approx 51\%$. CRRA's scale-invariance does the simplifying; γ = 1 (log utility) gives the growth-optimal Kelly portfolio (Part 03's subject).

2. Why Dynamic ≠ Repeated Static

  • With constant opportunities, Merton's answer coincides with myopic mean-variance-like weights — the famous "as if static" result.
  • With stochastic opportunities (predictable returns, stochastic vol or rates), the optimal control adds intertemporal hedging demands (Merton's term): hold assets that pay off when investment opportunities deteriorate. Formally the HJB's cross-derivative terms $V_{Wx}$ tilt $\pi^*$ away from myopic weights.
  • Example: mean-reverting equity premium ⇒ long-horizon investors hold more equity than myopic ones (bad returns today predict better opportunities tomorrow — equities partially self-hedge across time).
  • This is the structural argument behind horizon-dependent advice — and it lives or dies on the predictability model (estimation risk: Part 04's Black-Litterman discipline).

3. The Control Toolkit for Allocation

  • State augmentation: predictors, vol, rates, liabilities (Part 05) enter as extra states $x$; HJB in $(W, x, t)$; affine/quadratic structures (linear dynamics + CRRA/exponential utility) reduce to ODE systems — the tractable class this series exploits repeatedly.
  • Constraints: leverage caps, no-shorting, drawdown floors → variational inequalities / reflected controls (the boundary machinery of HFT Part 09).
  • Transaction costs: proportional costs create a no-trade region around $\pi^*$ (Davis-Norman): trade only at the band edges — the practically decisive modification (rebalance bands, not continuous trading).
  • Numerics: low-dimensional HJB by finite differences/policy iteration; high-dimensional by regression Monte Carlo or deep-control methods (the ML masterclass's reinforcement-learning bridge).

Key Takeaways

  • Merton: $\pi^* = (\mu - r)/(\gamma\sigma^2)$ — the HJB benchmark every refinement perturbs.
  • Stochastic opportunity sets add intertemporal hedging demands — the genuinely dynamic content of dynamic allocation.
  • Transaction costs convert continuous control into no-trade bands — the most implementation-relevant result.
  • Affine/CRRA structures keep multi-state problems ODE-tractable; constraints enter as boundaries.
