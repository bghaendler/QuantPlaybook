The Random Behaviour of Assets

Returns Modeling, Time-Scaling Properties, and Normality Testing

1. Simple vs. Logarithmic Returns

Returns represent relative price growth, making them comparable across assets. In quantitative finance, we distinguish between two primary return metrics:

  • Simple (Arithmetic) Return ($R^s_t$): Relative change in price over a step. $$R^s_t = \frac{S_{t+1} - S_t}{S_t} = \frac{S_{t+1}}{S_t} - 1$$ Multi-period projection: Multiplicative over time. $$1 + R_{t+\tau} = (1 + R_t)(1 + R_{t+1})\dots(1 + R_{t+n-1})$$
  • Logarithmic (Compound) Return ($R^c_t$): Natural log of price ratios. $$R^c_t = \ln\left(\frac{S_{t+1}}{S_t}\right)$$ Multi-period projection: Additive over time, making it exceptionally convenient for continuous-time modeling. $$R_{t+\tau} = R_t + R_{t+1} + \dots + R_{t+n-1}$$
Conversion Rule: $R^s_t = \exp(R^c_t) - 1 \quad \iff \quad R^c_t = \ln(1 + R^s_t)$

2. Scaling with Time Step ($\delta t$)

To transition returns from discrete intervals (e.g. daily/weekly) to continuous time, we must scale the parameters with the time step $\delta t$:

  • Scaling of the Mean (Drift): The average expected asset return scales linearly with the size of the time step: $$\text{Mean} = \mu \delta t$$ where $\mu$ is the annualized drift (growth rate).
  • Scaling of standard deviation (Volatility): Under independence assumptions, variances (not standard deviations) are additive. Thus, the variance scales linearly with $\delta t$: $$\text{Variance} = \sigma^2 \delta t$$ Consequently, the volatility scales with the square root of the step: $$\text{Volatility} = \sigma \sqrt{\delta t}$$ where $\sigma$ is the annualized volatility.
Key Scaling Intuition: Variance $\propto \delta t \implies$ Volatility $\propto \sqrt{\delta t}$

3. Discrete and Continuous Asset Models

Combining time-scaling properties with random draws, we build standard models for asset price path simulations:

Discrete-Time Random Walk

If $\phi \sim N(0, 1)$ is a standard normal variable, the asset return over a step $\delta t$ is:

$$R_i = \frac{S_{i+1} - S_i}{S_i} = \mu \delta t + \sigma \phi \sqrt{\delta t}$$

Rearranged for Monte Carlo simulations: $$S_{i+1} - S_i = \mu S_i \delta t + \sigma S_i \phi \sqrt{\delta t}$$

Continuous-Time SDE (Geometric Brownian Motion)

Taking the limit as $\delta t \to 0$, the discrete shock term $\phi \sqrt{\delta t}$ transitions to the Wiener increment $dW_t$ where $\text{E}[dW_t] = 0$ and $\text{E}[dW_t^2] = dt$:

$$dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu dt + \sigma dW_t$$

No Randomness Case ($\sigma = 0$): reduces to ODE $\frac{dS}{dt} = \mu S \implies S(t) = S_0 e^{\mu t}$.

Problem A: Log Return Additivity & Relationship

Prove that logarithmic returns are additive over multiple time periods, whereas arithmetic returns are multiplicative, and derive their exact mathematical relationship.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the Arithmetic return of a multi-period series: Let an asset price evolve as $S_0, S_1, \dots, S_n$. The simple return over the entire period $R^s_{0,n}$ is: $$1 + R^s_{0,n} = \frac{S_n}{S_0} = \frac{S_1}{S_0} \times \frac{S_2}{S_1} \times \dots \times \frac{S_n}{S_{n-1}} = \prod_{i=1}^n (1 + R^s_{i-1, i})$$ This shows arithmetic returns are multiplicative, requiring compounding multiplication.
  2. State the Logarithmic return: The log return over a single step $R^c_{i-1, i}$ is: $$R^c_{i-1, i} = \ln\left(\frac{S_i}{S_{i-1}}\right)$$
  3. Derive Multi-Period Log Return Additivity: Summing single-period log returns from $i=1$ to $n$: $$\sum_{i=1}^n R^c_{i-1, i} = \sum_{i=1}^n \ln\left(\frac{S_i}{S_{i-1}}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{S_1}{S_0} \times \frac{S_2}{S_1} \times \dots \times \frac{S_n}{S_{n-1}}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{S_n}{S_0}\right) = R^c_{0,n}$$ Because of the logarithmic product-to-sum identity, multi-period log return is simply the sum of individual log returns.
  4. Derive the Relationship: From definitions, $\frac{S_t}{S_{t-1}} = 1 + R^s_t$. Taking the natural log of both sides: $$R^c_t = \ln\left(\frac{S_t}{S_{t-1}}\right) = \ln(1 + R^s_t)$$ Exponentiating both sides yields: $$R^s_t = e^{R^c_t} - 1$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why are arithmetic returns multiplicative? If you make 10% today and lose 10% tomorrow, your total return is not 0%. Your money grew to 110%, then fell to 99%. Multiplication preserves the exact compounding path.

2. Why use log returns? Working with products (multiplication) in statistics and probability is extremely difficult. Logs transform multiplication into simple addition, which is vastly easier to analyze mathematically.

3. Why do the sums cancel out? When you sum log fractions, the numerator of one term cancels with the denominator of the next (a telescoping series). Only the starting and final prices remain, representing a direct, additive path.

4. Why convert between them? Simple returns are intuitive for humans and reporting, but logarithmic returns are required for continuous-time mathematical models (like Black-Scholes). Converting lets us toggle between human intuition and rigorous math.

Problem B: The Square Root of Time Volatility Scaling

Derive why the standard deviation of asset returns scales with the square root of time ($\sigma \sqrt{t}$) rather than linearly with time.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State Independence Assumption: Assume returns $R_1, R_2, \dots, R_N$ across $N$ disjoint time intervals of length $\delta t$ are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables, each with variance $\sigma^2_d = \text{Var}(R_i)$.
  2. Apply Variance Additivity: For independent random variables, the variance of their sum is the sum of their variances: $$\text{Var}\left( \sum_{i=1}^N R_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^N \text{Var}(R_i) = N \times \sigma^2_d$$
  3. Relate Steps to Time Horizon: Let the total time horizon be $T = N \delta t$, which implies $N = \frac{T}{\delta t}$. Substituting $N$: $$\text{Var}(\text{Total Return over } T) = \frac{T}{\delta t} \sigma^2_d = T \times \left( \frac{\sigma^2_d}{\delta t} \right)$$
  4. Derive Standard Deviation Scaling: Standard deviation (volatility) is the square root of variance: $$\sigma(T) = \sqrt{\text{Var}(\text{Total Return})} = \sqrt{T \times \left(\frac{\sigma^2_d}{\delta t}\right)} = \left(\frac{\sigma_d}{\sqrt{\delta t}}\right) \sqrt{T}$$ Defining the annualized volatility as $\sigma = \frac{\sigma_d}{\sqrt{\delta t}}$ gives the famous square-root-of-time scaling rule: $$\sigma(T) = \sigma \sqrt{T}$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why assume independence? Independence means that what the stock market does today has no bearing on what it does tomorrow. While not 100% true in reality, it is a highly accurate baseline assumption for asset pricing models.

2. Why sum variances instead of standard deviations? Standard deviations represent absolute uncertainty widths, which don't add up directly because random ups and downs cancel each other out over time. Variances represent total "energy" or "variability", which accumulates cleanly.

3. Why does time divide the variance? If you want to scale up from 1 day to 252 days (a typical trading year), you sum 252 days of daily variance to get the yearly variance.

4. Why the square root? Since yearly variance is 252 times daily variance, yearly volatility (which is the square root of variance) must be the square root of 252 times daily volatility: $\sigma_{annual} = \sigma_{daily} \sqrt{252}$. This explains why long-term uncertainty grows slower than a straight line.

Problem C: Lognormal Distribution of Asset Prices

Show why asset prices follow a lognormal distribution under Geometric Brownian Motion, and derive the probability density function (PDF).

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the SDE for Geometric Brownian Motion: $$dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t$$
  2. Apply Itô's Lemma to $f(S) = \ln S$: The derivatives are $\frac{df}{dS} = \frac{1}{S}$ and $\frac{d^2f}{dS^2} = -\frac{1}{S^2}$. Applying Itô's formula: $$d(\ln S_t) = \left( \mu S_t \frac{1}{S_t} - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S_t^2 \frac{1}{S_t^2} \right) dt + \sigma S_t \frac{1}{S_t} dW_t$$ $$d(\ln S_t) = \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right) dt + \sigma dW_t$$
  3. Integrate to find the price level: $$\ln S_t - \ln S_0 = \left( \mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \right)t + \sigma W_t \implies S_t = S_0 \exp\left( \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t \right)$$ Since $W_t \sim N(0, t)$, the log asset price $\ln S_t$ is normally distributed: $$\ln S_t \sim N\left( \ln S_0 + \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t \, , \, \sigma^2 t \right)$$
  4. Formulate the Lognormal PDF: Let $x$ represent the price $S_t$. Performing a probability change of variable yields the lognormal density function: $$f(x; \mu, \sigma, t) = \frac{1}{x \sigma \sqrt{2\pi t}} \exp\left( -\frac{\left(\ln(x/S_0) - (\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t\right)^2}{2\sigma^2 t} \right) \quad \text{for } x > 0$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why use Geometric Brownian Motion? In standard Brownian motion, the stock price could jump down by $10 even if the price is $1, meaning the price could go negative. In GBM, jumps scale with the stock price (percentage returns), guaranteeing the price never drops below zero.

2. Why take the log? Taking the log transforms the stock price (which multiplies over time) into log-price (which adds up over time). This removes the dependency on the price level.

3. What is that $-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ term? This is the "Itô drift correction." In a random world, volatility actually acts as a drag on geometric growth. If a stock goes up 50% and down 50% repeatedly, you lose money. The correction term accounts for this variance drag.

4. Why is the PDF divided by $x$? Because a stock price cannot go negative, the distribution is skewed to the right (has a long tail representing potential unlimited upside). The $1/x$ term accounts for this asymmetric skew in the coordinate mapping.

View Normality Verification Steps

Quant traders use two standard processes to verify if empirical return data fits the lognormal assumption:

  1. Standardization (Z-score Scaling): Raw returns $r_t$ are converted to Z-scores $Z_t$ by subtracting the sample mean and dividing by the sample standard deviation, ensuring a mean of 0 and variance of 1: $$Z_t = \frac{r_t - \mu}{\sigma}$$
  2. Theoretical Q-Q Plot Matching:
    • Log-returns are converted to scaled Z-scores.
    • Z-scores are sorted in ascending order: $Z_{(1)} \le Z_{(2)} \le \dots \le Z_{(N)}$.
    • For each rank index $i$, the corresponding empirical quantile probability is estimated as $P_i = \frac{i - 0.5}{N}$ (Hazen's plotting position).
    • The theoretical standard normal quantile is calculated using the inverse standard normal cumulative distribution (CDF): $\text{Theoretical Quantile}_i = \Phi^{-1}(P_i)$.
    • Sorted empirical Z-scores are plotted against these theoretical quantiles. Deviation from the $y=x$ reference line indicates heavy tails (S-shape curve) or asymmetrical skewness (bended curve).
