Beating Markowitz with Hierarchical Risk Parity

López de Prado's HRP: why mean-variance optimization is unstable, how machine-learning clustering builds robust portfolios without inverting the covariance matrix, and the out-of-sample evidence.

1. Why Markowitz Fails Out of Sample

  • Matrix inversion instability: $\Sigma^{-1}$ amplifies estimation error — the smallest eigenvalues (least reliably estimated) get the largest weights; near-singular $\Sigma$ (correlated assets, $n$ near $T$) makes weights explode (ARM Part 10).
  • Condition number: the more diversified the opportunity set, the more correlated and ill-conditioned $\Sigma$ becomes — Markowitz is worst exactly where you most want diversification ("Markowitz's curse").
  • Result: concentrated, unstable, frequently-rebalanced weights that underperform even naive 1/N out of sample (DeMiguel et al.'s uncomfortable finding).

2. The HRP Algorithm

Three steps, no matrix inversion:

  1. Tree clustering: convert the correlation matrix to a distance $d_{ij} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \rho_{ij})}$, then hierarchically cluster assets — grouping similar assets into a dendrogram (the ML step).
  2. Quasi-diagonalization: reorder $\Sigma$ so similar assets sit together — clusters become near-block-diagonal, exposing structure.
  3. Recursive bisection: split the tree top-down, allocating risk between each pair of clusters inversely to their variance (inverse-variance within, recursively) — weight flows down the hierarchy.

No $\Sigma^{-1}$ is ever computed — the instability source is removed by construction; the hierarchy replaces the unstable global optimization with stable local allocations.

3. Why It Works Better

  • Robustness: avoiding inversion means estimation error doesn't get amplified — weights are stable and don't lurch on small input changes.
  • Respects structure: the hierarchy mirrors how markets actually cluster (asset classes, sectors); risk is diversified across clusters then within — economically sensible and statistically gentle.
  • Out-of-sample evidence: López de Prado's Monte Carlo and empirical studies show HRP achieves lower out-of-sample variance than mean-variance and equal-weight, with far lower turnover — robustness, not in-sample optimality, is the win (the recurring theme of the sentiment/max-diversification talks).
  • Cost benefit: stable weights ⇒ low turnover ⇒ low transaction costs (HFT Part 07) — a real-money advantage MV's churn destroys.

4. Caveats & Practice

  • HRP is a risk allocator — it ignores expected returns; pair with conservative return views (Black-Litterman, RSI Part 04) if you have an edge, or use as-is for risk-parity mandates.
  • Clustering choices (linkage method, distance) matter — validate stability across choices; it's less parameter-free than advertised.
  • Benchmark honestly against 1/N and risk parity (the portfolio-workshop discipline) — HRP's edge is real but modest, and lies in stability/turnover, not headline return.

Key Takeaways

  • Mean-variance fails out of sample because $\Sigma^{-1}$ amplifies estimation error — worst when diversification is greatest.
  • HRP clusters assets, quasi-diagonalizes, and allocates risk by recursive bisection — never inverting the covariance matrix.
  • The payoff is robustness and low turnover: lower out-of-sample variance than MV and 1/N with far less churn.
  • It's a return-agnostic risk allocator; combine with return views if available, and benchmark against simple baselines.
