High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure — Part 05

Intraday Microstructure Seasonality Modeling

The U-shapes of the trading day: volume, volatility, and spread profiles, how to estimate them robustly, and how schedules and signals must deseasonalize.

1. The Stylized Intraday Shapes

  • Volume: U-shaped (often "J-shaped") — heavy at the open (overnight information), trough at midday, surge into the close (benchmark/index flows, MOC auctions). The close share has grown structurally with passive investing.
  • Volatility: elevated open (price discovery), midday lull, modest close pickup; scheduled macro releases (8:30/10:00/14:00 ET) put spikes inside the U.
  • Spreads: widest at the open (information asymmetry, thin books), tighten through the day, near-minimum at the close — spread and volatility seasonality move together; depth runs inverse.
  • Cross-market clocks: FX follows the Tokyo→London→NY session relay; futures inherit the cash-open of their underlying.

2. Estimating the Seasonal Profile

For a quantity $x_{d,b}$ (day $d$, intraday bin $b$), the standard multiplicative decomposition:

$$x_{d,b} = T_d \cdot s_b \cdot \epsilon_{d,b}, \qquad \hat{s}_b = \mathrm{median}_d\left(\frac{x_{d,b}}{\sum_b x_{d,b}}\right)\;\text{(normalized)},$$
  • Medians over means — Part 04's tails make mean profiles hostage to a few event days.
  • Smooth: fit the binned medians with a low-order spline/Fourier basis (the day is periodic — 2–4 harmonics capture the U); avoids 5-minute-bin noise.
  • Condition: separate profiles for expiry days, rebalance days, half days, earnings names; one pooled curve mixes regimes (Part 04's warning again).
  • Update: exponentially weight recent days — the close-heavy migration means static profiles drift stale within quarters.

3. Deseasonalizing Signals and Risk

  • Volatility per bin: intraday z-scores must divide by $s_b^{vol}$ — otherwise every signal "fires" at the open simply because variance does. Realized-vol forecasting at horizon < 1 day is mostly seasonality plus a small dynamic residual.
  • "Volume time": reclock the day by cumulative expected volume $\tau(t) = \sum_{b \le t}\hat{s}_b$ — in volume time, returns are closer to homoskedastic and arrival processes closer to Poisson; many HFT models are built in this clock and only mapped back to wall time at the end.
  • Spread-aware tactics: passive ratios should rise when the profile says spreads are wide (open) and urgency is low; crossing the spread at 9:31 costs multiples of 15:50.

4. Seasonality in Execution Schedules

  • VWAP: the benchmark is the volume profile — a VWAP algo trades $\hat{s}_b \times Q$ per bin; profile error is the dominant VWAP tracking error, hence the median/conditioning care above.
  • IS/Almgren-Chriss with seasonality: time-varying $\sigma_b$, $\eta_b$ tilt the optimal trajectory — more in liquid cheap bins, less at the expensive open (Part 07 formalizes).
  • Auction participation: the close auction is now 5–15% of equity volume — schedules end with a deliberate MOC slice, sized from the auction's own seasonal share.
  • Evaluation: compare algos in deseasonalized cost units; otherwise the time-of-day mix of parent orders masquerades as skill.

Key Takeaways

  • Volume U/J-shape, open-heavy volatility, open-wide spreads — with macro releases and the growing close auction superimposed.
  • Estimate profiles with normalized medians + smooth periodic bases, conditioned on day type, exponentially refreshed.
  • Deseasonalize (or switch to volume time) before signaling, forecasting, or comparing costs.
  • VWAP lives and dies on the profile; IS schedules tilt toward cheap-liquidity bins and respect the closing auction.
