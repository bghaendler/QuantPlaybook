Black '76 Model for Commodity Options

Pricing options on futures rather than spot: why commodities trade off the forward curve, and how Black-76 became the lingua franca of energy and rates options.

1. Why Futures, Not Spot?

For commodities the spot is often not a tradable hedge instrument: storage is costly or impossible (power), convenience yields are unobservable, and the physical market is fragmented. The futures contract is the liquid underlying, and its price $F$ already embeds storage, convenience yield and seasonality. Pricing options directly on $F$ sidesteps the unobservable carry entirely — the curve is the model input.

2. The Formula

Under the risk-neutral measure a futures price is a martingale (zero drift — entering a future costs nothing), $dF = \sigma F\, dW$. The Black-76 prices are BSM with carry $b = 0$:

$$c = e^{-rT}\left[F N(d_1) - K N(d_2)\right], \qquad p = e^{-rT}\left[K N(-d_2) - F N(-d_1)\right],$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(F/K) + \sigma^2 T/2}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}.$$

Everything is discounted because the payoff $\max(F_T - K, 0)$ arrives at expiry while the futures position itself carries no cost. For options on margined futures where the premium is also margined, drop the discounting ($r = 0$): the Asay (1982) variant used on several futures exchanges.

3. Commodity-Specific Wrinkles

  • Expiry mismatch: the option typically expires days before its underlying future. Use the option's expiry $T$ for variance, the future's level for $F$ — and beware Samuelson vol effects between the two dates.
  • Samuelson effect: futures vol rises as maturity approaches (information concentrates near delivery). The vol term structure is downward in time-to-maturity; calendar-spread options live off this.
  • Seasonality: natural gas and power have strongly seasonal curves and vols — each contract month is essentially its own underlying with its own smile.
  • Mean reversion: spot commodities mean-revert (Schwartz one-factor); on a futures of fixed maturity this shows up as lower effective vol for long-dated contracts rather than a changed formula.
  • Negative prices: April 2020 WTI and frequent power prices break lognormality — desks switch to Bachelier (normal vol) quoting when curves approach zero.

4. Greeks & Quoting Conventions

Delta is in futures contracts: $\Delta_c = e^{-rT} N(d_1)$ — note even an arbitrarily deep ITM call has delta below 1 by the discount factor. Vega and gamma mirror BSM on $F$. Implied vols are quoted per contract month against strike (the "vol smile by month"); ATM here means $K = F$ — at-the-money-forward — so Black-76 needs no carry inputs at all: $F$, $K$, $T$, $r$, $\sigma$ and nothing else. This operational simplicity is why the model also colonized interest-rate land: caps/floors (on forward rates) and swaptions (on forward swap rates) are quoted in Black-76 vols, with the rate playing the role of $F$.

5. Worked Example: WTI Call

$F = 80$ \$/bbl (Dec contract), $K = 85$, $T = 0.5$, $r = 4\%$, $\sigma = 35\%$.

$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(80/85) + 0.5 \times 0.1225 \times 0.5}{0.35\sqrt{0.5}} = \frac{-0.0606 + 0.0306}{0.2475} = -0.121, \qquad d_2 = -0.369.$$ $$c = e^{-0.02}\left[80 \times N(-0.121) - 85 \times N(-0.369)\right] = 0.980\left[80 \times 0.4518 - 85 \times 0.3561\right] \approx 5.76.$$

The call costs ≈ \$5.76/bbl (\$5,760 per 1,000-bbl contract); delta = $0.980 \times 0.4518 \approx 0.44$ futures.

Key Takeaways

  • Black-76 = BSM with $b = 0$: futures are risk-neutral martingales, so only $F, K, T, r, \sigma$ enter.
  • The futures curve absorbs storage/convenience/seasonality — no carry estimation needed.
  • Samuelson effect and per-month smiles structure commodity vol surfaces.
  • Asay variant ($r=0$) for fully margined premium; Bachelier when prices can go negative.
  • The same formula prices caps, floors and swaptions — Black-76 is the universal "options on forwards" quote.
