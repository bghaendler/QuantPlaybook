Derivatives: Fundamentals of Futures & Forwards

Exploring obligations, delivery models, index multipliers, and exchange vs. OTC clearing mechanics.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: a forward is a handshake — "I'll buy your wheat next September at €200/ton, whatever the price is then." A future is the same handshake, standardized and run through an exchange so neither side has to trust the other.

  • Obligation, not option: both sides must transact at expiry — there's no walking away (that right to walk away is what options charge a premium for, two sections ahead).
  • The clearinghouse is the genius part: it steps between buyer and seller, becoming everyone's counterparty, and demands a deposit (margin) that's topped up daily as prices move — so nobody ever owes more than a day's move.
  • Settlement comes in two flavors: physical (actual barrels, actual wheat — rare in practice) and cash (just the price difference changes hands — how index futures work). The sandbox below walks both.
  • Index multipliers turn points into money: an S&P 500 future at 5,000 with a 50-dollar multiplier controls 250,000 dollars of exposure per contract — leverage is built into the contract design.

Settlement & Delivery Modality

Over-the-Counter Forwards

Bilateral, customizable agreements settled directly between two parties:

  • Tailored Sizing: Contracts are fully bespoke (e.g. customized delivery dates, unique grades).
  • Counterparty Credit Risk: Performance depends on the other party's creditworthiness. Governed by bilateral collateral margin agreements.

Exchange-Traded Futures

Standardized contracts traded on centralized central exchanges (e.g. CME):

  • Highly Standardized: Contract dimensions, expiration increments, and delivery grading are rigidly predefined.
  • Central Clearinghouse: Default risk is completely bypassed through clearinghouses requiring initial and daily maintenance margins.
