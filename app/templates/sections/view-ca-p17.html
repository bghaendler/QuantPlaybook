Complex Analysis for Quants — Part 17

Infinite Trigonometric Integrals & Jordan's Lemma

Fourier-type integrals $\int e^{iax}f(x)dx$ by residues: Jordan's lemma, the half-plane rule, and the direct line to characteristic-function inversion — the series' capstone.

1. Jordan's Lemma

For $a > 0$ and $f \to 0$ uniformly on the upper semicircle $C_R$:

$$\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{C_R} e^{iaz}f(z)\,dz = 0.$$

The point: $|e^{iaz}| = e^{-a\,\mathrm{Im}\,z}$ decays exponentially in the upper half-plane, so mere decay of $f$ (any rate, even $1/R$) suffices — no two-degree gap needed. The sharp bound $\int_0^\pi e^{-aR\sin\theta}d\theta < \pi/(aR)$ uses $\sin\theta \ge 2\theta/\pi$. Half-plane rule: close where the exponential dies — $e^{iax}$ with $a > 0$ upward, $a < 0$ downward. Choosing wrong makes the arc explode; this single sign-check is the most common bug in hand-computed inversions.

2. The Canonical Computation

$\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{\cos ax}{1 + x^2}dx$ ($a > 0$): write $\cos ax = \mathrm{Re}\,e^{iax}$, close upward, pole at $z = i$ with residue $\frac{e^{ia\cdot i}}{2i} = \frac{e^{-a}}{2i}$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{e^{iax}}{1 + x^2}dx = 2\pi i\cdot\frac{e^{-a}}{2i} = \pi e^{-a} \;\Rightarrow\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{\cos ax}{1+x^2}dx = \pi e^{-a}.$$

Read probabilistically: this is the statement that the Cauchy distribution's characteristic function is $e^{-|a|}$ — transform pair computed both directions now (Part 16 inverted it). Companion result via an indented contour at the origin: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\sin x}{x}dx = \pi$ (half-residue of $e^{iz}/z$) — the sinc integral underlying truncation windows in FFT pricing grids.

3. The General Fourier-Inversion Pattern

Distribution functions from characteristic functions (Gil-Pelaez):

$$\mathbb{P}(X > x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iux}\varphi(u)}{iu}\right]du,$$

an oscillatory infinite integral of exactly this part's type: the $1/u$ singularity is the indented origin pole (contributing the $\frac{1}{2}$), the oscillation $e^{-iux}$ picks its closing half-plane by the sign of $x$, and decay of $\varphi$ plays $f \to 0$ in Jordan's lemma. Heston semi-closed pricing ($P_1, P_2$ probabilities), Merton jump-diffusion CDFs, and CDS-bootstrap survival probabilities in affine intensity models are all this one integral with different $\varphi$.

4. From Contours to Production Code

  • Damping = line shifting: Carr-Madan's $e^{\alpha k}$ factor moves the integration line off the $u = 0$ singularity — a deformation (Part 10) chosen inside the analyticity strip (Part 05); residues crossed during shifts account for the alternative formulas (covered call, delta variants).
  • Truncation & oscillation control: the integrand decays like $|\varphi(u)|/u$; choose $u_{max}$ from the model's known decay (Gaussian-type: fast; VG/CGMY: power-law — integrate further). ML-style bounds (Part 09) give rigorous truncation errors.
  • Quadrature: oscillatory integrals favor FFT on uniform grids (Simpson weights) or adaptive Gauss on $[0, u_{max}]$; for extreme strikes, saddlepoint/steepest-descent deformations (Part 10) tame the oscillation entirely.
  • Sanity tests: $\varphi \equiv e^{-u^2/2}$ must reproduce Black-Scholes; $\pi e^{-a}$ and $\mathrm{sinc}$ integrals make exact unit tests for your inversion code.

Key Takeaways

  • Jordan's lemma: exponential half-plane decay of $e^{iaz}$ closes Fourier integrals with minimal decay of $f$; close where the exponential dies.
  • $\int\frac{\cos ax}{1+x^2}dx = \pi e^{-a}$: the Cauchy characteristic-function pair, the family's flagship.
  • Gil-Pelaez/Heston/Carr-Madan inversions are this machinery: indented origin pole, damping as line-shift, residues on crossing.
  • Numerics inherit the theory: analyticity strips choose damping, decay rates choose truncation, exact pairs make unit tests.
