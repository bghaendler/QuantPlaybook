L3: PDEs & Transition Density Functions

Trinomial Random Walks, Kolmogorov Equations, and Similarity Solutions

1. Forward Kolmogorov (Fokker-Planck)

Consider a discrete trinomial random walker at $(y', t')$ transitioning from states one step earlier ($t' - \delta t$) with up/down probabilities $\alpha$ and stationary probability $1-2\alpha$:

$$p(y', t') = \alpha p(y' + \delta y, t' - \delta t) + (1 - 2\alpha)p(y', t' - \delta t) + \alpha p(y' - \delta y, t' - \delta t)$$

Expanding up to $O(\delta t)$ and $O(\delta y^2)$ via multi-variable Taylor series and simplifying yields:

$$\delta t \frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} \approx \alpha \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$$

Taking the continuous limit as $\delta t, \delta y \to 0$ with the spatial scaling $\delta y \sim O(\delta t^{1/2})$ defines the diffusion coefficient $c^2$ and yields the Forward Kolmogorov Equation (FKE):

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2} \quad \text{where} \quad c^2 = \lim_{\delta t, \delta y \to 0} \frac{\alpha \delta y^2}{\delta t}$$

FKE Focus: Describes probability propagation with respect to the future state $(y', t')$.

2. Backward Kolmogorov (BKE)

The Backward Kolmogorov Equation treats the future state $(y', t')$ as fixed and describes the transition probability density as a function of the earlier starting state variables $(y, t)$:

$$p(y, t) = \alpha p(y + \delta y, t + \delta t) + (1 - 2\alpha)p(y, t + \delta t) + \alpha p(y - \delta y, t + \delta t)$$

Expanding forward from $(y, t)$ yields the continuous-time relation:

$$\delta t \frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + \alpha \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y^2} \approx 0$$

Substituting $c^2$ and taking the limit results in the Backward Kolmogorov Equation:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + c^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y^2} = 0$$

Notice the sign change in the time derivative! Because $t < t'$, the backward equation is a backward-parabolic PDE which is well-posed when integrated backward in time.

BKE Focus: Describes probability propagation with respect to the initial state $(y, t)$.

3. Similarity Solution & Transition Probability Density Function

To solve the forward diffusion equation, we seek a similarity ansatz $p(y', t') = t'^a f(\xi)$ with similarity variable $\xi = y'/t'^b$. Substituting this reduction and requiring time-invariance forces $b = 1/2$. Conserving probability mass $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p \, dy' = 1$ forces the scaling exponent $a = -1/2$. This collapses the PDE into a single ODE:

Integrating the Exact ODE

Writing the left-hand side as an exact product derivative:

$$-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{d\xi}\left(\xi f(\xi)\right) = c^2 \frac{d^2 f}{d\xi^2}$$

Integrating once and separating variables gives: $\ln f(\xi) = -\frac{\xi^2}{4c^2} + A$. Normalizing the Gaussian integral leads to the transition density function $p(y, t; y', t')$:

$$p(y, t; y', t') = \frac{1}{2c\sqrt{\pi(t' - t)}} \exp\left( -\frac{(y' - y)^2}{4c^2(t' - t)} \right)$$

Statistical Moments Interpretation

This similarity solution matches the PDF of a Normal Distribution:

$$y' \sim N\left(y, \, 2c^2(t' - t)\right)$$

  • Mean (Expected Position): $\mathbb{E}[y'] = y$ (the walker remains centered on the starting position).
  • Variance (Spread): $\text{Var}(y') = 2c^2(t' - t)$ (the variance scales linearly with time, indicating linear diffusion).

Problem A: Forward Kolmogorov Equation (FKE) Derivation

Derive the continuous 1D Forward Kolmogorov Equation (FKE) starting from a discrete trinomial random walk framework.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the Discrete Trinomial Step Relation: A walker at position $y'$ at time $t'$ must have been one step earlier ($t' - \delta t$) at $y'+\delta y$ (moving down with probability $\alpha$), $y'-\delta y$ (moving up with probability $\alpha$), or remained at $y'$ (probability $1-2\alpha$): $$p(y', t') = \alpha p(y' + \delta y, t' - \delta t) + (1 - 2\alpha)p(y', t' - \delta t) + \alpha p(y' - \delta y, t' - \delta t)$$
  2. Perform Multi-Variable Taylor Series Expansion: Expand the terms on the RHS about $(y', t' - \delta t)$: $$p(y' \pm \delta y, t' - \delta t) = p(y', t' - \delta t) \pm \delta y \frac{\partial p}{\partial y'} + \frac{1}{2}\delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2} + O(\delta y^3)$$ Substitute these back into the step relation: $$p(y', t') = (1-2\alpha)p + \alpha \left[ 2p + \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2} + O(\delta y^4) \right]$$ $$p(y', t') = p(y', t' - \delta t) + \alpha \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$$
  3. Expand the Time LHS Term: Expand $p(y', t')$ about the earlier time $t' - \delta t$: $$p(y', t') = p(y', t' - \delta t) + \delta t \frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} + O(\delta t^2)$$
  4. Equate, Cancel and Take the Continuous Limit: Combining expansions: $$p(y', t' - \delta t) + \delta t \frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = p(y', t' - \delta t) + \alpha \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$$ $$\delta t \frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = \alpha \delta y^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2} \implies \frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = \left(\frac{\alpha \delta y^2}{\delta t}\right) \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$$ Taking the limits as $\delta t, \delta y \to 0$ defining the constant diffusion coefficient $c^2 = \frac{\alpha \delta y^2}{\delta t}$ yields the FKE: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why start with discrete probability? We model the random walker like a board game. At any tick of the clock, the walker can only jump left, jump right, or stay still. This is an intuitive discrete framework that serves as our mathematical foundation.

2. Why use Taylor Series expansions? A Taylor series is a mathematical magnifying glass. It takes a discrete formula and expresses it as a smooth, continuous equation, breaking down the jumps into directional sensitivities (derivatives).

3. why do the odd terms cancel out? Because the walker is just as likely to jump left as right (symmetrical walk), the directional up/down speeds ($\delta y \frac{\partial p}{\partial y'}$) cancel each other out, leaving only the stationary drift and the squared volatility spread.

4. Why define $c^2$ as a limit? For the continuous limit to make sense, as the time steps get infinitely small, the spatial jumps must also shrink. But to keep the random walk active, they must shrink at a specific relative speed: spatial step squared divided by time step must remain constant ($c^2$). This represents the continuous rate of random dispersion.

Problem B: Similarity Variable Reduction

Prove that substituting the similarity variable $\xi = \frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}$ into the Heat Equation $\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2}$ collapses it into a single Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE).

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the Ansatz and derivatives: Let $\theta(x, t) = t^{-1/2} f(\xi)$ where $\xi = x t^{-1/2}$. We apply the multivariable chain rule to find partial derivatives of $\theta$ in terms of $f(\xi)$.
  2. Compute Spatial Partial Derivatives: $$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = t^{-1/2} \frac{df}{d\xi} \frac{\partial \xi}{\partial x} = t^{-1/2} f'(\xi) (t^{-1/2}) = t^{-1} f'(\xi)$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} = t^{-1} \frac{d(f')}{d\xi} \frac{\partial \xi}{\partial x} = t^{-1} f''(\xi) (t^{-1/2}) = t^{-3/2} f''(\xi)$$
  3. Compute Temporal Partial Derivative: Using the product rule on $\theta(x, t) = t^{-1/2} f(\xi)$: $$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} f(\xi) + t^{-1/2} f'(\xi) \frac{\partial \xi}{\partial t}$$ Since $\frac{\partial \xi}{\partial t} = x \left( -\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} \right) = -\frac{1}{2} \xi t^{-1}$: $$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} f(\xi) - \frac{1}{2} \xi t^{-3/2} f'(\xi) = -\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} \left[ f(\xi) + \xi f'(\xi) \right]$$
  4. Substitute into PDE and Collapse: Substitute the derivatives into the original Heat Equation $\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2}$: $$-\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} \left[ f(\xi) + \xi f'(\xi) \right] = \frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2} f''(\xi)$$ Divide both sides by $\frac{1}{2} t^{-3/2}$ to eliminate all explicit $t$ variables: $$- (f(\xi) + \xi f'(\xi)) = f''(\xi) \implies f''(\xi) + \xi f'(\xi) + f(\xi) = 0$$ This is a single variable ODE: $f'' + (\xi f)' = 0$.
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What is a "similarity variable"? Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) are extremely hard to solve because they contain multiple changing variables (space and time). A similarity variable is a smart ratio ($\xi = x/\sqrt{t}$) that merges space and time into a single combined variable, turning a hard PDE into a much simpler single-variable ODE.

2. Why scale time as $t^{-1/2}$? As time grows, the random walkers spread out. The probability at any single point must decrease to conserve total probability (which must sum to 1). Scaling the height by $1/\sqrt{t}$ keeps the total area under the probability curve constant.

3. What does "collapse" mean? By substituting our derivatives, we observe a mathematical miracle: the time variable $t$ completely cancels out of the equation. This proves our ratio assumption was correct.

4. Why is the ODE easier to solve? We can write $f'' + (\xi f)' = 0$ as $\frac{d}{d\xi}(f' + \xi f) = 0$. Integrating this is a simple matter of high-school calculus, giving us the famous Gaussian bell-curve solution directly.

Problem C: Transition PDF Verification

Verify that the transition probability density function $p(y, t; y', t') = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \tau}} e^{-\frac{z^2}{2\tau}}$ (where $\tau = t' - t$ and $z = y' - y$) solves the continuous Forward Kolmogorov Equation.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Set up FKE and variables: We want to show $\frac{\partial p}{\partial t'} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial y'^2}$ (setting $c^2 = 1/2$ for standard scaling). Let $p(y', t') = (2\pi \tau)^{-1/2} e^{-\frac{z^2}{2\tau}}$ where $\tau = t'$ (initial time $t=0$, initial position $y=0$) and $z = y'$.
  2. Compute Time Derivative $\frac{\partial p}{\partial \tau}$: Using the product rule: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial \tau} = -\frac{1}{2}(2\pi \tau)^{-3/2}(2\pi) e^{-\frac{z^2}{2\tau}} + (2\pi \tau)^{-1/2} \left( \frac{z^2}{2\tau^2} \right) e^{-\frac{z^2}{2\tau}}$$ $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial \tau} = \left( -\frac{1}{2\tau} + \frac{z^2}{2\tau^2} \right) p = \frac{1}{2\tau}\left( \frac{z^2}{\tau} - 1 \right) p$$
  3. Compute First Spatial Derivative $\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}$: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = -\frac{z}{\tau} p$$
  4. Compute Second Spatial Derivative $\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial z^2}$: Using the product rule on $\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}$: $$\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial z^2} = -\frac{1}{\tau} p - \frac{z}{\tau} \frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = -\frac{1}{\tau} p - \frac{z}{\tau} \left( -\frac{z}{\tau} p \right) = \left( -\frac{1}{\tau} + \frac{z^2}{\tau^2} \right) p = \frac{1}{\tau}\left( \frac{z^2}{\tau} - 1 \right) p$$ Comparing results: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial \tau} = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{\tau}\left( \frac{z^2}{\tau} - 1 \right) p \right] = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial z^2}$$ This confirms the transition PDF is a valid solution!
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What does verification accomplish? It proves that our solved Gaussian bell-curve equation is correct. If you plug it into both sides of the heat-spreading PDE, both sides must evaluate to the exact same formula.

2. What does the time derivative represent? It measures how fast the peak of the bell curve drops and flattens out as time ticks forward.

3. What does the second spatial derivative represent? It measures the curvature (concavity) of the bell curve. A sharp curve means high probability difference, driving fast spreading; a flat curve means spreading has slowed down.

4. Why does the math match? The rates match perfectly, showing that the physical rate of heat spreading (or probability dispersion) is exactly balanced by the curvature of the Gaussian density. This gives us confidence in our statistical models.
