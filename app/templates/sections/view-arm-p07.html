Advanced Risk Management

Limitations of Classical VaR Measures

What VaR cannot tell you: tail-blindness, non-subadditivity, gaming, procyclicality — the formal backtests that expose failures, and the fixes (ES and friends).

1. Structural Limitations

  • Tail-blind by construction: VaR is a quantile — it says nothing about losses beyond it. Two books with identical 99% VaR can have ES differing by 10× (sold deep OTM puts vs. plain equity).
  • Not subadditive: $\mathrm{VaR}(A + B) > \mathrm{VaR}(A) + \mathrm{VaR}(B)$ is possible (the coherent-risk course's defaultable-bond example) — merging desks can raise measured risk, breaking limit aggregation logic and rewarding artificial de-mergers.
  • Gameable: the quantile invites payoff shaping — sell risk just beyond the 99th percentile (digital-style tail bets); VaR falls, true risk rises. Quantile optimization is the adversarial failure mode of quantile regulation.
  • Procyclical: rolling-window VaR shrinks in calm and explodes in stress — exactly backwards as a brake; it amplified deleveraging in 2008 (margin/limits tied to VaR).
  • One number, one horizon: liquidity-blind (a 1-day horizon assumes you can exit), correlation-regime-blind, and silent on which factor drives the risk.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Risk management quantifies potential portfolio losses under normal and extreme market conditions, using VaR and Expected Shortfall.

  • Value-at-Risk (VaR) is the maximum loss expected under normal market conditions at a given confidence level.
  • Dynamic models like GARCH adjust risk limits as market volatility clusters and spikes.

2. Backtesting: Catching Failure Formally

  • Kupiec POF test: exceptions $x$ in $n$ days should be Binomial$(n, 1-c)$; likelihood ratio $LR = -2\ln\frac{(1-p)^{n-x}p^x}{(1-\hat{p})^{n-x}\hat{p}^x} \sim \chi^2_1$. With 250 days at 99%: expect 2.5; >7 rejects at 5%.
  • Christoffersen independence test: exceptions must not cluster — a first-order Markov LR test on the exception sequence; clustering (the Part 06 regime signature) fails risk models that pass on counts.
  • Basel traffic lights: green ≤4, yellow 5–9 (capital multiplier rises 3→4), red ≥10 exceptions per 250 days — the supervisory wrapper around Kupiec.
  • ES backtesting is harder (ES is not elicitable alone) but feasible jointly with VaR (Fissler-Ziegel scoring, Acerbi-Székely tests) — the objection that "ES can't be backtested" is obsolete.

3. The Fixes

  • Expected Shortfall: $\mathrm{ES}_c = \mathbb{E}[L \mid L > \mathrm{VaR}_c]$ — tail-aware, subadditive (coherent), harder to game with beyond-quantile payoffs; FRTB's ES₉₇.₅ with factor-level liquidity horizons addresses three limitations at once.
  • Stressed calibration: fixed crisis windows (Part 05) cut procyclicality.
  • Spectral measures: weight quantiles by a risk-aversion function — a coherent family generalizing ES, useful for bespoke appetite statements.
  • Complements, not replacements: sensitivity limits, stress/scenario floors, concentration metrics and drawdown control around any statistical headline (Part 02's layered limits).

4. Using VaR Honestly

VaR remains the right tool for what it does: a common-currency, backtestable, daily summary of ordinary risk. The honest usage contract: (i) quote ES alongside; (ii) publish the backtest record with the number; (iii) attach the assumptions note (window, weighting, revaluation method — Parts 01, 05); (iv) never let the quantile be a target (Goodhart's law applies to risk measures with full force); (v) keep humans responsible for the tail the statistic cannot see.

Key Takeaways

  • VaR is tail-blind, can be non-subadditive, gameable and procyclical — limitations of the measure, before any model error.
  • Kupiec counts + Christoffersen independence + traffic lights make failure detectable; clustered exceptions are the regime alarm.
  • ES (with joint VaR-ES backtests) is the structural fix; stressed windows and layered limits handle the rest.
  • Quote VaR with ES, its backtest record and its assumptions — and never as a target.
