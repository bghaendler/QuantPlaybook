First-Order Greeks (Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega, Rho)

The standard sensitivities of the generalized Black-Scholes price — closed forms, signs, shapes, and the P&L identity that ties them together.

1. Closed Forms (generalized BSM with carry $b$)

With $d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (b + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$, $d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$, $\phi$ the standard normal pdf:

GreekCallPut
Delta $\partial V/\partial S$$e^{(b-r)T} N(d_1)$$e^{(b-r)T} (N(d_1) - 1)$
Gamma $\partial^2 V/\partial S^2$$\dfrac{e^{(b-r)T} \phi(d_1)}{S \sigma \sqrt{T}}$  (same for call & put)
Vega $\partial V/\partial \sigma$$S e^{(b-r)T} \phi(d_1) \sqrt{T}$  (same for both)
Theta $\partial V/\partial t$$-\dfrac{S e^{(b-r)T}\phi(d_1)\sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} - (b-r)Se^{(b-r)T}N(d_1) - rKe^{-rT}N(d_2)$$-\dfrac{S e^{(b-r)T}\phi(d_1)\sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} + (b-r)Se^{(b-r)T}N(-d_1) + rKe^{-rT}N(-d_2)$
Rho $\partial V/\partial r$$T K e^{-rT} N(d_2)$ ($b\ne r$ case: $-TV_c$ term differs)$-T K e^{-rT} N(-d_2)$

2. Shapes & Signs Worth Memorizing

  • Delta: call ∈ $(0, e^{(b-r)T})$, S-shaped in $S$; ATM call delta ≈ 0.5 (more precisely $N(\sigma\sqrt{T}/2 + b\sqrt{T}/\sigma)$, slightly above ½). Often (mis)read as the risk-neutral probability of finishing ITM — that is $N(d_2)$, not $N(d_1)$.
  • Gamma: bell-shaped in $S$, peaks just below ATM, explodes as $T \to 0$ at the strike ($\Gamma \sim 1/(S\sigma\sqrt{T})$) — pin risk for hedgers.
  • Vega: bell-shaped, peaks near ATM(F), grows with $\sqrt{T}$ — long-dated options are vega instruments, short-dated are gamma instruments.
  • Theta: typically negative for long options; deep-ITM European puts can have positive theta (price below intrinsic, pulled up by discounting).
  • Rho: calls positive, puts negative; matters mostly for long-dated and rates-linked options.

3. The Greeks Are Not Independent: BS Equation as P&L Identity

The pricing PDE is a constraint among Greeks:

$$\Theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma + b S \Delta = rV.$$

For a delta-hedged book this reads: theta pays for gamma. The instantaneous hedged P&L over $dt$ when realized vol is $\sigma_{real}$:

$$d\Pi \approx \tfrac{1}{2} \Gamma S^2 \left(\sigma_{real}^2 - \sigma_{imp}^2\right) dt,$$

so a long-gamma position profits when the market moves more than the implied vol you paid — the fundamental theorem of option market-making.

4. Worked Example

$S = K = 100$, $T = 0.25$, $r = b = 5\%$, $\sigma = 20\%$: $d_1 = (0.0125 + 0.005)/0.1 = 0.175$, $\phi(d_1) = 0.3925$.

  • $\Delta_c = N(0.175) = 0.5695$ — hedge a short 100-lot call with 5,695 shares.
  • $\Gamma = 0.3925/(100 \times 0.1) = 0.0393$ per \$ — a \$1 move shifts delta by ≈ 0.04.
  • Vega $= 100 \times 0.3925 \times 0.5 = 19.6$ per unit vol → \$0.196 per vol point.
  • $\Theta \approx -[100 \times 0.3925 \times 0.2/(2\times0.5)] - 5\% \times 75e^{-0.0125}\times N(d_2) \approx -11.5$/yr → ≈ −3.2 cents/day.

5. Hedging Practice Notes

  • Delta is hedged continuously (cheap); gamma and vega are hedged with other options (expensive) — books target gamma/vega limits, not zero.
  • Under the smile, practitioners quote sticky-strike vs sticky-delta deltas; the "smile-adjusted" delta adds $\text{Vega} \times \partial\sigma/\partial S$.
  • Discrete hedging error over interval $\Delta t$ has standard deviation ∝ vega × $\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$ — hedge frequency trades transaction costs against replication noise.

Key Takeaways

  • Delta = $e^{(b-r)T}N(d_1)$; Gamma and Vega are identical for calls and puts and proportional to $\phi(d_1)$.
  • Gamma spikes near expiry ATM; vega grows with $\sqrt{T}$.
  • $\Theta + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\Gamma + bS\Delta = rV$: theta is the rent on gamma.
  • Hedged P&L ∝ $\Gamma S^2(\sigma_{real}^2 - \sigma_{imp}^2)$ — Greeks define the vol trade.
  • $N(d_2)$, not delta, is the risk-neutral ITM probability.
