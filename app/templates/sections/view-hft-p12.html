High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Identifying Microstructure Price Relationships

The capstone: lead-lag structure, price discovery shares, cointegration across venues and instruments — finding where price formation actually happens.

1. Lead-Lag Relationships

Related instruments (ETF vs. basket, futures vs. cash, cross-listings, venue feeds) do not move simultaneously — one usually leads by milliseconds to seconds:

  • Cross-correlation functions of returns at fine lags: the asymmetry of $\rho(\tau)$ vs. $\rho(-\tau)$ locates the leader. Use the Hayashi-Yoshida estimator on raw asynchronous ticks — naive synchronized sampling at fine grids biases correlations toward zero (the Epps effect).
  • Classic findings: index futures lead cash by ~seconds; SPY and ES co-lead within milliseconds; the consolidated SIP lags direct feeds — the literal latency-arbitrage gap (Part 06).
  • Lead-lag horizons have compressed from minutes (1990s) to milliseconds — the measured footprint of the HFT arms race.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: High-frequency trading dynamics rely on analyzing limit order book imbalances and price formation at millisecond scales.

  • Lead-lag analysis shows which exchange or asset reacts first, giving high-frequency traders a split-second head start.
  • Order book imbalances predict short-term price moves within milliseconds.

2. Cointegration at High Frequency

Prices of the "same" asset on different venues (or tight substitutes) are I(1) individually but their spread is stationary — a cointegration relation with error-correction dynamics:

$$\Delta p^A_t = \alpha_A(p^B_{t-1} - p^A_{t-1} - \mu) + \dots, \qquad \Delta p^B_t = -\alpha_B(\cdot) + \dots$$

The adjustment speeds $\alpha$ say who corrects toward whom: the leader barely adjusts ($\alpha \approx 0$), the follower does the converging. This vector-error-correction (VECM) frame underlies both stat-arb pairs at high frequency and the price-discovery metrics below. Estimation care: event-clock sampling, robust SEs for heteroskedastic microstructure noise, and sub-second alignment (Part 11's joins).

Plain English Notes

  • The bid-ask spread is the transaction cost or tax paid to execute a trade immediately.
  • Cointegration identifies assets that move together, allowing mean-reverting arbitrage strategies at high frequency.

3. Price Discovery Metrics

  • Information shares (Hasbrouck): from the VECM, decompose the variance of the common efficient-price innovation across venues — bounds per venue (upper/lower from Cholesky orderings); midpoints quoted in practice.
  • Component shares (Gonzalo-Granger): weights of the common permanent factor — cheaper, complements Hasbrouck.
  • Empirical regularities: primary listing venues and futures dominate discovery; dark venues contribute volume but little discovery (they reference lit prices — Part 06's pegging); discovery share shifts intraday with the seasonality of Part 05.
  • Use: route information-sensitive child orders to (or time them by) the discovering venue's feed; build fair-value signals from the leader, execute on the lagger.

  • Smart order routers dynamically split trades across exchanges and dark pools to find the best liquidity.

4. From Relationships to Strategies (and Their Limits)

  • Latency/stat arb: trade the lagger when the leader moves — profitable iff (predicted move) > (spread + fees + adverse-selection cost); the Part 01–03 cost stack is the strategy filter.
  • ETF arbitrage: NAV-basis error correction with creation/redemption as the hard anchor; inventory and hedge legs priced via the impact models of Parts 02–03.
  • Decay discipline: these edges erode as others find them — measured lead-lag shrinkage is the canonical example of alpha decay; continuous re-measurement (the Part 11 pipeline) is not optional.
  • Causality caveat: lead-lag is correlation in time, not causation — common reactions to a third feed (news, index ticks) mimic leadership; condition on event windows before believing a leader.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Market impact is the price change caused by your own order size depleting the order book.

  • The bid-ask spread is the transaction cost or tax paid to execute a trade immediately.
  • Inventory risk is the cost of holding too many long or short shares while the market moves against you.

Key Takeaways

  • Measure lead-lag with Hayashi-Yoshida on raw ticks — synchronized fine grids fall to the Epps effect.
  • Venue/instrument pairs are cointegrated; VECM adjustment speeds reveal who follows whom.
  • Hasbrouck information shares & Gonzalo-Granger weights quantify where price discovery happens — futures and primary lits dominate, dark pools reference.
  • Lead-lag edges must clear the full microstructure cost stack and are perpetually decaying — measurement is the moat.
