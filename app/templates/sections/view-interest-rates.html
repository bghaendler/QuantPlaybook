Module 1.1: Interest Rates & Discount Bonds

Core definitions, compounding frequencies, yield curves, the short rate, money market accounts, and discount factors.

1. Understading compounding frequencies

Interest represents the rent paid by a borrower of money to a lender over a period of time $[t, T]$. With a principal loan of 1 unit at time $t$, the borrower pays back the principal plus an interest amount $R$ at maturity $T$. The compounding frequency determines how we annualize and quote this return:

Simple Rate (LIBOR) $L(t, T)$

Quoted daily as a simple linear rate. Used extensively for interbank reference rates.

$$ 1 + R = 1 + (T - t)L(t, T) $$

Continuous Compounding (Yield) $y(t, T)$

Reinvested continuously at logarithmic rates. Standard for analytical interest rate models.

$$ 1 + R = e^{(T - t)y(t, T)} $$

For any positive interest return ($R > 0$), simple compounding interest rates are mathematically higher than their continuously compounded yield counterparts: $L(t, T) > y(t, T)$.

We also define the $n$-fold compounded interest rate $y_n(t, T)$ corresponding to the lending period $[t, T]$ through:

$n$-Fold Compounded Rate $y_n(t, T)$

Compounded $n$ times per year (e.g., $n=2$ for semi-annual, $n=4$ for quarterly, $n=12$ for monthly).

$$ P(t, T) = \left( 1 + \frac{y_n(t, T)}{n} \right)^{-n(T-t)} $$

Elementary calculus shows that as the compounding frequency $n$ approaches infinity, we obtain continuous compounding as the limit:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} y_n(t, T) = y(t, T) $$

EPFL Session Spotlight

  • Term Structure: Prevailing term structure is obtained by freezing $t$ and varying $T$. Economic fluctuations cause this curve to shift, twist, and bend.
  • Short Rate $r(t)$: At the short end, compounding frequencies coincide:
    $$ \lim_{T \to t} L(t, T) = \lim_{T \to t} y(t, T) = r(t) $$
    This is the rate earned on a loan over a short period $[t, t + dt]$.
  • Discount Bond: Zero-coupon bond pays 1 at maturity $T$. Its price curve represents the discount curve, typically smooth, decreasing, and satisfying $P(T, T) = 1$.

2. The Money Market Account

Investing 1 unit of currency at the short rate $r(t)$, and continuously reinvesting it, grows into the money market account $B(t)$.

The local return is given by $B(t + dt) = B(t)(1 + r(t)dt)$. With normalization $B(0) = 1$, we obtain the continuous limit:

$$ B(t) = e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds} $$

The Money Market Account is a risk-free asset because its return over $[t, t+dt]$ is determined by $r(t)$ which is known at the beginning of the time interval (i.e. it is locally previsible and lacks local volatility).

Practice Exercise: Simple Compounding vs continuous yield

A financial institution borrows $100 face value on the interbank LIBOR market for a period of 6 months ($\tau = 0.5$ years). The quoted 6-month LIBOR simple rate is $L(t, T) = 6\%$.
Calculate the discount bond price $P(t, T)$ and the corresponding continuously compounded yield $y(t, T)$.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution
  1. Calculate the Discount Factor (ZCB Price) $P(t, T)$:

    Using the simple compounding price relation: $$ P(t, T) = \frac{1}{1 + (T - t)L(t, T)} = \frac{1}{1 + 0.5 \times 0.06} = \frac{1}{1.03} \approx \mathbf{0.970874} $$ Meaning the institution buys a discount bond paying 100 for a price of $97.09.

  2. Calculate the Continuously Compounded Yield $y(t, T)$:

    Using the continuous compounding yield relation: $$ P(t, T) = e^{-(T - t)y(t, T)} \implies y(t, T) = -\frac{\ln P(t, T)}{T - t} $$ $$ y(t, T) = -\frac{\ln(0.970874)}{0.5} = -\frac{-0.029559}{0.5} \approx 0.059118 \text{ (or } \mathbf{5.91\%}\text{)} $$

Verification: Consistent with the mathematical inequality $L(t, T) > y(t, T)$, the simple rate is exactly $6.00\%$ while the continuously compounded yield is $5.91\%$.
