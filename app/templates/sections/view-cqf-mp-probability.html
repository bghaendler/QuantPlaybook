Probability & Statistics

Expectation Algebra, Central Limit Theorem, Probability Distributions, and Maximum Likelihood Estimation
Probability and Statistics provide the framework for measuring uncertainty, describing random outcomes, and making inferential predictions from empirical data. This section reviews probability axioms, set diagrams, conditional probability, Bayes' rule, random variables, expectation algebra, probability distributions, Central Limit Theorem (CLT), and statistical methods (such as Maximum Likelihood Estimation and Time Series analysis).

1.1 Preliminaries & Scale

The foundations of probability theory describe operations over experimental outcomes:

  • Experiment: A repeatable process giving rise to a set of outcomes.
  • Event ($E$): A collection (or set) of one or more outcomes.
  • Sample Space ($\Omega$): The set of all possible outcomes. For a fair six-sided die, $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$.

The probability of an event $E$ is bounded strictly on the probability scale: $0 \le P(E) \le 1$. If $E'$ is the complementary event (not $E$), then:

$$P(E') = 1 - P(E)$$

1.2 Diagrammatic Probability & Sets

Visualizing sample spaces and event relationships is critical in multivariable probability. For two events $A$ and $B$, the Addition Rule accounts for joint intersections:

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

Academic Example (Tri-Set Vet Client Survey): Out of 100 clients, 25 own dogs ($D$), 53 own cats ($C$), and 40 own tropical fish ($T$). With 15 owning $D \cap C$, 10 owning $C \cap T$, 11 owning $D \cap T$, and 7 owning all three ($D \cap C \cap T$), we find:
• $P(\text{Dogs only}) = \frac{25 - (8 + 4 + 7)}{100} = \frac{6}{100} = 6\%$
• $P(\text{No tropical fish}) = \frac{60}{100} = 60\%$

1.3 Conditional Probability & Bayes' Rule

The probability of an event $B$ scales dynamically if we know a dependent event $A$ has already occurred:

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \implies P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \times P(B)$$

Because $P(A \cap B) = P(B \cap A)$, equating these products yields Bayes' Theorem (prior-to-posterior mapping):

$$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) \times P(B)}{P(A)}$$

Worked Academic Example: A bag contains 9 normal fair coins ($N$) and 1 double-headed coin ($H$). We pull one out and get 5 heads in a row. What is the probability it is the double-headed coin?
• Prior: $P(H) = 0.1$, $P(N) = 0.9$
• Likelihood: $P(\text{5H}|H) = 1$, $P(\text{5H}|N) = \frac{1}{32}$
• Marginal: $P(\text{5H}) = 1 \times 0.1 + \frac{1}{32} \times 0.9 = \frac{41}{320} \approx 12.8\%$
• Posterior: $P(H|\text{5H}) = \frac{1 \times 0.1}{41/320} = \frac{32}{41} \approx 78\%$

1.6 & 1.7 Random Variables & Continuous Distributions

A Random Variable (RV) is a function mapping outcomes from the sample space $\Omega$ to the real line $\mathbb{R}$.

For Continuous RVs, the probability of achieving any exact value is zero ($P(X=x) = 0$). Instead, we define a Probability Density Function (PDF) $f(x)$ where the probability of falling in the interval $(a, b)$ is the area under the curve:

$$P(a < X < b) = \int_a^b f(x)dx \quad \text{subject to} \quad \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(x)dx = 1$$

Conversely, the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) $F(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t)dt$, meaning $f(x) = \frac{d}{dx}F(x)$.

1.9 - 1.11 Expectation Algebra & Higher Moments

Expectation $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and Variance $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ represent first and second moments. Higher normalized central moments describe structural characteristics of probability distributions:

Skewness (Asymmetry): $$\text{Skew} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^3]}{\sigma^3}$$ Measures directional bias (0 for symmetric normal curves, positive for right fat-tailed returns).
Kurtosis (Tail Risk / Fatness): $$\text{Kurt} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^4]}{\sigma^4}$$ Normal distributions have Kurt = 3. Excess Kurtosis ($\text{Kurt}-3$) represents tail-shock severity.

1.13 Crucial Distributions in Quantitative Finance

Three primary probability structures model discrete counts and continuous random asset pricing moves:

Binomial Distribution $X \sim B(n, p)$ models finite success trials: $$P(X=x) = \binom{n}{x} p^x (1-p)^{n-x}$$ Mean: $np$
Variance: $np(1-p)$
Poisson Distribution $X \sim Po(\lambda)$ models arrival rates in space/time: $$P(X=r) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^r}{r!}$$ Mean: $\lambda$
Variance: $\lambda$
Normal Distribution $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ models continuous shifts: $$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$ Z-transform: $Z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1)$

2.1 & 2.2 Statistics, Estimators & Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Statistics deals with drawing inferences from samples of data drawn from an underlying population.

  • Sample Mean ($\bar{X}$): An estimator for population mean $\mu$. It is unbiased because $\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}] = \mu$. $$\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$
  • Sample Variance ($S^2$): An estimator for population variance $\sigma^2$. To ensure it is unbiased, we divide by $n-1$ (Bessel's Correction): $$S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2$$
  • Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE): Finds parameter values $\theta$ that maximize the probability density of observing the sample data $x_1, \dots, x_n$. We construct the Likelihood function $L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i; \theta)$, and solve by maximizing the Log-Likelihood: $$\ln L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \ln f(x_i; \theta) \implies \frac{d}{d\theta}\ln L(\theta) = 0$$
Academic Example (Normal Distribution MLE): For $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the log-likelihood is: $$\ln L(\mu, \sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\ln(\sigma^2) - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}$$ Setting the partial derivatives to zero yields the MLE estimators: $\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{X}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum (x_i - \bar{X})^2$ (which is biased, illustrating that MLE estimators are asymptotically unbiased but may be biased in small samples).

2.3 & 2.4 Simple Linear Regression & Time Series Analysis

Simple Linear Regression: Models the relationship between dependent variable $Y$ and independent variable $X$ via a straight line plus error: $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$. The ordinary least squares (OLS) estimates minimize the sum of squared residuals $\sum \epsilon_i^2$:

$$\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)} \quad , \quad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{Y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{X}$$

Pearson Correlation Coefficient ($r$): Standardizes covariance to $[-1, 1]$: $r = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} = \hat{\beta}_1 \frac{\sigma_X}{\sigma_Y}$.

Time Series Analysis: A sequence of data points indexed in time. A time series is weakly stationary if its mean is constant and autocovariances depend only on the time lag $k$, not on absolute time. Key models include:

Autoregressive model AR(p): Current value depends linearly on past values: $$X_t = c + \sum_{i=1}^p \phi_i X_{t-i} + \epsilon_t$$
Moving Average model MA(q): Current value depends on current and past white noise: $$X_t = \mu + \epsilon_t + \sum_{j=1}^q \theta_j \epsilon_{t-j}$$
