Complex Analysis for Quants

Residue Theorem & Polynomial Contour Integration

The grand assembly: loop integrals = 2πi × (sum of enclosed residues). Computation rules, worked rational-function examples, and the checklist that prevents the standard mistakes.

1. The Theorem

For $f$ holomorphic on and inside a simple closed (counterclockwise) contour $\gamma$ except at isolated singularities $z_1, \dots, z_k$ inside:

$$\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i\sum_{j=1}^{k}\mathrm{Res}_{z_j} f.$$

Proof in one breath: deform $\gamma$ into $k$ small circles (Part 10), Laurent-expand on each (Part 13), integrate term by term — only $a_{-1}$ survives (Part 09). Everything in the series so far is a lemma for this line.

2. Computing Residues

  • Simple pole: $\mathrm{Res}_{z_0} f = \lim_{z\to z_0}(z - z_0)f(z)$.
  • Quotient shortcut ($f = g/h$, simple zero of $h$): $\mathrm{Res}_{z_0} = \dfrac{g(z_0)}{h'(z_0)}$ — the fastest and least error-prone rule; use it whenever possible.
  • Pole of order $m$: $\mathrm{Res}_{z_0} = \dfrac{1}{(m-1)!}\lim_{z\to z_0}\dfrac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}\bigl[(z - z_0)^m f(z)\bigr]$.
  • Series extraction: multiply/divide known Taylor series and read the $z^{-1}$ coefficient — often easier than the derivative formula for order ≥ 3, and the only way at essential singularities.

3. Worked Rational Examples

(a) $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2}\frac{z^2}{(z-1)(z^2+9)}dz$: poles at $1, \pm 3i$; only $z = 1$ inside $|z| = 2$. Residue $= \frac{1}{10}$ ⇒ integral $= \frac{\pi i}{5}$.

(b) $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=4}\frac{z^2}{(z-1)(z^2+9)}dz$: now all three poles inside. By the quotient rule at $\pm 3i$: $\frac{z^2}{(z-1)\cdot 2z}\big|_{z=\pm3i} = \frac{\pm3i}{2(\pm3i - 1)}$. Sum of all residues $= \frac{1}{10} + \frac{9}{10} = 1$ ⇒ integral $= 2\pi i$. (Sanity check: for large $|z|$, $f \sim 1/z$, and $\oint z^{-1} = 2\pi i$ — the "residue at infinity" confirming the sum.)

(c) Higher-order pole: $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{e^{z}}{z^3}dz$: triple pole; residue = coefficient of $z^2$ in $e^z$ = $\frac{1}{2}$ ⇒ integral $= \pi i$. Series beats the double-derivative formula.

4. Error-Proofing Checklist

  • Which poles are inside? Compute moduli against the contour radius — the #1 mistake is summing a pole that sits outside (or on!) the contour. Poles on the contour ⇒ principal values/indented contours, a different calculation.
  • Orientation: clockwise traversal flips the sign ($-2\pi i\sum$).
  • Cancel before classifying: common zeros of numerator/denominator change pole orders (Part 14).
  • Cross-check: sum of all residues (including at ∞) of a rational function is 0; for $\deg(\text{denom}) \ge \deg(\text{num}) + 2$, the large-circle integral → 0 — free consistency tests, as in example (b).
  • Numerical verification: parameterize and quadrature the contour (50 lines of NumPy) — the habit that catches algebra slips before they reach a pricing formula.

Key Takeaways

  • $\oint f = 2\pi i \sum \mathrm{Res}$ over enclosed singularities — deformation + Laurent + the $z^{-1}$ integral, assembled.
  • Use $g/h'$ for simple poles, series extraction for high orders and essential points.
  • Always audit pole locations vs. the contour and the traversal direction.
  • The residues-sum-to-zero and decay checks (and quick numerical quadrature) make the calculus self-verifying.
