Complex Analysis for Quants

Cauchy's Integral Theorem

Holomorphic on and inside a closed contour ⇒ the loop integral vanishes. The theorem, why it's true, and the contour-deformation freedom it licenses.

1. The Statement

If $f$ is holomorphic on a simply connected domain $D$ and $\gamma$ is any closed contour in $D$:

$$\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0.$$

Two classical proofs illuminate different facets: via Green's theorem, writing the integral as two real line integrals whose curls are exactly the Cauchy-Riemann combinations ($v_x + u_y$ and $u_x - v_y$, both zero); and Goursat's proof, which needs only the existence of $f'$ (no continuity assumption) by quadrisecting triangles — the result is genuinely about complex differentiability, not smoothness.

2. What "Simply Connected" Guards Against

The domain must have no holes enclosing singularities. The canonical counterexample is Part 09's:

$$\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz}{z} = 2\pi i \ne 0$$

— $1/z$ is holomorphic on the punctured plane, but the puncture at 0 sits inside the loop. Cauchy's theorem is thus a precise dichotomy: closed-loop integrals vanish unless the loop encloses a singularity, and then the value measures the singularity (Part 11's formula, Part 15's residues).

3. The Real Power: Contour Deformation

If two contours $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ share endpoints (or are both closed) and $f$ is holomorphic in the region between them:

$$\int_{\gamma_1} f\,dz = \int_{\gamma_2} f\,dz.$$

You may slide contours freely through holomorphic territory without changing the value. This is the daily-use consequence:

  • Shrink to the singularity: any loop around a pole equals a tiny circle around it — reducing all closed integrals to the one computation $\oint z^{-1}dz = 2\pi i$.
  • Shift integration lines: Carr-Madan/Lewis pricing moves the Fourier inversion line $\mathrm{Im}\,u = \alpha$ up and down inside the analyticity strip — legitimate because of deformation invariance; crossing a pole while shifting picks up an explicit residue correction (how the Lewis formula's delta/covered-call variants relate).
  • Saddlepoint methods: deform through the saddle of $e^{n\phi(z)}$ for steepest-descent asymptotics — the engine behind saddlepoint tail approximations of loss distributions (CDO tranches, portfolio VaR).

4. Immediate Corollaries

  • Antiderivatives exist on simply connected domains: $F(z) = \int_{z_0}^z f\,d\zeta$ is well-defined (path-independent) and $F' = f$.
  • Independence of radius: $\oint_{|z|=r} f\,dz$ is constant in $r$ while no singularity is crossed — the basis for letting $r \to 0$ or $\infty$ in estimates.
  • Morera's converse: if all triangle integrals of a continuous $f$ vanish, $f$ is holomorphic — the standard tool for proving analyticity of integrals depending on a parameter (e.g. that $\varphi(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iuX}]$ is analytic in its strip: differentiate under the integral, justified by Morera + Fubini).

Key Takeaways

  • Holomorphic inside a closed loop ⇒ integral is 0; CR equations are the curl conditions making Green's theorem vanish.
  • Nonzero loop integrals are entirely caused by enclosed singularities — the value is information about them.
  • Deformation freedom: shrink loops to poles, shift Fourier-inversion lines within analyticity strips, pass through saddles.
  • Morera's converse certifies analyticity of parameter integrals — the rigor behind characteristic-function strips.
