Q1. Under which of the following measures is the simple forward rate process $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ a martingale? $\square \text{ risk-neutral measure } \mathbb{Q}$
$\square \ T_0\text{-forward measure } \mathbb{Q}^{T_0}$
$\square \ T_1\text{-forward measure } \mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$
$\square \text{ objective measure } \mathbb{P}$

Solution:

According to the change of numeraire framework, the $T_0$-bond price process discounted by the $T_1$-bond numeraire is a martingale under the corresponding $T_1$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$:

$$\frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \text{ is a } \mathbb{Q}^{T_1}\text{-martingale}$$

The simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is defined by a linear transform of this ratio:

$$F(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0} \left( \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} - 1 \right)$$

Because the ratio process is a $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$-martingale, any linear combination of it maintains the martingale property under the same probability measure. Therefore, $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$.

Correct Answer: $T_1$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$

Q2. Which of the following statements are true? (Select all that apply) $\square$ The instantaneous $T$-forward rate process $f(t, T)$ is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.
$\square$ The instantaneous $T$-forward rate process $f(t, T)$ is a martingale under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.
$\square$ The $T$-yield process $y(t, T)$ is a martingale under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.
$\square$ The money market account discounted by the $T$-bond price process, $\frac{B(t)}{P(t,T)}$, is a martingale under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.

Solution:

  • Statement 1 is False: Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the instantaneous forward rate $f(t,T)$ has a non-zero drift dictated by the HJM drift condition: $\alpha(t, T) = \sigma(t,T) \int_t^T \sigma(t,u)^T du$. Therefore, it cannot be a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$.
  • Statement 2 is True: Under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$, the Girsanov drift adjustment cancels the HJM drift term, meaning the instantaneous $T$-forward rate process $f(t, T)$ is indeed a martingale under its corresponding $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.
  • Statement 3 is False: The yield process $y(t,T) = \frac{1}{T-t}\int_t^T f(t,u)du$ is an average of instantaneous forward rates across a continuum of maturities $u \in [t, T]$. Each rate $f(t,u)$ is a martingale under its own individual forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^u$, not broadly under $\mathbb{Q}^T$.
  • Statement 4 is True: This is a foundational rule of the change of numeraire technique. If the $T$-bond $P(t, T)$ is chosen as the numeraire (which defines the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$), then any tradable asset price process divided by the numeraire must be a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^T$. Since the money market account $B(t)$ represents a tradable asset, $\frac{B(t)}{P(t, T)}$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^T$.

Correct Answers:

  1. The instantaneous $T$-forward rate process $f(t, T)$ is a martingale under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.
  2. The money market account discounted by the $T$-bond price process, $\frac{B(t)}{P(t,T)}$, is a martingale under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$.

Q3. Assume that the short rate process is constant $r(t) \equiv r$. Which of the following statements are true? (Select all that apply) $\square \text{ Any } T\text{-forward measure equals the risk-neutral measure } \mathbb{Q}^T = \mathbb{Q}$.
$\square \text{ Any } T\text{-forward rate process is constant } f(t, T) \equiv r$.
$\square \text{ The time-0 forward curve is linear } f(0, T) = r + T$.
$\square \text{ The risk-neutral measure equals the objective measure } \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{P}$.

Solution:

  • Statement 1 is True: The Radon-Nikodym density that changes the probability measure from $\mathbb{Q}$ to $\mathbb{Q}^T$ is: $$\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} = \frac{e^{-\int_0^T r(s)ds}}{P(0, T)}$$ If $r(t) \equiv r$ is constant, then $P(0, T) = e^{-rT}$ and the integral is $\int_0^T r(s)ds = rT$. This yields: $$\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} = \frac{e^{-rT}}{e^{-rT}} = 1$$ Since the density derivative is exactly 1, no probability distortion occurs, meaning $\mathbb{Q}^T = \mathbb{Q}$.
  • Statement 2 is True: The relation between the ZCB price and forward rates is $P(t, T) = \exp\left(-\int_t^T f(t, u)du\right)$. Under a constant rate $r$, the bond price is $P(t, T) = e^{-r(T-t)}$. Taking the derivative yields $f(t, T) = r$ for all $t$ and $T$.
  • Statement 3 is False: As shown above, the forward curve is a flat constant line $f(0, T) \equiv r$, not linear.
  • Statement 4 is False: The change of measure between the objective measure $\mathbb{P}$ and the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ is dictated by Girsanov's theorem and relies on the market price of risk $\lambda(t)$. Even if the short rate is constant, the market can assign a non-zero risk premium, keeping $\mathbb{Q} \neq \mathbb{P}$.

Correct Answers:

  1. Any $T$-forward measure equals the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}^T = \mathbb{Q}$.
  2. Any $T$-forward rate process is constant $f(t, T) \equiv r$.

Q4. Assume that the short rate process is a standard Brownian motion $r(t) = W^*(t)$ under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$. Which of the following statements are true? (Select all that apply) $\square$ This is a special case of the CIR short rate model.
$\square$ This is a special case of the Vasicek short rate model.
$\square$ The $T$-forward rate volatility is a constant $\sigma(t, T) = 1$.
$\square$ The $T$-forward rate volatility is linear $\sigma(t, T) = T - t$.
$\square$ The volatility of the $T$-bond return process is linear $v(t, T) = -(T - t)$.
$\square$ The drift of the short rate process under the objective measure $\mathbb{P}$ can be mean-reverting of the form $dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + dW(t)$ for a $\mathbb{P}$-standard Brownian motion $W(t)$.

Solution:

The short rate dynamics are given by $dr(t) = dW^*(t)$, which implies drift $\mu = 0$ and volatility $\sigma = 1$.

  • Statement 1 is False: The CIR model has volatility proportional to $\sigma \sqrt{r(t)}$, not a constant 1.
  • Statement 2 is True: The Vasicek model is defined by $dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma_V dW^*(t)$. Setting $\kappa = 0$ and $\sigma_V = 1$ gives exactly $dr(t) = dW^*(t)$.
  • Statement 3 is True: The forward rate volatility in the Vasicek model is $\sigma(t, T) = \sigma_V e^{-\kappa(T-t)}$. Since $\kappa = 0$ and $\sigma_V = 1$, this simplifies to $\sigma(t, T) = 1 \cdot e^0 = 1$.
  • Statement 4 is False: As shown in Statement 3, the volatility is a constant 1, not linear.
  • Statement 5 is True: The bond return volatility in the HJM framework is $v(t, T) = -\int_t^T \sigma(t, u)du$. Substituting the constant $\sigma(t, u) = 1$ yields: $$v(t, T) = -\int_t^T 1 du = -(T-t)$$
  • Statement 6 is True: Under Girsanov's theorem, we change measure between $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{P}$ via standard Brownian transformation: $dW^*(t) = dW(t) + \lambda(t)dt$, where $\lambda(t)$ is the market price of risk. Under $\mathbb{P}$, the dynamics of the short rate become $dr(t) = \lambda(t)dt + dW(t)$. If the market price of risk $\lambda(t)$ takes the form $\kappa(\theta - r(t))$, the short rate under $\mathbb{P}$ is mean-reverting.

Correct Answers:

  1. This is a special case of the Vasicek short rate model.
  2. The $T$-forward rate volatility is a constant $\sigma(t, T) = 1$.
  3. The volatility of the $T$-bond return process is linear $v(t, T) = -(T - t)$.
  4. The drift of the short rate process under the objective measure $\mathbb{P}$ can be mean-reverting of the form $dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + dW(t)$ for a $\mathbb{P}$-standard Brownian motion $W(t)$.
