High-Frequency Trading & Market Microstructure

Estimating Impact Models via Statistical Volatility

Putting numbers on impact: volatility-anchored calibration, regression designs that survive endogeneity, and the diagnostics that catch a mis-fit model.

1. Volatility as the Natural Unit

Impact is quoted in units of volatility because both scale the same way with information flow: cost in bps is noisy and instrument-specific; cost in daily sigmas transfers across assets. The square-root law in calibration form:

$$\frac{\Delta p}{\sigma_{day}} = Y\left(\frac{Q}{V_{day}}\right)^{\delta}, \qquad \text{fit } (Y, \delta) \text{ by regression in logs:}$$ $$\ln\left(\frac{|\Delta p|}{\sigma}\right) = \ln Y + \delta\,\ln\left(\frac{Q}{V}\right) + \epsilon.$$

Typical output: $\delta \approx 0.5$, $Y \approx 0.5$–$1$. The estimate of $\sigma$ itself comes from the realized-vol toolkit (5-minute RV, or the EWMA/GARCH layer of the equities course) — impact estimation inherits all the care of volatility estimation.

2. Regression Designs (and Their Traps)

  • Kyle-λ regressions: $\Delta m_t = \lambda\,\mathrm{SOF}_t + \epsilon_t$ — mid change on signed order flow over fixed intervals; sign trades by Lee-Ready (trade vs. mid) when aggressor flags are absent.
  • Metaorder datasets: the clean way (broker/proprietary data with known parent orders): regress implementation shortfall on participation; public-data proxies must reconstruct metaorders and inherit splitting noise.
  • Endogeneity, the central trap: traders size orders conditional on liquidity and expected moves — naive OLS conflates impact with timing alpha. Mitigations: control for momentum, use scheduled/uninformed flows (index rebalances) as natural experiments, instrument flow with exogenous shifters.
  • Survivorship of completed orders: cancelled-on-adverse-move parents bias measured impact downward; condition on completion regime.

3. From Estimates to an Almgren-Chriss Cost Model

Map the fitted curve to model parameters: with temporary impact $h(v) = \eta v$ and permanent $g(v) = \gamma v$ per unit time,

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{cost}] = \frac{\gamma}{2}Q^2 + \eta\sum_k \frac{n_k^2}{\tau_k}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}[\text{cost}] = \sigma^2\sum_k \tau_k x_k^2,$$

($n_k$ slice sizes, $x_k$ remaining inventory). Calibrate $\eta$ from short-burst executions (pure temporary), $\gamma$ from post-completion price levels (the permanent residue ≈ 1/2–2/3 of peak impact empirically). The fitted $(\sigma, \eta, \gamma)$ triple feeds the optimal scheduler of Part 07 — estimation quality propagates straight into execution quality.

4. Diagnostics & Validation

  • Out-of-sample shortfall prediction: the only test that matters — predicted vs. realized cost by participation bucket; a good model is unbiased per bucket with stable $Y$.
  • Decay check: plot average price path during/after metaorders — peak at completion, partial reversion to the permanent level; no reversion suggests information-dominated samples, full reversion suggests pure mechanical impact.
  • Cross-asset stability: in volatility/participation units, parameters should travel across names; instability flags data or endogeneity problems.
  • Regime sensitivity: recalibrate by volatility regime — impact coefficients are themselves vol-dependent (liquidity evaporates exactly when σ spikes).

Key Takeaways

  • Quote and fit impact in volatility units: $\Delta p/\sigma = Y(Q/V)^\delta$ — parameters then transfer across assets.
  • Endogeneity (flow timed to liquidity and alpha) is the estimation killer; use clean metaorders or exogenous flows.
  • Map fits to Almgren-Chriss $(\eta, \gamma)$ via burst costs and post-trade reversion levels.
  • Validate on out-of-sample shortfall by participation bucket and watch the decay profile.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Estimating market impact requires calibrating models to statistical market volatility. Volatility dictates how easily the order book absorbs large volume spikes.

  • Vol Scaling: Higher market volatility leads to wider spreads and thinner books, increasing the price impact of your trades.
  • Kyle's Lambda: A parameter measuring the illiquidity of the market—how much the price moves per unit of traded volume.
  • Real-Time Calibration: High-frequency execution desks constantly recalibrate impact coefficients as volume and volatility fluctuate throughout the day.
