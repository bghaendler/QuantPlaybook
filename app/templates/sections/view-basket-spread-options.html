Basket & Spread Options (Kirk's Approximation)

Options on portfolios and differences of assets: why sums of lognormals force approximation, and the moment-matching toolkit that prices them.

1. The Common Problem

Both products are options on a linear combination $B = \sum_i w_i S_i$ — positive weights for baskets, mixed signs for spreads. Under multivariate GBM, $B$ is a sum of correlated lognormals: no closed-form density exists. The toolbox: (i) match moments of $B$ to a tractable law; (ii) condition on a one-dimensional factor; (iii) integrate numerically (FFT/quadrature in low dimension); (iv) Monte Carlo with a geometric control.

2. Baskets: Lognormal Moment Matching

The first two moments of $B_T$ are exact:

$$M_1 = \sum_i w_i F_i, \qquad M_2 = \sum_{i,j} w_i w_j F_i F_j\, e^{\rho_{ij}\sigma_i\sigma_j T}, \qquad F_i = S_i e^{b_i T}.$$

Fit a lognormal with $\sigma_B^2 T = \ln(M_2/M_1^2)$ and price with Black-76 on $M_1$ ("Levy approximation" for baskets). Refinements: shifted lognormal or Johnson distributions (capture skew), Ju's Taylor expansion, Curran-style conditioning on the geometric average — the most accurate analytic family. Accuracy is excellent for typical index baskets (similar vols, positive ρ); it degrades when weights are mixed-sign or dispersion is extreme. Note basket vol < average vol: $\sigma_B \approx \sqrt{\bar\rho}\,\bar\sigma$ for similar assets — the diversification discount that makes basket options cheaper than baskets of options, with the gap = the price of correlation.

3. Spreads: Kirk's Approximation

For $\max(F_1 - F_2 - K, 0)$ the basket trick fails ($B$ can be negative). Kirk (1995): group $F_2 + K$ and treat the ratio $Z = F_1/(F_2 + K)$ as lognormal. Then

$$c \approx e^{-rT}\left[F_1 N(d_1) - (F_2 + K) N(d_2)\right],$$ $$\sigma_Z = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 - 2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 W + \sigma_2^2 W^2}, \qquad W = \frac{F_2}{F_2 + K},$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln\left(F_1/(F_2+K)\right) + \sigma_Z^2 T/2}{\sigma_Z\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma_Z\sqrt{T}.$$

Exact at $K = 0$ (it is Margrabe); very accurate for $K$ small relative to $F_2$; biased for large strikes/vols because $F_2 + K$ (lognormal + constant) is not lognormal. Fixes: Bjerksund-Stensland (2006) spread formula (better boundary treatment), second-order corrections (Li-Deng-Zhou), or exact 2D quadrature:

$$c = e^{-rT}\int \text{BS-call}\big(F_1 e^{\rho\sigma_1\sqrt{T} z - \rho^2\sigma_1^2T/2},\ K + F_2(z)\big)\, \phi(z)\, dz,$$

conditioning on the second asset's driver — one numerical integral, machine-precision accurate.

4. Risk & Correlation Skew

  • Cega: baskets are long ρ in vol terms (higher correlation → higher basket vol → higher option value); spreads are short ρ. Equal and opposite books partially internalize.
  • Correlation skew: equity correlations rise in sell-offs; a single flat ρ misprices OTM basket puts (too cheap) — index smiles embed exactly this, and dispersion desks trade the residual.
  • Decomposition hedging: delta per asset from the approximation formulas is reliable; vega allocation across assets and the ρ mark are the judgment calls.
  • Energy spreads add a twist: legs with different units (heat rate) and seasonal vol surfaces per leg — Kirk runs per delivery month.

Key Takeaways

  • Sums of lognormals have no closed law — basket/spread pricing is approximation engineering.
  • Baskets: match $M_1, M_2$ to a lognormal; basket vol ≈ $\sqrt{\bar\rho}\,\bar\sigma$ — correlation is the discount.
  • Kirk: lognormal-shift trick, exact at $K=0$ (Margrabe), weakens as $K$ grows.
  • Conditioning integrals give near-exact spread prices at trivial cost — use them as benchmarks.
  • Cega signs: baskets long ρ, spreads short ρ; correlation skew is the systematic model risk.
