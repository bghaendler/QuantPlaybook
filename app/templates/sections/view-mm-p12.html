Mathematical Methods in Financial Markets

Short Rate Mechanics (Vasicek vs. CIR)

The two canonical mean-reverting short-rate models side by side: dynamics, distributions, bond prices, and the trade-offs that decide which one you reach for.

1. The Common Skeleton: Mean Reversion

Both models write the short rate as a pull toward a long-run level $\theta$ at speed $\kappa$, plus noise:

$$\text{Vasicek:}\quad dr = \kappa(\theta - r)\,dt + \sigma\,dW, \qquad \text{CIR:}\quad dr = \kappa(\theta - r)\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{r}\,dW.$$

The single difference — $\sigma$ vs. $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ — cascades into everything: distribution shape, positivity, bond formulas and calibration behavior. Half-life of a shock in both: $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \kappa$ (κ = 0.5 ⇒ ~1.4 years), the most intuitive way to quote mean-reversion speed.

Pre-2008, "Vasicek goes negative" was a defect; the negative-rate era made it a feature. CIR's $\sqrt{r}$ also reappears as the variance process in Heston — learn it once, use it twice.

3. Bond Pricing: Both Are Affine

Both admit exponential-affine bond prices $P(t, T) = A(\tau)e^{-B(\tau)r_t}$, $\tau = T - t$, with the ODE machinery of Part 07:

$$\text{Vasicek:}\quad B(\tau) = \frac{1 - e^{-\kappa\tau}}{\kappa}, \qquad \ln A = \left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(B - \tau) - \frac{\sigma^2 B^2}{4\kappa},$$ $$\text{CIR:}\quad B(\tau) = \frac{2(e^{\gamma\tau} - 1)}{(\gamma + \kappa)(e^{\gamma\tau} - 1) + 2\gamma}, \qquad \gamma = \sqrt{\kappa^2 + 2\sigma^2},$$

with the matching $A(\tau)$ power formula. Yields are affine in $r$: $y(\tau) = -\frac{\ln A}{\tau} + \frac{B}{\tau}r$ — so each model generates a one-factor family of yield curves (normal, inverted, mildly humped), all perfectly correlated across maturities: the known one-factor limitation that multi-factor and HJM models address.

4. Choosing in Practice

  • Analytical convenience: Vasicek wins — Gaussian closed forms for bonds and bond options (Jamshidian); Hull-White is exactly Vasicek with time-dependent $\theta(t)$ fitted to today's curve.
  • Positivity / realism near zero: CIR wins (with Feller satisfied); intensity models for credit reuse CIR precisely because default intensities must stay positive.
  • Simulation: Vasicek exact and trivial; CIR needs the noncentral-χ² exact sampler or full-truncation Euler (Part 09's warning).
  • Calibration habit: fit κ from autocorrelation/half-life, θ from the long-run mean, σ from residual vol — then accept that one factor can't match cap/swaption surfaces and move to Hull-White/multi-factor for desk use.

Key Takeaways

  • Same drift, different diffusion: $\sigma$ (Vasicek) vs. $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ (CIR) drives every downstream difference.
  • Vasicek = Gaussian, tractable, can go negative; CIR = noncentral χ², positive under Feller $2\kappa\theta \ge \sigma^2$.
  • Both are affine: $P = Ae^{-Br}$ with ODE-solvable coefficients — the gateway to the whole affine class.
  • One factor ⇒ perfectly correlated curve moves; real desks graduate to Hull-White (fitted Vasicek) or multi-factor.
