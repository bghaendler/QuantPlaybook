Corporate Bonds & Credit Spreads

Explore how corporations issue bonds through investment banks, manage credit ratings, and pay credit spreads to compensate investors for default risk.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: a corporate bond is a government bond plus a worry. The company might not pay you back — so it must offer extra yield over Treasuries. That extra, the credit spread, is the market's price tag on default risk.

  • Spreads are quoted in basis points: 100 bps = 1%. "Treasuries + 90 bps" means: whatever the government pays, plus 0.90% for the risk and illiquidity of lending to this company instead.
  • Ratings are shorthand, not gospel: AAA→BBB is "investment grade" (pension funds may buy); BB and below is "high yield"/junk (many mandates forbid it). The cliff between BBB and BB drives real selling — a downgrade across it is a "fallen angel".
  • The spread hides a default forecast: roughly, spread ≈ default probability × loss-if-default. A 200 bps spread with 40% expected recovery implies the market sees ~3.3% annual default odds — decode any bond in the demo below.
  • Issuing is a negotiated sale: unlike government auctions, companies hire investment banks (the DCM desk) to structure, rate and place the bond — the simulator shows how maturity, features and rating add up to the spread.

Debt Capital Markets (DCM) Advising

Unlike governments who use public auctions, companies appoint an Investment Bank to manage and underwrite bond issuances. The DCM desk advises on maturity, structure, currency, and helps arrange a credit rating:

1. Special Features Callable, Convertible, or plain vanilla fixed rate structures.
2. DCM Pricing Pricing against treasury benchmarks plus an extra risk spread.

Demo: Decoding the Default Probability Hidden in a Spread

To a first approximation, the spread compensates expected loss: $s \approx PD \times (1 - R)$, so the market-implied annual default probability is $PD \approx s / (1 - R)$, with $R$ the recovery rate (typically ~40% for senior unsecured).

In plain English: the spread is a forecast you can invert. Real spreads also contain compensation for illiquidity and risk aversion, so market-implied default probabilities run higher than historical default rates — the gap is the "credit risk premium" investors earn for bearing the uncertainty.
