Discounting & Present Values

Understand the mathematics of present valuation: from single-sum discount factors to mortgage amortization schedules, perpetuities, and NPV/IRR project analysis.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Discounting is compounding run backwards. If money grows over time, then money promised in the future must be worth less today — and the discount factor tells you exactly how much less.

  • One number rules them all: the discount factor $DF = 1/(1+r)^T$ converts any future euro into today's euros. Every bond, loan, project and pension valuation is just cash flows × discount factors, summed.
  • Your mortgage is an annuity: the bank computes one fixed payment whose discounted total exactly equals what you borrowed. Early payments are mostly interest; late ones mostly principal — the simulator below shows the split month by month.
  • A perpetuity is shockingly simple: infinite annual payments of PMT are worth just PMT ÷ r today. €50k/year forever at 6% = €833k — distant payments are discounted into irrelevance.
  • NPV > 0 means "do the project": the IRR is the break-even discount rate — drag the slider below across 7% and watch the project flip from profitable to value-destroying.

Visualization: What a Future €100 Is Worth Today

The present value of €100 received in $T$ years decays exponentially with maturity — and the decay speed is set by the rate. Drag the slider; the dashed curve keeps 2% as a reference.

In plain English: this curve is why pensions and long-dated bonds are so sensitive to rates. At 2%, €100 due in 30 years is still worth €55 today; at 6% it's worth only €17. Small rate moves barely touch near-term cash but violently reprice distant promises — that asymmetry is "duration", and it's the beating heart of fixed-income risk.
