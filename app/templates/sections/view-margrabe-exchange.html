Exchange Options: Margrabe's Formula

The right to swap one risky asset for another — priced exactly with no interest rate in sight, by the first great change-of-numeraire argument.

1. The Contract and the Formula

Payoff at $T$: $\max(S_1(T) - S_2(T), 0)$ — receive asset 1, deliver asset 2. With both assets GBM with yields $q_1, q_2$ and correlation $\rho$ (Margrabe 1978):

$$V = S_1 e^{-q_1 T} N(d_1) - S_2 e^{-q_2 T} N(d_2),$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_1/S_2) + (q_2 - q_1 + \hat\sigma^2/2)T}{\hat\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \hat\sigma\sqrt{T},$$ $$\hat\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2}.$$

Black-Scholes with asset 2 playing the role of the strike and the cash account: the risk-free rate cancels entirely.

2. The Numeraire Trick

Measure everything in units of asset 2: define the relative price $X = S_1/S_2$. The payoff is $S_2(T)\max(X_T - 1, 0)$. Under the measure associated with numeraire $S_2 e^{-q_2 t}$, $X$ is a driftless lognormal with volatility $\hat\sigma$ (volatility of the ratio — variances add, covariance subtracts). The problem collapses to a vanilla call on $X$ with strike 1 and zero rates:

$$V = S_2 e^{-q_2 T}\, \mathbb{E}^{S_2}\left[\max(X_T - 1, 0)\right] = S_2 e^{-q_2 T}\left[X_0' N(d_1) - N(d_2)\right],$$

which rearranges to Margrabe. This is the prototype change of numeraire: the same move later yields the forward measure (caplets), the swap measure (swaptions), and FX quanto adjustments. Why does $r$ vanish? The hedge holds the two assets against each other; no cash position is ever needed.

3. Sanity Checks & Special Cases

  • $S_2$ deterministic ($\sigma_2 = 0$, $q_2 = r$): $S_2(T) = K$ ⇒ plain Black-Scholes. ✔
  • $\rho \to 1$, $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$: $\hat\sigma \to 0$ — exchanging an asset for its twin is worthless (beyond the forward difference). Exchange options are short correlation.
  • Symmetry: $V(S_1, S_2) + \text{forward}(S_2 - S_1) = V(S_2, S_1)$ — exchange-option put-call parity.
  • Margrabe with strike ($\max(S_1 - S_2 - K, 0)$) breaks the trick (three assets in the payoff) — that is the spread-option problem (Kirk's approximation).

4. Applications

  • Stock-for-stock M&A: an exchange offer is literally this payoff; merger-arb desks price deal optionality with Margrabe.
  • Outperformance options: $\max(S_1 - S_2, 0)$ on normalized prices = relative-performance payoffs in structured notes (see Outperformance Note section).
  • Convertible/exchangeable bonds: the exchange feature into another company's shares.
  • Best-of building block: $\max(S_1, S_2) = S_2 + \max(S_1 - S_2, 0)$ — Margrabe is one Stulz rainbow building block (next section).
  • Crack/spark spreads with zero strike: exact pricing where Kirk would approximate.

5. Worked Example

$S_1 = S_2 = 100$, $\sigma_1 = 30\%$, $\sigma_2 = 20\%$, $\rho = 0.5$, $q_1 = q_2 = 0$, $T = 1$:

$$\hat\sigma = \sqrt{0.09 + 0.04 - 2(0.5)(0.3)(0.2)} = \sqrt{0.07} = 26.5\%.$$ $$d_1 = \frac{0 + 0.035}{0.265} = 0.132, \quad d_2 = -0.132 \;\Rightarrow\; V = 100\,[N(0.132) - N(-0.132)] = 100 \times 0.105 \approx 10.5.$$

Drop correlation to $-0.5$: $\hat\sigma = 43.6\%$, $V \approx 17.3$ — a 65% price increase from correlation alone. The correlation sensitivity (cega) is the dominant risk, and the hardest input to hedge or estimate.

Key Takeaways

  • Margrabe: BS on the ratio $S_1/S_2$ with $\hat\sigma^2 = \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2$; $r$ cancels.
  • The proof is the original change-of-numeraire argument — ancestor of forward and swap measures.
  • Exchange options are short correlation; cega is the key risk.
  • Exact for zero-strike spreads; with a strike you're in Kirk-approximation territory.
  • M&A exchanges, outperformance notes and best-of payoffs all reduce to Margrabe blocks.
