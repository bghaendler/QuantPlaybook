Commodities: An Introduction

Sectors, Physical Supply Chains, Investment Motives, and Curve Term Structures

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: commodities are the only asset class you can drop on your foot. Barrels, bushels and bars have storage costs, delivery logistics and weather risk — which is why almost everyone trades the futures, not the stuff itself, and why their price curves have personalities of their own.

  • No dividends, no earnings — just supply and demand: a commodity pays you nothing for holding it (it costs you storage). Its investment case is diversification and inflation protection, not income.
  • Contango vs. backwardation is the key vocabulary: futures curve sloping up (contango) = storage costs dominate; sloping down (backwardation) = people pay a premium to have the stuff now (scarcity, convenience yield).
  • The curve shape decides your returns: a fund holding futures must "roll" expiring contracts into later ones — in contango it systematically buys high and sells low (roll cost); in backwardation the roll pays you. Long-run commodity index returns are mostly this roll, not spot moves.
  • Each sector marches to its own drum: energy follows OPEC and geopolitics, agriculture follows weather, metals follow industry and central banks (gold) — the diversification argument within the asset class is as strong as the one for it.

Motives for Commodity Allocation: Low Correlation

A primary driver for adding commodities to asset allocation models is their low correlation with traditional stocks and bonds. They react to inflation and supply shocks differently, offering powerful diversification benefits during market corrections.

1.2 Spot-Futures Arbitrage: The Pricing Model

Futures contract values are calculated through the Cost of Carry arbitrage identity:

$$ F(t, T) = S(t) e^{(r + w - c)(T - t)} $$

  • $r$: Risk-free interest rate.
  • $w$: Physical storage, warehouse, and insurance fees.
  • $c$: Convenience Yield (implied benefit of holding physical stock, e.g. preventing structural factory shutdowns due to shortages).

Electricity Asymptote: Because electrical energy cannot be cost-effectively stored at massive grid scales ($w \to \infty$), standard carry arbitrage breaks down. Price is set instantaneously by supply and demand.
