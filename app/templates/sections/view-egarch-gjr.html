EGARCH & GJR-GARCH Extensions

Asymmetric volatility: letting bad news raise volatility more than good news — the econometric answer to the leverage effect.

1. What Plain GARCH Misses

In GARCH(1,1) the variance responds to $\epsilon_{t-1}^2$ — the size of the shock only. Empirically, negative equity returns raise future volatility substantially more than positive returns of the same magnitude (the leverage effect). The news impact curve — $\sigma_t^2$ as a function of $\epsilon_{t-1}$ — should be asymmetric, steeper on the left. Two canonical fixes: GJR (threshold) and EGARCH (log-variance).

2. GJR-GARCH — Glosten, Jagannathan & Runkle (1993)

Add an indicator-activated extra ARCH term for negative shocks:

$$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \left(\alpha + \gamma\, \mathbb{1}_{\{\epsilon_{t-1} < 0\}}\right) \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\, \sigma_{t-1}^2.$$
  • Positive shock impact: $\alpha$. Negative shock impact: $\alpha + \gamma$. Leverage effect ⇔ $\gamma > 0$.
  • Typical equity estimates: $\alpha \approx 0.02$, $\gamma \approx 0.10$, $\beta \approx 0.92$ — most of the news response comes from the asymmetric term.
  • Stationarity (symmetric $z$): $\alpha + \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma + \beta < 1$; long-run variance $\bar\sigma^2 = \omega / (1 - \alpha - \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma - \beta)$.

The closely related Threshold GARCH (TGARCH/Zakoian) applies the same idea to conditional standard deviation rather than variance.

3. EGARCH — Nelson (1991)

Model the logarithm of variance, removing positivity constraints entirely:

$$\ln \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \beta \ln \sigma_{t-1}^2 + \alpha\left(|z_{t-1}| - \mathbb{E}|z_{t-1}|\right) + \gamma\, z_{t-1}, \qquad z_t = \epsilon_t/\sigma_t.$$
  • Size effect ($\alpha$): unusually large shocks of either sign raise log-variance.
  • Sign effect ($\gamma$): with $\gamma < 0$, negative shocks raise variance, positive shocks damp it.
  • No parameter constraints needed — $\ln\sigma^2$ can be any real number; stationarity requires only $|\beta| < 1$.
  • Shocks act multiplicatively on variance, so a single extreme observation can have a very large impact (a known fragility in estimation).

Engle & Ng's sign-bias tests formally check whether residual asymmetry remains after fitting; in equity data plain GARCH almost always fails them while GJR/EGARCH pass.

5. Which to Use?

  • Equity indices: asymmetric models materially improve fits (log-likelihood, BIC) and VaR backtests; GJR is the common default for its robustness.
  • FX: asymmetry is weak (no natural "down" direction) — symmetric GARCH often suffices.
  • Risk vs. pricing: for derivatives, GARCH option-pricing models (Duan; Heston-Nandi closed form) translate these dynamics into smiles with realistic negative skew.
  • Student-t or skewed-t innovations are standard companions, since even asymmetric GARCH leaves fat-tailed standardized residuals.

Key Takeaways

  • Leverage effect ⇒ asymmetric news impact: bad news moves vol more.
  • GJR adds $\gamma\,\mathbb{1}_{\{\epsilon<0\}}\epsilon^2$; leverage ⇔ $\gamma > 0$; stationarity uses $\alpha + \gamma/2 + \beta$.
  • EGARCH models $\ln\sigma_t^2$ — sign and size effects, no positivity constraints.
  • Model choice shows up directly in VaR/ES accuracy and in implied-skew generation.
