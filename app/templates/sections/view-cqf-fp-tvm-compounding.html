Compounding & Future Values

Explore the mathematical foundations of cash growth over time, from multi-frequency compounding to short-term money market conventions.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Compounding means earning interest on your interest. Money parked at a fixed rate doesn't grow in a straight line — it snowballs, because each period's interest joins the principal and starts earning too.

  • Frequency matters, but less than you'd think: compounding monthly instead of annually at the same quoted rate earns you a bit more — but the jump from monthly to continuous (infinitely often) is pennies. The values converge to a hard ceiling.
  • That ceiling is Euler's number: compound €1 at 100% "infinitely often" for a year and you get exactly €2.71828… — Euler's number $e$, not infinity. This is why $e^{rT}$ is finance's universal growth factor.
  • Time is the real superpower: over one year, simple vs. compound interest barely differ. Over 30 years they diverge enormously — the charts below make this visible.
  • Rule of 72: money doubles in roughly 72 ÷ rate% years (at 8%: ~9 years). A back-of-envelope trick bankers have used for centuries — test its accuracy in the demo below.

Discrete & Continuous Compounding

Interest can be compounded at different frequencies. The more frequent the compounding interval, the higher the Effective Annual Rate (EAR) actually achieved by the investor.

Continuous Limit Formula As compounding frequency $m \rightarrow \infty$:
FV = PV × e^(r × T)
Euler's number $e \approx 2.71828$ serves as the continuous compounding growth factor.

In plain English: doublings are the honest way to feel exponential growth. At 8%, money doubles every ~9 years — so 36 working years means four doublings: €1 becomes €16, not €4. Each doubling takes the same time but adds more euros than all previous ones combined. The Rule of 72 stays within a few percent of the exact answer for any realistic rate, which is why it has survived since Luca Pacioli (1494).

In plain English: for deposits shorter than a year, banks don't bother with exponents — interest is just rate × days ÷ basis. The catch is the basis: USD markets pretend the year has 360 days, GBP uses 365. Lending dollars at "3%" for a real 365-day year actually pays 3% × 365/360 ≈ 3.04% — the convention quietly works in the lender's favor. Always ask which day count a quoted rate uses.
