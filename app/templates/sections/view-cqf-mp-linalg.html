Linear Algebra

Vectors, Matrices, Eigenvalues, and Systems of Linear Equations

Problem 1: Solving Systems of Linear Equations via Gaussian Elimination

Solve the following system of linear equations using Gaussian elimination, representing the row operations step-by-step:

$$\begin{aligned} x + y + z &= 6 \\ 2x - y + z &= 3 \\ x + 2y - z &= 2 \end{aligned}$$

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Represent as an Augmented Matrix: $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & 2 \end{array} \right]$$
  2. Eliminate $x$ from the Second and Third Rows: Perform Row 2: $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: $$R_2 = [2 - 2(1), -1 - 2(1), 1 - 2(1) \mid 3 - 2(6)] = [0, -3, -1 \mid -9]$$ Perform Row 3: $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$: $$R_3 = [1 - 1, 2 - 1, -1 - 1 \mid 2 - 6] = [0, 1, -2 \mid -4]$$ Our matrix is now: $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \end{array} \right]$$
  3. Swap Rows to Obtain a Pivot of 1: Swap Row 2 and Row 3 ($R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3$) for easier calculation: $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & -9 \end{array} \right]$$
  4. Eliminate $y$ from the Third Row: Perform Row 3: $R_3 \to R_3 + 3R_2$: $$R_3 = [0 + 3(0), -3 + 3(1), -1 + 3(-2) \mid -9 + 3(-4)] = [0, 0, -7 \mid -21]$$ Our upper-triangular matrix is: $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & 0 & -7 & -21 \end{array} \right]$$
  5. Solve via Back-Substitution:
    • From Row 3: $-7z = -21 \implies z = 3$.
    • From Row 2: $y - 2z = -4 \implies y - 2(3) = -4 \implies y = 2$.
    • From Row 1: $x + y + z = 6 \implies x + 2 + 3 = 6 \implies x = 1$.
    The unique solution is $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$.
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why use matrices for systems of equations? In quantitative finance, we often deal with hundreds of variables simultaneously (e.g. tracking weights of 100 stocks in a portfolio). Writing out algebraic equations like $x + y + z = 6$ gets messy. An augmented matrix bundles the coefficient grid and the answer vector into a single grid that computers can solve rapidly.

2. What is Gaussian Elimination? Think of it as a systematic way of doing "elimination by addition/subtraction" that you learned in middle school, but standardized. We want to convert the matrix into an upper-triangular form—meaning we clear out the numbers in the bottom-left corner to create a staircase of zeros. This isolates the last variable ($z$), letting us solve it first.

3. What is Back-Substitution? Once we clear the bottom row to $[0, 0, -7 \mid -21]$, we know exactly what $z$ is ($z = 3$). We then work our way *backwards* up the staircase: plug $z=3$ into the second row to find $y$, and finally plug both $y=2$ and $z=3$ into the top row to find $x$. It's a domino effect!

4. How is this used in finance? This is the exact algorithm running under the hood when bootstrapping a yield curve. We have a set of cash bond prices and coupon rates (the matrix $A$) and we solve for the discount factors (the vector $\mathbf{x}$) that match the market prices (the vector $\mathbf{b}$).

Problem 2: Eigenvalue Decomposition & Diagonalization

Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the symmetric matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ and verify its orthogonal diagonalization.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Set up the Characteristic Equation: To find eigenvalues $\lambda$, solve $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3-\lambda & 2 \\ 2 & -\lambda \end{bmatrix} = 0 \implies (3-\lambda)(-\lambda) - (2)(2) = 0$$ $$\lambda^2 - 3\lambda - 4 = 0 \implies (\lambda - 4)(\lambda + 1) = 0$$ Thus, the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 4$ and $\lambda_2 = -1$.
  2. Compute the Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 4$: Solve $(A - 4I)\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{0}$: $$\begin{bmatrix} 3-4 & 2 \\ 2 & 0-4 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{0} \implies \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & -4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ Both rows simplify to $-x + 2y = 0 \implies x = 2y$. Choosing $y = 1$ gives eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Normalized: $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$.
  3. Compute the Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = -1$: Solve $(A - (-1)I)\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{0} \implies (A + I)\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{0}$: $$\begin{bmatrix} 3+1 & 2 \\ 2 & 0+1 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{0} \implies \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ Both rows simplify to $2x + y = 0 \implies y = -2x$. Choosing $x = 1$ gives eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$. Normalized: $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$.
  4. Verify Orthogonality: Because $A$ is symmetric, eigenvectors belonging to distinct eigenvalues must be orthogonal: $$\mathbf{q}_1 \cdot \mathbf{q}_2 = \left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}\right)\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\right)\left(-\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}\right) = \frac{2}{5} - \frac{2}{5} = 0$$ This confirms $\mathbf{q}_1 \perp \mathbf{q}_2$.
  5. Construct the Diagonalization: Let $Q = [\mathbf{q}_1 \mid \mathbf{q}_2] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $D = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$. Since $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^T = Q^{-1}$), we have: $$A = Q D Q^T = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$ Diagonalization is verified.
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What is an eigenvector? Usually, when you multiply a vector by a matrix, the vector gets rotated and stretched into a new direction. However, for every matrix, there are a few special directions where the vector *does not rotate at all*. It just gets stretched or shrunk along its original line. These special directions are eigenvectors, and the scaling factors are eigenvalues.

2. What is Diagonalization? Diagonalization is like choosing a new coordinate system aligned perfectly with these eigenvectors. In this new coordinate system, the complicated matrix behaves like a simple diagonal matrix, where all off-diagonal entries are zero. It is the mathematical equivalent of uncoupling a complex system of interconnected gears so we can analyze each gear individually.

3. Why is real symmetry special? When a matrix is symmetric (like a reflection across the diagonal), its eigenvectors are guaranteed to be at exact right angles (orthogonal) to each other. This is called the Spectral Theorem. In 2D, this means the transformation stretches a circle into an ellipse along two perpendicular axes.

4. How is this used in finance? This is the foundation of portfolio risk management. If we have a covariance matrix of stock returns, it is symmetric. Its eigenvalues represent the variance of uncorrelated risk factors (Principal Components). The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue represents the "Market Factor" or the direction of maximum risk, while the smaller ones represent industry or stock-specific risks. This is how Principal Component Analysis (PCA) decomposes risk in bond yield curves!

Linear Algebra is the mathematical foundation for modeling multidimensional systems. This cheat sheet consolidates key concepts: vector spaces, matrix representations, determinants, solving systems of linear equations, orthogonal operators, and eigendecomposition. In quantitative finance, these tools underpin portfolio optimization (mean-variance analysis), asset covariance structure modeling, and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for yield curve dynamics.

1. Vectors, Inner Products & Spaces

Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ represent directed quantities in $n$-dimensional space. We define the fundamental operations:

  • Dot (Inner) Product: $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v}$. Geometrically, $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\| \|\mathbf{v}\| \cos\theta$.
  • Vector Norm (Length): $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2}$.
  • Orthogonality: Two vectors are orthogonal iff their inner product is zero: $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 \iff \mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ (i.e. $\theta = 90^\circ$).
  • Vector Projection: Projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ is: $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|^2}\mathbf{v}$.

Vector Spaces & Bases: A set of vectors is linearly independent if no vector can be written as a linear combination of others. A basis of a space $V$ is a set of linearly independent vectors that span $V$. The number of vectors in the basis is the dimension of the space.

2. Matrices, Operations & Determinants

An $m \times n$ matrix $A$ acts as a linear transformation mapping vectors from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^m$:

  • Matrix Multiplication: For $C = AB$, the entry $c_{ij}$ is the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ and column $j$ of $B$: $c_{ij} = \sum_{k} a_{ik} b_{kj}$.
  • Transpose ($A^T$): Swaps rows and columns: $(A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}$. Symmetric matrices satisfy $A^T = A$. Skew-symmetric satisfy $A^T = -A$.
  • Trace ($\text{tr}(A)$): Sum of diagonal elements: $\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ii}$.
  • Determinant ($\det(A)$ or $|A|$): A scalar mapping of square matrices. For a $2\times 2$ matrix, $\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = ad - bc$.

Cofactor Expansion: For larger matrices, $\det(A) = \sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij} C_{ij}$ where the cofactor $C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} M_{ij}$ and $M_{ij}$ is the minor determinant formed by deleting row $i$ and column $j$. Geometrically, the determinant scales the hyper-volume of a unit parallelotope.

3. Systems of Linear Equations & Gaussian Elimination

A system of $m$ linear equations in $n$ variables can be written as $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$:

a₁₁x₁ + a₁₂x₂ + ... + a₁ₙxₙ = b₁
a₂₁x₁ + a₂₂x₂ + ... + a₂ₙxₙ = b₂
...
aₘ₁x₁ + aₘ₂x₂ + ... + aₘₙxₙ = bₘ

Gaussian Elimination: Performs row operations on the augmented matrix $[A | \mathbf{b}]$ to reduce it to row echelon form (REF):

  • Multiply a row by a non-zero scalar.
  • Add/subtract a multiple of one row to another.
  • Interchange two rows.

Rank and Solvability: The rank of $A$, $\text{rank}(A)$, is the number of non-zero rows in its REF. The system has:
• No solution: $\text{rank}(A) < \text{rank}([A|\mathbf{b}])$ (inconsistent).
• Unique solution: $\text{rank}(A) = \text{rank}([A|\mathbf{b}]) = n$.
• Infinitely many solutions: $\text{rank}(A) = \text{rank}([A|\mathbf{b}]) < n$ (possesses $n - \text{rank}(A)$ free variables).

4. Matrix Inverses & Orthogonal Operators

A square matrix $A$ is invertible iff its determinant is non-zero ($\det(A) \neq 0$). The inverse satisfies $A A^{-1} = A^{-1} A = I$.

Inverse Formula (Cofactor Method):

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} C^T = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \text{adj}(A)$$

where $C$ is the cofactor matrix and $C^T$ is the adjugate matrix.

Orthogonal Matrices ($Q$): A square matrix is orthogonal if its transpose equals its inverse:

$$Q^T Q = Q Q^T = I \implies Q^T = Q^{-1}$$

Properties of Orthogonal Transforms:
• Rows/columns form an orthonormal basis: $\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{q}_j = \delta_{ij}$.
• Preserves inner products and lengths: $(Q\mathbf{u}) \cdot (Q\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}$ and $\|Q\mathbf{v}\| = \|\mathbf{v}\|$.
• The determinant is always $|Q| = \pm 1$ (representing rotations ($+1$) or reflections ($-1$)).

5. Eigenvalues, Diagonalization & Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

A matrix $A$ acting on a non-zero eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$ simply scales it by eigenvalue $\lambda$:

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v} \implies (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$

Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic equation $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$. If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, it can be diagonalized:

$$A = P D P^{-1} \implies D = P^{-1} A P$$

where $D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n)$ and $P = [\mathbf{v}_1 | \dots | \mathbf{v}_n]$.

Spectral Theorem: If $A$ is a real symmetric matrix ($A = A^T$), its eigenvalues are real, and its eigenvectors can be chosen to be mutually orthogonal, meaning $P$ is an orthogonal matrix $Q$, yielding $A = Q D Q^T$.

Application: Covariance Matrices & PCA In finance, the asset covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is symmetric and positive semi-definite ($\mathbf{x}^T \Sigma \mathbf{x} \ge 0$). Eigendecomposition yields orthogonal eigenvectors representing "Principal Components" (uncorrelated risk factors).

By sorting eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \dots \ge \lambda_n$, the first few components explain the majority of portfolio variance: $$\text{Variance Explained} = \frac{\lambda_1 + \dots + \lambda_k}{\sum \lambda_i}$$ This forms the mathematical basis for yield curve factor analysis (identifying Shift, Twist, and Butterfly movements).
