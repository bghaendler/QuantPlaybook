1. Eigenvalue Decomposition

A matrix $A$ acting on an eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$ simply scales it by eigenvalue $\lambda$:

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$$

Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic polynomial:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, it admits the decomposition:

$$A = PDP^{-1}$$

where $D$ is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues and $P$ is the matrix of eigenvectors.

In quantitative finance, eigendecomposition is key for PCA and portfolio variance decomposition.

2. Matrix Inverse & Linear Systems

For the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, the solution is obtained by inverting $A$:

$$\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$$

For a 2×2 matrix, the inverse is computed via the adjoint formula:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \cdot \text{adj}(A)$$

where the determinant of $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$ is:

$$\det(A) = ad - bc$$

Solving systems of linear equations appears in bond portfolio optimization and yield curve fitting.
