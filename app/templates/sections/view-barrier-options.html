Barrier Options: Down & Out, Up & In, etc.

Path-dependent vanillas: options that are born or die when spot crosses a level — cheaper than vanillas, closed-form under Black-Scholes, and the canonical test of hedging models.

1. The Taxonomy

Eight standard contracts: {call, put} × {up, down} × {in, out}, barrier $H$, optional rebate $R$ paid if the option dies (out) or never lives (in).

  • Knock-out: vanilla unless/until $S$ touches $H$, then worthless (plus rebate). "Down-and-out call": $H < S_0$.
  • Knock-in: worthless unless $S$ touches $H$, then becomes vanilla.
  • In-out parity (same $H$, $K$, $T$, no rebate): $\text{In} + \text{Out} = \text{Vanilla}$ — price one, get the other.

Why trade them? Premium reduction: a down-and-out put costs a fraction of the vanilla put because it cancels in exactly the scenarios it would pay most. Buyers accept scenario risk for cheapness; sellers (structurers) embed them to fund headline coupons.

2. Closed Forms via the Reflection Principle

Under GBM, the joint density of $(S_T, \text{extremum})$ is known: for a Brownian motion with drift $\nu$, the reflected path argument gives

$$\mathbb{P}\left(\min_t S_t \le H,\ S_T > x\right) = \left(\frac{H}{S_0}\right)^{2\nu/\sigma^2} \mathbb{P}\left(S_T^{refl} > x\right)$$

with the reflected process started at $H^2/S_0$. Merton (1973) priced the down-and-out call; Rubinstein-Reiner (1991) completed the catalogue with six building blocks $A$–$F$ combining terms like

$$S e^{(b-r)T}\left(\frac{H}{S}\right)^{2(\mu+1)} N(\eta y) - K e^{-rT}\left(\frac{H}{S}\right)^{2\mu} N(\eta y - \eta\sigma\sqrt{T}), \qquad \mu = \frac{b - \sigma^2/2}{\sigma^2},$$

assembled per contract type. E.g. the down-and-out call ($H \le K$): $C_{DO} = C_{BS} - C_{DI}$ where the down-and-in part is the reflection term. Double barriers (Ikeda-Kunitomo) need infinite image-charge series; discrete monitoring uses the Broadie-Glasserman-Kou shift $H \to He^{\pm0.5826\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}$ to correct continuous formulas.

3. The Hedging Problem: Barriers Are Where Models Fight

  • Pin risk at $H$: a knock-out's value jumps to zero across the barrier; delta is discontinuous and gamma explodes near $H$, especially close to expiry. A reverse knock-out (barrier in-the-money, e.g. up-and-out call with $H > K$) embeds a digital of size $(H - K)$ at the barrier — the worst case.
  • Vega sign flips: more vol means more payoff and more knock-out probability; net vega changes sign with spot — single-vol Black-Scholes pricing is structurally wrong.
  • Model dependence: barrier prices differ markedly between local vol, stochastic vol, and SLV calibrated to the same vanilla smile — the barrier reads the smile dynamics, not just today's smile. FX desks calibrate SLV mixtures to liquid one-touches for exactly this reason.
  • Static replication: Derman-Ergener-Kani / Carr-Chou construct portfolios of vanillas matching the barrier value along $H$ — robust to some model risk, exact only under flat-vol assumptions.

4. Worked Intuition: Down-and-Out Put

Vanilla put $K = 100$, knock-out $H = 70$, $S_0 = 100$. The vanilla pays most in crashes — precisely the paths that hit 70 first. So the DO put keeps only the "mild decline" payoffs ($70 < S_T < 100$ without ever touching 70) and might cost 25–40% of the vanilla. Its in-counterpart, the down-and-in put (activated by the crash), holds the remaining 60–75% — and is exactly the option embedded short in barrier reverse convertibles: the investor has sold crash insurance that switches on at the worst moment.

5. Practice Notes

  • Monitoring: continuous vs. daily-close vs. at-expiry barriers differ materially (BGK correction); contract wording on the fixing source matters.
  • Barrier shifts: sellers book an internal shifted barrier (hedge as if $H$ were closer) to buy room for hedging slippage — a quantified safety margin charged in the price.
  • Manipulation risk around popular FX barriers is real; large knock-outs attract spot defense/attack flows.
  • Rebates decompose as one-touch digitals — price with the Rubinstein-Reiner touch formulas.

Key Takeaways

  • In + Out = Vanilla; barrier options are cheap because they cancel in their own worst scenarios.
  • GBM closed forms come from the reflection principle with power-of-$(H/S)$ image terms.
  • Near the barrier: discontinuous delta, exploding gamma, sign-flipping vega — pin risk is the defining hazard.
  • Prices are smile-dynamics-dependent: same vanillas, different barrier values across LV/SV/SLV.
  • Discrete monitoring: shift the barrier by $e^{0.5826\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}$ (Broadie-Glasserman-Kou).
