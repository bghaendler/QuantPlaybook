Coin-Tossing & Random Walk Limits

From the simplest possible random experiment to Brownian motion: how scaling a coin-tossing game correctly produces the continuum model of finance.

1. The Symmetric Random Walk

Let $\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2, \dots$ be i.i.d. fair coin tosses with $\mathbb{P}(\epsilon_i = +1) = \mathbb{P}(\epsilon_i = -1) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. The partial sum

$$M_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \epsilon_i$$

is the symmetric random walk. Its three structural properties are inherited by everything built on top of it:

  • Independent increments: $M_{n_2} - M_{n_1}$ and $M_{n_4} - M_{n_3}$ are independent for $n_1 \le n_2 \le n_3 \le n_4$.
  • Martingale: $\mathbb{E}[M_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = M_n$ — fair game, no predictable profit.
  • Variance grows linearly: $\operatorname{Var}(M_n) = n$, so typical displacement is $\sqrt{n}$, not $n$.

2. Quadratic Variation: The Crucial Observation

The quadratic variation of the walk up to time $n$ is

$$[M]_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (M_i - M_{i-1})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \epsilon_i^2 = n,$$

which is deterministic — true path by path, not just on average. This is the discrete ancestor of $dW^2 = dt$, the identity that generates the Itô correction term and ultimately the $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 V_{SS}$ term of the Black-Scholes equation.

3. The Scaled Walk & Donsker's Limit

To get a continuous-time limit, speed up time and shrink the step size in the right ratio. Define the scaled walk

$$W^{(n)}(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\, M_{\lfloor nt \rfloor}.$$

Each unit of time now contains $n$ tosses of size $1/\sqrt{n}$. Then $\operatorname{Var}(W^{(n)}(t)) \to t$, and by the Central Limit Theorem $W^{(n)}(t) \Rightarrow \mathcal{N}(0, t)$. Donsker's invariance principle upgrades this pointwise convergence to convergence of the whole path:

$$W^{(n)} \xrightarrow{\;d\;} W \quad \text{(Brownian motion), as } n \to \infty.$$

The scaling $\Delta x = \sqrt{\Delta t}$ is forced: scaling steps like $\Delta t$ gives a degenerate (constant) limit; scaling slower than $\sqrt{\Delta t}$ explodes. Diffusion lives precisely on the square-root frontier.

5. From Coin Tossing to Asset Prices

A binomial asset model is a coin-tossing game in disguise: over $\Delta t$, the log-price moves $\pm\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$ with risk-neutral probabilities tilted to give drift $(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t$. As $\Delta t \to 0$ the model converges to GBM:

$$\ln S_t = \ln S_0 + \left(r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t,$$

which is why Cox-Ross-Rubinstein binomial prices converge to Black-Scholes prices. Every binomial tree pricer is a coin-tossing limit argument executed numerically.

Key Takeaways

  • The symmetric walk has independent increments, the martingale property, and variance $n$.
  • Quadratic variation $[M]_n = n$ holds path-by-path — the origin of $dW^2 = dt$.
  • Brownian motion is the Donsker limit of walks scaled by $\Delta x = \sqrt{\Delta t}$.
  • Reflection-principle results for the walk become barrier/lookback option formulas.
  • Binomial trees are coin-tossing approximations whose prices converge to Black-Scholes.
