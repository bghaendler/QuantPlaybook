2.1 Bootstrapping Example

Building up the term structure of interest rates from liquid market instruments (LIBOR, Futures, and Swaps) step-by-step.

The Bootstrapping Principle

Bootstrapping is the standard industry method used by trading desks to construct a continuous discount curve $P(t_0, T)$ from discrete market quotes. The core idea is to build the term structure sequentially from the shortest maturities to the longest maturities, using each solved discount factor to price the next, longer-dated instrument.

On our spot date $t_0 = \text{1 October 2012}$ under the Actual/360 day-count convention, we solve for three distinct curve instruments:

  • Short-End: Simple LIBOR deposits are used for maturities up to 3 months.
  • Mid-Curve: Eurodollar Futures contracts act as proxies for forward rates between 3 months and 1.5 years.
  • Long-End: Spot-starting interest rate swaps (up to 30 years) complete the curve.

Linear Interpolation of Simple Rates

When maturities overlap (e.g., the first futures reset $T_1$ falls between the 1-month and 3-month LIBOR dates $S_2$ and $S_3$), we linearly interpolate simple deposit rates to find the boundary discount factor:

$$L(t_0, T_1) = q L(t_0, S_2) + (1-q) L(t_0, S_3)$$ $$q = \frac{\delta(T_1, S_3)}{\delta(S_2, S_3)}$$

This same simple rate interpolation is used to bridge the gap between Eurodollar futures and the first annual swap payment $U_1$. For subsequent swap cash flows, we apply the inverted swap rate formula iteratively.
