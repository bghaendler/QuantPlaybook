Brownian Motion & Itô Isometry

The standard Brownian Motion $W(t)$ is the mathematical foundation for continuous continuous-time random processes. A process is a standard Brownian motion if:

  • $W(0) = 0$ and $t \mapsto W(t)$ is almost surely continuous.
  • Independent Increments: $W(t) - W(s)$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_s$ for all $t > s \ge 0$.
  • Normal Increments: $W(t) - W(s) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t - s)$.

Unlike ordinary calculus curves which are smooth, Brownian paths are extremely jagged. They have infinite total variation but a finite, deterministic Quadratic Variation $[W]_t = t$. This is defined as the limit of squared increments over partitions:

$$ [W]_t = \lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^n (W(t_i) - W(t_{i-1}))^2 = t \quad \text{almost surely} $$

Theorem (Itô Isometry): For any square-integrable adapted process $\sigma(t)$, the stochastic integral $I(t) = \int_0^t \sigma(s) dW(s)$ is a martingale and satisfies:

$$ \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \int_0^t \sigma(s) dW(s) \right)^2 \right] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_0^t \sigma(s)^2 ds \right] $$

If $\sigma(s)$ is deterministic, the integral $I(t)$ is normally distributed with mean $0$ and variance $\int_0^t \sigma(s)^2 ds$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Brownian motion is the mathematical way to describe pure random jitter. Because it's so jagged, you can't integrate it like regular smooth equations.

  • Infinite Jaggedness: If you zoom in on a Brownian path, it looks just as jagged as it did from far away. It travels an infinite distance, but because it constantly backtracks, it only wanders a distance that scales with $\sqrt{t}$ on average.
  • Quadratic Variation ($[W]_t = t$): If you square the tiny changes and sum them up, the randomness cancels out perfectly and you get exactly the time elapsed $t$! This is why $dW^2 = dt$ in stochastic calculus.
  • Itô Isometry: This is a powerful variance-conservation law. It says that the variance (risk) of your accumulated trading profits is exactly the integral of the squared sizing of your bets ($\sigma(s)^2$) over time.
  • Example: If you trade with constant volatility $\sigma$, the variance of your portfolio grows exactly as $\sigma^2 t$. If your trading volatility increases linearly, the risk scales as $\int_0^t (as)^2 ds = \frac{1}{3}a^2 t^3$.

Practice Exercise: Calculating Integral Variances

Compute the variance and the probability distribution of the stochastic integral $I(t) = \int_0^t s^2 dW(s)$ at time $t = 2$. What is the probability that $I(2) \ge 3$?

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution

To find the distribution of $I(t) = \int_0^t s^2 dW(s)$, we apply Itô Isometry:

  1. Since the integrand $\sigma(s) = s^2$ is deterministic, the stochastic integral is normally distributed:
    $$I(t) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \operatorname{Var}(I(t))\right)$$
  2. Apply Itô Isometry to calculate the variance:
    $$\operatorname{Var}(I(t)) = \mathbb{E}[I(t)^2] = \int_0^t (s^2)^2 ds = \int_0^t s^4 ds = \left[ \frac{s^5}{5} \right]_0^t = \frac{t^5}{5}$$
  3. Evaluate the variance at $t = 2$:
    $$\operatorname{Var}(I(2)) = \frac{2^5}{5} = \frac{32}{5} = 6.4$$
    The standard deviation is $\sqrt{6.4} \approx 2.53$. So $I(2) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 6.4)$.
  4. We want to find $P(I(2) \ge 3)$. Standardize the variable:
    $$Z = \frac{3 - 0}{\sqrt{6.4}} = \frac{3}{2.5298} \approx 1.186$$
  5. Use standard normal distribution properties:
    $$P(Z \ge 1.19) = 1 - \Phi(1.19) = 1 - 0.8830 = 0.1170$$

Answer: The variance is exactly $6.4$. The probability that $I(2) \ge 3$ is approximately **$11.70\%$**.
