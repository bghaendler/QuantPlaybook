4. Change of Measure & Girsanov Theorem

Change of Measure & Girsanov's Theorem

In quantitative finance, we often need to switch between the Real-World Probability Measure $\mathbb{P}$ (used for forecasting and historical analysis) and the Risk-Neutral Probability Measure $\mathbb{Q}$ (used for derivative pricing).

Girsanov's Theorem states that we can change the drift of a Brownian motion by changing the probability measure. Given a "drift shift" or market price of risk process $\lambda(t)$ adapted to $\mathcal{F}_t$, we define a new process:

$$ dW^*(t) = dW(t) + \lambda(t) dt $$

This process $W^*(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion under the new probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$. The two measures are equivalent (they agree on what is possible vs. impossible), and they are linked by the Radon-Nikodym derivative density process $\zeta(t)$:

Radon-Nikodym Derivative ($\zeta(t)$):

$$ \zeta(t) = \left. \frac{d\mathbb{Q}}{d\mathbb{P}} \right|_{\mathcal{F}_t} = \exp\left( -\int_0^t \lambda(s) dW(s) - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t \lambda(s)^2 ds \right) $$

By definition, $\zeta(t)$ is a stochastic exponential $\mathcal{E}\left( -\int \lambda dW \right)$, meaning it is a positive martingale under $\mathbb{P}$ with $\mathbb{E}[\zeta(t)] = 1$ for all $t$.

To ensure that $\zeta(t)$ is a true martingale (and doesn't collapse to a strict local martingale), we require Novikov's Condition: $\mathbb{E}\left[\exp\left(\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T \lambda(t)^2 dt\right)\right] < \infty$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Imagine you are playing a rigged coin toss game where heads comes up 60% of the time. Girsanov's theorem is like putting on "probability glasses" that re-weight the odds so the coin appears perfectly fair (50/50). It doesn't change the physical path of the coin; it just changes how we *measure* its likelihood.

  • Measure Change: We change the drift of our paths by changing the likelihood weights of those paths. To make an upward-drifting stock look flat, we weight the downward paths higher and the upward paths lower.
  • Radon-Nikodym Derivative ($\zeta$): This is the exact weighting factor. If a path goes way up, $\zeta$ becomes very small (discounting its probability). If it goes way down, $\zeta$ becomes very large.
  • $\zeta(t)$ Martingale Property: The average weighting factor must stay exactly 1.0. Some paths get weighted 10x higher, others 10x lower, but on average, the total probability space weights sum to 1.
  • Example: A stock has a real drift of 12%. By shifting the measure to $\mathbb{Q}$ where the drift is 4% (risk-free), we can compute expectations easily without having to estimate investors' risk preferences.

Practice Exercise: Finding Radon-Nikodym Martingales

Let $\lambda$ be a constant. Find the expectation $\mathbb{E}[e^{-\lambda W(t)}]$ under the real-world measure $\mathbb{P}$ where $W(t)$ is standard Brownian motion, and use this to prove that $\mathbb{E}[\zeta(t)] = 1$ for the Radon-Nikodym density process.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution
  1. Since $W(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t)$, the random variable $-\lambda W(t)$ is normally distributed: $-\lambda W(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^2 t)$.
  2. Recall that for a normal random variable $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_y, \sigma_y^2)$, its moment generating function is:
    $$\mathbb{E}[e^Y] = \exp\left( \mu_y + \frac{1}{2} \sigma_y^2 \right)$$
  3. Apply this to $Y = -\lambda W(t)$ with $\mu_y = 0$ and $\sigma_y^2 = \lambda^2 t$:
    $$\mathbb{E}[e^{-\lambda W(t)}] = \exp\left( 0 + \frac{1}{2} \lambda^2 t \right) = e^{\frac{1}{2} \lambda^2 t}$$
  4. Now write down the Radon-Nikodym density process for a constant shift $\lambda$:
    $$\zeta(t) = e^{-\lambda W(t) - \frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t} = e^{-\lambda W(t)} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t}$$
  5. Take the expectation. Since $e^{-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t}$ is deterministic, it factors out:
    $$\mathbb{E}[\zeta(t)] = \mathbb{E}[e^{-\lambda W(t)} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t}] = e^{-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t} \mathbb{E}[e^{-\lambda W(t)}]$$
  6. Substitute the expectation from Step 3:
    $$\mathbb{E}[\zeta(t)] = e^{-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t} \cdot e^{\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 t} = e^0 = 1$$

Conclusion: The expectation of the Radon-Nikodym derivative is exactly $1.0$ at all times. This conservation of average probability weights confirms that $\zeta(t)$ is a valid density process and a martingale.
