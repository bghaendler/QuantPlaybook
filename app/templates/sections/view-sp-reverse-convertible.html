Classic Reverse Convertible (ZCB + Short Put)

The yield machine: a bond whose fat coupon is the premium of a put the investor has sold — equity downside dressed as fixed income.

1. Structure & Payoff

Term typically 6–18 months, underlying a single stock or index, strike $K$ (usually 100% of initial spot). The investor receives a high fixed coupon $c$ unconditionally, and at maturity:

$$V_T = \begin{cases} N(1 + c), & S_T \ge K \\[4pt] N\frac{S_T}{K} + Nc \;\;\text{(physical: shares worth } S_T\text{)}, & S_T < K \end{cases}$$

Decomposition: long ZCB + short ATM put (cash- or physically-settled):

$$\text{RC} = N e^{-yT}(1 + c)\big|_{\text{bond leg}} - p(S_0, K, T).$$

The coupon is funding interest plus the put premium: with 1y rates at 3% and a 1y ATM put on a 30-vol stock at ~10%, the headline coupon is ~13% — none of it is alpha; all of it is risk premium.

2. The Risk Asymmetry

  • Upside capped at the coupon; downside is equity-like below the strike (minus the coupon cushion). Breakeven at $S_T = K(1 - c)$ approximately.
  • The investor's position is the textbook covered-call-equivalent profile (short put = long stock + short call, by parity) — they own the stock's crashes without its rallies.
  • Issuers select high-vol underlyings for fat coupons; the buyer is systematically short the most expensive puts on the board, often single names with earnings/event risk.
  • Behavioral hazard: coupon framing ("13% yield") obscures that expected return ≈ bond yield once the put's expected loss is netted — the volatility risk premium is the only systematic edge, and it accrues to diversified, sized sellers, not concentrated retail positions.

3. Pricing & Greeks (Desk View)

Issuer hedges by buying the put back in the market (or warehousing short-put risk): the note's fair value = bond − put, with the margin between fair value and the 100 issue price as P&L. Investor-side Greeks (long note): short put ⇒ positive delta, short gamma, short vega, long theta. Marks fall fast on a sell-off (delta + vega both hurt), recover slowly — exactly the "down the elevator" profile. Physical settlement adds a delivery option worth a few basis points to the issuer (deliver the worst into the close) and ensures the investor's loss arrives as a depreciated stock position, not cash.

4. Variations

  • Discount certificate: same economics packaged as buying the stock at a discount with capped upside (zero coupon variant).
  • Strike below spot (e.g. 90%): smaller coupon, an OTM put sold instead — first step toward the barrier version.
  • Barrier Reverse Convertible: replace the vanilla short put with a down-and-in put — coupon nearly as high, loss only if a barrier (e.g. 60–70%) is breached. The dominant retail structure; next section.
  • Worst-of RC: short put on the worst of 2–3 names — correlation-cheapened, dispersion-loaded.

Key Takeaways

  • Reverse convertible = ZCB + short ATM put; the coupon is interest + put premium, fully explained.
  • Risk profile: capped upside, equity downside — short vol, short gamma, long theta.
  • High coupons track high implied vol (and issuer name selection), not expected return.
  • Physical settlement and underlying choice quietly favor the issuer; read them as option terms.
  • The barrier and worst-of variants are the same trade with cheaper (riskier) puts sold.
