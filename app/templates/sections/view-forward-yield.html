Continuously Compounded Forward Rate (Forward Yield)

Reading Module covering the mathematical formulation of continuously compounded forward rates, its limit behaviors, and numerical comparisons.

Definition and Core Equation

In analogy to the continuously compounded spot rate (yield) $y(t, T)$, we define the continuously compounded forward rate (forward yield) with reference period $[T_0, T_1]$ prevailing at time $t \le T_0$ as:

$$ Y(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0} \log \left( \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} \right) $$

This formulation represents the continuously compounded annualized rate of return that can be locked in today at date $t$ for an investment spanning the future time interval $[T_0, T_1]$.

Spot Convergence ($t = T_0$)

When the evaluation calendar date $t$ reaches the start of the reference loan period $T_0$, the continuously compounded forward rate converges exactly to the spot yield over $[T_0, T_1]$:

$$ Y(T_0, T_0, T_1) = y(T_0, T_1) $$

Proof / Intuition: Substituting $t = T_0$ into the definition and noting that the zero-coupon bond price at maturity is exactly 1 ($P(T_0, T_0) = 1$):

$$ Y(T_0, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{T_1 - T_0} \log \left( \frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)} \right) = y(T_0, T_1) $$

Instantaneous Forward Limit ($\delta \to 0$)

For an infinitesimally small reference period, as $T_1 \to T_0 = T$, the continuously compounded forward rate converges to the standard instantaneous forward rate with maturity $T$:

$$ \lim_{T_1 \to T_0 = T} Y(t, T_0, T_1) = f(t, T) $$

Step-by-Step Derivation:

Let $\delta = T_1 - T_0$. Re-expressing the yield in limit terms:

$$ \lim_{\delta \to 0} \frac{\log P(t, T_0) - \log P(t, T_0 + \delta)}{\delta} $$

This is the exact limit definition of the negative first partial derivative of the logarithm of bond prices with respect to maturity: $-\frac{\partial \ln P(t, T)}{\partial T} = f(t, T)$.

Relationship: Simple Forward Rate vs. Forward Yield

Just as simple spot rates are higher than their continuously compounded yield counterparts, the simple forward rate $F(t, T_0, T_1)$ is always strictly higher than the continuously compounded forward rate (forward yield) $Y(t, T_0, T_1)$ for any positive interest rate environment:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) > Y(t, T_0, T_1) $$

Proof / Algebra:

Recall the zero-coupon bond pricing formulations under both compounding conventions:

Simple Forward Rate: $$ \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} = 1 + \delta F(t, T_0, T_1) $$
Continuously Compounded Forward: $$ \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} = e^{\delta Y(t, T_0, T_1)} $$

Equating both bond ratios yields the exact transcendental conversion relation:

$$ 1 + \delta F(t, T_0, T_1) = e^{\delta Y(t, T_0, T_1)} \implies Y(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{\log\bigl(1 + \delta F(t, T_0, T_1)\bigr)}{\delta} $$

Since $\log(1 + x) < x$ for all $x > 0$, substituting $x = \delta F$ directly proves that $Y(t, T_0, T_1) < F(t, T_0, T_1)$ strictly holds.
