Advanced Risk Management

Classic Risk Categorizations: Market, Credit, Liquidity, Operational

The four canonical risk types: definitions, measurement paradigms, capital treatment — and the interactions between them that define real crises.

1. Market Risk

Loss from moves in prices and rates (equity, IR, FX, commodity, vol). Measured by sensitivities (delta/DV01/vega/CS01), VaR/ES on a 1–10 day horizon, plus stress tests. Capital: FRTB's ES₉₇.₅ with liquidity horizons by risk factor, or the standardized sensitivities-based approach. Character: high-frequency data, near-continuous P&L feedback — the best-measured of the four, hence the temptation to over-rely on it (Part 02's matrix).

2. Credit Risk

Loss from obligor default or deterioration. The canonical decomposition:

$$\mathbb{E}[L] = PD \times LGD \times EAD,$$

with portfolio tails driven by default correlation (the asymptotic single-factor Gaussian model behind Basel's IRB capital formula — conditional PD $\;N\!\big(\frac{N^{-1}(PD) + \sqrt{\rho}\,z}{\sqrt{1-\rho}}\big)$ at the 99.9th systematic percentile). Two measurement cultures: structural (Merton-style, equity-implied — see the credit course) and reduced-form (intensities from CDS spreads). Character: skewed, lumpy losses; long horizons (1y+); scarce default data ⇒ heavy model risk in correlations (the 2008 lesson via CDO copulas).

3. Liquidity Risk (Two Distinct Animals)

  • Market liquidity risk: cost/impossibility of exiting positions — bid-ask widening, depth evaporation (the entire HFT impact stack); measured by liquidation-cost-adjusted VaR, time-to-liquidate buckets, FRTB liquidity horizons.
  • Funding liquidity risk: inability to roll obligations — margin spirals, deposit runs, repo haircut jumps; measured by LCR/NSFR ratios, cash-flow ladders, survival horizons under stressed runoff.
  • The two interact viciously: falling prices ⇒ margin calls (funding) ⇒ forced sales (market) ⇒ falling prices — Brunnermeier-Pedersen liquidity spirals; LTCM and 2008 are this loop, not standalone categories.

4. Operational Risk (and the Boundary Map)

Loss from failed people, processes, systems, external events (fraud, cyber, legal, model error — Part 01 lives here taxonomically). Measured by loss-event databases, scenario workshops, KRIs; capital via the Standardized Measurement Approach (business-indicator based — the AMA's internal models were retired for unverifiability). Character: extreme skew — decades of small losses punctuated by firm-threatening singles (Société Générale's €4.9bn, BCCI-class legal events); EVT-style severity tails (Part 03) apply.

Boundary cases are where losses hide: a mis-marked book (op risk) discovered in a sell-off (market) that triggers collateral calls (liquidity) on a downgraded counterparty (credit). Categorization allocates ownership — but stress design must cross the boundaries deliberately.

Key Takeaways

  • Market: well-measured, sensitivity + ES paradigm. Credit: PD×LGD×EAD with correlation-driven tails (Basel IRB's single-factor percentile).
  • Liquidity splits into market vs. funding — and their spiral interaction is the historical crisis engine.
  • Operational risk is severity-tail dominated; loss data + scenarios + SMA capital, with model risk as a member.
  • Categories assign ownership and method; real events traverse all four — design stresses across the boundaries.

Plain English Notes

1. Market Risk

The Big Picture: Risk management quantifies potential portfolio losses and structural regulations, accounting for fat tails and correlation breakdowns.

  • Value-at-Risk (VaR) is the maximum loss expected under normal market conditions at a given confidence level.
  • Expected Shortfall calculates the average loss in extreme scenarios, correcting VaR's failure to measure tail severity.

2. Credit Risk

The Big Picture: Risk management quantifies potential portfolio losses and structural regulations, accounting for fat tails and correlation breakdowns.

  • The bid-ask spread is the transaction cost or tax paid to execute a trade immediately.
  • Basel regulations dictate the size of the capital buffers banks must hold to survive extreme shocks.

3. Liquidity Risk (Two Distinct Animals)

  • Value-at-Risk (VaR) is the maximum loss expected under normal market conditions at a given confidence level.
  • Expected Shortfall calculates the average loss in extreme scenarios, correcting VaR's failure to measure tail severity.

4. Operational Risk (and the Boundary Map)

The Big Picture: Risk management quantifies potential portfolio losses and structural regulations, accounting for fat tails and correlation breakdowns.

  • Value-at-Risk (VaR) is the maximum loss expected under normal market conditions at a given confidence level.
  • Fat tails mean extreme market crashes happen much more frequently than standard bell curves predict.
