Guest Lecture

A Behavioral Economics Approach to AI Safety

What decades of human-behavior research teaches us about aligning AI systems — and what quant finance, the original "incentives gone wrong" laboratory, contributes to the conversation.

1. The Alignment Problem Through a Behavioral Lens

  • Specification gaming = Goodhart's law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." An AI optimizing a proxy reward exploits it exactly as a trader games a risk limit (ARM Part 07's VaR-gaming) — the same failure, different agent.
  • Reward misspecification mirrors principal-agent problems: the designer (principal) can't fully specify intent; the optimizer (agent) maximizes the letter, not the spirit.
  • Bounded rationality: humans satisfice under cognitive limits; AI systems have their own bounded objectives and blind spots — neither is the idealized rational agent of classical theory.

2. Lessons Finance Learned the Hard Way

  • Metrics get gamed: VaR shaping (sell tail risk just beyond the quantile), ratings arbitrage (2008 CDOs), benchmark-hugging — every quantitative control invites optimization against it. AI reward functions will be gamed the same way.
  • Tail risks dominate: systems optimized for normal-times performance fail catastrophically in the tail (ARM Part 06) — alignment must be evaluated on rare, high-stakes cases, not average behavior.
  • Feedback loops & reflexivity: models that act change their environment (portfolio insurance 1987, flash crashes) — deployed AI shapes the world it learns from.
  • Crowding/herding: homogeneous strategies amplify shocks — diverse, uncorrelated approaches are more robust (a portfolio-theory insight for AI ecosystems).

3. Behavioral Tools for Safer Systems

  • Robustness over point-optimization: as fractional Kelly (RSI Part 03) sizes down for estimation error, safe AI should optimize conservatively against objective uncertainty rather than maximizing a possibly-wrong reward.
  • Circuit breakers & limits: finance's hard backstops (notional caps, kill switches) — model-independent constraints that hold when the optimizer is wrong.
  • Three lines of defense (ARM Part 02): independent oversight of AI systems, not self-certification — the governance structure transfers.
  • Reverse stress testing: start from "what catastrophic behavior must never happen?" and search for inputs that cause it — applied to AI as red-teaming.

Key Takeaways

  • Specification gaming is Goodhart's law; reward misspecification is the principal-agent problem — alignment failures have behavioral-economics analogues.
  • Finance's lessons transfer: metrics get gamed, tails dominate, feedback loops bite, crowding amplifies.
  • Safety tools mirror risk tools: robust (not point) optimization, hard limits, independent oversight, reverse stress testing.
  • Quant finance is a century-long experiment in optimizers gaming objectives — a rich source of priors for AI safety.
