1.14.1 The Central Limit Theorem

Let $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables, each with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$:

$$Z_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - n\mu}{\sigma \sqrt{n}} \ \xrightarrow{d} \ N(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Underlying Concept: Regardless of the base distribution's shape (even if highly skewed, uniform, or binary coin flips), the sample mean $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$ converges to a standard Gaussian normal distribution curve as $n$ increases!

The sample mean's theoretical variance scales down systematically: $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$.
