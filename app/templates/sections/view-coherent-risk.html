Coherent Risk Measures & Expected Shortfall

What properties should a risk number satisfy? The axiomatic framework of Artzner et al., why VaR fails it, and why Expected Shortfall became the regulatory standard.

1. The Coherence Axioms (Artzner, Delbaen, Eber, Heath 1999)

A risk measure $\rho(X)$ on portfolio loss/value $X$ is coherent if it satisfies four axioms:

AxiomStatementMeaning
Monotonicity$X_1 \le X_2 \Rightarrow \rho(X_1) \ge \rho(X_2)$Better payoffs need less capital
Translation invariance$\rho(X + c) = \rho(X) - c$Adding cash reduces risk one-for-one
Positive homogeneity$\rho(\lambda X) = \lambda \rho(X)$, $\lambda \ge 0$Risk scales with position size
Subadditivity$\rho(X_1 + X_2) \le \rho(X_1) + \rho(X_2)$Diversification never adds risk

2. Why VaR Is Not Coherent

Value-at-Risk at level $\alpha$ is a quantile of the loss distribution $L$:

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha(L) = \inf\{\ell : \mathbb{P}(L \le \ell) \ge \alpha\}.$$

VaR satisfies the first three axioms but fails subadditivity for non-elliptical distributions. Classic counterexample: two independent defaultable bonds, each defaulting with probability 4% and losing 100 (else 0). At 95%:

$$\text{VaR}_{95\%}(\text{each bond}) = 0, \qquad \text{VaR}_{95\%}(\text{both}) > 0,$$

because the probability that at least one defaults is $1 - 0.96^2 \approx 7.8\% > 5\%$. Concentrating in one bond looks safer than diversifying — a perverse incentive. VaR also says nothing about how bad the tail is beyond the quantile, and tail sizes can be manipulated without moving the quantile.

3. Expected Shortfall (CVaR)

Expected Shortfall averages the tail beyond VaR:

$$\text{ES}_\alpha(L) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\int_\alpha^1 \text{VaR}_u(L)\, du = \mathbb{E}\left[L \mid L \ge \text{VaR}_\alpha(L)\right] \;\;(\text{continuous } L).$$

ES is coherent (subadditive for all distributions), tail-sensitive, and convex — which also makes ES-constrained portfolio optimization a tractable convex program (Rockafellar & Uryasev's linearization). For a normal loss $L \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha = \mu + \sigma\, z_\alpha, \qquad \text{ES}_\alpha = \mu + \sigma\, \frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1-\alpha},$$

e.g. at 97.5%: $z_\alpha = 1.96$, $\text{ES} = \mu + 2.34\,\sigma$. Under normality, $\text{ES}_{97.5\%} \approx \text{VaR}_{99\%}$ — exactly why Basel chose 97.5% ES to replace 99% VaR.

4. Regulatory Shift: FRTB

The Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (Basel III/IV market-risk framework) replaced 99% 10-day VaR with 97.5% liquidity-adjusted ES, computed over stressed periods with liquidity horizons from 10 to 120 days by risk factor. Motivations: tail-blindness of VaR in 2008, non-subadditivity, and gaming of quantile-based capital. Trade-off: ES estimates need more tail data (higher estimation variance) and ES is not elicitable, complicating direct backtesting — in practice the VaR levels underlying ES are backtested instead.

5. Spectral & Distortion Generalizations

ES is one member of the family of spectral risk measures $\rho_\phi(L) = \int_0^1 \phi(u)\,\text{VaR}_u(L)\,du$ with $\phi$ a non-decreasing weight function integrating to 1 — coherence is equivalent to weighting worse outcomes (weakly) more. ES uses a flat weight on the worst $(1-\alpha)$ tail; risk-averse users can choose weights increasing toward the worst outcomes. This unifies coherent measures with distortion measures from insurance pricing.

Key Takeaways

  • Coherence = monotonicity + translation invariance + homogeneity + subadditivity.
  • VaR violates subadditivity → can penalize diversification and ignores tail severity.
  • $\text{ES}_\alpha$ = average loss beyond VaR; coherent, convex, optimization-friendly.
  • Normal case: $\text{ES}_\alpha = \mu + \sigma\phi(z_\alpha)/(1-\alpha)$; ES@97.5% ≈ VaR@99%.
  • FRTB moved regulatory capital from VaR to stressed, liquidity-adjusted ES.
