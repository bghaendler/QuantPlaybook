Government Bills, Notes & Bonds

Deconstruct sovereign debt markets: from zero-coupon short-term T-bills traded at a discount, to coupon-bearing medium-term Treasury notes and long-term bonds.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: government securities are IOUs from the state — the closest thing finance has to a "risk-free" promise, which is why their yields are the reference point every other asset is priced against.

  • Bills don't pay interest — they're sold cheap: a T-bill pays exactly 100 at maturity; you earn the gap between the discounted purchase price and 100. The simulator below walks one through auction and resale.
  • Notes and bonds pay coupons: fixed cash every six months plus 100 at the end. Price is just all those cash flows discounted (the Discounting section's machinery, applied).
  • Price and yield are a seesaw: when market yields rise, existing bonds' fixed coupons look stingy, so their prices fall — and vice versa. The chart below shows the curve, and its bend (convexity) matters too.
  • Premium / par / discount decoded: coupon > yield → price above 100 (premium); coupon = yield → exactly 100 (par); coupon < yield → below 100 (discount). Try it in the pricer.

Visualization: The Price-Yield Seesaw

The price of the bond configured above, plotted across yields from 0% to 12%. The dot marks your current YTM; the dashed crosshair marks par. The curve bows — that bend is convexity, and longer maturities bow harder.

In plain English: switch the maturity selector above from 1 year to 10 years and watch the curve steepen — the same 1% yield move barely dents a 1-year note but repriced a 10-year bond by ~8%. That sensitivity is duration; the curve's bow working in your favor (gaining more on yield drops than losing on rises) is convexity. 2022's bond rout was this chart, lived.
