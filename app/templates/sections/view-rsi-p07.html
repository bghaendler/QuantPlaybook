Finite vs. Infinite Horizon Optimization Practicality

When the elegant infinite-horizon policy is the right tool and when the horizon genuinely matters: turnpike theorems, terminal effects, and the implementation verdict.

2. The Turnpike Phenomenon

The key practical fact: for most of a long horizon, the finite-horizon policy sits very close to the infinite-horizon stationary policy — $Q(t) \to Q_\infty$ (the algebraic-Riccati solution) rapidly as $T - t$ grows. The time-dependence concentrates in the final stretch:

  • Far from maturity: $\pi^*(t,x) \approx \pi^*_\infty(x)$ — the "turnpike." A 30-year investor behaves like an infinite-horizon one for the first ~25 years.
  • Near maturity: terminal effects kick in — de-risking toward the payout, liability-matching tightening (Part 05's glide path), consumption draw-down adjustments.
  • Convergence speed ≈ the mean-reversion/discount rate: faster-reverting factors ⇒ shorter terminal zone. This is why the elegant stationary policy is so widely used — it's correct for the bulk of any long mandate.

3. When the Horizon Genuinely Matters

  • Hard terminal dates: target-date funds, a pension's de-risking deadline, an option-like liability with a fixed maturity — the glide path is the product; infinite-horizon is wrong here.
  • Short horizons: the whole problem is "terminal zone" — no turnpike to ride; tactical/event-driven mandates need the finite formulation.
  • Path-dependent objectives: drawdown constraints, high-water-mark fees, ratchet floors — these break stationarity regardless of horizon length (state-dependent policies, Part 03).
  • Non-stationary opportunity sets: structural regime change means "long-run" averages don't exist — neither formulation's parameters are stable; re-estimation cadence dominates (the honest limit of all these models).

4. The Implementation Verdict

  • Default to the stationary policy for long open-ended mandates (endowments, sovereign funds, perpetual strategies) — simpler, robust, and turnpike-justified; recompute the algebraic Riccati as parameters update.
  • Overlay a terminal glide path when a real date exists — solve the finite Riccati ODE but recognize most of the action is the last 10–20% of the horizon.
  • Estimation error dwarfs horizon subtlety: the difference between finite and infinite policies far from maturity is usually smaller than the uncertainty in μ and factor loadings — spend modeling effort on robust inputs (Part 04), not horizon precision.
  • Series synthesis: Parts 01–02 gave the control core, 03–04 the practical sizing and input discipline, 05–06 the liability and factor machinery — this part says: use the stationary solution by default, add horizon structure only where a genuine deadline or path-dependence demands it, and never let either dominate the estimation-risk budget.

Key Takeaways

  • Finite horizon ⇒ time-varying glide-path policy (Riccati ODE); infinite ⇒ stationary policy (algebraic Riccati).
  • Turnpike: the finite policy hugs the stationary one except near maturity — the stationary solution is right for the bulk of any long mandate.
  • Horizon matters for hard target dates, short/tactical mandates, and path-dependent objectives.
  • Default stationary + terminal glide path where dated; estimation error outweighs horizon nuance — invest effort in robust inputs.
