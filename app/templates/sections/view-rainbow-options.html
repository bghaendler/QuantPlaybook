Rainbow Options: Min/Max of Two Assets (Stulz)

Calls and puts on the better or worse of two assets — closed forms with bivariate normals, and the purest expression of correlation as a price.

1. The Stulz (1982) Building Block: Call on the Minimum

Payoff $\max(\min(S_1, S_2) - K, 0)$. With each asset's $d$-terms $d_i = \frac{\ln(S_i/K) + (b_i + \sigma_i^2/2)T}{\sigma_i\sqrt{T}}$, the ratio vol $\hat\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2}$, and bivariate normal CDF $M$:

$$c_{min} = S_1 e^{(b_1 - r)T} M\!\left(d_1,\ \tfrac{\ln(S_2/S_1) + (b_2 - b_1 - \hat\sigma^2/2)T}{\hat\sigma\sqrt{T}};\ \rho_1\right) + S_2 e^{(b_2 - r)T} M\!\left(d_2,\ \cdot\ ;\ \rho_2\right) - K e^{-rT} M\!\left(d_1 - \sigma_1\sqrt{T},\ d_2 - \sigma_2\sqrt{T};\ \rho\right),$$

with $\rho_i = (\sigma_i - \rho\sigma_j)/\hat\sigma$. Each term reads naturally: receive asset $i$ when it finishes above $K$ and below its sibling; pay $K$ when both finish above $K$.

2. The Min/Max Algebra

Everything else follows from one identity:

$$\max(S_1, S_2) + \min(S_1, S_2) = S_1 + S_2 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; c_{max}(K) = c_1(K) + c_2(K) - c_{min}(K),$$

where $c_i$ are vanilla calls. Puts via parity-style relations, e.g. $p_{min}(K) = Ke^{-rT} - PV(\text{min}) + c_{min}(K)$ with $PV(\min) = c_{min}(0)$ from Margrabe: $\min(S_1,S_2) = S_2 - \max(S_2 - S_1, 0)$. The whole two-color rainbow family — best-of, worst-of, calls and puts — assembles from vanillas, Margrabe and the Stulz $c_{min}$.

3. Correlation Is the Product

PositionCorrelation exposureIntuition
Long call on max (best-of)Short ρDispersion lets the winner run away
Long call on min (worst-of call)Long ρNeed both up: they must move together
Long put on min (worst-of put)Short ρDispersion guarantees a loser to deliver

Structured-product flow is dominated by short worst-of puts (inside autocallables/BRCs): retail is systematically selling dispersion, dealers are long it — driving the implied-correlation market and dispersion trading desks that recycle this risk via index-vs-single-name vol.

4. Risk Profile

  • Cega (∂V/∂ρ) is first-order: for an ATM worst-of put, moving ρ from 0.8 to 0.5 can change value by tens of percent.
  • Cross-gamma $\partial^2 V/\partial S_1 \partial S_2$: hedging one asset changes the delta of the other — rebalancing churn concentrated when assets are near each other (the min is about to switch identity).
  • Skew interaction: worst-of puts load on each asset's downside skew; flat-vol Stulz underprices them. Practice: local-vol or SLV per asset + a (possibly stochastic) correlation overlay, with Stulz as control.
  • Implied correlation marks are model-laden: realized ρ is unstable and spikes toward 1 in crashes — exactly when short-dispersion books hurt.

5. Beyond Two Colors

Three assets → trivariate normals (Johnson 1987 generalizes Stulz to $n$ assets with $n$-variate CDFs); beyond ~3–4, Monte Carlo wins. Related family: quanto rainbows (FX-correlation overlay), Himalaya/Napoleon (multi-period best-of with lock-outs — path-dependent, simulation only), and basket options (correlation enters through the sum rather than the order statistics — see next section).

Key Takeaways

  • Stulz's $c_{min}$ + the identity max + min = sum generate all two-asset rainbow prices.
  • Bivariate normal CDFs with correlations $\rho, \rho_1, \rho_2$ are the computational core.
  • Best-of = short correlation; worst-of call = long; worst-of put = short — know your cega sign.
  • Retail structures make the street long dispersion; implied correlation is the clearing price.
  • Cross-gamma and skew interaction make flat-vol formulas a starting point, not a book price.
