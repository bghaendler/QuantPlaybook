CAPM & Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT)

The two classical equilibrium answers to "what return should an asset earn?" — single-factor beta pricing versus multi-factor no-arbitrage pricing.

1. CAPM: From Markowitz to Equilibrium

If every investor holds a mean-variance efficient portfolio (Markowitz) and shares the same expectations, the aggregate market portfolio $M$ is itself efficient, and every asset's expected excess return is proportional to its covariance with the market:

$$\mathbb{E}[R_i] - r_f = \beta_i \left(\mathbb{E}[R_M] - r_f\right), \qquad \beta_i = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(R_i, R_M)}{\operatorname{Var}(R_M)}.$$

This is the Security Market Line (SML). Only systematic risk (covariance with the market) is rewarded; idiosyncratic risk is diversifiable and earns nothing.

An asset plotting above the SML has positive Jensen's alpha $\alpha_i = \mathbb{E}[R_i] - r_f - \beta_i(\mathbb{E}[R_M] - r_f)$ — the standard performance measure for active managers.

3. APT: Pricing by No-Arbitrage (Ross, 1976)

APT drops the equilibrium machinery and assumes only a linear factor structure for returns:

$$R_i = \mathbb{E}[R_i] + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_{ik} F_k + \epsilon_i,$$

with mean-zero factors $F_k$ and idiosyncratic noise $\epsilon_i$ diversifiable across a large universe. If a well-diversified zero-cost portfolio with zero factor exposures could earn a non-zero return, that would be an arbitrage. Ruling this out forces expected returns to be linear in the betas:

$$\mathbb{E}[R_i] = r_f + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_{ik}\, \lambda_k,$$

where $\lambda_k$ is the risk premium of factor $k$. CAPM is the special case $K = 1$ with the market as the sole factor.

4. Worked Example: Two-Factor APT

Factors: industrial production (premium $\lambda_1 = 4\%$) and inflation surprise ($\lambda_2 = -1\%$), $r_f = 3\%$. A stock with $\beta_1 = 1.2$, $\beta_2 = 0.5$:

$$\mathbb{E}[R] = 3\% + 1.2 \times 4\% + 0.5 \times (-1\%) = 7.3\%.$$

If the market consensus forecast for the stock is $9\%$, it is cheap relative to its factor risks: a diversified long position hedged to zero factor exposure against correctly-priced assets captures the $1.7\%$ mispricing — the logic underlying modern factor investing and statistical arbitrage.

5. Empirical Standing

  • CAPM anomalies: the empirical SML is too flat (low-beta stocks earn too much — "betting against beta"); size, value, momentum and quality earn returns unexplained by beta.
  • Fama-French: the 3-factor (market, SMB, HML) and 5-factor extensions are APT-style responses that absorb most anomalies.
  • APT's weakness: it does not name the factors — selection is empirical (macro factors, statistical PCA factors, or characteristic-sorted portfolios).
  • Usage today: CAPM survives as the discount-rate convention in corporate finance; APT survives as the intellectual foundation of every multi-factor risk model (Barra, Axioma) and smart-beta product.

Key Takeaways

  • CAPM: one factor, equilibrium-derived; expected return is linear in market beta.
  • APT: many factors, arbitrage-derived; expected return is linear in factor betas.
  • Only systematic risk is priced in both; idiosyncratic risk diversifies away.
  • Alpha is the deviation from the SML/factor line — the active manager's target.
  • Modern factor models (Fama-French, Barra) are APT implementations.
