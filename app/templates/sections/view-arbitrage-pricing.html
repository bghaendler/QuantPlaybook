1. Arbitrage Bond Pricing

Given an adapted short rate process $r(t)$, the Arbitrage Pricing Theorem implies that discounted bond price processes are $\mathbb{Q}$-martingales for all maturities $T$.

Using the fact that $P(T, T) = 1$, this leads us to the fundamental bond pricing formula:

$$ P(t, T) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left[ e^{-\int_t^T r(s)ds} \right] $$

This formula states that the bond price is the conditional expectation, under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, of the exponential of the time-integrated short rate process. The dynamics of $P(t, T)$ directly depend on the specific model we choose for $r(t)$.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: A bond's price today is just the average expected future payoff, discounted back to today using the bank's interest rate.

  • The Risk-Neutral Measure ($\mathbb{Q}$): We've put on our "magic glasses" so that we don't have to worry about risk premiums. Under this measure, the bond's discounted price behaves like a fair game.
  • The Formula: Instead of discounting by a constant rate, we add up (integrate) the fluctuating short-term rates over the life of the bond.
  • Example: If you buy a zero-coupon bond paying $100 at maturity, its price today is calculated by simulating all possible paths the interest rate might take, discounting the $100 along each path, and taking the average.

Practice Exercise

Suppose you are pricing a 1-year zero-coupon bond paying $100. In a simplified discrete model under $\mathbb{Q}$, the short rate $r$ remains constant for the year but has two equally likely scenarios:

  • Scenario A (prob = 0.5): $r = 2\%$ (0.02)
  • Scenario B (prob = 0.5): $r = 6\%$ (0.06)

Use the Arbitrage Bond Pricing Theorem to calculate the exact bond price $P(0, 1)$ today.

👉 Show Step-by-Step Solution

The pricing formula states: $P(0, 1) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left[ 100 \cdot e^{-\int_0^1 r(s)ds} \right]$

Since the rate is constant over the year in each scenario, the integral is just $r \cdot 1$.

  1. Discount factor for Scenario A: $e^{-0.02} \approx 0.9802$
  2. Discount factor for Scenario B: $e^{-0.06} \approx 0.9418$
  3. Expected value: $0.5 \times (100 \times 0.9802) + 0.5 \times (100 \times 0.9418)$
  4. $P(0, 1) = 49.01 + 47.09 = 96.10$

Answer: The price of the bond today is approximately $96.10.
