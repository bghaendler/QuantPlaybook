Kelly Criterion & Optimal Bet Sizing

Maximizing the long-run growth rate of capital: the log-utility solution to "how much should I bet?" — and why practitioners bet less.

1. The Binary Bet Setup

Repeated independent bets: win probability $p$, lose probability $q = 1-p$. A winning bet pays odds $b$ per unit staked (lose: stake forfeited). Betting a constant fraction $f$ of wealth each round, wealth after $n$ rounds with $W$ wins and $L$ losses is

$$X_n = X_0 (1 + fb)^{W} (1 - f)^{L}.$$

The exponential growth rate of capital is

$$g(f) = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\ln\frac{X_n}{X_0} = p \ln(1 + fb) + q \ln(1 - f).$$

2. The Kelly Fraction

Maximizing $g(f)$ via $g'(f) = 0$ gives the celebrated formula:

$$f^* = \frac{pb - q}{b} = p - \frac{q}{b}.$$

Interpretation: edge over odds. With a fair coin ($p = 0.5$) paying $b = 2{:}1$, $f^* = 0.5 - 0.25 = 25\%$ of wealth per bet. If the edge $pb - q \le 0$, the optimal bet is zero — Kelly never bets without positive expectation.

Properties (Breiman, 1961): Kelly betting (i) maximizes the asymptotic growth rate almost surely, (ii) minimizes the expected time to reach any large wealth target, and (iii) almost surely out-grows any "essentially different" strategy in the long run.

3. Continuous Time: Merton's Fraction

For a GBM asset with drift $\mu$, volatility $\sigma$ and risk-free rate $r$, investing a constant fraction $f$ of wealth gives portfolio growth rate

$$g(f) = r + f(\mu - r) - \tfrac{1}{2} f^2 \sigma^2,$$

maximized at the continuous Kelly (Merton) fraction:

$$f^* = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma^2}, \qquad g(f^*) = r + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\mu - r}{\sigma}\right)^2 = r + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\text{SR}^2.$$

The maximum incremental growth rate equals half the squared Sharpe ratio. Example: $\mu - r = 6\%$, $\sigma = 20\%$ → $f^* = 0.06/0.04 = 150\%$ (leveraged!) — a first hint that full Kelly is aggressive.

4. Fractional Kelly & Drawdown Reality

The growth-rate parabola is flat near the top but the risk is not: betting $f = 2f^*$ produces zero excess growth with double the variance, and overbetting beyond $2f^*$ guarantees long-run ruin in growth terms. Full Kelly itself implies brutal swings: the probability of ever halving your bankroll before doubling it is about $1/3$, and the probability that wealth ever falls below a fraction $x$ of its start is approximately $x$ itself.

Half-Kelly ($f = f^*/2$) delivers ~75% of the optimal growth with roughly half the volatility — the standard practitioner compromise. Fractional Kelly is equivalent to full Kelly under a more risk-averse power utility.

5. Estimation Risk: The Practical Killer

$f^*$ depends on parameters you do not know. Because over-betting is punished far more than under-betting, parameter uncertainty asymmetrically argues for smaller bets: if your estimate $\hat{\mu}$ is noisy, the certainty-equivalent optimal fraction shrinks. Rule of thumb: treat estimated edges with suspicion, size at a fraction (¼ to ½) of estimated Kelly, and recompute as evidence accumulates. Thorp applied exactly this discipline from blackjack card-counting to Princeton-Newport's convertible arbitrage.

Key Takeaways

  • Kelly maximizes expected log wealth: $f^* = (pb - q)/b$ discrete, $f^* = (\mu - r)/\sigma^2$ continuous.
  • Max growth = $r + \frac{1}{2}\text{SR}^2$ — the Sharpe ratio is a growth statement.
  • Overbetting is fatal: growth hits zero at $2f^*$ and turns negative beyond.
  • Half-Kelly: 75% of growth, half the variance — the standard real-world choice.
  • Parameter uncertainty always pushes the optimal bet below naive Kelly.
