L2: Binomial Option Pricing Model

Multi-Period Lattices, Delta Hedging, and American Boundary Conditions

1. Delta Hedging & Risk Elimination

The binomial model assumes that over a small step $\delta t$, the stock price $S$ can only transition to an upward state $uS$ (with probability $p$) or a downward state $vS$ (with probability $1-p$):

$$0 < v < 1 < u$$

By constructing a hedged portfolio consisting of **one option ($V$)** and a **short position of $\Delta$ shares** of stock, we eliminate all random volatility:

$$\Pi = V - \Delta S$$

Equating portfolio values across both upward ($\Pi^+$) and downward ($\Pi^-$) paths at $t + \delta t$ lets us solve for the risk-free **Delta Hedging Ratio ($\Delta$)**:

$$\Delta = \frac{V^+ - V^-}{(u - v)S} = \frac{\text{Range of Option Payoffs}}{\text{Range of Stock Prices}}$$

Hedge Principle: Choosing $\Delta$ completely eliminates $dW_t$ shocks!

2. Risk-Neutral Expectations

Because the hedged portfolio's future value is guaranteed, no-arbitrage rules dictate it must earn the risk-free rate: $\Pi_{t+\delta t} = \Pi_t e^{r \delta t}$.

Substituting $\Delta$ yields the fundamental **discrete pricing expectation**:

$$V = e^{-r \delta t} \left( p' V^+ + (1 - p') V^- \right)$$

where $p'$ is the **risk-neutral probability** of an upward stock movement:

$$p' = \frac{e^{r \delta t} - v}{u - v}$$

Notice that the real-world probability $p$ and stock drift $\mu$ are entirely absent. Option pricing depends solely on the risk-neutral probability $p'$, meaning risk-preferences are irrelevant.

Discounting Rate: Expectations are discounted strictly by the risk-free rate $r$.

3. Discrete SDE Calibration & Continuous Limit

To calibrate the discrete step multipliers to match a continuous-time lognormal random walk SDE ($dS = \mu S dt + \sigma S dW_t$), we match the mean and variance of stock price changes over a step $\delta t$:

Parameter Calibration Formulas

Step length: $\delta t = T / N$

Up multiplier: $u = 1 + \sigma \sqrt{\delta t}$

Down multiplier: $v = 1 - \sigma \sqrt{\delta t}$

Transition to Black-Scholes PDE

Taking the limit as step size $\delta t \to 0$, expanding option value $V(S, t)$ using multi-variable Taylor series, and substituting the delta hedge yields $\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$ and converges the discrete pricing formula directly to the continuous **Black-Scholes Partial Differential Equation**:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + r S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - r V = 0$$

Problem A: Risk-Neutral Probability Derivation

Prove that under a risk-neutral probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the probability of an upward stock price movement in a binomial step is $p = \frac{e^{r\delta t} - d}{u - d}$.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the Risk-Neutral Growth Condition: In a risk-neutral world, the expected future value of any traded asset must grow at the risk-free interest rate $r$: $$\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q}[S_{t+\delta t} \mid S_t] = S_t e^{r \delta t}$$
  2. Formulate Binomial Expectation: Under the binomial framework, the stock can transition to $u S_t$ with probability $p$, or $d S_t$ with probability $1-p$: $$\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q}[S_{t+\delta t} \mid S_t] = p (u S_t) + (1 - p) (d S_t)$$
  3. Equate and Divide by $S_t$: Combining the two equations: $$S_t e^{r \delta t} = S_t [p u + (1 - p) d]$$ Dividing both sides by the current stock price $S_t$: $$e^{r \delta t} = p u + d - p d$$
  4. Isolate Risk-Neutral Probability $p$: Group the $p$ terms on one side: $$e^{r \delta t} - d = p (u - d) \implies p = \frac{e^{r \delta t} - d}{u - d}$$ Since $p$ must satisfy $0 \le p \le 1$, this requires the no-arbitrage condition $d < e^{r\delta t} < u$.
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What is a "risk-neutral" world? In the real world, investors are scared of risk and demand higher returns to hold risky stocks. In a risk-neutral world, investors don't care about risk. Therefore, every single asset (stocks, bonds, options) grows at the exact same safe bank rate ($r$).

2. Why formulate expectations? We write the expected future stock price as a simple average of its two possible futures (up or down) weighted by their probabilities.

3. Why equate them? We force the stock's binomial average growth to equal the safe bank growth. Since we know the bank rate ($r$), the up factor ($u$), and the down factor ($d$), probability ($p$) is the only unknown left.

4. Why must $d < e^{r\delta t} < u$? This is the golden rule of no-arbitrage. If the bank rate was higher than the up multiplier ($e^{r\delta t} > u$), you would borrow money to put in the bank because the stock is guaranteed to underperform. If the stock was always better, everyone would buy the stock and borrow from the bank. Keeping the bank rate in the middle keeps the game fair.

Problem B: Replicating Portfolio Derivation

Prove that an option payoff can be perfectly replicated by a portfolio of $\Delta$ shares of stock and a cash bank position $B$, and derive the formulas for both.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. Set up Replicating Portfolio: Let a portfolio consist of $\Delta$ shares of stock (price $S$) and $B$ cash in the bank. The starting value of this portfolio is: $$V_0 = \Delta S + B$$
  2. Write Up/Down Replication Equations: At $t+\delta t$, the stock is either $uS$ or $dS$. The bank position grows to $B e^{r \delta t}$. For the portfolio to perfectly copy the option payoffs ($V_u$ and $V_d$): $$\Delta (uS) + B e^{r \delta t} = V_u \quad \text{(Upward Path)}$$ $$\Delta (dS) + B e^{r \delta t} = V_d \quad \text{(Downward Path)}$$
  3. Subtract to Solve for $\Delta$: Subtract the Downward equation from the Upward equation to eliminate the bank cash term $B$: $$\Delta uS - \Delta dS = V_u - V_d \implies \Delta S(u - d) = V_u - V_d \implies \Delta = \frac{V_u - V_d}{S(u - d)}$$
  4. Substitute $\Delta$ to Solve for $B$: Plugging $\Delta$ back into the Upward equation: $$\left( \frac{V_u - V_d}{S(u - d)} \right) uS + B e^{r \delta t} = V_u \implies u \frac{V_u - V_d}{u - d} + B e^{r \delta t} = V_u$$ $$B e^{r \delta t} = V_u - \frac{u V_u - u V_d}{u - d} = \frac{V_u(u-d) - u V_u + u V_d}{u - d} = \frac{u V_d - d V_u}{u - d}$$ Discounting to time 0: $$B = e^{-r\delta t} \left( \frac{u V_d - d V_u}{u - d} \right)$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. What is a "replicating portfolio"? If a synthetic combination of a stock and bank deposits mimics an option's payout in every possible state, it must cost the exact same as the option today. Otherwise, you could buy the cheap one, sell the expensive one, and pocket free risk-free profit (arbitrage).

2. Why write up/down equations? We create two target equations: "If the stock goes up, copy the up option value" and "If the stock goes down, copy the down option value." This is a system of two linear equations with two unknowns ($\Delta$ and $B$).

3. Why subtract them? Subtracting cancels the bank cash $B$ completely, leaving us with an easy equation to solve for the stock ratio $\Delta$. This is the famous option Delta, representing how many shares of stock you need to buy to hedge.

4. What does the solved cash amount $B$ represent? $B$ is the amount of money you must borrow (if negative) or lend (if positive) to construct the replica portfolio. It guarantees the cash balances out correctly at expiry.

Problem C: Multi-Step European Pricing Closed-Form

Derive the multi-step binomial formula for a European option over $N$ periods, proving it is represented by a binomial probability distribution.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:
  1. State the One-Step Backward Induction: We know $V_j = e^{-r\delta t} [p V_{j+1}^u + (1-p) V_{j+1}^d]$.
  2. Iterate to Two Steps: Substituting the one-step results into a two-step tree: $$V_0 = e^{-r\delta t} \left[ p \left( e^{-r\delta t} [p V_{uu} + (1-p) V_{ud}] \right) + (1-p) \left( e^{-r\delta t} [p V_{du} + (1-p) V_{dd}] \right) \right]$$ Simplifying (noting $V_{ud} = V_{du}$ for recombining lattices): $$V_0 = e^{-2r\delta t} \left[ p^2 V_{uu} + 2p(1-p)V_{ud} + (1-p)^2 V_{dd} \right]$$
  3. Generalize to $N$ Steps: By mathematical induction, the coefficients are the terms of the binomial expansion $(p + (1-p))^N$. For $N$ steps, the option value at time 0 is: $$V_0 = e^{-r N \delta t} \sum_{j=0}^N \binom{N}{j} p^j (1-p)^{N-j} V_{N, j}$$ where $V_{N, j}$ is the payoff at terminal node $j$.
  4. Apply to European Call Option: At expiry, $V_{N, j} = \max(S_0 u^j d^{N-j} - K, 0)$, yielding the completed multi-step closed-form formula: $$V_0 = e^{-r N \delta t} \sum_{j=0}^N \binom{N}{j} p^j (1-p)^{N-j} \max(S_0 u^j d^{N-j} - K, 0)$$
Why do we do this? (Plain English Layman's Walkthrough)

1. Why backward induct? In option pricing, we only know the payoffs at the very end (expiry). To find the option price today, we must work backward step-by-step, discounting and averaging probabilities at each junction.

2. What is a recombining lattice? A stock that goes up then down reaches the exact same price as a stock that goes down then up ($S_{ud} = S_{du}$). Recombining paths means we don't have to track billions of separate paths, keeping the computation incredibly fast.

3. What does $\binom{N}{j}$ represent? This is the binomial coefficient (combinations). It tells us exactly how many unique paths lead to a specific terminal node. For example, if you toss a coin 3 times, there are 3 different ways to get exactly 2 heads.

4. Why does this look like probability? The term $p^j (1-p)^{N-j}$ is the probability of choosing a specific path. Multiplying this probability by the path combinations and summing them up is simply taking a giant weighted average of all possible payouts at expiry, then discounting it back to today's dollars.

View American Early Exercise boundary mathematics

The **American option style** allows holder execution at *any* intermediate time node before expiration. This changes the backward induction step:

  1. Calculate continuation value: At node $(i, j)$ at step $i$, we first solve for the discounted risk-neutral expectation: $$V_{\text{continuation}} = e^{-r\delta t} \left( p' V_{i+1, j+1} + (1 - p') V_{i+1, j} \right)$$
  2. Compare with early exercise: We compute the immediate payoff of executing option today: $$V_{\text{exercise}} = \max(S_{i, j} - K, 0) \quad \text{(Call)} \quad , \quad V_{\text{exercise}} = \max(K - S_{i, j}, 0) \quad \text{(Put)}$$
  3. Take the maximum: The final node option price is the maximum of continuation vs exercise: $$V_{i, j} = \max(V_{\text{continuation}}, V_{\text{exercise}})$$ If $V_{\text{exercise}} > V_{\text{continuation}}$, early exercise is optimal, increasing option value at the root! This typically occurs for **American Put options** when stock price falls deeply, as the immediate payout is worth more than waiting due to positive discounting drag.
