L4: Probabilistic Methods for Interest Rates

Martingale Measures, Girsanov Transformations, and Option Pricing under the Forward Measure

Stochastic Calculus Foundations

Pricing interest rate derivatives requires a rigorous probabilistic framework. We define a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ with a filtration $\{\mathcal{F}_t\}$ generated by a multi-dimensional Brownian motion $W_t$.

Doléans-Dade Exponential: The stochastic exponential of a continuous local martingale is the unique solution to $\mathrm{d}\mathcal{E}_t = \mathcal{E}_t \gamma_t \cdot \mathrm{d}W_t$, defined as: $$\mathcal{E}_t\left(\int_0^\cdot \gamma_u \cdot \mathrm{d}W_u\right) = \exp\left( \int_0^t \gamma_u \cdot \mathrm{d}W_u - \frac{1}{2}\int_0^t |\gamma_u|^2 \mathrm{d}u \right)$$

Girsanov's Theorem

Let $W_t$ be a standard Brownian motion under the physical measure $\mathbb{P}$. If $\theta(t)$ is an adapted process satisfying the Novikov condition: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}}\left[ \exp\left( \frac{1}{2}\int_0^T |\theta_u|^2 \mathrm{d}u \right) \right] < \infty$$ Then we can define an equivalent probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ via the Radon-Nikodým derivative: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbb{Q}}{\mathrm{d}\mathbb{P}} = \mathcal{E}_T\left(\int_0^\cdot -\theta_u \cdot \mathrm{d}W_u\right)$$ Under $\mathbb{Q}$, the process $W^\theta_t = W_t + \int_0^t \theta_u \mathrm{d}u$ is a standard Brownian motion.

Short-Rate Dynamics & Cash Accounts

The instantaneous short rate $r(t)$ is modelled as a stochastic differential equation: $$\mathrm{d}r(t) = \mu_t \mathrm{d}t + \sigma_t \cdot \mathrm{d}X(t)$$ To establish a risk-neutral pricing framework, we introduce the money market cash account $A(t)$ that starts at $1$ and accumulates at the instantaneous short rate $r(t)$: $$A(t) = \exp\left( \int_0^t r(s)\mathrm{d}s \right) \implies \mathrm{d}A(t) = r(t)A(t)\mathrm{d}t$$

The Fundamental Asset Pricing Formula

A family of zero-coupon bond prices $\{B(t,U)\}$ is called arbitrage-free if there exists an equivalent probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ (spot martingale measure) under which the discounted bond price: $$Z(t,U) = \frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)}$$ is a martingale. Since $B(U,U) = 1$, we can express any bond price today by taking the expectation of the terminal payment discounted by the money market account: $$B(t,U) = A(t) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{B(U,U)}{A(U)} \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \exp\left( -\int_t^U r(s)\mathrm{d}s \right) \Bigg| \mathcal{F}_t \right]$$

Deriving ZCB Dynamics under $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{P}$

To value options on a zero-coupon bond, we must find the SDE of the bond price $B(t,U)$ itself. By using Girsanov's theorem, Garsanov shifts, and the Martingale Representation Theorem on the discounted helper martingale $M(t) = Z(t,U)\eta(t)$ under $\mathbb{P}$, we derive that the bond price follows a geometric Brownian motion under $\mathbb{Q}$ [PDF3: 4]: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}B(t,U)}{B(t,U)} = r(t)\mathrm{d}t + b^\theta(t,U) \cdot \mathrm{d}X^\theta(t)$$ where $b^\theta(t,U)$ is the deterministic or stochastic bond volatility, and $X^\theta$ is the standard Brownian motion under $\mathbb{Q}$.

Physical Dynamics & The Market Price of Risk

Translating this back to the physical measure $\mathbb{P}$ using Girsanov's Brownian motion relation $\mathrm{d}X^\theta(t) = \mathrm{d}X(t) + \theta(t)\mathrm{d}t$: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}B(t,U)}{B(t,U)} = \left( r(t) + b^\theta(t,U) \cdot \theta(t) \right)\mathrm{d}t + b^\theta(t,U) \cdot \mathrm{d}X(t)$$ The term $\theta(t)$ is the market price of interest rate risk. It represents the premium (excess return) required by investors per unit of bond volatility risk $b^\theta(t,U)$. Note that the volatility coefficient $b^\theta(t,U)$ is invariant under change of measure, because changes of measure only affect the drift.

The $U$-Forward Martingale Measure $\mathbb{P}_U$

A European call option maturing at $U$ on a bond maturing at $V$ ($U < V$) has the payoff $(B(U,V) - K)^+$. Pricing this directly under the risk-neutral spot martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}$ is highly difficult: $$C(t) = A(t) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{(B(U,V) - K)^+}{A(U)} \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right]$$ This contains two correlated stochastic variables: the terminal bond price $B(U,V)$ and the discount factor $1/A(U)$. To simplify this, we define the $U$-forward martingale measure $\mathbb{P}_U$ using the bond $B(t,U)$ as the numeraire.

Change of Numeraire & Girsanov Transformation

We define the Radon-Nikodým derivative process $\lambda_t = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbb{P}_U}{\mathrm{d}\mathbb{Q}} | \mathcal{F}_t]$ as: $$\lambda_t = \frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)B(0,U)}$$ Applying Girsanov's theorem, the new Brownian motion under the forward measure $\mathbb{P}_U$ is: $$\mathrm{d}X^U(t) = \mathrm{d}X^\theta(t) - b^\theta(t,U)\mathrm{d}t$$ By change of measure, the stochastic discount factor vanishes from inside the expectation: $$C(t) = B(t,U) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}\left[ (B(U,V) - K)^+ \ \big|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right]$$

Martingale Forward Bond Price & Analytical Option Value

Define the forward bond price as $F_B(t,U,V) = B(t,V)/B(t,U)$. Under $\mathbb{P}_U$, $F_B$ has no drift and is a martingale: $$\frac{\mathrm{d}F_B(t,U,V)}{F_B(t,U,V)} = \left( b^\theta(t,V) - b^\theta(t,U) \right) \cdot \mathrm{d}X^U(t)$$ If the volatilities are deterministic (e.g. Hull-White or Vasicek), the forward price is lognormal, giving an analytical Black-Scholes formula: $$C(t) = B(t,V) N(d_+) - K B(t,U) N(d_-)$$ $$d_\pm = \frac{\ln\left( \frac{B(t,V)}{K B(t,U)} \right) \pm \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2_{U,V}(t,U)}{\sigma_{U,V}(t,U)}, \quad \sigma^2_{U,V}(t,U) = \int_t^U |b^\theta(s,V) - b^\theta(s,U)|^2 \mathrm{d}s$$

Exercise 1. The Radon-Nikodým Martingale Proof

Question: Prove that the Radon-Nikodým derivative process $\lambda_t = \frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)B(0,U)}$ is a standard martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

Solution:
By definition, the discounted bond price $Z(t,U) = \frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)}$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$.
Therefore, for any $s \le t$:
$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[Z(t,U) \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = Z(s,U)$$ We can rewrite $\lambda_t$ as: $$\lambda_t = \frac{1}{B(0,U)} Z(t,U)$$ Since $B(0,U)$ is a known constant determined at $t=0$, we have: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\lambda_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{Z(t,U)}{B(0,U)} \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_s \right] = \frac{1}{B(0,U)} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[Z(t,U) \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = \frac{Z(s,U)}{B(0,U)} = \lambda_s$$ This proves that the Radon-Nikodým process $\lambda_t$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$.

Exercise 2. Abstract Bayes' Formula Transformation

Question: Using the Abstract Bayes' formula, prove that the call option pricing expectation simplifies to $C(t) = B(t,U) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}\left[ (B(U,V) - K)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_t \right]$ under the forward measure.

Solution:
Under the spot measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the call option price is: $$C(t) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{A(t)}{A(U)} (B(U,V) - K)^+ \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right]$$ The Radon-Nikodým derivative of the forward measure $\mathbb{P}_U$ w.r.t $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\lambda_U = \frac{1}{A(U)B(0,U)}$. The conditional expectation process is $\lambda_t = \frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)B(0,U)}$.
Applying the Abstract Bayes' formula: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}[\psi \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\psi \lambda_U \mid \mathcal{F}_t]}{\lambda_t}$$ Let $\psi = (B(U,V) - K)^+$. Then: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}[(B(U,V) - K)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ (B(U,V) - K)^+ \frac{1}{A(U)B(0,U)} \ \Big|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right]}{\frac{B(t,U)}{A(t)B(0,U)}}$$ Simplifying the constants $B(0,U)$ in the numerator and denominator yields: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}[(B(U,V) - K)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = \frac{A(t)}{B(t,U)} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{(B(U,V) - K)^+}{A(U)} \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right]$$ Multiplying both sides by $B(t,U)$ yields: $$B(t,U) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}_U}[(B(U,V) - K)^+ \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = A(t) \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[ \frac{(B(U,V) - K)^+}{A(U)} \ \Bigg|\ \mathcal{F}_t \right] = C(t)$$ This proves the forward measure transformation.
