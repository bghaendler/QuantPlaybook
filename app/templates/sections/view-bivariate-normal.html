Bivariate Normal Distribution (Drezner, Genz)

The function $M(a, b; \rho)$ behind compound options, Americans with dividends, barriers and two-asset exotics — and the quadrature algorithms that compute it.

1. Definition & Where It Appears

$$M(a, b; \rho) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \int_{-\infty}^{a}\!\!\int_{-\infty}^{b} \exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2 - 2\rho xy + y^2}{2(1-\rho^2)}\right) dy\, dx.$$

Pricing needs it whenever a payoff involves two Gaussian events: Geske compound options and Roll-Geske-Whaley ($\rho = \sqrt{T_1/T_2}$ between decision dates), Bjerksund-Stensland 2002, partial-time barriers, two-asset rainbows (Stulz), quanto-barriers, and two-period Bermudans. No closed form exists; the game is fast, uniformly accurate quadrature.

2. Useful Identities (Implement These First)

  • Marginals: $M(a, \infty; \rho) = N(a)$; independence: $M(a, b; 0) = N(a)N(b)$.
  • Perfect correlation: $M(a, b; 1) = N(\min(a, b))$; $M(a, b; -1) = \max(0, N(a) + N(b) - 1)$.
  • Symmetries: $M(a, b; \rho) = M(b, a; \rho)$; complement $M(-a, -b; \rho) = 1 - N(a) - N(b) + M(a, b; \rho)$.
  • Sign flip: $M(a, -b; -\rho) = N(a) - M(a, b; \rho)$ — lets you keep $\rho \ge 0$ internally.
  • Derivative in ρ (Plackett): $\dfrac{\partial M}{\partial \rho} = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{a^2 - 2\rho ab + b^2}{2(1-\rho^2)}\right)$ — the basis of "integrate over ρ" algorithms and instant cega.

3. Drezner (1978) & Drezner-Wesolowsky (1990)

Drezner's classic uses Gauss-Hermite-type quadrature on a transformed integral — 4–5 points per dimension, ~10⁻⁶ accuracy; Drezner-Wesolowsky integrate Plackett's identity over ρ with Gauss-Legendre nodes:

$$M(a,b;\rho) = N(a)N(b) + \int_0^{\rho} \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{1-r^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{a^2 - 2rab + b^2}{2(1-r^2)}\right) dr,$$

a smooth 1-D integral — short code, ~10⁻⁷ accuracy for moderate ρ. Both degrade as $|\rho| \to 1$ (the integrand spikes); older Haug-book implementations inherit exactly this weakness.

4. Genz (2004): The Modern Standard

Genz refines Drezner-Wesolowsky with (i) higher-order Gauss-Legendre rules chosen by |ρ| bands, and (ii) for $|\rho| > 0.925$, a singularity-extracting variable transformation that handles the near-degenerate case analytically plus a small smooth correction. Result: ~14–15 digit accuracy uniformly in $(a, b, \rho)$ at a cost of 6–20 exponential evaluations. His TVPACK/`bvnd` code (and its translations in QuantLib, SciPy `owens_t`-based routines, etc.) is the de facto reference. Rule of thumb: if your library's bivariate CDF isn't Genz-derived, test it at $\rho = \pm 0.99$ before trusting barrier or compound prices.

5. Numerical Hygiene for Pricing Use

  • Consistency with your $N(x)$: identities like in-out parity rely on $M$ and $N$ agreeing to full precision; mix vendors and parity breaks at the 10⁻⁸ level — visible in calibrated Greeks.
  • Greeks: differentiate analytically using $\partial M/\partial a = \phi(a) N\!\left(\frac{b - \rho a}{\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\right)$ rather than bumping — exact and cheap.
  • ρ near ±1 arises naturally (compound options with $T_1 \to T_2$; partial barriers with windows shrinking) — precisely where cheap routines fail; use Genz.
  • Owen's T-function is an equivalent formulation ($M$ in terms of $T(h, a)$) with its own high-accuracy algorithms (Patefield-Tandy).

Key Takeaways

  • $M(a,b;\rho)$ powers compound, American-dividend, partial-barrier and rainbow formulas.
  • Plackett's ρ-derivative identity turns the 2-D integral into a smooth 1-D one — the basis of Drezner-Wesolowsky and Genz.
  • Genz (2004): uniform machine-precision accuracy including $|\rho| \to 1$ — the production standard.
  • Use the analytic partials of $M$ for Greeks; keep $M$ and $N$ from the same precision family.
  • Test any implementation at extreme ρ and via the boundary identities before shipping.
