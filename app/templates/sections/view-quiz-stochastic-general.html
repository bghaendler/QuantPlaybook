Question 1 (Stochastic Calculus) Multiple Choice

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, a stock price $S(t)$ follows Geometric Brownian Motion dynamics: $$dS(t) = r S(t) dt + \sigma S(t) dW^*(t)$$ where $W^*(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion and $r > 0$ is the constant short rate. Define the discounted stock price process $Z(t) = e^{-rt} S(t)$. Which of the following statements is true about $Z(t)$?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

We want to find the stochastic differential of the process $Z(t) = e^{-rt} S(t)$. Using Itô's product rule (or integration by parts) on $U(t) = e^{-rt}$ and $S(t)$:

$$ dZ(t) = d(e^{-rt} S(t)) = S(t) d(e^{-rt}) + e^{-rt} dS(t) + d[e^{-rt}, S]_t $$

First, calculate the differentials of each component:

  1. Discount Factor Differential: Since $e^{-rt}$ is a deterministic function of bounded variation, its differential is simply: $$ d(e^{-rt}) = -r e^{-rt} dt $$
  2. Stock Price Differential: The given SDE is: $$ dS(t) = r S(t) dt + \sigma S(t) dW^*(t) $$
  3. Quadratic Covariation: Because the discount factor has zero quadratic variation, the cross-product is zero: $$ d[e^{-rt}, S]_t = 0 $$

Now, substitute these into the product rule formula:

\begin{align*} dZ(t) &= S(t) \left( -r e^{-rt} dt \right) + e^{-rt} \left( r S(t) dt + \sigma S(t) dW^*(t) \right) + 0 \\ &= -r e^{-rt} S(t) dt + r e^{-rt} S(t) dt + \sigma e^{-rt} S(t) dW^*(t) \\ &= \sigma Z(t) dW^*(t) \end{align*}

Since the drift coefficient of $dZ(t)$ is exactly zero, $Z(t)$ is a local martingale. Since it is a positive process driven by Brownian motion, it is a true martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$. This is a fundamental result in financial mathematics: the discounted price process of any tradable asset is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure.

Correct Answer: $Z(t)$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$ and has dynamics $dZ(t) = \sigma Z(t) dW^*(t)$.

Question 2 (Short Rate Models) Multiple Choice

Compare the Vasicek and Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) short rate models: $$\text{Vasicek: } dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma dW^*(t)$$ $$\text{CIR: } dr(t) = \kappa(\theta - r(t))dt + \sigma \sqrt{r(t)} dW^*(t)$$ Which of the following statements correctly distinguishes the mathematical properties of these two models?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Analysis:

Let's analyze the properties of the two models:

  1. Positivity of Rates:
    • In the Vasicek model, the diffusion coefficient $\sigma$ is constant. The short rate $r(t)$ is normally distributed: $$ r(t) \sim \mathcal{N}\left( r(0)e^{-\kappa t} + \theta(1-e^{-\kappa t}), \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1-e^{-2\kappa t}) \right) $$ Since a normal distribution has infinite supports on both sides, there is a strictly positive probability $\mathbb{P}[r(t) < 0] > 0$ that the interest rate becomes negative at any time $t > 0$.
    • In the CIR model, the diffusion coefficient is $\sigma \sqrt{r(t)}$. As $r(t) \to 0$, the volatility goes to zero. The drift $\kappa(\theta - r(t)) \to \kappa\theta > 0$ pushes the rate back to positive territory. If the Feller condition: $$ 2\kappa\theta \geq \sigma^2 $$ holds, the mean reversion is strong enough relative to the noise that the rate never reaches the boundary $0$ and remains strictly positive ($r(t) > 0$ for all $t$). Even if $2\kappa\theta < \sigma^2$, the rate can touch $0$ but will instantly bounce back, making it non-negative.
  2. Transition Probability Distributions:
    • Vasicek: Gaussian (normal distribution).
    • CIR: Non-central Chi-squared distribution.
  3. Analytical Closed-Form ZCB Pricing:
    • Both models belong to the class of Affine Term Structure Models where the zero-coupon bond price is given by $P(t, T) = A(t, T) e^{-B(t, T) r(t)}$. Both have analytical formulas for $A(t, T)$ and $B(t, T)$ via ordinary differential equations.

Correct Answer: The CIR model guarantees non-negative interest rates, and if the Feller condition $2\kappa\theta \geq \sigma^2$ is satisfied, $r(t)$ remains strictly positive; whereas the Vasicek model has a strictly positive probability of negative interest rates.

Question 3 (HJM Framework) Multiple Choice

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the instantaneous forward rate dynamics in the Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) framework are given by: $$df(t, T) = \alpha(t, T) dt + \sigma(t, T) dW^*(t)$$ If we assume a constant instantaneous forward rate volatility function $\sigma(t, T) \equiv \sigma$ (Ho-Lee model structure), what is the unique risk-neutral drift function $\alpha(t, T)$ required to avoid arbitrage?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

In the Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) framework, to prevent arbitrage, the drift coefficient $\alpha(t, T)$ of the forward rates under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ is uniquely determined by the volatility structure $\sigma(t, T)$ through the HJM drift condition:

$$ \alpha(t, T) = \sigma(t, T) \int_t^T \sigma(t, u) du $$

Since the volatility function is assumed to be a constant $\sigma(t, T) \equiv \sigma$:

  1. Evaluate the integral term: $$ \int_t^T \sigma(t, u) du = \int_t^T \sigma du = \sigma \left[ u \right]_t^T = \sigma(T - t) $$
  2. Multiply by the volatility function: $$ \alpha(t, T) = \sigma \times \left( \sigma(T - t) \right) = \sigma^2 (T - t) $$

This is the standard drift term in the Ho-Lee model, which is the constant-volatility Gaussian HJM model. Note that the drift depends explicitly on the time-to-maturity $(T - t)$, indicating that instantaneous forward rates must grow faster for longer maturities to compensate for the accumulative volatility risk and prevent arbitrage.

Correct Answer: $\alpha(t, T) = \sigma^2 (T - t)$

Question 4 (Forward Measures) Multiple Choice

Let $\mathbb{Q}$ be the risk-neutral measure with money-market account $B(t) = e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds}$ as the numeraire. We define the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$ by choosing the zero-coupon bond $P(t, T)$ as the numeraire. What is the Radon-Nikodym derivative $\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}}$ on $\mathcal{F}_T$?

Show Mathematical Solution & Derivation

Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation:

Under the Change of Numeraire theorem, if we change the numeraire from an old asset $U(t)$ (defining measure $\mathbb{Q}^U$) to a new asset $N(t)$ (defining measure $\mathbb{Q}^N$), the Radon-Nikodym derivative that changes the probability measure on $\mathcal{F}_t$ is given by:

$$ \left. \frac{d\mathbb{Q}^N}{d\mathbb{Q}^U} \right|_{\mathcal{F}_t} = \frac{N(t) / N(0)}{U(t) / U(0)} $$

Let's map our specific assets and measures to this general formula:

  1. Old Measure ($\mathbb{Q}^U$): The risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ uses the money-market account as numeraire, so $U(t) = B(t) = e^{\int_0^t r(s) ds}$. Since $B(0) = 1$, we have: $$ \frac{U(t)}{U(0)} = \frac{B(t)}{1} = B(t) $$
  2. New Measure ($\mathbb{Q}^N$): The $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$ uses the zero-coupon bond $P(t, T)$ as numeraire, so $N(t) = P(t, T)$. At time 0, $N(0) = P(0, T)$, so we have: $$ \frac{N(t)}{N(0)} = \frac{P(t, T)}{P(0, T)} $$
  3. Radon-Nikodym derivative at time $t$: $$ \left. \frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} \right|_{\mathcal{F}_t} = \frac{P(t, T) / P(0, T)}{B(t)} = \frac{P(t, T)}{P(0, T) B(t)} $$
  4. Evaluating at the terminal maturity $t = T$: Since $P(T, T) = 1$, we get: $$ \left. \frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} \right|_{\mathcal{F}_T} = \frac{1}{P(0, T) B(T)} = \frac{e^{-\int_0^T r(s) ds}}{P(0, T)} $$

This Radon-Nikodym density allows us to compute expectations under the $T$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$, which simplifies option pricing significantly by getting rid of the stochastic discount factor within the expectation.

Correct Answer: $\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^T}{d\mathbb{Q}} = \frac{1}{P(0, T) B(T)} = \frac{e^{-\int_0^T r(s) ds}}{P(0, T)}$
