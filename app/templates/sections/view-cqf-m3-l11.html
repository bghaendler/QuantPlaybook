L11: Advanced Volatility Modeling

Deterministic Local Volatility, Uncertain Volatility Models (BSB PDE), and Optimal Static Hedging

Deterministic Local Volatility $\sigma(S, t)$

If we assume the option market is perfectly correct, implied volatility contains all necessary information about future price distributions. To price exotic derivatives consistently, we extract a **local volatility** surface $\sigma(S, t)$ from observed market vanilla option prices over strikes and expirations.

Local Volatility Framework: Introduced by Bruno Dupire, Emanuel Derman, and Mark Rubinstein, this deterministic volatility model posits that the asset volatility is a direct function of spot price $S$ and time $t$. The standard Black-Scholes PDE remains valid: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma(S,t)^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + (r-q) S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - r V = 0$$

The Fokker-Planck Derivation

We define the transition probability density function $p(S^*, t^*; S, T)$, governed by the Fokker-Planck equation forward in maturity $T$: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial T} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial S^2}\left(\sigma^2 S^2 p\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial S}\left((r-q) S p\right) - q p$$ Differentiating the European call integral twice w.r.t strike $E$ yields the risk-neutral density (Breeden-Litzenberger): $$p = e^{(r-q)(T-t^*)} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial E^2}$$ Substituting $p$ into the call integral time derivative and integrating by parts yields **Dupire's Local Variance Formula**: $$\sigma^2_{loc}(K=E, T) = \frac{\frac{\partial V}{\partial T} + (r-q)E\frac{\partial V}{\partial E} + q V}{\frac{1}{2}E^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial E^2}}$$

The Uncertain Volatility Model (Black-Scholes-Barenblatt PDE)

In contrast to deterministic local volatility, the **Uncertain Volatility Model** (developed by Marco Avellaneda, Antonio Levy, and Arnon Paras in 1995) makes no assumptions about the correctness of implied volatility. Volatility $\sigma$ is treated as an uncertain parameter constrained to lie within a defined band, without assigning any probability distribution: $$\sigma^- \le \sigma \le \sigma^+$$ This reflects Frank Knight's classical distinction between randomness (known probabilities) and uncertainty (unknown probabilities, known boundaries).

The Non-linear Black-Scholes-Barenblatt (BSB) PDE

To hedge an option portfolio $\Pi = V - \Delta S$, we apply Ito's lemma and choose $\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$ to eliminate asset price randomness. The remaining volatility risk cannot be hedged away. To find the worst-case (pessimistic) option price $V^-$, we select the volatility within the band that minimizes the portfolio's return: $$\frac{\partial V^-}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(\Gamma) S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V^-}{\partial S^2} + (r-q)S \frac{\partial V^-}{\partial S} - r V^- = 0$$ Where the volatility is selected dynamically based on the sign of option Gamma ($\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 V^-}{\partial S^2}$): $$\sigma(\Gamma) = \begin{cases} \sigma^+, & \text{if } \Gamma < 0 \\ \sigma^-, & \text{if } \Gamma > 0 \end{cases}$$ For the best-case price $V^+$, the opposite selection applies. Because the volatility choice depends on the second derivative of the price itself, the resulting BSB PDE is highly non-linear, meaning option portfolios are non-additive: $$\text{Value}(A + B) \neq \text{Value}(A) + \text{Value}(B)$$

Compressing Volatility Spreads via Static Hedging

Because the uncertain volatility model produces wide bid-ask pricing spreads for complex exotic options (e.g. up-and-out barrier calls), traders can narrow these spreads by purchasing or selling highly liquid, traded vanilla options. This process is called **Optimal Static Hedging**.

Portfolio Optimization: The net worst-case value of the statically hedged portfolio is: $$\text{Value}(\text{Exotic}) = \max_{q_i} \left( V_{NL} \left( \text{Exotic} + \sum_{i=1}^N q_i \text{Vanilla}_i \right) - \sum_{i=1}^N q_i \text{CostVanilla}_i \right)$$ Where $V_{NL}$ is the non-linear BSB price of the combined portfolio, and $\text{CostVanilla}_i$ represents the market purchase or sale price of the vanilla options.

Worked Case Study: 100 Call

Consider a 6-month European call option at a $100$ strike, priced under uncertain volatility $\sigma \in [20\%, 30\%]$ with $r = 5\%$:

  • Unhedged BSB Range: Worst-case $V^- = 6.89$ (priced at $20\%$), Best-case $V^+ = 9.63$ (priced at $30\%$). Net pricing spread = $2.74$.
  • Statically Hedged Range: Using $0.5$ of a 90-strike call and $0.5$ of a 110-strike call narrows the pricing spread: Buy price becomes $7.67$, Sell price becomes $8.71$. Net pricing spread = $1.04$.
  • Optimal Quantities: Solving the optimization yields $q_1 = 0.62$ (90 call) and $q_2 = 0.53$ (110 call), narrowing the spread to its absolute mathematical minimum.

Exercise 1. Forward vs. Backward PDEs

Question: What is the difference between the Backward PDE and the Forward PDE, and what are their respective uses? Use the Breeden-Litzenberger density result to explain.

Solution:
- **Backward PDE:** Marching backwards in time from a known option payoff $(S_T - K)^+$ at maturity $T$ to find today's option value $V(t, S)$. It is the pricing workhorse for specific contracts.
- **Forward PDE:** Evolves the entire call price surface forward in maturity time $T$ for each strike $K$. Useful for calibrating volatility smiles.
- **Breeden-Litzenberger Density:** The second derivative of the call price w.r.t. strike $K$ yields the risk-neutral probability density function ($f_{S_T}$), which is positive by definition: $$f_{S_T}(K) = e^{rT} \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2} \ge 0 \iff \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2} \ge 0$$

Exercise 3. Time-Dependent vs. Local Volatility BS PDEs

Question: What are the governing PDEs if volatility is $\sigma(t)$ vs. $\sigma(S, t)$? Does the analytical BS formula hold? Express implied volatility mathematically as an average.

Solution:
- **Deterministic $\sigma(t)$:** The Black-Scholes formula **remains valid**. Implied volatility $\sigma_{imp}$ is the root-mean-square average variance over the option's life: $$\sigma_{imp} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T - t} \int_t^T \sigma^2(\tau) \, \mathrm{d}\tau}$$ - **Local $\sigma(S, t)$:** The asset is no longer log-normal at maturity; thus, **closed-form BS solutions fail**. We must use numerical finite difference or Monte Carlo methods.

Exercise 4. Log-Forward Moneyness Coordinates transform

Question: Derive the Forward PDE in log-forward moneyness coordinates $y = \ln(K/F(T))$ and isolate local variance $\sigma^2$.

Solution:
Using $K = F(T) e^y$ and applying the chain rule: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial K} = \frac{1}{K}\frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \quad K^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial K^2} = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial y^2} - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \quad \frac{\partial C}{\partial T}\Big|_K = \frac{\partial C}{\partial T}\Big|_y - (r-q)K\frac{\partial C}{\partial K}$$ Substituting these into the Dupire forward PDE yields: $$\frac{\partial C}{\partial T}\Big|_y = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \left(\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial y^2} - \frac{\partial C}{\partial y}\right) - q C$$ Isolating $\sigma^2$ yields the simplified local variance formula: $$\sigma^2_{loc} = \frac{2\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial T} + q C\right)}{\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial y^2} - \frac{\partial C}{\partial y}}$$

Exercise 5. Gatheral's Total Variance Local Volatility

Question: Write Gatheral's total variance representation of local volatility in terms of standard implied volatility $\sigma(K, T)$ derivatives.

Solution:
Applying chain rules to $w(y, T) = \sigma^2 T$ inside Gatheral's total variance equation leads to the expanded implied volatility surface formula: $$\sigma_{loc}(K, T) = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2 + 2 T \sigma \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial T} + 2(r - q) T K \sigma \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial K}}{\left( 1 - \frac{K y}{\sigma} \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial K} \right)^2 + T K \sigma \left( \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial K} - \frac{1}{4} T K \sigma \left( \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial K} \right)^2 + K \frac{\partial^2 \sigma}{\partial K^2} \right)}}$$
