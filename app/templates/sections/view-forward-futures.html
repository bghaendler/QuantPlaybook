Maths Primer Interest Rates

1.2 Forward and Futures Rates

Locking in borrowing rates today using Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs) and resettled Interest Rate Futures contracts, including the convexity adjustment.
Forward and futures contracts allow market participants to lock in borrowing rates today. Daily margin settlement in futures creates a convexity adjustment relative to OTC forwards.

Forward Rate Agreements (FRA)

A Forward Rate Agreement (FRA) is an over-the-counter (OTC) contract traded between two parties at calendar date $t$ that allows them to lock in a borrowing or lending rate today over a future period $[T_0, T_1]$:

  • A future period $[T_0, T_1]$ with length denoted by $\delta = T_1 - T_0$.
  • A fixed interest rate $K$ and a notional principal amount $N$.

At the payment date $T_1$, the holder of the FRA contract receives the net interest payoff:

$$ \text{Payoff}(T_1) = N \delta \bigl( L(T_0, T_1) - K \bigr) $$

How FRAs Lock In Rates

Suppose you plan to take a floating-rate loan of principal $N$ at time $T_0$, which matures at $T_1$. You will owe simple spot interest of $\delta L(T_0, T_1) N$ at maturity. To eliminate this cash flow uncertainty, you enter into a receiver FRA as the holder:

Floating rate loan interest owed: − δ L(T₀, T₁) N
Floating interest received from FRA: + δ L(T₀, T₁) N
Fixed interest paid via FRA: − δ K N

Notice that the floating rate cash flows cancel out perfectly. The net cash flow you pay at $T_1$ is strictly the fixed interest $\delta K N$, successfully locking in rate $K$.

Analytical Derivation of the FRA Value

Recall the definition of the simple spot rate: $L(T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \left( \frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)} - 1 \right)$. Substituting this into the payoff yields:

$$ \text{Payoff}(T_1) = N \left( \frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)} - 1 - \delta K \right) = N (I_1 - I_2) $$

1. Valuation of Component $I_1$

Component $I_1$ pays $\frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)}$ at date $T_1$. Since this cash flow is unknown at time $t$ but becomes fully known at $T_0$, its value at time $T_0$ is found by discounting from $T_1$ using the $T_0$-discount bond:

$$ V_{I_1}(T_0) = P(T_0, T_1) \times \frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)} = 1 $$

Since its value is exactly 1 unit of cash guaranteed at $T_0$, its present value today at time $t$ is simply the price of a ZCB maturing at $T_0$:

$$ V_{I_1}(t) = P(t, T_0) $$

2. Valuation of Component $I_2$

Component $I_2$ represents the fixed payment $1 + \delta K$ at date $T_1$. This is a completely deterministic, risk-free future cash flow known today at time $t$.

By the standard rules of risk-free discounting, its present value today is simply found by multiplying by the zero-coupon bond price maturing at $T_1$:

$$ V_{I_2}(t) = (1 + \delta K) P(t, T_1) $$
$$ V_{\text{FRA}}(t, T_0, T_1) = N \bigl( P(t, T_0) - (1 + \delta K)P(t, T_1) \bigr) $$

Simple & Instantaneous Forward Rates

The fair rate $K$ which renders the FRA value zero today is defined as the simple forward rate prevailing at time $t$ for lending period $[T_0, T_1]$:

$$ F(t, T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{\delta} \left( \frac{P(t, T_0)}{P(t, T_1)} - 1 \right) $$

When the evaluation time $t$ reaches the loan start date $T_0$, the forward rate converges to the standard simple spot rate: $F(T_0, T_0, T_1) = L(T_0, T_1)$.

Using this rate, we can re-express the FRA value elegantly as:

$$ V_{\text{FRA}}(t, T_0, T_1) = N P(t, T_1) \delta \bigl( F(t, T_0, T_1) - K \bigr) $$

Instantaneous Forward Rate $f(t, T)$

If we let the length of the lending period contract to zero (i.e. letting $T_1 \to T_0 = T$ or $\delta \to 0$), we obtain the instantaneous forward rate with maturity $T$:

$$ f(t, T) = \lim_{T_1 \to T} F(t, T, T_1) = -\frac{\partial \ln P(t, T)}{\partial T} $$

This is the rate that can be locked in today at $t$ for an instantaneous loan at maturity $T$. Integrating this relation gives the ZCB price in terms of the instantaneous forward rates:

$$ P(t, T) = e^{-\int_t^T f(t, u) du} $$

Futures vs. Forwards: Convexity Adjustment

Similar to an FRA, an interest rate futures contract allows investors to manage or hedge exposure to simple spot interest rates over a future period $[T_0, T_1]$.

However, unlike FRAs, futures are exchange-traded and daily marked-to-market (resettled). Instead of holding a growing exposure with a counterparty, any change in value is settled in cash at the end of each trading day, resetting the contract's current market value to zero.

Daily Settlement Quotation

At any date $t \le T_0$, the futures price is quoted as:

$$ P_{\text{futures}}(t) = 100 \times \bigl(1 - R_{\text{futures}}(t)\bigr) $$

The Convexity Gap

Because of daily resettlement, the futures rate is a martingale under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, whereas simple forward rates are martingales under the forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_1}$. This creates a positive convexity adjustment:

$$ \text{Convexity Adjustment} = R_{\text{futures}} - F $$
