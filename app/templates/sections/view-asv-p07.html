Asymptotics of Stochastic Volatility

CEV-Heston and Kou/CGMY Hybrid Frameworks

Combining the building blocks: local-vol backbones under stochastic variance, jumps on top of SV — what each hybrid fixes, what it costs, and how the asymptotics divide the calibration.

1. Why Hybrids Exist

Each pure family fails one empirical demand (Parts 04–05): Lévy smiles don't evolve (skew term-structure wrong); Heston short-dated skews are too flat (diffusive $T\to0$); CEV/local vol fits any single surface but implies wrong forward smiles (smile flattens as spot moves — sticky-strike dynamics). Hybrids assign each block the regime it's good at:

  • CEV-Heston (SVCJ-without-jumps / "SV-CEV"): $dS = \sqrt{v}\,S^{\beta}\,dW_1$, CIR $v$ — backbone (β) controls how ATM vol rides spot; SV (κ, θ, ξ, ρ) controls curvature and term-structure. The λ-SABR/Heston-CEV class used widely in rates LSV engines.
  • Bates / Kou-Heston (SV + jumps): Heston plus compound-Poisson jumps (Gaussian = Bates; double-exponential = Kou-flavored) — jumps make the short end steep (Part 05's linear-in-T mechanism), SV makes the long end evolve and flatten correctly (Part 03).
  • CGMY-CIR time change: run a CGMY clock at stochastic speed $\int v_s ds$ (Carr-Geman-Madan-Yor + CIR) — infinite-activity jumps with vol clustering; characteristic function still closed-form (composition of Laplace transforms): the Fourier stack survives.

This is the series' central practical message: asymptotics make hybrid calibration well-posed — each parameter block is pinned by a regime where the others are asymptotically silent, replacing one big ill-posed fit with staged identifiable ones (the ARM Part 01 ill-posedness cure, by mathematics instead of regularization).

3. Costs and Cautions

  • Parameter count: Bates has 8+ — without the staged identification above, calibrations wander (many (κ, ξ, λ) trade-offs fit one surface); regularize toward stable historical estimates.
  • Moment bookkeeping compounds: the hybrid's critical moments are the minimum of the legs' — jump tails and Heston explosions both constrain damping/quanto integrals (Parts 04–05 checks, run on the composite).
  • Hedging interpretation changes: jump risk is unhedgeable in the diffusive sense — the measure choice (market price of jump risk) is an explicit input; two desks with identical fits can carry different deltas legitimately.
  • Numerics stay Fourier-friendly (all named hybrids keep closed-form characteristic functions) — but branch-cut discipline (CA Part 08) becomes more delicate as exponents compose.

4. A Staged Calibration Recipe (Bates-Type)

  1. Fit jump parameters to the shortest maturities' wings (1–4 weeks) — SV contributes $O(\sqrt{T})$ there, jumps $O(T)$ dominate the steepness.
  2. Fix jumps; fit ρ and an initial $v_0$ to the 1–6m ATM skew and level.
  3. Fit κ, θ, ξ to the 1y+ term-structure of ATM vol and skew decay (the ergodic anchors).
  4. Global polish with all parameters in a narrow trust region; verify staged parameters barely move — if they do, the surface disagrees with the model family (report, don't force).

Key Takeaways

  • Hybrids assign regimes: jumps own the short end, SV the term-structure, backbone/leverage the smile dynamics.
  • Asymptotic separation makes 8-parameter calibrations well-posed via staged fits — the practical payoff of Parts 01–06.
  • Composite moment explosions = min over legs; re-run convergence checks on the hybrid, not the parts.
  • Closed-form characteristic functions survive composition — the Fourier pricing stack carries the whole family.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Hybrid models (like CEV-Heston) combine local volatility and stochastic volatility to capture both short-term skew and long-term smile dynamics.

  • Local-Stochastic Vol (LSV): The gold standard for FX and equity desks, combining the exact fitting of local vol with the realistic dynamics of stoch vol.
  • Calibration Hurdles: Calibrating hybrid models is complex, requiring solving multi-dimensional PDEs or using fast asymptotic approximations.
  • Smile Dynamics: Ensures that when the stock price jumps, the volatility smile moves in a realistic direction (dynamic smile stability).
