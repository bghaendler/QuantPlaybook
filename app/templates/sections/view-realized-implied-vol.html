Realized vs. Implied Volatility Dynamics

Two views of the same quantity: volatility measured from history versus volatility priced by the options market — and the premium that separates them.

1. Realized (Historical) Volatility

The model-free measurement from observed returns. Using $n$ intraday or daily log-returns $r_i$ over a window:

$$\text{RV} = \sqrt{\frac{252}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i^2} \quad \text{(annualized, zero-mean convention)}.$$

High-frequency realized variance (sum of squared 5-minute returns) converges to integrated variance $\int \sigma_t^2 dt$ as sampling frequency rises — until microstructure noise (bid-ask bounce) contaminates it; 5-minute sampling is the standard compromise. Range-based alternatives (Parkinson, Garman-Klass, Yang-Zhang) extract more information per day from OHLC data, with Parkinson's high-low estimator about 5× more efficient than close-to-close.

2. Implied Volatility

The $\sigma$ that equates the Black-Scholes formula to the observed option price:

$$C_{mkt} = C_{BS}(S, K, T, r, \sigma_{imp}).$$

It is a forward-looking, risk-neutral quantity: the market's variance forecast plus compensation for bearing volatility risk. IV varies by strike (smile/skew) and maturity (term structure); a single options market therefore prices an entire surface $\sigma_{imp}(K, T)$. Model-free variance measures (the VIX) aggregate the surface:

$$\text{VIX}^2 \approx \frac{2}{T}\,e^{rT} \int_0^\infty \frac{Q(K)}{K^2}\, dK,$$

the replication cost of a variance swap from a strip of out-of-the-money options.

3. The Volatility Risk Premium

Systematically, implied exceeds subsequently-realized volatility:

$$\text{VRP}_t = \sigma_{imp,t}^2 - \mathbb{E}_t[\text{RV}_{t \to t+T}^2] > 0 \;\text{ on average}.$$

For the S&P 500, implied vol has historically exceeded realized by ~2–4 vol points on average. Why? Buyers of options (crash protection) pay a premium to sellers who bear convexity risk concentrated in the worst states. Consequences:

  • Short-vol strategies (selling delta-hedged straddles, variance swaps) earn an insurance-like premium punctuated by severe drawdowns (Feb 2018 "Volmageddon", Mar 2020).
  • IV is a biased but informative forecast of RV: in forecasting regressions $RV = a + b\,IV + \epsilon$, $b < 1$ but IV usually subsumes historical predictors.

4. Dynamics: How the Two Interact

  • Mean reversion: both series revert; vol-of-vol is itself volatile and spikes are faster than decays (up the escalator, down the elevator — inverted: vol jumps up, bleeds down).
  • Spot-vol correlation: equity index IV moves inversely with spot (leverage effect), formalized by negative $\rho$ in stochastic-vol models and by the skew-stickiness of the surface.
  • Term-structure regimes: contango (upward IV curve) in calm markets — short-dated IV below long-run mean; backwardation in stress.
  • Gamma P&L identity: a delta-hedged option earns $\tfrac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2(\sigma_{real}^2 - \sigma_{imp}^2)\,dt$ per instant — trading "realized vs. implied" is literally the P&L of hedged option books.

Key Takeaways

  • RV measures what happened ($\mathbb{P}$); IV prices what might happen ($\mathbb{Q}$) plus a risk premium.
  • IV > subsequent RV on average — the volatility risk premium funds option sellers.
  • Delta-hedged option P&L ∝ $\Gamma S^2(\sigma_{real}^2 - \sigma_{imp}^2)$: the spread is directly tradable.
  • VIX-style indices are model-free option-strip aggregates of implied variance.
  • Both series mean-revert; spikes are asymmetric and correlate negatively with equity returns.
