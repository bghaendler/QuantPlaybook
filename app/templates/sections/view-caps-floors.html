Interest Rate Derivatives

2. Caps and Floors

Caps and Floors

If an institution has issued a floating rate note (FRN) and has to pay floating rates, it will want to protect itself against too high rates. In this case, it would buy a cap. On the other hand, if an investor invested in a floating rate note and receives floating rates, they will want to protect themselves against too low rates. In this case, they would buy a floor.

Caps and floors are structured as strips of call-like and put-like options on interest rates, respectively. Just as calls and puts are related, caps and floors are linked through a cap-floor parity relation, which connects their values to a corresponding swap contract.

We will price caps and floors using the forward measures developed in the previous sections, specifically utilizing the famous Black's and Bachelier's pricing formulas.

Key Highlights

  • Caplet & Floorlet: Single period interest rate options.
  • Cap & Floor: Strips of caplets and floorlets over multiple reset dates.
  • ATM relation: Under ATM strike rate, a cap's price equals the floor's price.
  • Market standard: Quoting caps/floors in terms of implied Black or Normal volatility parameters.

Caplet Structure and Bond Put Parity

A caplet with reset date $T_0$ and settlement date $T_1 = T_0 + \delta$ pays the holder at time $T_1$ the positive difference between the simple spot rate $L(T_0, T_1)$ and the strike rate $\kappa$. Specifically, the cash flow at time $T_1$ is:

$$ \text{Cash Flow at } T_1 = \delta \bigl(L(T_0, T_1) - \kappa\bigr)^+ $$

where $\delta = T_1 - T_0$ is the accrual factor (e.g., $0.25$ for 3 months, $0.5$ for 6 months). The caplet protects the holder against rising interest rates.

Mathematical Relation to Bond Put Options

The payoff of the caplet at $T_1$ is fully known at reset date $T_0$. We can discount the payoff back to $T_0$ by multiplying by the bond price $P(T_0, T_1)$:

$$ \text{Value at } T_0 = P(T_0, T_1) \delta \bigl(L(T_0, T_1) - \kappa\bigr)^+ $$

Recall that simple spot rate and bond price satisfy $1 + \delta L(T_0, T_1) = \frac{1}{P(T_0, T_1)}$. Rearranging terms allows us to write:

$$ P(T_0, T_1) \delta \bigl(L(T_0, T_1) - \kappa\bigr)^+ = (1 + \delta\kappa) \left( \frac{1}{1 + \delta\kappa} - P(T_0, T_1) \right)^+ $$

This is exactly equivalent to $(1 + \delta\kappa)$ times the payoff of a put option on the $T_1$-bond with maturity $T_0$ and strike price $K_{bond} = \frac{1}{1 + \delta\kappa}$.

By absence of arbitrage, the spot price at time $t \le T_0$ of the caplet is:

$$ Cpl(t, T_0, T_1) = (1 + \delta\kappa) \times P_{put}\left(t, T_0, T_1; \frac{1}{1 + \delta\kappa}\right) $$

Cap-Floor Swap Parity

A cap is a strip of caplets with reset/settlement dates $T_0 < T_1 < \dots < T_n$. At maturity $T_n$, the total cap price is the sum of its individual caplets:

$$ Cp(t) = \sum_{i=1}^n Cpl(t, T_{i-1}, T_i) $$

A floor is a strip of floorlets protecting against low interest rates. Its $i$-th floorlet pays $\delta (\kappa - L(T_{i-1}, T_i))^+$ at $T_i$. Its total price is:

$$ Fl(t) = \sum_{i=1}^n Fll(t, T_{i-1}, T_i) $$

By taking the difference of payoffs, we get $\delta(L - \kappa)^+ - \delta(\kappa - L)^+ = \delta(L - \kappa)$. Summing up these discounted payoffs proves the Cap-Floor Parity Relation:

$$ Cp(t) - Fl(t) = V_p(t) $$

where $V_p(t)$ is the value of a payer swap with fixed rate $\kappa$, notional 1, and the same tenor structure $T_0 < \dots < T_n$ as the cap and floor.

Swap Moneyness Definitions

The swap rate that renders the payer swap value $V_p(t) = 0$ is called the forward swap rate $R_{swap}(t)$:

$$ R_{swap}(t) = \frac{P(t, T_0) - P(t, T_n)}{\sum_{i=1}^n \delta P(t, T_i)} $$

Log-Normal

Black's Pricing Formula

Black's formula assumes that the forward simple spot rate $F(t, T_{i-1}, T_i)$ is log-normally distributed under the $T_i$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$:

$$ dF(t, T_{i-1}, T_i) = F(t, T_{i-1}, T_i)\,\sigma_B\,dW^{T_i}(t) $$

Under this log-normality assumption, the $i$-th caplet and floorlet prices are:

Caplet Price:

$$ Cpl_i = \delta P(t, T_i) \left( F_i \Phi(d_1) - \kappa \Phi(d_2) \right) $$

Floorlet Price:

$$ Fll_i = \delta P(t, T_i) \left( \kappa \Phi(-d_2) - F_i \Phi(-d_1) \right) $$

where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF, $\tau = T_{i-1}-t$, and:

$$ d_{1,2} = \frac{\log(F_i/\kappa) \pm \frac{1}{2}\sigma_B^2\tau}{\sigma_B\sqrt{\tau}} $$

$\sigma_B$ is the relative volatility (Black volatility) which is identical for all caplets belonging to the same cap.

Gaussian

Bachelier's Pricing Formula

Bachelier's formula assumes that the forward simple spot rate $F(t, T_{i-1}, T_i)$ is normally distributed (Gaussian) under the $T_i$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$:

$$ dF(t, T_{i-1}, T_i) = \sigma_N\,dW^{T_i}(t) $$

Under this normality assumption, the $i$-th caplet and floorlet prices are:

Caplet Price:

$$ Cpl_i = \delta P(t, T_i)\,\sigma_N\sqrt{\tau} \left( D \Phi(D) + \phi(D) \right) $$

Floorlet Price:

$$ Fll_i = \delta P(t, T_i)\,\sigma_N\sqrt{\tau} \left( -D \Phi(-D) + \phi(D) \right) $$

where $\phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal PDF, $\tau = T_{i-1}-t$, and:

$$ D = \frac{F_i - \kappa}{\sigma_N\sqrt{\tau}} $$

$\sigma_N$ is the normal volatility (absolute volatility or basis point volatility). It is typically roughly 100 times smaller than $\sigma_B$.
