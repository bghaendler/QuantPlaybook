Black-Scholes PDE Parabolic Framework

The Black-Scholes equation as a parabolic PDE: derivation by hedging, reduction to the heat equation, boundary conditions as contracts, and what parabolicity buys you.

1. Derivation in Five Lines (Hedging Argument)

Hold the option $V(S,t)$, short $\Delta$ shares: $\Pi = V - \Delta S$. Itô (Part 10) gives

$$d\Pi = \left(V_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2V_{SS}\right)dt + \left(V_S - \Delta\right)dS.$$

Choose $\Delta = V_S$ — the $dS$ (risky) term vanishes; the portfolio is instantaneously riskless and must earn $r$: $d\Pi = r\Pi\,dt$. Equate:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0.$$

Note what's absent: $\mu$. The drift hedges away — the economic content of risk-neutral pricing, visible directly in the PDE.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The Black-Scholes PDE is derived by constructing a risk-free portfolio of the option and the stock, eliminating random noise.

  • Delta Hedging: We hold exactly the derivative shares of stock to cancel out the option's price risk.
  • No Arbitrage: Since the hedged portfolio has zero risk, it must earn exactly the risk-free interest rate.

2. Why "Parabolic" Matters

For $a V_{xx} + b V_{xt} + c V_{tt} + \dots$, the discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ classifies: elliptic (<0), parabolic (=0), hyperbolic (>0). Black-Scholes has $b = c = 0$ ⇒ parabolic — the diffusion/heat family. Consequences:

  • Smoothing: kinked payoffs become $C^\infty$ instantly before expiry — gamma is finite for $t < T$ and blows up only at the kink at expiry.
  • One time direction: well-posed solved backwards from the terminal payoff (diffusion forwards in $\tau = T - t$); solving it the wrong way is exponentially unstable.
  • Maximum principle: solutions attain extremes on the boundary — the PDE-level reason for no-arbitrage price bounds and monotonicity in volatility.
  • Numerics: the parabolic toolkit applies verbatim: explicit/implicit/Crank-Nicolson finite differences with the $\Delta t \lesssim \Delta S^2$ stability constraint for explicit schemes.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Parabolic PDEs describe diffusion processes (like heat spreading). In finance, this means option values diffuse smoothly from expiration.

  • Smoothing Payoffs: The sharp, angled payoff of an option at expiration diffuses into a smooth pricing curve today.
  • Stability: Parabolic PDEs have stable solutions that can be solved backward using standard numerical grids.

3. Reduction to the Heat Equation

Substitute $S = Ke^x$, $t = T - \frac{2\tau}{\sigma^2}$, $V = K e^{\alpha x + \beta\tau} u(x, \tau)$ with $\alpha = -\frac{1}{2}(k-1)$, $\beta = -\frac{1}{4}(k+1)^2$, $k = \frac{2r}{\sigma^2}$:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}.$$

Black-Scholes is the heat equation in log-price coordinates — option value diffuses backwards from the payoff exactly as heat spreads from an initial temperature profile. The fundamental solution (heat kernel) $\frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\tau}}e^{-x^2/4\tau}$ becomes, after untransforming, the lognormal risk-neutral density; convolving the call payoff against it reproduces the Black-Scholes formula — the PDE route to the same Gaussian integral as Part 04.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: We solve the Black-Scholes PDE by transforming it into the standard heat equation from physics, using change-of-variables.

  • Log-Price Mapping: Mapping the logarithm of the stock price removes variable coefficients, turning it into a constant-coefficient heat equation.
  • Known Solution: Allows quants to reuse centuries of physics math to write the closed-form Black-Scholes formula.

4. Boundary & Terminal Conditions = The Contract

ContractTerminal ($t = T$)Spatial boundaries
European call$(S - K)^+$$V(0,t) = 0$; $V \sim S - Ke^{-r\tau}$ as $S \to \infty$

American put$(K - S)^+$Free boundary $S^*(t)$: $V = K - S$, $V_S = -1$ (smooth pasting) — an obstacle problem, $\mathcal{L}V \le 0$, $V \ge$ payoff

Same operator every time; only the conditions change. That is the practical power of the PDE view: one solver, many products — and exotic features (barriers, early exercise, discrete dividends as jump conditions $V(S, t_d^-) = V(S - D, t_d^+)$) enter as conditions, not new theory.

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: The boundary and terminal conditions of the PDE are what define the specific option contract being priced.

  • Terminal Condition: The option payoff at expiration serves as the terminal condition of the equation.
  • Boundary Conditions: Define what happens at the extremes, such as when the stock price goes to zero or infinity.

Key Takeaways

  • Delta hedging kills the $dW$ term and the drift with it: the PDE contains $r$, never $\mu$.
  • Parabolic ⇒ smoothing, backward time direction, maximum principle, and the standard FD toolkit.
  • Log-price + scaling transforms BS exactly into $u_\tau = u_{xx}$; the heat kernel is the lognormal density in disguise.
  • The operator is the model; terminal/boundary conditions are the contract — barriers are Dirichlet data, American exercise is a free boundary.
