Q1. Suppose that $W(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion. Which of the following processes are martingales?
$\square \ W(t)^2$
$\square \ W(t)^3 - \int_0^t W(s) ds$
$\square \ W(t)^2 - t$
$\square \ e^{2W(t) - 2t}$
$W(t)^2$ should not be selected

Applying Ito's lemma to $W(t)^2$ gives:

$$ dW(t)^2 = 2W(t)dW(t) + dt. $$

This cannot be a martingale since there is a non-zero drift term.

$e^{2W(t) - 2t}$ is correct

The process is adapted since $W(t)$ is adapted. For $t \le T$ we have:

\begin{align*} \mathbb{E}_t[e^{2W(T)-2T}] &= \mathbb{E}_t[e^{2(W(T)-W(t))} e^{2W(t)}] e^{-2T} \\ &= \mathbb{E}_t[e^{2(W(T)-W(t))}] e^{2W(t)-2T} \\ &= e^{2(T-t)} e^{2W(t)-2T} = e^{2W(t)-2t}, \end{align*}

where we have used independence of $W(T) - W(t)$ and $\mathcal{F}_t$, and the moment generating function of a Gaussian random variable.

Note on the other options:

  • $W(t)^3 - \int_0^t W(s)ds$: Has drift $2W(t) dt$. (Not a martingale)
  • $W(t)^2 - t$: $d(W(t)^2 - t) = 2W(t) dW(t) + dt - dt = 2W(t) dW(t)$. No drift term. (Martingale)
Q2. Suppose that $W(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion and define the process $Z(t) = t^2 W(t)$. Derive the volatility of $Z(t)$ using the integration by parts formula.

Solution: By applying the integration by parts formula (or Itô's product rule) to $f(t)W(t)$ where $f(t) = t^2$:

$$ dZ(t) = d(t^2 W(t)) = W(t)d(t^2) + t^2 dW(t) + d[t^2, W](t) $$

$$ dZ(t) = 2t W(t) dt + t^2 dW(t) + 0 $$

The volatility is the coefficient scaling the Brownian noise $dW(t)$. Therefore, the volatility is $t^2$.

Correct Answer: $t^2$

Q3. Suppose $X(t)$ is an Itô process satisfying $dX(t) = 2X(t)dt + X(t)dW(t)$ with $X(0) = 1$. What is the probability that $X(1) > 3$?

Solution: By applying Itô's lemma to $\log(X(t))$ we get the following solution for $X(1)$:

$$ X(1) = e^{3/2 + W(1)}. $$

Since the logarithm is an increasing function we have:

$$ \mathbb{P}[X(1) > 3] = \mathbb{P}[\log(X(1)) > \log(3)] = 1 - \Phi(\log(3) - 3/2), $$

where $\Phi$ represents the standard normal cumulative distribution function.

Correct Answer: $65.59\%$

Q4. Suppose $W_1(t)$ and $W_2(t)$ are two independent standard Brownian motions. What is the probability that both processes are larger than 1 at $t=1$?

Solution: We want to find $\mathbb{P}(W_1(1) > 1 \text{ and } W_2(1) > 1)$.

Since they are independent standard Brownian motions, $W_1(1)$ and $W_2(1)$ are independent standard normal variables $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

$$ \mathbb{P}(W_1(1) > 1) \times \mathbb{P}(W_2(1) > 1) = (1 - \Phi(1))^2 $$

Since $\Phi(1) \approx 0.8413$, the probability is $(1 - 0.8413)^2 = 0.1587^2 \approx 0.0252$.

Correct Answer: $2.52\%$

Q5. Suppose $W(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion. Calculate the probability $\mathbb{P}[1 < W(1) < W(3) - 1]$.

Solution: The condition $1 < W(1) < W(3) - 1$ can be rewritten into two independent events:

1) $W(1) > 1$

2) $W(3) - W(1) > 1$

By the definition of Brownian motion, the increments $W(1) - W(0)$ and $W(3) - W(1)$ are strictly independent.

$$ \mathbb{P}(W(1) > 1) = 1 - \Phi(1) \approx 0.1587 $$

The increment $W(3) - W(1) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2)$. To standardize it, we divide by $\sqrt{2}$:

$$ \mathbb{P}(W(3) - W(1) > 1) = \mathbb{P}\left(Z > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 1 - \Phi(0.7071) \approx 0.2397 $$

Multiplying the independent probabilities: $0.1587 \times 0.2397 = 0.0380$.

Correct Answer: $3.80\%$

Q6. Suppose $W(t)$ is a standard Brownian motion. Conditional on the event that $W(1)$ is positive, what is the probability that it is negative at $t=2$?

Solution: We want to find $\mathbb{P}(W(2) < 0 \mid W(1)> 0)$.

By Bayes' rule, this is $\frac{\mathbb{P}(W(2) < 0 \text{ and } W(1)> 0)}{\mathbb{P}(W(1) > 0)}$.

Let $X = W(1)$ and $Y = W(2) - W(1)$. Since both are standard normal $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and independent, we can represent them as coordinates $(X,Y)$ on a 2D plane.

We need $X > 0$ and $X + Y < 0 \implies Y < -X$.

Graphically, this is the region in the right half-plane ($X>0$) but below the line $Y = -X$. This forms a 45-degree wedge out of the full 360-degree plane, representing $\frac{1}{8}$ of the total probability.

Since $\mathbb{P}(W(1) > 0) = \frac{1}{2}$, the conditional probability is $\frac{1/8}{1/2} = \frac{1}{4}$.

Correct Answer: $25.00\%$

Q7. Which of the following transformed processes $Z(t)$ is again a standard Brownian motion?
$\square \ Z(t) = \frac{1}{2}W(4t)$
$\square \ Z(t) = \frac{1}{4}W(4t)$
$\square \ Z(t) = t W(1/t) \text{ for } t > 0, \text{ and } 0 \text{ for } t = 0$

Solution: A standard Brownian motion must have variance $t$, continuous paths, and stationary independent increments.

  • $Z(t) = \frac{1}{2}W(4t)$: $Var(Z(t)) = \frac{1}{4} Var(W(4t)) = \frac{1}{4}(4t) = t$. (Yes)
  • $Z(t) = \frac{1}{4}W(4t)$: $Var(Z(t)) = \frac{1}{16}(4t) = t/4 \neq t$. (Not standard)
  • $Z(t) = tW(1/t)$: This is the known time inversion of Brownian motion. $Var(tW(1/t)) = t^2 (1/t) = t$. By the Strong Law of Large Numbers, $\lim_{t \to 0} tW(1/t) = 0$, preserving continuity. (Yes)

Correct Answer: $Z(t) = \frac{1}{2}W(4t)$ and $Z(t) = tW(1/t)$

Q8. For which of the following processes can we find an equivalent probability measure through Girsanov's theorem such that the process becomes a standard Brownian motion?
$\square \ dX_1(t) = 5dt + dW(t)$
$\square \ dX_2(t) = 5dt + 2dW(t)$
$\square \ dX_3(t) = W(t)dt + dW(t)$

Solution: Girsanov's theorem can only change the drift of a process, never the volatility (quadratic variation). Therefore, the volatility term must already match standard Brownian motion (i.e., exactly $1 \times dW(t)$).

  • $dX_1(t) = 5dt + dW(t)$: Volatility is 1. We can set the risk premium shift to $\lambda(t) = -5$ to remove the drift. (Yes)
  • $dX_2(t) = 5dt + 2dW(t)$: Volatility is 2. No measure change can alter quadratic variation. (No)
  • $dX_3(t) = W(t)dt + dW(t)$: Volatility is 1. We can set $\lambda(t) = -W(t)$ (satisfies Novikov's condition). (Yes)

Correct Answer: $dX_1(t) = 5dt + dW(t)$ and $dX_3(t) = W(t)dt + dW(t)$

Q9. Consider a market where you can trade in the money market account with constant short rate $r = 5\%$ and two assets $S_1$ and $S_2$. Which specification of their returns under $\mathbb{P}$ is compatible with the Arbitrage Pricing Theorem?
1. $\frac{dS_1}{S_1} = 0.2dt + 0.15dW, \quad \frac{dS_2}{S_2} = 0.3dt + 0.25dW$
2. $\frac{dS_1}{S_1} = 0.25dt + 0.15dW, \quad \frac{dS_2}{S_2} = 0.1dt + 0.25dW$
3. $\frac{dS_1}{S_1} = 0.05dt + 0.15dW, \quad \frac{dS_2}{S_2} = 0.25dW$

Solution: The Arbitrage Pricing Theorem dictates that there must be no free lunch. All assets exposed to the same source of risk $dW(t)$ must offer the exact same unit compensation for that risk, known as the Market Price of Risk ($\lambda$).

$$ \lambda = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma} $$

Let's check the options for $r=0.05$:

  • Option 1:
    $\lambda_1 = (0.2 - 0.05)/0.15 = 1$
    $\lambda_2 = (0.3 - 0.05)/0.25 = 1$. Both $\lambda=1$. (Compatible!)
  • Option 2:
    $\lambda_1 = (0.25 - 0.05)/0.15 \approx 1.33$
    $\lambda_2 = (0.1 - 0.05)/0.25 = 0.2$. Arbitrage exists! (Not compatible)
  • Option 3:
    $\lambda_1 = (0.05 - 0.05)/0.15 = 0$
    $\lambda_2 = (0 - 0.05)/0.25 = -0.2$. Arbitrage exists! (Not compatible)

Correct Answer: The first specification (Option 1)
