Reduced-Form Intensity Models

Default as a surprise: model the hazard rate of an unpredictable stopping time, calibrate it to market spreads, and price credit like interest rates.

1. The Intensity Framework

Instead of explaining why default happens (structural), model when: default is the first jump of a point process with intensity $\lambda_t$ — the instantaneous default probability per unit time given survival:

$$\mathbb{P}(\tau \in [t, t+dt] \mid \tau > t, \mathcal{F}_t) = \lambda_t\, dt.$$

Survival probability mirrors discounting:

$$Q(t, T) = \mathbb{P}(\tau > T \mid \tau > t) = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{-\int_t^T \lambda_s\, ds}\right],$$

so the entire bond-pricing toolbox (affine models, HJM) transfers wholesale with $\lambda$ in the role of $r$. Default arrives as a total surprise (inaccessible stopping time) — exactly what diffusion-based structural models cannot produce, and why intensity models generate realistic short-dated spreads.

2. Pricing Defaultable Claims

A zero-recovery defaultable zero-coupon bond (doubly-stochastic/Cox setting, $\lambda$ independent of default itself):

$$\bar{P}(t, T) = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{-\int_t^T (r_s + \lambda_s)\, ds}\right]$$

— discount at the risky rate $r + \lambda$. With recovery $R$ (fraction of market value, Duffie-Singleton), the effective discount spread becomes the loss rate:

$$s \approx (1 - R)\,\lambda,$$

the credit triangle: spread = loss-given-default × hazard. A 200bp spread with 40% recovery implies λ ≈ 3.33%/yr and one-year survival ≈ 96.7%. CDS pricing equates premium and protection legs:

$$s_{CDS} \int_0^T D(u) Q(u)\, du \;=\; (1-R)\int_0^T D(u)\, (-dQ(u)),$$

with $D$ the risk-free discount; bootstrapping $Q(\cdot)$ from the CDS curve (piecewise-constant λ) is the market-standard ISDA model's core.

3. Specifying λ: Affine Machinery

  • Constant λ: exponential default time — the back-of-envelope and CDS-bootstrap workhorse.
  • CIR intensity: $d\lambda = \kappa(\theta - \lambda)dt + \sigma\sqrt{\lambda}\, dW$ — positive, mean-reverting, closed-form survival via affine exponents; add jumps for spread gapping.
  • Correlated $(r, \lambda)$: joint affine models price counterparty-risky rates trades (wrong-way risk when λ rises as exposure rises).
  • Stochastic λ matters for options on credit (CDS swaptions, callable bonds): spread vol is the new vol surface.

Hybrids (Duffie-Lando): structural fundamentals observed with noise generate an intensity — reconciling the two views formally.

5. Portfolio Credit & Correlation

For multi-name products, intensities must be correlated. Choices: common-factor intensities (doubly-stochastic), contagion/self-exciting models (Hawkes; default of one raises others' λ), or — historically dominant for CDOs — copulas gluing single-name survival curves together (next section). The 2008 lesson: independence-ish correlation calibrated in calm markets catastrophically understated joint tail risk.

Key Takeaways

  • Model the hazard λ, not the cause: survival = $\mathbb{E}[e^{-\int\lambda}]$, pricing = discount at $r + \lambda$.
  • Credit triangle: spread ≈ $(1-R)\lambda$ — the one formula every credit quant uses daily.
  • CDS curves bootstrap $Q(t,T)$ with piecewise-constant λ (ISDA standard model).
  • Affine/CIR intensities import the entire rates toolbox to credit, including spread options.
  • Reduced-form prices, structural explains; hybrids and contagion models bridge to portfolios.
