General Nth Order Linear Homogeneous Equations

Extension of constant coefficient linear differential operators to arbitrary orders, polynomial roots, and worked higher-order cases.

1. General Nth Order Homogeneous Formulation

The general form of an $n^{\text{th}}$-order linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients is:

$$a_n \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} + a_{n-1} \frac{d^{n-1} y}{dx^{n-1}} + \dots + a_1 \frac{dy}{dx} + a_0 y = 0$$

Substituting the trial exponential form $y = e^{\lambda x}$ produces an $n^{\text{th}}$-degree polynomial Auxiliary Equation:

$$a_n \lambda^n + a_{n-1} \lambda^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 \lambda + a_0 = 0$$

Solving the differential equation reduces entirely to finding the roots of this polynomial.

2. Three Root Cases & Solution Construction

Case 1: $n$ Distinct Roots

Each distinct real root $\lambda_i$ generates an independent solution $e^{\lambda_i x}$. The general solution is a linear combination of all $n$ terms:

$$y = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 x} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 x} + \dots + c_n e^{\lambda_n x}$$ Case 2: $r$-Fold Repeated Real Root

When a root $\lambda$ repeats $r$ times ($r \le n$), it generates $r$ linearly independent solutions by scaling with increasing powers of $x$. Only the repeated roots are scaled; all other roots are treated separately:

$$y = \left( c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2 + \dots + c_r x^{r-1} \right) e^{\lambda x}$$ Case 3: $r$-Fold Repeated Complex Conjugate Roots

When a complex root pair $p \pm iq$ is repeated $r$ times, the solutions take the form of $e^{px}$ multiplied by combinations of cosine and sine terms, with each repetition introducing an additional factor of $x$:

$$y = e^{px} \left[ \left( A_1 + A_2 x + \dots + A_r x^{r-1} \right)\cos qx + \left( B_1 + B_2 x + \dots + B_r x^{r-1} \right)\sin qx \right]$$

3. Worked Polynomial Examples

Worked Example 1: Fourth-Order Equation with Distinct Roots

Find general solution for $\lambda^4 - 5\lambda^2 + 6 = 0$. Factoring:

$$(\lambda^2 - 2)(\lambda^2 - 3) = 0 \implies \lambda = \pm\sqrt{2}, \quad \lambda = \pm\sqrt{3}$$

$$y = c_1 e^{\sqrt{2}x} + c_2 e^{-\sqrt{2}x} + c_3 e^{\sqrt{3}x} + c_4 e^{-\sqrt{3}x}$$

Worked Example 2: Sixth-Order Equation with Repeated Root

Find general solution for $\lambda^6 - 5\lambda^4 = 0 \implies \lambda^4(\lambda^2 - 5) = 0$:

  • $\lambda = 0$ is a fourfold repeated root ($r = 4$), generating: $1, x, x^2, x^3$ (since $e^{0x} = 1$).
  • $\lambda = \pm\sqrt{5}$ are distinct roots, contributing $e^{\sqrt{5}x}$ and $e^{-\sqrt{5}x}$.

$$y = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2 + c_4 x^3 + c_5 e^{\sqrt{5}x} + c_6 e^{-\sqrt{5}x}$$

Plain English Notes

The Big Picture: Solving higher-order equations (3rd order, 4th order, and beyond) works exactly like solving second-order ones—we just factor larger polynomials.

  • Polynomial Roots: Replacing derivatives with powers of $\lambda$ turns a high-order calculus equation into an $N$-th degree algebra polynomial. The roots of this polynomial dictate the shape of your solutions.
  • Distinct Roots: Every distinct real root gives you a clean exponential term $e^{\lambda_i x}$.
  • Repeated Roots: If a root repeats, you can't just list it twice because the solutions must be independent. You make them independent by multiplying the repeated terms by increasing powers of $x$ (e.g. $(c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2) e^{\lambda x}$).
  • Repeated Complex Roots: If a complex wave root repeats, its sines and cosines get scaled by increasing powers of $x$ too (e.g. $x e^{px} \cos qx$), representing growing oscillations.
