Lookback Options (Goldman-Sosin-Gatto)

Options on the running maximum or minimum: buy at the low, sell at the high — for a price. Closed forms from the law of the Brownian extremum.

1. Contract Types

  • Floating-strike lookback call: pays $S_T - m_T$ where $m_T = \min_{t \le T} S_t$ — you effectively bought at the minimum. Floating put pays $M_T - S_T$ (sold at the maximum, $M_T = \max S_t$). Never out of the money.
  • Fixed-strike lookback call: pays $\max(M_T - K, 0)$ — exercise against the best price seen. Fixed put: $\max(K - m_T, 0)$.
  • Partial/window lookbacks: extremum monitored over a sub-period (Heynen-Kat) — cheaper, common in structures.

Lookbacks monetize regret. Correspondingly they are expensive: a floating-strike lookback costs roughly twice the ATM vanilla under typical parameters.

2. The Mathematics: Law of the Maximum

For drifted Brownian motion $X_t = \nu t + \sigma W_t$, the reflection principle gives the joint law of $(X_T, M_T^X)$:

$$\mathbb{P}(M_T^X \ge a,\ X_T \le x) = e^{2\nu a/\sigma^2}\, N\!\left(\frac{x - 2a - \nu T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}\right), \quad a \ge \max(0, x).$$

Integrating payoffs against this density yields Goldman-Sosin-Gatto (1979). Floating-strike call, with $b$ the carry:

$$C_{fl} = S e^{(b-r)T} N(a_1) - m\, e^{-rT} N(a_2) + S e^{-rT} \frac{\sigma^2}{2b}\left[\left(\frac{S}{m}\right)^{-2b/\sigma^2} N\!\left(-a_1 + \frac{2b}{\sigma}\sqrt{T}\right) - e^{bT} N(-a_1)\right],$$ $$a_1 = \frac{\ln(S/m) + (b + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad a_2 = a_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T},$$

where $m$ is the minimum observed so far (at inception $m = S$). The first two terms are a vanilla struck at $m$; the bracketed term — the strike-bonus — prices the right to keep resetting the strike to new lows. (For $b = 0$ take the limit; Conze-Viswanathan supply the fixed-strike formulas.)

3. Properties & Greeks

  • Vol-loving beyond vanillas: value increases roughly linearly in σ with nearly double a vanilla's vega — the extremum distribution widens twice as fast as the terminal one. Lookbacks ≈ leveraged vol trades.
  • Delta resets: at a new minimum (floating call), the contract is momentarily ATM-on-the-strike; delta has a kink along $S = m$. Hedging is a continuous strike-rollover: Goldman-Sosin-Gatto showed the hedge is self-financing rolls of vanillas — the origin of their "options to buy at the low" replication story.
  • Monitoring frequency bites: a daily-monitored maximum is strictly below the continuous one; discrete corrections (Broadie-Glasserman-Kou $\beta = 0.5826$ shifts) or direct lattice/MC pricing handle real term sheets.

4. Numerical Example (flavor)

$S = m = 100$, $r = 5\%$, $b = 5\%$, $\sigma = 20\%$, $T = 1$: vanilla ATM call ≈ 10.45, floating-strike lookback call ≈ 16–17, fixed-strike ($K = 100$) lookback call ≈ 18–19. The premium over the vanilla is the expected depth of the minimum below the start — about $0.8\,\sigma\sqrt{T}$ in price terms for these parameters. Double the vol and the lookback premium roughly doubles; the vanilla's rises by less.

5. Where They Appear

  • Structured retail: "best-entry" certificates (strike = lowest monthly close of the first year) and "high-water-mark" payout notes — partial lookbacks in disguise.
  • Hedge-fund fees: high-water-mark performance fees are claims on the running maximum of NAV — lookback mathematics prices the manager's option.
  • Russian options (perpetual lookbacks) and drawdown insurance generalize the same extremum machinery.
  • Liquidity is thin; they trade as bespoke OTC or embedded features, priced off SLV models with the closed forms as controls.

Key Takeaways

  • Floating lookbacks trade at the realized extremum (never OTM); fixed lookbacks strike against it.
  • Closed forms (GSG/Conze-Viswanathan) integrate payoffs against the reflection-principle law of $(S_T, \text{extremum})$.
  • Price ≈ vanilla struck at the extremum-so-far + strike-bonus term for future resets.
  • ≈ 2× vanilla vega: lookbacks are concentrated volatility bets; discrete monitoring reduces value materially.
  • High-water marks and best-entry features make lookback math relevant far beyond the listed product.
